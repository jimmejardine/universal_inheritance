# Whataboutery: "Why Not Some Other Tax Instead?"

**Executive summary.** "Why not a wealth tax / land value tax / higher income tax / just close the loopholes?" is often a sincere question and sometimes a deflection; the honest answer is that most of the alternatives are complements, not substitutes, but none of them does the one thing this proposal does — tax the *stock* of wealth at the single moment of transmission, when the holder cannot re-optimise and the recipient has done nothing to earn it. The comparison table below is the core of the answer; the structural argument behind it is [`../03-case-for/piketty-r-vs-g.md`](../03-case-for/piketty-r-vs-g.md).

Rules of engagement: concede, one-liner, full answer. Master table in [README.md](README.md).

## The comparison table

| Criterion | Recipient-based inheritance tax (this proposal) | Annual wealth tax | Land value tax | Higher top income tax | Close existing IHT loopholes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Targets the wealth stock at transmission? | **Yes** — at the one moment the holder cannot respond | Taxes the stock, but continuously and while the owner can restructure | Land only; other asset classes untouched | No — barely touches existing stock or unrealised gains | Weakly — 40% estate basis retained |
| Behavioural distortion on the living | Minimal: 0% on all lifetime earning, saving, spending | Ongoing annual drag on savers, owners, entrepreneurs | Very low — near-textbook efficiency for the land element | High at margins on labour, realisation, incorporation | As now |
| Valuation burden | Once, per transfer event | **Every asset, every person, every year** | Annual, but land only (site value, well understood) | None (flows self-report) | As now, per estate |
| Avoidance surface | Narrow if lifetime-cumulative with anti-avoidance spine | Very large — proven by the repeal wave (Austria 1994, Germany 1997, Sweden 2007, France 2018) [1] | Small — land cannot move or hide | Income shifting, deferral, incorporation | Same thicket minus a few hedges |
| Political durability | High *if* paired with visible endowment ([`../05-history/abolitions.md`](../05-history/abolitions.md)) | Low — OECD net wealth taxes fell from 12 (1990) to 4 (2017) [1] | Untested at national scale in modern era; transition losers are loud | Moderate — but top rates cycle with governments | Low — reliefs historically regrow |
| Revenue potential | Moderate-to-large; structural aim primary ([`../02-proposal/thresholds.md`](../02-proposal/thresholds.md)) | Modest in practice everywhere tried — around 1% or less of tax revenue except Switzerland [1][2] | Substantial and steady | Substantial | Small (single-digit £bn) [3] |
| Breaks dynastic transmission? | **Yes — by construction** | Slowly, and only at confiscatory rates | No | No | No |

## "Why not a wealth tax instead?"

**Concede first.** The [Saez–Zucman case for annual wealth taxation](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/progressive-wealth-taxation/) targets the same underlying problem — concentrated stocks — and a modest annual levy polls well; the two instruments are intellectual cousins, not rivals.

**One-line answer.** An annual wealth tax must solve the valuation problem for every asset, every person, every year, while the living owner actively restructures against it — this proposal solves it once per lifetime, against an owner who is dead.

**Full answer.**

- **The wealth-tax repeal record is the inheritance-tax abolition story amplified:** twelve OECD countries ran net wealth taxes in 1990; by 2017 only four remained (France, Norway, Spain, Switzerland) [1], and France converted its ISF into a property-only levy in 2018 [2] — felled by valuation cost, avoidance, and emigration, with the ISF the canonical case.
- **Every difficulty in [practicality.md](practicality.md#you-cant-value-a-private-company-an-artwork-or-a-farm) recurs annually** instead of once, and against a live counterparty.
- **The once-off valuation is not even new work.** [Probate already compels a full estate valuation](https://www.gov.uk/valuing-estate-of-someone-who-died) today, tax due or not, and the US already values every inherited asset at death to set the heir's stepped-up capital-gains basis ([IRC §1014](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/26/1014)) — this proposal changes what turns on a valuation the system already performs; a wealth tax would have to invent ~40–60 new valuation events per estate ([`../03-case-for/wealth-tax-comparison.md`](../03-case-for/wealth-tax-comparison.md) §2).
- **The transfer moment is the unique point where the elasticity collapses:** the decedent cannot emigrate, restructure, or lobby ([`../03-case-for/piketty-r-vs-g.md`](../03-case-for/piketty-r-vs-g.md)).
- **The strongest current variant — Zucman's 2% minimum *floor* on the largest fortunes (G20 blueprint 2024; passed by the French National Assembly, February 2025) — gets its own answer** in [`../03-case-for/wealth-tax-comparison.md`](../03-case-for/wealth-tax-comparison.md) §5. Concede what is true: it genuinely repairs the income tax's regressivity at the top, on a base of only a few thousand households, and this project shares its diagnosis. Then the boundary: on [Zucman's own figures](https://gabriel-zucman.eu/files/Zucman2026SlidesLSE.pdf) billionaire wealth has compounded at 7.5% a year in real terms since 1987 — a 2% floor slows the dynasty without ending the transmission. Complement (plausibly a transition-period bridge instrument), not substitute.
- **Verdict:** if a country can run only one stock-targeting tax, transmission is the high-leverage point; a wealth tax is a possible supplement, not a substitute.

## "Why not a land value tax instead?"

**Concede first.** Almost everything Georgists say about LVT is right: land rents are unearned, the tax is close to distortion-free, land cannot flee, and housing dysfunction ([`../01-problem/housing.md`](../01-problem/housing.md)) is substantially a land problem. This project should be seen as an ally of LVT, and the overlap is genuine — both tax the unearned.

**One-line answer.** LVT taxes one asset class's *flow of rent*; it does nothing about the transmission of financial, business, and offshore wealth, which is where the largest dynasties actually live — so it complements the inheritance tax and cannot replace it.

**Full answer.**

- **A dynasty holding equities, bonds, private-company stakes, and art pays no LVT on any of it.**
- **LVT deliberately leaves transmission untouched:** the landowning family that pays its annual site-value charge still passes the estate, and the rank it confers, intact — the dynastic-break objective ([`../16-second-order/dynasties-and-power.md`](../16-second-order/dynasties-and-power.md)) is simply outside LVT's scope.
- **The right relationship is a portfolio:** LVT disciplines land rents year by year; the inheritance tax breaks concentration at generational handover. They share the moral premise (tax the unearned, spare the earned) and the political coalition ([`../06-palatability/coalition.md`](../06-palatability/coalition.md)).
- Engaging Georgists as allies rather than rivals is the recommended posture in [`../12-political-adoption/policy-entrepreneurs.md`](../12-political-adoption/policy-entrepreneurs.md).

## "Why not just raise income tax at the top?"

**Concede first.** Top income tax is administratively mature, well-understood, and progressive; if the problem were merely high incomes, it would be the whole answer.

**One-line answer.** Income tax touches flows, and when the return on inherited capital outruns growth (*r > g*), no politically feasible flow tax stabilises the *stock* distribution — you cannot income-tax your way out of a dynasty that already exists.

**Full answer.** This is the structural heart of the whole project, argued in full at [`../03-case-for/piketty-r-vs-g.md`](../03-case-for/piketty-r-vs-g.md):

- **Existing fortunes largely compound as unrealised gains that income tax never sees.** The heirs of the great estates owe income tax only on what they choose to realise, and step-up/uplift rules can erase even that at each generational handover ([fairness.md](fairness.md#double-taxation-i-already-paid-tax-on-that-money)).
- **Every marginal point of income tax bears on labour** — the earned channel the meritocratic case wants to *protect*.
- **The proposal's division of labour is deliberate:** lighter on earning, decisive on unearned receipt.
- Raising top income-tax rates is a separate debate with separate merits; it is not a substitute for a transmission tax because it does not act on the same variable.

## "Why not just close the existing loopholes?"

**Concede first.** UK IHT's relief thicket — business and agricultural property reliefs, the seven-year rule, trust treatments — is indefensible on its own terms, and closing it would be a genuine improvement worth supporting on the way to anything better.

**One-line answer.** Do close them — it is the natural first legislative step ([`../12-political-adoption/stepping-stones.md`](../12-political-adoption/stepping-stones.md)) — but a patched 40% estate tax still carries the three structural defects that killed its cousins: estate basis (the "death tax" frame), no lifetime cumulation (the gift escape), and no visible beneficiary (the political-economy asymmetry).

**Full answer.** Loophole-closing accepts the existing architecture, and the architecture is the problem:

- **An estate-based tax invites the grieving-family framing** however clean its base ([`../05-history/abolitions.md`](../05-history/abolitions.md), error #2 in the failure pattern).
- **Without lifetime cumulation of receipts, early giving remains a legal exit** ([avoidance.md](avoidance.md#theyll-just-gift-everything-early)).
- **Revenue flowing to the general pot leaves the tax politically naked** — concentrated losers, diffuse winners — which is how the reliefs grew back last time and would again. Reliefs are the sediment of decades of lobbying; draining them without changing the political economy that deposited them buys perhaps a decade.
- **That said, the sequencing case for starting here is strong** and is made in [`../15-transition/sequencing.md`](../15-transition/sequencing.md) and [`../12-political-adoption/stepping-stones.md`](../12-political-adoption/stepping-stones.md): a loophole-closed, recipient-converted IHT is the natural chrysalis of this proposal.

## Verdict: complements, not substitutes — but one keystone

None of the alternatives is an enemy. A sane long-run tax system might well contain LVT and a reformed income tax alongside this proposal, and the coalition arithmetic improves when each camp treats the others as allies ([`../06-palatability/coalition.md`](../06-palatability/coalition.md)). But on the specific problem this project diagnoses — the recongealing of hereditary economic dynasties and the collapse of the equal-start premise ([`../01-problem/README.md`](../01-problem/README.md)) — the recipient-based inheritance tax is the highest-leverage single instrument, because it alone acts on the stock, at transmission, against a counterparty who cannot respond, with proceeds that visibly fund every citizen's start.

Whataboutery in debate is usually a request to fight on easier ground; the answer is to welcome the allies and return to the variable that matters.
