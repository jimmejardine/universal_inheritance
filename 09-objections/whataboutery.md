# Whataboutery: "Why Not Some Other Tax Instead?"

**Executive summary.** "Why not a wealth tax / land value tax / higher income tax / just close the loopholes?" is often a sincere question and sometimes a deflection; the honest answer is that most of the alternatives are complements, not substitutes, but none of them does the one thing this proposal does — tax the *stock* of wealth at the single moment of transmission, when the holder cannot re-optimise and the recipient has done nothing to earn it. The comparison table below is the core of the answer; the structural argument behind it is [`../03-case-for/piketty-r-vs-g.md`](../03-case-for/piketty-r-vs-g.md).

Rules of engagement: concede, one-liner, full answer. Master table in [README.md](README.md).

## The comparison table

| Criterion | Recipient-based inheritance tax (this proposal) | Annual wealth tax | Land value tax | Higher top income tax | Close existing IHT loopholes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Targets the wealth stock at transmission? | **Yes** — at the one moment the holder cannot respond | Taxes the stock, but continuously and while the owner can restructure | Land only; other asset classes untouched | No — barely touches existing stock or unrealised gains | Weakly — 40% estate basis retained |
| Behavioural distortion on the living | Minimal: 0% on all lifetime earning, saving, spending | Ongoing annual drag on savers, owners, entrepreneurs | Very low — near-textbook efficiency for the land element | High at margins on labour, realisation, incorporation | As now |
| Valuation burden | Once, per transfer event | **Every asset, every person, every year** | Annual, but land only (site value, well understood) | None (flows self-report) | As now, per estate |
| Avoidance surface | Narrow if lifetime-cumulative with anti-avoidance spine | Very large — proven by repeal wave (France, Sweden, Germany, Austria) (to verify) | Small — land cannot move or hide | Income shifting, deferral, incorporation | Same thicket minus a few hedges |
| Political durability | High *if* paired with visible endowment ([`../05-history/abolitions.md`](../05-history/abolitions.md)) | Low — most OECD wealth taxes repealed (to verify count) | Untested at national scale in modern era; transition losers are loud | Moderate — but top rates cycle with governments | Low — reliefs historically regrow |

| Breaks dynastic transmission? | **Yes — by construction** | Slowly and only at confiscatory rates | No | No | No |

## "Why not a wealth tax instead?"

**Concede first.** The Saez–Zucman case for annual wealth taxation targets the same underlying problem — concentrated stocks — and a modest annual levy polls well; the two instruments are intellectual cousins, not rivals.

**One-line answer.** An annual wealth tax must solve the valuation problem for every asset, every person, every year, while the living owner actively restructures against it — this proposal solves it once per lifetime, against an owner who is dead.

**Full answer.** The wealth-tax repeal record is the inheritance-tax abolition story amplified: a dozen OECD countries ran net wealth taxes in 1990 and only a handful remain (to verify exact count), felled by valuation cost, avoidance, and emigration — France's ISF being the canonical case. Every difficulty in [practicality.md](practicality.md#you-cant-value-a-private-company-an-artwork-or-a-farm) recurs annually instead of once, and against a live counterparty. The transfer moment is the unique point where the elasticity collapses: the decedent cannot emigrate, restructure, or lobby ([`../03-case-for/piketty-r-vs-g.md`](../03-case-for/piketty-r-vs-g.md)). Verdict: if a country can run only one stock-targeting tax, transmission is the high-leverage point; a wealth tax is a possible supplement, not a substitute.

## "Why not a land value tax instead?"

**Concede first.** Almost everything Georgists say about LVT is right: land rents are unearned, the tax is close to distortion-free, land cannot flee, and housing dysfunction ([`../01-problem/housing.md`](../01-problem/housing.md)) is substantially a land problem. This project should be seen as an ally of LVT, and the overlap is genuine — both tax the unearned.

**One-line answer.** LVT taxes one asset class's *flow of rent*; it does nothing about the transmission of financial, business, and offshore wealth, which is where the largest dynasties actually live — so it complements the inheritance tax and cannot replace it.

**Full answer.** A dynasty holding equities, bonds, private-company stakes, and art pays no LVT on any of it. LVT also deliberately leaves transmission untouched: the landowning family that pays its annual site-value charge still passes the estate, and the rank it confers, intact — the dynastic-break objective ([`../16-second-order/dynasties-and-power.md`](../16-second-order/dynasties-and-power.md)) is simply outside LVT's scope. The right relationship is a portfolio: LVT disciplines land rents year by year; the inheritance tax breaks concentration at generational handover; they share the moral premise (tax the unearned, spare the earned) and the political coalition ([`../06-palatability/coalition.md`](../06-palatability/coalition.md)). Engaging Georgists as allies rather than rivals is the recommended posture in [`../12-political-adoption/policy-entrepreneurs.md`](../12-political-adoption/policy-entrepreneurs.md).

## "Why not just raise income tax at the top?"

**Concede first.** Top income tax is administratively mature, well-understood, and progressive; if the problem were merely high incomes, it would be the whole answer.

**One-line answer.** Income tax touches flows, and when the return on inherited capital outruns growth (*r > g*), no politically feasible flow tax stabilises the *stock* distribution — you cannot income-tax your way out of a dynasty that already exists.

**Full answer.** This is the structural heart of the whole project, argued in full at [`../03-case-for/piketty-r-vs-g.md`](../03-case-for/piketty-r-vs-g.md). Existing fortunes largely compound as unrealised gains that income tax never sees; the heirs of the great estates owe income tax only on what they choose to realise, and step-up/uplift rules can erase even that at each generational handover ([fairness.md](fairness.md#double-taxation-i-already-paid-tax-on-that-money)). Meanwhile every marginal point of income tax bears on labour — the earned channel the meritocratic case wants to *protect*. The proposal's division of labour is deliberate: lighter on earning, decisive on unearned receipt. Raising top income-tax rates is a separate debate with separate merits; it is not a substitute for a transmission tax because it does not act on the same variable.

## "Why not just close the existing loopholes?"

**Concede first.** UK IHT's relief thicket — business and agricultural property reliefs, the seven-year rule, trust treatments — is indefensible on its own terms, and closing it would be a genuine improvement worth supporting on the way to anything better.

**One-line answer.** Do close them — it is the natural first legislative step ([`../15-transition/stepping-stones.md`](../15-transition/sequencing.md)) — but a patched 40% estate tax still carries the three structural defects that killed its cousins: estate basis (the "death tax" frame), no lifetime cumulation (the gift escape), and no visible beneficiary (the political-economy asymmetry).

**Full answer.** Loophole-closing accepts the existing architecture, and the architecture is the problem. An estate-based tax invites the grieving-family framing however clean its base ([`../05-history/abolitions.md`](../05-history/abolitions.md), error #2); without lifetime cumulation of receipts, early giving remains a legal exit ([avoidance.md](avoidance.md#theyll-just-gift-everything-early)); and revenue flowing to the general pot leaves the tax politically naked — concentrated losers, diffuse winners — which is how the reliefs grew back last time and would again. History's pattern is that reliefs are the sediment of decades of lobbying; draining them without changing the political economy that deposited them buys perhaps a decade. That said, the sequencing case for starting here is strong and is made in [`../15-transition/sequencing.md`](../15-transition/sequencing.md) and [`../12-political-adoption/stepping-stones.md`](../12-political-adoption/stepping-stones.md): a loophole-closed, recipient-converted IHT is the natural chrysalis of this proposal.

## Verdict: complements, not substitutes — but one keystone

None of the alternatives is an enemy. A sane long-run tax system might well contain LVT and a reformed income tax alongside this proposal, and the coalition arithmetic improves when each camp treats the others as allies ([`../06-palatability/coalition.md`](../06-palatability/coalition.md)). But on the specific problem this project diagnoses — the recongealing of hereditary economic dynasties and the collapse of the equal-start premise ([`../01-problem/README.md`](../01-problem/README.md)) — the recipient-based inheritance tax is the highest-leverage single instrument, because it alone acts on the stock, at transmission, against a counterparty who cannot respond, with proceeds that visibly fund every citizen's start. Whataboutery in debate is usually a request to fight on easier ground; the answer is to welcome the allies and return to the variable that matters.
