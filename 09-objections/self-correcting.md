# "It Fixes Itself": The Self-Correction Objections

**Executive summary.** The self-correction family — fortunes are squandered within three generations, regression to the mean dilutes dynasties, the rich lists are full of new names — is the most sophisticated form of "not a problem", because it concedes the diagnosis would matter and denies only the persistence. The measured evidence runs hard the other way: top-wealth persistence is far stronger than the folk proverb claims, the aggregate inherited share is rising even where individual families churn, and a professional industry exists precisely to defeat the dissipation the objection relies on.

Rules of engagement: concede, one-liner, full answer. Master table in [README.md](README.md). The persistence evidence lives in [`../01-problem/wealth-concentration.md`](../01-problem/wealth-concentration.md).

A note on why this file exists separately from [spend-it-all.md](spend-it-all.md): that file answers the prediction that the rich will *deliberately* deplete their fortunes to spite the tax; this file answers the claim that fortunes deplete *naturally*, so no tax is needed. The two are opposites — one predicts the policy fails by working too well, the other that it is redundant — and they are sometimes deployed by the same objector in the same conversation, which is worth pointing out when it happens.

## "Family wealth is squandered within three generations anyway"

**Concede first.** Individual dynasties really do collapse — the Vanderbilts are the canonical case, and "shirtsleeves to shirtsleeves in three generations" exists as a proverb in many languages because everyone can name an example. Division among heirs, incompetent successors, and spendthrift generations are all real forces.

**One-line answer.** The proverb is folklore, not data: surname studies tracking elite families across centuries find wealth and status persisting for ten-plus generations, and the aggregate inherited share of top wealth is rising, not decaying.

**Full answer.** Separate the anecdotes from the measurements:

- **The persistence literature is unambiguous in direction.** Gregory Clark's surname studies (*The Son Also Rises*) find long-run status persistence far higher than parent–child correlations suggest — social position reverting to the mean over ten to fifteen generations, not three (to verify). Clark & Cummins track rare English surnames through probate records from 1858 onward and find wealth persistence across five-plus generations (to verify). Barone & Mocetti's Florence study is the extreme datapoint: the surnames atop the city's tax rolls in 1427 remain disproportionately atop its earnings distribution in 2011 — six centuries later (to verify). See [`../01-problem/wealth-concentration.md`](../01-problem/wealth-concentration.md) for the evidence base.
- **The proverb's cross-cultural ubiquity is evidence about morality tales, not economics.** A saying that exists identically in English, Japanese ("rice paddies to rice paddies"), and Italian is performing a social function — consoling the unrich, warning the heir — that does not depend on being statistically true.
- **Survivorship of the story, not the fortune.** Crashed dynasties are narratively visible (the Vanderbilt yacht auctions); quiet persisters are not. The du Ponts, Rockefellers, Mellons, Waltons, Mars and Cargill families, the Wallenbergs, and the Grosvenor estate (three-and-a-half centuries and counting) do not generate cautionary tales precisely because nothing happens to them (examples to verify).
- **Even where the folk pattern held, look at when.** Much of the observed twentieth-century dynastic decay occurred *under* 70–80% estate taxation and wartime capital destruction ([`../05-history/us-estate-tax.md`](../05-history/us-estate-tax.md), [`../05-history/uk-estate-duty.md`](../05-history/uk-estate-duty.md)). Citing that era's churn as evidence that no tax is needed is citing the tax's own success against it.

## "Regression to the mean takes care of it"

**Concede first.** Regression to the mean in talent is real: the founder's heirs are, on average, less exceptional than the founder — that is arithmetic, and the heir-underperformance literature this project cites elsewhere depends on it.

**One-line answer.** Talent regresses; capital does not — a portfolio does not get worse at compounding because its owner is mediocre, and the entire wealth-management industry exists to make ownership skill-free.

**Full answer.** The objection confuses two different quantities:

- **Ability regresses; balances compound.** A fortune in index funds, London property, or a professionally managed family office grows at the market rate regardless of the heir's gifts. Regression to the mean applies to what the heir can *create*, not to what the heir *holds* — which is exactly why dynastic capital migrates toward skill-free asset classes ([`../01-problem/rentier-economy.md`](../01-problem/rentier-economy.md)).
- **The wealth-defence industry is the counterforce the proverb forgets.** Discretionary trusts, perpetual "dynasty trusts" in jurisdictions that abolished the rule against perpetuities, family offices, spendthrift clauses, and professional trustees are a multi-billion-pound apparatus engineered to do one thing: stop the third generation squandering the money ([`../07-implementation/trusts-and-vehicles.md`](../07-implementation/trusts-and-vehicles.md), [`../10-opposition/astroturf-and-lobbying.md`](../10-opposition/astroturf-and-lobbying.md) on the industry as a political interest). Historic aristocracies solved the same problem with entail and primogeniture for five hundred years; the tools changed, the function did not.
- **Division among heirs dilutes per-head, not per-family** — and at the scales the tax targets, dilution does not rescue the principle. A £2bn fortune split among four children is four £500m fortunes; meanwhile fertility decline means fewer ways to split each generation.

## "Look at the rich lists: it's all new names at the top"

**Concede first.** The very top of the rich lists does show genuine churn — the largest fortunes of 2025 are overwhelmingly founder fortunes, not nineteenth-century names, and that churn is real Schumpeterian turnover worth celebrating ([`../00-capitalism/markets-vs-dynasties.md`](../00-capitalism/markets-vs-dynasties.md)).

**One-line answer.** The founders at the top today are the dynasts of tomorrow — the churn you see is one founder generation ageing, and the UBS finding that 2023's new billionaire wealth came more from inheritance than from founding is that generation's baton beginning to pass.

**Full answer.**

- **A rich list is a snapshot; the objection needs a trend.** The relevant series — the inherited share of top wealth — is rising: Forbes' own self-made scores have drifted down, and the inherited share of aggregate wealth in France rose from roughly 45% in the 1970s to about 60% by the 2010s ([`../01-problem/wealth-concentration.md`](../01-problem/wealth-concentration.md), figures to verify).
- **The post-war churn was manufactured, not natural.** The founder-heavy top of the late twentieth century followed the great mid-century compression — high estate taxes, capital destruction, and regulation — which cleared the field the founders then filled ([`../03-case-for/historical-precedents-of-success.md`](../03-case-for/historical-precedents-of-success.md)). The churn the objection celebrates is downstream of the policy family it opposes.
- **The stakes are in front of us, not behind.** The great wealth transfer (~$85–100tn US, to verify) is the founder generation's holdings moving to heirs over the next two decades ([`../01-problem/great-wealth-transfer.md`](../01-problem/great-wealth-transfer.md)). Whether the 2045 rich list looks Schumpeterian or Buddenbrooks depends on what transfer taxation does now.

## The unifying reply

The self-correction family loses either way, and it is worth making the fork explicit:

| If fortunes persist (what the evidence shows) | If fortunes really dissipated in three generations |
|---|---|
| The dynastic problem is real and compounding; the tax is necessary | Inheritance preserves no long-run family stewardship — so the strongest *pro*-inheritance arguments (patient family capital, bequest-motivated saving) collapse with it |
| The wealth-defence industry keeps winning | The tax would be cheap: little revenue forgone, little behaviour changed, and the threshold already spares every ordinary family |
| Three channels of harm run at full volume ([`../00-capitalism/the-self-destruction-thesis.md`](../00-capitalism/the-self-destruction-thesis.md)) | Three generations of unearned advantage were still consumed *during* the dissipation — the mobility damage is done while the money burns down |

An objector cannot hold "wealth dissipates naturally, so no tax is needed" and "the tax would destroy carefully stewarded family businesses" simultaneously ([family.md](family.md#youll-destroy-family-farms-and-businesses), [`../04-case-against/family-business.md`](../04-case-against/family-business.md)) — the first claim is a direct denial of the second's premise. In debate, ask which of the two they are asserting, and answer that one.
