# "They'll Just Spend It All": The Depletion Objections

**Executive summary.** The depletion family of objections — the rich will consume everything, divert everything to charity, or deliberately die broke, so the tax collects nothing — is unique in this repo because its premise, taken seriously, describes the policy *succeeding*: capital returned to circulation or to genuine philanthropy, and no dynasty formed, is the objective, with revenue a secondary benefit. The empirical kicker is that the very wealthy demonstrably cannot consume fast enough to matter.

Rules of engagement: concede, one-liner, full answer. Master table in [README.md](README.md). Behavioural evidence lives in [`../16-second-order/savings-consumption.md`](../16-second-order/savings-consumption.md).

## "The rich will just spend it all before they die"

**Concede first.** A 100% marginal rate above the threshold is the strongest possible incentive to not die holding excess wealth, so late-life consumption should be expected to rise at the margin; any honest model says so.

**One-line answer.** Good — that is substantially the point: wealth consumed returns to circulation as spending, wages, and VAT, and a fortune consumed is a dynasty that never forms.

**Full answer.** Unpack what "spending it all" physically means: buying goods and services — which employs people, pays taxed wages, bears VAT, and transfers the money to counterparties who are overwhelmingly less wealthy than the spender. The Treasury collects along the way; the capital stock is not burned, it changes hands through markets instead of through bloodlines. Andrew Carnegie's dictum — the man who dies rich dies disgraced — envisioned exactly this: surplus wealth administered and dispersed within the holder's lifetime rather than entailed on heirs ([`../05-history/carnegie-1889.md`](../05-history/carnegie-1889.md)). The objection imagines it as a gotcha; Carnegie proposed it as the ideal. Then the empirical ceiling: consumption's share of income and wealth falls steeply as wealth rises — there are only so many meals, houses, and yachts a household can use — and at the billion-pound scale, genuine consumption cannot make a dent within a lifetime (magnitudes to verify against consumption-by-wealth-percentile studies). The residual channels — asset-price effects of decumulation, luxury-sector composition — are second-order questions treated in [`../16-second-order/asset-prices.md`](../16-second-order/asset-prices.md) and [`../16-second-order/savings-consumption.md`](../16-second-order/savings-consumption.md).

**Deep treatment:** [`../16-second-order/savings-consumption.md`](../16-second-order/savings-consumption.md) · [`../16-second-order/asset-prices.md`](../16-second-order/asset-prices.md)

## "They'll give it all to charity and you'll collect nothing"

**Concede first.** The charitable exemption is a genuine diversion channel, it will be used at scale, and history shows foundation structures being used to preserve family control while claiming the relief — the risk is real and specific.

**One-line answer.** Charitable diversion is a designed feature, not a leak — genuine philanthropy is the Carnegie outcome the policy wants — and the exemption comes with anti-cycling rules so that "charity" which quietly keeps the family in the money is pierced and taxed.

**Full answer.** Separate the two things this objection bundles. *Genuine* charitable giving — capital irrevocably committed to public purposes — is a policy success: it ends the dynasty (the family no longer owns it), and it funds public goods without touching the Treasury. If the tax's main behavioural effect were a philanthropic surge, that would be a strange definition of failure. *Fake* charity — the perpetual family foundation with heirs as salaried trustees, assets circling back through benefits in kind, or control (rather than money) as the real bequest — is the actual threat, and it is met with anti-cycling design: independence requirements for governance, minimum annual payout rules, prohibition of benefit to the donor's family, and look-through where control functions as ownership ([`../02-proposal/carveouts.md`](../02-proposal/carveouts.md), [`../07-implementation/trusts-and-vehicles.md`](../07-implementation/trusts-and-vehicles.md)). The unresolved and genuinely hard residue — mega-foundations as unaccountable private power even when honestly charitable — is examined in [`../16-second-order/charitable-giving.md`](../16-second-order/charitable-giving.md) and [`../16-second-order/dynasties-and-power.md`](../16-second-order/dynasties-and-power.md).

## "They'll die broke and the tax raises nothing"

**Concede first.** It is arithmetically possible: a cohort of wealthy households that fully decumulates leaves the 100% band empty, and any revenue projection must model that behavioural response rather than assume static estates ([`../02-proposal/thresholds.md`](../02-proposal/thresholds.md) does).

**One-line answer.** "Die broke" is the policy's structural objective achieved at zero enforcement cost — no dynasty, capital dispersed through markets and philanthropy during life — and the endowment's funding plan is stress-tested against exactly this scenario rather than depending on maximal estates.

**Full answer.** Keep the objective hierarchy straight: this is a distributional-primary policy ([practicality.md](practicality.md#it-raises-trivial-revenue-so-why-bother)). The problem being solved is hereditary concentration of wealth and the power it converts into ([`../01-problem/wealth-concentration.md`](../01-problem/wealth-concentration.md), [`../01-problem/political-capture.md`](../01-problem/political-capture.md)). A wealthy generation that dissipates its fortunes among employees, vendors, charities, and up-to-threshold family transfers has solved that problem *voluntarily* — the tax worked as a deterrent works, without collecting. Empirically, full decumulation is the opposite of observed behaviour: under the existing 40% UK IHT — already a strong incentive to die light — estates keep growing and aggregate bequest flows keep rising (to verify against HMRC/ONS series); the very wealthy systematically die with large estates because accumulation is driven by control, status, and precaution more than by spending plans ([economic.md](economic.md#it-kills-the-incentive-to-build-businesses)). The realistic revenue case therefore sits between the corners, and the endowment's fiscal bridge — including phase-in funding while behaviour settles — is designed on those middle scenarios in [`../15-transition/revenue-bridge.md`](../15-transition/revenue-bridge.md) and [`../02-proposal/endowment.md`](../02-proposal/endowment.md).

## The unifying reply

All three objections assume the policy's goal is to maximise Treasury receipts, then observe that behaviour will reduce receipts. But the goal is to end the untaxed transmission of dynastic fortunes, and each behaviour the objections predict — consuming, giving, dying light — is a *route to that goal*, differing only in who ends up with the money (workers and vendors, charities, or up-to-threshold heirs and the endowment fund). The tax cannot be dodged by depletion, because depletion is compliance. The only true failure modes are avoidance that preserves dynastic transmission in disguise — covered in [avoidance.md](avoidance.md) — and revenue falling short of the endowment's needs, which is a bridging problem ([`../15-transition/revenue-bridge.md`](../15-transition/revenue-bridge.md)), not a refutation.
