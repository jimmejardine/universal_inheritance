# Fairness Objections

**Executive summary.** The fairness objections — double taxation, punished prudence, envy, and "it's my money" — are the ones most often delivered with genuine moral conviction, and each contains a truth worth conceding before the rebuttal. The recipient basis of the proposal dissolves most of them structurally: the person who earned and was taxed is never taxed again, because the tax falls on someone who has never paid a penny on this money.

Rules of engagement: concede, one-liner, full answer. Master table in [README.md](README.md). The philosophical foundations are in [`../03-case-for/ethical.md`](../03-case-for/ethical.md).

## "Double taxation: I already paid tax on that money"

**Concede first.** Often the wealth was indeed built from taxed income, and the intuition that the same person shouldn't be taxed twice on the same pound is sound as far as it goes.

**One-line answer.** The tax falls on the heir, who has never paid tax on this money — to the recipient it is untaxed income — so on a recipient basis there is no double taxation to object to.

**Full answer.** Taxes attach to *persons*, not to pounds. Every pound in the economy is taxed repeatedly as it changes hands: your taxed salary pays a plumber, who pays income tax on it, and VAT when he spends it, and his employee pays tax again. Nobody calls this double taxation, because each tax event corresponds to a *different person receiving* the money. A bequest is exactly such an event: money moving from one person to another who did nothing to earn it. Declining to tax that transfer while taxing every wage is the anomaly, not the correction. Two further points sharpen it. *First*, much large-estate wealth has never been taxed even once: unrealised capital gains escape UK CGT entirely via the death uplift, and US heirs receive stepped-up basis (to verify per jurisdiction) — for the biggest fortunes "I already paid tax on that" is frequently false. *Second*, this is precisely why the proposal is recipient-based ([`../02-proposal/mechanism.md`](../02-proposal/mechanism.md)): it taxes the heir's windfall receipt, a category the tax system otherwise treats more gently than a nurse's overtime.

## "It punishes prudence and rewards profligacy"

**Concede first.** Take two identical earners: one saves, one spends. The saver's estate faces the tax; the spender's doesn't. Stated that way, the asymmetry is real.

**One-line answer.** Below £500,000 per recipient prudence is entirely untouched, and above it the comparison is no longer between the two earners — both of whom lived exactly the lives they chose — but between their *children*, neither of whom earned anything.

**Full answer.** Both earners were taxed identically in life; both consumed or saved freely; both died having disposed of their own money on their own terms. The tax alters neither of their lives. The difference arises one generation on, and there the moral intuition flips: why should the saver's child receive millions untaxed while the spender's child gets nothing, when the children's own prudence is identical (namely, unknown)? Note also that the spender did not escape tax — consumption bears VAT and excise as it happens — and that the prudent family below the threshold, which is over 90% of families (to verify against [`../02-proposal/thresholds.md`](../02-proposal/thresholds.md)), passes everything intact. Finally, if the objection is that a 100% marginal rate encourages late-life spending, that behavioural response is examined — and partly welcomed — in [spend-it-all.md](spend-it-all.md).

## "It's the politics of envy"

**Concede first.** Envy is a bad basis for policy, and some rhetoric on the redistributive left genuinely is resentment-flavoured; a case built on grudging the successful their success would deserve the label.

**One-line answer.** The intellectual lineage here is Paine, Mill, Carnegie, and Theodore Roosevelt — an industrialist and two liberals and a Republican president, none of them envious of anyone — and the argument is about the *heir's* desert, not the *builder's* success.

**Full answer.** Envy targets the successful living. This proposal leaves the successful living completely alone: 0% on everything they earn, hold, spend, and enjoy until death ([economic.md — "It's a tax on success"](economic.md#its-a-tax-on-success)). What it targets is the transformation of one person's success into another person's unearned rank — which is not envy but the same principle conservatives apply everywhere else: rewards should track contribution. Andrew Carnegie, who had rather little to be envious of, made exactly this argument in *The Gospel of Wealth* (1889), and Roosevelt in 1906 proposed steep inheritance taxation as a defence of the republic, not an attack on wealth ([`../05-history/carnegie-1889.md`](../05-history/carnegie-1889.md), [`../05-history/roosevelt-1906.md`](../05-history/roosevelt-1906.md)). In debate, the strongest move is to refuse the frame and restate the meritocratic one: this is the policy for people who *believe in* earned success ([`../06-palatability/framing.md`](../06-palatability/framing.md)).

## "It's my money"

**Concede first.** Legitimately earned wealth carries strong disposal rights, and during your entire life this proposal does not qualify them in any way: spend, give (within recipients' lifetime allowances), found, endow — all untouched.

**One-line answer.** At the moment of inheritance it is precisely *not* your money any more — the whole question is whether it should become, untaxed and unearned, somebody else's.

**Full answer.** "My money" is doing quiet work in this sentence: the objection is really a claim about the *heir's* right to receive, dressed as the *owner's* right to hold. Those are distinct — John Stuart Mill drew exactly this line in 1848, defending the right to bequeath while denying any unlimited right to receive ([`../05-history/mill-1848.md`](../05-history/mill-1848.md)). And the right to receive is not a natural fact but a legal artefact: inheritance operates through probate courts, intestacy statutes, perpetuity rules, and forced-heirship regimes that the state already defines and has always defined. Property is a bundle of rights constructed by law; inheritance law is one strand of the bundle, and societies have continuously rewritten it (abolition of primogeniture and entail, spousal shares, and the existing 40% IHT). Asking what the transmission rules *should be* is not an invasion of property — it is the ordinary business of defining it ([`../03-case-for/ethical.md`](../03-case-for/ethical.md)). The honest residue of the objection — the donor's liberty interest in directing their wealth after death — is a genuine value conflict, steelmanned rather than dismissed in [`../04-case-against/liberty-autonomy.md`](../04-case-against/liberty-autonomy.md).
