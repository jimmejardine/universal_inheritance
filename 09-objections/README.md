# 09 — Objections: The Debate Armoury

**Executive summary.** This section is the quick-fire armoury for every common "oh, but that won't work because…" — each objection stated in its strongest colloquial form, answered with a concession, a one-line rebuttal, and a full counterargument, with a link to the deep treatment elsewhere in the repo. It is deliberately distinct from [`../04-case-against/`](../04-case-against/README.md), which steelmans the five serious philosophical objections at length; this section arms a person who has thirty seconds on the doorstep, in the comment thread, or on air.

## How to use this section

**Rules of engagement, applied in every entry:**

1. **Concede what is true.** Most objections contain a real insight — often one this project's own history section documents. Conceding it first is both honest and disarming.
2. **Never strawman.** Each objection is stated in the strongest colloquial form it actually circulates in.
3. **One sentence first, then the full version.** Every entry gives a rebuttal that survives being cut off after one sentence, then the complete argument with evidence and design references.

**Division of labour with neighbouring sections:**

| Section | Role | This section's relationship to it |
|---|---|---|
| [`../04-case-against/`](../04-case-against/README.md) | Steelmans the five serious objections at full length | 09 gives the short-form rebuttal and links back for the honest residue |
| [`../10-opposition/`](../10-opposition/README.md) | Anticipates how attacks will be professionally run (media, lobbying, lawfare) | 09 answers the arguments; 10 answers the campaigns |
| [`../14-communications/debate-toolkit.md`](../14-communications/debate-toolkit.md) | Message-tested phrasings and formats | 09 supplies the raw arguments the toolkit polishes |
| [`../07-implementation/`](../07-implementation/README.md), [`../08-capital-flight/`](../08-capital-flight/README.md) | The engineering behind the rebuttals | Most "full answers" below resolve to a design file in these sections |

**Deploying a rebuttal, in order:** (1) the concession — it buys the credibility everything else spends; (2) the one-liner from the table below — it must survive interruption; (3) the full answer from the linked file, only if the exchange allows it.

## Master table of objections

| # | Objection | One-line rebuttal | Where |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | "It kills the incentive to build businesses" | The founder keeps every penny for life, passes £500k per child, and evidence says dynastic bequest is a minor driver of accumulation | [economic.md](economic.md#it-kills-the-incentive-to-build-businesses) |
| 2 | "People will stop saving and the capital stock will collapse" | Taxing a transfer changes who owns capital, not whether it exists; theory is ambiguous, measured effects small | [economic.md](economic.md#people-will-stop-saving-and-the-capital-stock-will-collapse) |
| 3 | "Founders will leave, or never start" | Exit is a solved design problem (exit tax, 10-year tail, asset situs); the £50k endowment creates more founders than the tax deters | [economic.md](economic.md#founders-will-leave-or-never-start) |
| 4 | "It's a tax on success" | The successful person is never taxed a penny — the tax falls on a recipient whose receipt is, by definition, not their success | [economic.md](economic.md#its-a-tax-on-success) |
| 5 | "The rich will always find a way around it" | Avoidance is a design variable, not a law of nature; every historical escape route maps to a specific, now-closed engineering failure | [avoidance.md](avoidance.md#the-rich-will-always-find-a-way-around-it) |
| 6 | "Lawyers will run rings round HMRC" | Lawyers feed on complexity; one threshold, one rate, and few reliefs starve them of raw material | [avoidance.md](avoidance.md#lawyers-will-run-rings-round-hmrc) |
| 7 | "It'll only catch the moderately rich; the billionaires escape" | That was a fact about estate-based, trust-blind designs — each billionaire door (exit, trusts, valuation, foundations) has a named fix | [avoidance.md](avoidance.md#itll-only-catch-the-moderately-rich-the-billionaires-escape) |
| 8 | "They'll just gift everything early" | Lifetime cumulation abolishes the clock: every gift counts against the same £500k lifetime total, so early giving changes the date, not the amount | [avoidance.md](avoidance.md#theyll-just-gift-everything-early) |
| 9 | "Some will still avoid it, so it's pointless" | Income tax is evaded and nobody abolishes it — demanding zero leakage only of this tax is asymmetric perfectionism | [avoidance.md](avoidance.md#the-asymmetric-perfectionism-fallacy) |
| 10 | "Double taxation: I already paid tax on that money" | The heir has never paid tax on it — to the recipient it is untaxed income, and much big-estate wealth was never taxed even once | [fairness.md](fairness.md#double-taxation-i-already-paid-tax-on-that-money) |
| 11 | "It punishes prudence and rewards profligacy" | Below £500k per recipient prudence is untouched; above it, the comparison is between two heirs, neither of whom earned anything | [fairness.md](fairness.md#it-punishes-prudence-and-rewards-profligacy) |
| 12 | "It's the politics of envy" | The lineage is Paine, Mill, Carnegie, and Roosevelt — the argument is about the heir's desert, not the builder's success | [fairness.md](fairness.md#its-the-politics-of-envy) |
| 13 | "It's my money" | At the moment of inheritance it is precisely not your money any more — the question is whether it becomes someone else's, untaxed and unearned | [fairness.md](fairness.md#its-my-money) |
| 14 | "You'll destroy family farms and businesses" | No operating asset is ever force-sold: 30-year deferral at gilts + 1%, or a passive state equity stake instead of cash | [family.md](family.md#youll-destroy-family-farms-and-businesses) |
| 15 | "I can't even leave my house to my kids" | £500k per recipient means a home passes to two children untouched up to £1m — several times the average UK house price, and indexed to stay that way | [family.md](family.md#i-cant-even-leave-my-house-to-my-kids) |
| 16 | "Parents have a right to provide for their children" | Everything that constitutes providing remains — plus £500k per child; only unearned capital beyond that is capped, and every parent's child now gets £50k at 25 | [family.md](family.md#parents-have-a-right-to-provide-for-their-children) |
| 17 | "It breaks the natural bond between generations" | The bond is made of care and time, not seven-figure transfers — the same argument defended primogeniture and opposed public schooling | [family.md](family.md#it-breaks-the-natural-bond-between-generations) |
| 18 | "You're taxing grief" | Over 90% of recipients owe nothing and never file; for the rest, deferral means nobody is forced to sell anything while mourning | [family.md](family.md#youre-taxing-grief) |
| 19 | "You can't value a private company, an artwork, or a farm" | These exact assets are valued daily for CGT, existing IHT, divorce, and insurance — the question is regime quality, not possibility | [practicality.md](practicality.md#you-cant-value-a-private-company-an-artwork-or-a-farm) |
| 20 | "The government will just waste the money" | The revenue never enters the discretionary pot — it is hypothecated to a named £50k endowment paid directly to every citizen at 25 | [practicality.md](practicality.md#the-government-will-just-waste-the-money) |
| 21 | "It raises trivial revenue, so why bother" | Historical yields reflect 40%-with-loopholes regimes; this base is the full inheritance flow, and the aim is structural (no dynasties), not just fiscal | [practicality.md](practicality.md#it-raises-trivial-revenue-so-why-bother) |
| 22 | "The admin costs will eat the revenue" | Collection cost scales with complexity, not rate: one threshold, one rate, few reliefs, one lifetime ledger is simpler than the current relief thicket | [practicality.md](practicality.md#the-admin-costs-will-eat-the-revenue) |
| 23 | "First inheritance, next they'll take your house" | Slopes need mechanisms: the threshold is indexed to the higher of wages and house prices, and cutting it means facing tens of millions of endowment beneficiaries | [slippery-slope.md](slippery-slope.md#first-inheritance-next-theyll-take-your-house) |
| 24 | "This is communism" | Communism abolishes private property; this universalises it — and Paine, Mill, Carnegie, and Roosevelt were nobody's communists | [slippery-slope.md](slippery-slope.md#this-is-communism) |
| 25 | "The state owns your children's future" | The endowment is unconditional cash with no strings; under the status quo it is parental wealth that owns children's futures | [slippery-slope.md](slippery-slope.md#the-state-owns-your-childrens-future) |
| 26 | "Once the register exists, it'll be used for a wealth tax" | The register records transfers, not holdings — it cannot shortcut a wealth tax, and by that standard PAYE and the Land Registry already are one | [slippery-slope.md](slippery-slope.md#once-the-register-exists-itll-be-used-for-a-wealth-tax) |
| 27 | "Why not a wealth tax instead?" | A wealth tax must value everything, every year, against a living owner who restructures; this values once, against an owner who cannot respond | [whataboutery.md](whataboutery.md#why-not-a-wealth-tax-instead) |
| 28 | "Why not a land value tax instead?" | LVT is an ally but taxes one asset class's rent — it never touches the financial and business wealth where the real dynasties live | [whataboutery.md](whataboutery.md#why-not-a-land-value-tax-instead) |
| 29 | "Why not just raise income tax at the top?" | Income tax touches flows; when r > g, no flow tax stabilises the stock distribution — you cannot income-tax away an existing dynasty | [whataboutery.md](whataboutery.md#why-not-just-raise-income-tax-at-the-top) |
| 30 | "Why not just close the existing loopholes?" | Do — as step one; but a patched 40% estate tax keeps the death-tax frame, the gift escape, and no visible beneficiary, which is how the loopholes grew last time | [whataboutery.md](whataboutery.md#why-not-just-close-the-existing-loopholes) |
| 31 | "The rich will just spend it all before they die" | Substantially the point: consumed wealth returns to circulation and employs people, and a fortune consumed is a dynasty that never forms | [spend-it-all.md](spend-it-all.md#the-rich-will-just-spend-it-all-before-they-die) |
| 32 | "They'll give it all to charity and you'll collect nothing" | Genuine philanthropy is the Carnegie outcome the policy wants; fake foundations are pierced by anti-cycling rules | [spend-it-all.md](spend-it-all.md#theyll-give-it-all-to-charity-and-youll-collect-nothing) |
| 33 | "They'll die broke and the tax raises nothing" | "Die broke" is the structural objective achieved at zero enforcement cost — and empirically, the very wealthy do not die broke even under today's 40% rate | [spend-it-all.md](spend-it-all.md#theyll-die-broke-and-the-tax-raises-nothing) |
| 34 | "Family wealth is squandered within three generations anyway" | That's a proverb, not a dataset: surname studies find elite wealth persisting ten-plus generations, and the inherited share of top wealth is rising, not decaying | [self-correcting.md](self-correcting.md#family-wealth-is-squandered-within-three-generations-anyway) |
| 35 | "Regression to the mean takes care of it" | Talent regresses; capital compounds — a portfolio doesn't get worse at growing because its owner is mediocre, and the trust industry exists to guarantee that | [self-correcting.md](self-correcting.md#regression-to-the-mean-takes-care-of-it) |
| 36 | "Look at the rich lists: it's all new names at the top" | Today's founders are tomorrow's dynasts — 2023 was the first year new billionaire wealth came more from inheritance than from founding | [self-correcting.md](self-correcting.md#look-at-the-rich-lists-its-all-new-names-at-the-top) |

## Coverage notes

The table aims to be exhaustive for objections heard *in the wild* — doorstep, phone-in, comment thread. Objections that arrive only in academic or professional form (optimal-tax critiques, ECHR property-rights jurisprudence, specific trust-law counters) live with their answers in [`../04-case-against/`](../04-case-against/README.md), [`../10-opposition/legal-challenges.md`](../10-opposition/legal-challenges.md), and [`../07-implementation/`](../07-implementation/README.md) respectively.
