# Foreign Ownership: Does the Tax Sell the Country to Foreigners?

**Executive summary.** The objection runs: domestic dynasts must disperse at every generation while foreign individuals, corporations, funds, and sovereign wealth — all outside the tax's reach — accumulate without limit, so the tax amounts to a standing sell order on the nation's corporations, land, and assets, filled by whoever never dies here. Half of this is simply wrong on the design: the situs nexus taxes the gratuitous transfer of UK-situated assets *regardless of where donor or heir lives*, so a foreign family can no more warehouse UK land or UK-registered shares across generations than a domestic one — mirroring today's IHT, under which UK assets are chargeable whatever the deceased's residence [1]. The other half is genuine and must be conceded precisely: entities that never execute a transfer between natural persons — sovereign funds, listed foreign corporations, institutional money — sit outside a transfer tax by construction, because this is an instrument against *hereditary* concentration, not against *foreign* or *institutional* concentration; those are different diseases with their own, already-existing instruments, which this file names (per repo doctrine: named, not bundled) rather than annexes.

## Findings

- **The dual nexus is symmetric between foreign and domestic dynasts.** Tax applies if the recipient is UK-resident *or* the property is UK-situated ([`../02-proposal/avoidance.md`](../02-proposal/avoidance.md) §5): a foreign heir inheriting UK land from a foreign donor is charged exactly like a British heir. Current law already works this way — a person who is not a long-term UK resident pays IHT on their UK assets [1][2] — the proposal changes the rate and the base's tightness, not the territorial principle.
- **There is no gift-early escape for anyone, foreign families included.** Under *current* IHT a foreign parent can hand a UK asset to foreign heirs and escape entirely by surviving seven years [16]; this design abolishes that window — the inter-vivos gift *is* the taxable event, at market value, on the recipient's ledger, regardless of both parties' residence, and the charge is secured on the UK asset itself (§1.1).
- **Envelopes are pierced, and the piercing has a current-law precedent.** Holding UK assets through a foreign company does not re-site them where the structure is artificial: chains are looked through on substance tests ([`../02-proposal/avoidance.md`](../02-proposal/avoidance.md) §5), and since April 2017 the UK itself has taxed foreign close companies' UK residential property as non-excluded property (IHTA 1984 Sch A1) [3] — proof the look-through is legislatable, not aspirational.
- **The genuine gap is the immortal institutional holder, and it should be conceded, not patched.** A sovereign wealth fund or a genuinely diversified foreign corporation never makes a gratuitous transfer to a natural person that any situs rule can reach. Neither does a domestic pension fund. A transfer tax does not regulate institutional ownership at all — foreign or domestic — and pretending otherwise would misdescribe the instrument (§2).
- **The companion shelf already exists and is internationally normal.** The UK runs a beneficial-ownership register for overseas entities (2022), an annual charge on company-enveloped dwellings (ATED), a 2% non-resident SDLT surcharge, and mandatory national-security screening across 17 sectors [4][5][6][7]; New Zealand, Canada, and Australia currently ban or heavily restrict foreign purchase of existing homes, and Denmark and Switzerland have restricted non-resident property purchase for decades [8][9][10][11][12][13]. Managing foreign acquisition of positional assets is compatible with being an open economy — every one of these jurisdictions courts FDI.
- **The distributional baseline blunts the headline fear.** The top of the UK asset market is *already* majority-international: overseas investors held a record 58.8% of UK quoted shares at end-2024 (UK individuals 11.6%) [14], and prime central London has repriced for a decade on internationally mobile demand [15]. Dispersal sales change the flow at the margin of an ownership structure that long ago globalised; and the counter-flow — the National Endowment Fund's in-specie stakes, insurers, foundations, employee-ownership trusts, and ~890,000 newly capitalised 25-year-olds a year — is also standing at the bid (§4).
- **Foreign investment is not merely spared; the core retention pitch applies to foreign investors absolutely.** A foreign investor's returns, remittances, and exit are never touched, and a foreign investor who dies abroad owes nothing except on UK-situs holdings passing gratuitously — corporate and fund-structured FDI never touches the tax at all. "The owner never gets a bill" is literally, structurally true for them (§5, design argument).
- **The honest costs:** situs enforcement discounts UK positional assets for foreign dynastic buyers (priced in [`asset-prices.md`](asset-prices.md) §3 and accepted as the dynastic premium leaving the market), and a genuinely foreign-substance holding company owning UK businesses passes abroad untaxed — the same leak today's IHT lives with, bounded by the companion shelf rather than closed by this instrument.

## 1. What the dual nexus already catches

State the rule precisely, because the objection usually misstates it. Tax is triggered if **either** (a) the recipient is UK-resident, **or** (b) the transferred property is UK-situated — with situs following established categories: real estate at its physical location, registered shares at the register's jurisdiction, business assets at the permanent establishment, IP at the registering authority with a substance override, and holding-company chains looked through on OECD Pillar Two substance tests ([`../02-proposal/avoidance.md`](../02-proposal/avoidance.md) §5).

Run the four party combinations:

| Donor | Heir | UK-situs assets (land, UK-registered shares, UK business assets) | Foreign-situs assets |
|---|---|---|---|
| UK-resident | UK-resident | Taxed | Taxed (residence nexus) |
| UK-resident | Foreign | Taxed (situs nexus) | Taxed within the donor-side exit/tail rules ([`../08-capital-flight/exit-tax-design.md`](../08-capital-flight/exit-tax-design.md)) |
| Foreign | UK-resident | Taxed | Taxed above headroom, after the on-ramp ([`../08-capital-flight/inbound-wealth.md`](../08-capital-flight/inbound-wealth.md) §1) |
| **Foreign** | **Foreign** | **Taxed — the row the objection forgets** | Outside scope (no nexus) |

The bottom-left cell is the answer to "foreign dynasts accumulate while domestic dynasts disperse." A Monaco-resident family passing a Scottish estate, a Mayfair terrace, or a controlling block of a UK-registered company to Monaco-resident children is charged on the same lifetime-ledger terms as a family in Leeds. Dynastic warehousing of UK-situs assets is closed to foreigners *exactly as it is closed to natives* — the tax discriminates by asset location and by heredity, never by passport.

This is not a novelty of the proposal. Under current law, someone who is not a long-term UK resident still pays IHT on UK assets — "Inheritance Tax is only paid on your UK assets, for example property or bank accounts in the UK" [1] — and since 6 April 2025 the whole regime keys on residence rather than domicile, with long-term residents chargeable worldwide [2]. The proposal inherits a functioning territorial principle and tightens the mesh: no seven-year taper, lifetime cumulation, and the envelope look-through below.

**Envelopes.** The classic evasion of situs is interposition: put the Mayfair house inside a BVI company, and what passes at death is a foreign-registered share. Two answers. First, the design's substance override and chain look-through re-site artificial holding structures to their underlying assets ([`../02-proposal/avoidance.md`](../02-proposal/avoidance.md) §5), backed by the trust-equivalent recharacterisation machinery for ownerless and orphan structures ([`corporations.md`](corporations.md) §2). Second, this is settled UK legislative territory, not invention: Finance (No. 2) Act 2017 inserted Schedule A1 into IHTA 1984, removing from "excluded property" any value attributable to UK residential property held through non-UK close companies or partnerships [3]. The situs net has been extended through foreign wrappers before, by ordinary Finance Act, and held.

**What the nexus does not reach** — stated as precisely as what it does: (i) foreign-situs assets passing between foreign persons (no nexus, correctly — that is someone else's tax base); (ii) a *genuinely* foreign operating company with real substance that happens to own UK subsidiaries — the German family conglomerate with a Midlands factory passes at the conglomerate level abroad, and only an artificiality test, not ownership of UK assets per se, brings it back in; and (iii) any holder that never makes a gratuitous transfer to a natural person at all. That third category is the serious one, and it gets its own section.

### 1.1 The lifetime-gift move: "when is that ownership ever taxed?"

The sharpest form of the objection is a timing attack: *the foreign parent does not wait to die — they transfer their UK holdings to their foreign heirs now, from abroad, perhaps papered as a sale taxed (or not) under some other country's rules. When is that ownership ever taxed?* Four layers, each answered by an actual design rule.

**Layer 1 — gifts are receipts; there is no seven-year window to run out.** The comparison with current law is the whole answer, so state it explicitly. Under today's IHT, a lifetime gift is a potentially exempt transfer: "no tax is due on any gifts you give if you live for 7 years after giving them" [16] — so under *current* law the foreign parent's move works perfectly, on UK-situs assets, with a modest actuarial gamble. This design abolishes the construct: the tax point for a gift is the **date of receipt** at market value ([`../02-proposal/mechanism.md`](../02-proposal/mechanism.md) §4), every gift cumulates on the recipient's lifetime ledger with no taper and no death-reset ([`../02-proposal/avoidance.md`](../02-proposal/avoidance.md) §1), and the situs limb of the nexus applies to gifts exactly as to bequests. The inter-vivos transfer of a UK asset from a foreign donor to a foreign heir is not a way of avoiding the taxable event — it *is* the taxable event, on that day, whoever and wherever the parties are. The move the objection describes is an escape from the tax the UK has, not from the tax this repo proposes.

**Layer 2 — enforcement does not depend on foreign cooperation, because the charge is secured on the asset itself.** A foreign recipient of UK-situs assets carries a UK situs ledger, and the obvious retort — "HMRC cannot audit a family in Singapore" — misses where the security sits. Current law already imposes a statutory charge on the property itself for unpaid IHT: IHTA 1984 s.237 attaches an "Inland Revenue charge" to the transferred asset when tax on a chargeable transfer goes unpaid [17]. The design generalises that mechanism into a gate rather than a lien: no registration of the transfer at HM Land Registry or on a UK share register without a clearance certificate showing the ledger charge settled — the registration-or-blockage principle specified for structures touching UK assets ([`../07-implementation/trusts-and-vehicles.md`](../07-implementation/trusts-and-vehicles.md) §4; [`corporations.md`](corporations.md) §2(d)). An unregistered "transfer" of UK land leaves the heirs holding a title they cannot prove, sell, mortgage, or let through any regulated channel; the asset itself is the hostage, and it cannot leave. No treaty, no exchange of information, and no foreign court is required for the base case.

**Layer 3 — the enveloping variant** (gift the shares of the BVI company that owns the UK land — foreign-situs paper, so no situs trigger) is the move §1 above already answers: artificial holding chains are looked through to their underlying UK assets on substance tests, and the UK has run precisely this look-through in live legislation since 6 April 2017, when F(No.2)A 2017 inserted Schedule A1 into IHTA 1984 and made offshore close-company interests non-excluded property to the extent their value derives from UK residential property [3]. The design's only innovation is breadth: the deemed-situs look-through applies to *all* UK-situs asset classes held through closely held foreign envelopes, not to dwellings alone. Gifting the envelope is gifting the contents.

**Layer 4 — the disguised-sale variant** ("it wasn't a gift; the heirs *bought* it, and any tax on that deal belongs to another country"). Split it on substance. A **genuine sale at full market value — to an heir or to anyone — is not a taxable event under this tax, and that is the correct boundary, not a leak**: nothing was received gratuitously; the heirs paid full price with real money of their own; the parent's fortune changed *form* (UK asset → foreign cash) but no hereditary transmission occurred, and the eventual gratuitous transmission of that foreign cash between foreign persons is another jurisdiction's tax base — the gap the multilateral-floor agenda exists to address ([`../02-proposal/avoidance.md`](../02-proposal/avoidance.md) §7; [`../08-capital-flight/international-coordination.md`](../08-capital-flight/international-coordination.md)), not one a situs rule could or should reach. What the receipt definition *does* catch is every sale that is a gift wearing a price tag: a sale at undervalue is a receipt of the undervalue element, valued under the hard-valuation rules ([`../02-proposal/avoidance.md`](../02-proposal/avoidance.md) §6); a vendor-financed sale whose loan is later forgiven, waived, or left to lapse produces a receipt equal to the forgiven balance at the date of forgiveness; and circular funding — the parent gifting the heirs the purchase money offshore so they can "buy" the UK asset — is two transfers, with the cash leg landing on the heirs' ledger the moment they are in scope and the substance-over-form receipt definition and general anti-abuse backstop pricing the arrangement as what it is ([`../02-proposal/avoidance.md`](../02-proposal/avoidance.md), headline commitments; [`corporations.md`](corporations.md) §1 on substance-over-form attribution). The clearance gate of Layer 2 is where the paperwork meets daylight: to register the transfer, the parties must evidence the price actually paid, from funds actually the buyer's.

**The one-line answer:** that ownership is taxed at every gratuitous transfer of the UK-situs asset or its envelope — lifetime or death, whoever and wherever the parties are — and never on a true sale, because a true sale is not inheritance.

## 2. The genuine gap: immortal foreign holders

A transfer tax fires on a gratuitous transfer between natural persons (or on the trust-equivalent events built to mimic one). Three classes of foreign holder never pull that trigger:

1. **Sovereign wealth funds and state entities.** Norway's fund, Gulf sovereign capital, state-owned enterprises: no owner ever dies; no heir ever receives.
2. **Listed foreign corporations and their pension/insurance cousins.** Ownership churns through markets, not bequests; the corporate veil here is not a dodge but a genuinely dispersed register.
3. **Institutional funds** — index funds, private-equity vehicles with diversified LPs — where no identifiable family enjoys the assets and no distribution is a gift.

Can design reach them? Honestly: partly, and only where it should.

- **What is designable.** Where an entity is a *family in a wrapper* — concentrated gratuitous funding, discretionary family benefit, retained family control — the trust-equivalent checklist recharacterises it and taxes distributions and deemed receipts, with the UK Relevant Property Regime's 10-year periodic charge as the drafted precedent ([`../02-proposal/avoidance.md`](../02-proposal/avoidance.md) §2; [`corporations.md`](corporations.md) §§1–2). And where opaque entities *hold UK positional assets*, an annual holding charge is proven machinery: ATED has levied a banded annual charge on company-enveloped UK dwellings above £500,000 since 2013 — £4,600 to £303,450 a year at 2026–27 rates [4] — making perpetual corporate warehousing of housing a metered, not free, strategy. An adopting state can extend ATED-logic (annual or decennial charges on entity-held land and heritage assets that lack a transparent, dispersed register) without touching the transfer tax's architecture.
- **What leaks, irreducibly.** A periodic charge on *all* entity-held UK assets — every factory owned by a foreign listed company, every gilt held by a sovereign fund — would not be an anti-avoidance rule; it would be an annual wealth tax on inbound capital, the instrument whose survival record and politics this project examined and declined ([`../03-case-for/wealth-tax-comparison.md`](../03-case-for/wealth-tax-comparison.md)). The gap is not closable at acceptable cost, because it is not actually a gap in *this* tax: a domestic pension fund, a UK-listed PLC, and an Oxford college are equally immortal and equally untaxed. The tax has never had anything to say about institutional ownership, foreign or domestic.

So the honest statement, for the manifesto to make before opponents make it look like a discovery: **this tax is an instrument against hereditary concentration, not against foreign or institutional concentration.** It ends the transmission of private fortunes to untested heirs; it does not decide how much of the country's capital should be owned by non-residents, by states, or by funds. Those are real questions — about housing, food security, critical infrastructure, national security — and they have their own instruments, surveyed next. The repo's scope discipline ([`../07-implementation/human-capital.md`](../07-implementation/human-capital.md), [`../08-capital-flight/inbound-wealth.md`](../08-capital-flight/inbound-wealth.md) §3, [`../03-case-for/wealth-tax-comparison.md`](../03-case-for/wealth-tax-comparison.md) §6) applies with full force: companion instruments are **named, not bundled** — a transfer-tax bill carrying foreign-ownership riders hands the opposition both a wider target and a proof of the slippery slope.

## 3. The companion-instrument shelf, verified

What already exists — in the UK and among close peers — to govern foreign acquisition of positional assets. Every entry below is live law, verified against primary sources; none needs this proposal to exist, and this proposal needs none of them to function.

| Instrument | Jurisdiction | What it does | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| Register of Overseas Entities | UK | Overseas entities must register beneficial owners at Companies House before buying, selling, leasing, or charging UK land; retrospective to 1999 acquisitions in England & Wales; criminal penalties and transaction blocks for non-compliance | In force 1 August 2022 (Economic Crime (Transparency and Enforcement) Act 2022) [6] |
| Annual Tax on Enveloped Dwellings (ATED) | UK | Annual banded charge on companies (and partnerships with corporate partners) holding UK dwellings above £500,000: £4,600/yr (£500k–£1m band) to £303,450/yr (£20m+ band) for 2026–27 | In force since 2013 [4] |
| Non-resident SDLT surcharge | UK (England & NI) | 2 percentage points on top of all residential SDLT rates for non-resident buyers (individuals, companies, trusts) | In force 1 April 2021 [5] |
| National Security and Investment Act 2021 | UK | Mandatory government notification and screening of acquisitions in 17 sensitive sectors (defence, AI, energy, satellites, quantum, etc.), with call-in, condition, block, and unwind powers | In force [7] |
| Overseas Investment Amendment Act 2018 | New Zealand | Classified residential land as "sensitive": non-resident overseas persons generally barred from buying existing homes | In force 22 October 2018; relaxed December 2025 by a narrow pathway letting investor-visa holders buy one home above NZ$5m from March 2026 [8][9] |
| Prohibition on the Purchase of Residential Property by Non-Canadians Act | Canada | Bans non-Canadians from buying residential property (≤3 dwelling units, in metropolitan areas), with student/worker/spousal exceptions | In force 1 January 2023; extended to 1 January 2027 [10] |
| FIRB regime + established-dwellings ban | Australia | Standing foreign-investment screening (FIRB), plus a ban on foreign persons (including temporary residents and foreign-owned companies) buying established dwellings from 1 April 2025 — extended in the 2026–27 Budget to 30 June 2029 | In force [11] |
| Acquisition of Real Property Act | Denmark | Buyers without permanent Danish residence or five years' cumulative residence need Ministry of Justice permission to acquire property; summerhouses restricted even for EU nationals | Long-standing, in force [12] |
| Lex Koller | Switzerland | Non-resident foreigners need cantonal authorisation to acquire residential property; holiday-home permits capped nationally (~1,500/yr); further tightening under federal consultation in 2026 | In force since 1985 [13] |

Three lessons the table teaches, all favourable to the proposal's politics:

1. **Restricting foreign acquisition of homes and land is mainstream policy among open, capital-hungry democracies** — not a fringe reaction the proposal would have to invent or defend from first principles.
2. **The instruments are separable.** No country above bundled its foreign-buyer rules into a transfer tax; each stands or falls on its own housing, security, or sovereignty argument. The adopting country that wants a tighter shelf can legislate it in either order.
3. **The shelf is adjustable in both directions** — New Zealand's 2025 carve-out and Switzerland's 2026 tightening moved opposite ways in the same year [9][13] — which is exactly why it should not be welded to a tax designed for permanence ([`../02-proposal/constitutional-design.md`](../02-proposal/constitutional-design.md)).

## 4. The distributional reality check: who buys the dispersal?

The objection's image is a fire-sale of Britain to foreign buyers. Size it against the verified baseline and the counter-flow.

**The baseline is already international at the top.** Overseas investors held a record 58.8% of UK quoted shares at end-2024, against 11.6% for UK individuals [14] — the largest ownership migration in the market's history happened decades before this proposal, without any inheritance-tax cause ([`corporations.md`](corporations.md) §4). Prime central London property has likewise been priced by internationally mobile money for a generation, and has spent a decade *derating* as milder taxes (non-dom abolition, SDLT surcharges) squeezed it [15]. For quoted equity and trophy property, "selling to foreigners" is substantially a description of the status quo, not a prediction about the tax.

**The flow is small against the stock.** Over £100bn a year passes in UK inheritances and gifts, against a ~£13.6tn household wealth stock ([`asset-prices.md`](asset-prices.md) §1) — and only the above-threshold, non-exempt slice of that flow becomes dispersal sales. What the tax changes at the margin is that some previously never-traded blocks (family company stakes, estates, land) reach the market each year at mortality speed. The buyers of that marginal supply will include foreign capital roughly in proportion to its existing market presence — which at the top is majority — *unless* other bidders show up. They do, by design:

- **The National Endowment Fund** takes illiquid above-threshold receipts in-specie — non-voting, self-liquidating stakes — standing between "heir must fire-sell" and "foreign acquirer of first resort" ([`corporations.md`](corporations.md) §4; [`../02-proposal/mechanism.md`](../02-proposal/mechanism.md) §4). For strategically sensitive enterprises this is the explicit answer to the geopolitical first-mover objection ([`../04-case-against/capital-flight.md`](../04-case-against/capital-flight.md) §5): in-specie settlement *increases* domestic anchoring of exactly the assets the objection worries about.
- **Domestic institutions.** Annuitisation channels late-life wealth into insurer reserves; foundations and employee-ownership trusts absorb operating businesses ([`asset-prices.md`](asset-prices.md) §7; [`charitable-giving.md`](charitable-giving.md); [`corporations.md`](corporations.md) §4).
- **~890,000 newly capitalised 25-year-olds a year** — up to ~£45bn of annual endowment flow ([`asset-prices.md`](asset-prices.md) §2.1) — buying at the entry level: not the bidders for the estate or the founder block, but the counter-rotation that re-domesticates the *base* of the ownership pyramid (UK individuals' 11.6% is the number the endowment is built to raise).
- **Heritage machinery for the nationally significant.** Acceptance-in-lieu and in-specie settlement route pre-eminent art, archives, and heritage property to museums and public collections rather than the auction block ([`asset-prices.md`](asset-prices.md) §8); the dedicated treatment of country houses, collections, and landmark assets is in [`trophy-assets.md`](trophy-assets.md).

**The honest residual:** at the trophy tier, the marginal buyer of a dispersed £30m asset is very likely foreign or institutional, and prime prices fall as dynastic demand exits — a repricing this project classifies as the dynastic premium leaving the market, not as a loss to defend against ([`asset-prices.md`](asset-prices.md) §3). Where a specific asset class's foreign absorption is judged intolerable — housing above all — the §3 shelf, not this tax, is the dial to turn.

## 5. Still attracting foreign investment

*Labelled a design argument throughout: the distinction below is how the regime is built, not a measured FDI forecast.*

The regime's pitch to foreign capital divides cleanly:

**Productive and greenfield investment: untouched, absolutely.** The repo's core retention proposition — "the owner never gets a bill" ([`../08-capital-flight/retention-attraction.md`](../08-capital-flight/retention-attraction.md)) — applies to the foreign investor in a stronger form than to any native. A foreign firm building a UK plant, a foreign fund backing UK ventures, a foreign founder scaling a UK subsidiary: their returns are never touched by this tax, their remittances and exit are never touched, and — dying outside the system, with only gratuitous transfers of UK-situs assets in scope — corporate- and fund-structured investors never generate a charging event at all. A tax on inheritance is, by construction, the one wealth-related tax a foreign corporate investor structurally cannot pay. Meanwhile the screening such investment already faces (NSI Act notification in 17 sensitive sectors [7]) is unchanged by the proposal, and the things FDI actually chases — market, skills, courts, infrastructure — are what the endowment side of the policy invests in ([`../02-proposal/endowment.md`](../02-proposal/endowment.md)).

**Positional-asset acquisition: managed, by the named shelf.** Land, existing housing, heritage — assets whose supply does not grow when money arrives — are where foreign purchase is a distributional question rather than an investment one, and where §3's instruments operate independently of the tax. The peer evidence is that drawing this line does not close an economy: Australia bans foreign purchase of established homes while running one of the OECD's most FDI-dependent economies through FIRB [11]; New Zealand, Denmark, and Switzerland court capital while restricting exactly this asset class [8][12][13].

**Wealthy individuals: the welcome mat exists and is honest.** The inbound-wealth design ([`../08-capital-flight/inbound-wealth.md`](../08-capital-flight/inbound-wealth.md)) gives arriving fortunes an entry ledger that never taxes pre-arrival wealth, a decade-long on-ramp before worldwide receipts bite, and the flypaper bargain stated openly: spend, invest, and build here freely; every UK-situs asset the fortune buys enters the situs net permanently, and if the dynasty stays, it ends here.

**The tension, stated rather than smoothed.** Heavier situs enforcement is not free for inbound capital: entry declarations, envelope look-through, registration-or-blockage, and the terminal situs charge all raise the effective cost of *holding UK positional assets in dynastic hands* — which is the point — and a rational foreign dynast discounts UK trophy property by the expected terminal charge, thinning one slice of inbound demand ([`asset-prices.md`](asset-prices.md) §3). The design accepts that discount as the price of symmetry: the alternative — exempting foreign holders from the situs net to keep their bid — would recreate, at 100%, the two-tier non-dom politics the UK spent 2008–2025 dismantling. No quantified net-FDI estimate is offered here because none exists to verify; the honest claim is directional and structural: the tax spares every productive channel completely, and the channels it burdens are the ones every peer listed above already burdens on purpose.

## Honest limits

- **The genuine-substance leak is real and permanent.** A foreign operating group with real substance that owns UK businesses transmits abroad at the group level, untaxed by the UK. Today's IHT lives with the same shape; the look-through catches artifice, not genuine foreign corporate ownership. Closing it fully would require taxing foreign shareholders on foreign share transfers — extraterritorial and unworkable.
- **The institutional-accumulation concern is conceded, not answered.** If the underlying worry is "too much of Britain owned by non-residents or by states," this tax neither causes nor cures it; §3's shelf is the honest referral, and an advocate who implies the tax fixes foreign ownership is overclaiming.
- **No quantitative estimate of the marginal foreign-absorption share of dispersal sales exists.** The 58.8% baseline [14] bounds intuition but does not measure the flow; a research gap, flagged for the modelling agenda in [`macro.md`](macro.md).
- **Companion-shelf efficacy is mixed in the evidence.** Foreign-buyer bans' price effects are contested (Canada's and Australia's are recent and under-evaluated; New Zealand partially reversed course in 2025 [9]); the shelf is presented here as *available and normal*, not as proven housing policy.
- **The ATED-extension idea in §2 is a sketch, not a design.** Periodic charges on entity-held land beyond dwellings would need its own avoidance, valuation, and treaty analysis before it could be claimed as closable machinery.
