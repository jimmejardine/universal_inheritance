# Asset Prices

**Executive summary.** When estates above the threshold are sold into the market rather than transmitted intact, turnover rises across housing, equities, farmland, and collectibles — with downward price pressure concentrated wherever current prices capitalise inheritance-tax reliefs or dynastic hoarding, and upward pressure at the entry level where the endowment arrives as purchasing power. This file works each asset class through the same discipline: verified anchors first, then transmission channels, then a bull case and a bear case argued with equal seriousness, then a tagged net judgement and a distributional ledger. The 2024–26 UK policy environment has, usefully, run several small natural experiments — the APR reform repricing farmland, the non-dom abolition repricing prime London, VAT repricing private schooling — that let the speculation be disciplined by observed elasticities. Directions are fairly confident, magnitudes are not, and this file resists the temptation to promise cheap houses.

## 1. The common mechanism

Under the current regime, a large estate's assets typically pass to heirs with little or no market transaction (and, in the US, a stepped-up basis rewarding retention). Under the proposal, above-threshold value must be converted: sold to pay the tax, transferred in-specie to the state fund, or given to charity (which itself typically diversifies). The result is structurally higher turnover of previously locked assets. Whether higher turnover moves *prices* depends on each market's depth and on who absorbs the supply — hence the asset-by-asset treatment below.

One overarching caution: annual death-driven asset flows are small relative to total asset stocks. Over £100bn a year now passes in UK inheritances and gifts [5], against a household wealth stock of £13.6tn (ONS Wealth and Assets Survey, April 2020 to March 2022) [6] — under 1% a year. Steady-state price effects are therefore modest in deep markets; the sharper effects are in thin, relief-distorted markets (farmland, AIM shares, trophy assets) and in the transition period.

## 2. Housing — owner-occupied

**Supply side.** Estates selling houses rather than transmitting them, plus earlier downsizing by the elderly ([savings-consumption.md](savings-consumption.md) §4), increases effective supply — concentrated in exactly the large-family-home segment that is under-occupied ([`../01-problem/housing.md`](../01-problem/housing.md)). Downward pressure starts at the top end (prime London/SE, where inherited property is most concentrated) and filters down as chains lengthen: the filtering literature supports the mechanism, though filtering is slow and leaky — Rosenthal estimates US housing filters down the income distribution at about 2.5% a year for rental stock but only about 0.5% for owner-occupied homes [1].

**Demand side.** The universal endowment adds first-time-buyer purchasing power at the entry level — an offsetting upward pressure examined seriously in [endowment-effects.md](endowment-effects.md) §5. The net distributional twist is a *rotation*: top-end prices soften, entry-level prices firm, the ratio between them compresses.

**Honest bounds.** No credible model gets dramatic national price falls from this channel alone; housing outcomes remain dominated by planning, credit, and construction policy. The claim to make publicly is about *access and turnover* — more family homes actually reaching the market each year — not a price crash.

### 2.1 The deep dive: anchors

- **The demand wave, sized.** ~892,000 UK residents were aged 25 at the ONS mid-2024 count ([`../02-proposal/revenue-model.md`](../02-proposal/revenue-model.md)); at £50k each the endowment injects up to ~£45bn/year of new capital into the cohort (£35–45bn once residence rules trim the cohort). Against ~1.1m annual UK residential transactions (1,102,010 in 2024, HMRC — verified in [endowment-effects.md](endowment-effects.md)) the flow is material at the entry level but not a wall of money nationally.
- **The capitalisation warning.** Carozzi, Hilber & Yu's Help-to-Buy evaluation (verified in [endowment-effects.md](endowment-effects.md) §5): a demand-side subsidy raised new-build *prices* with no detectable construction response in supply-constrained Greater London, but raised *construction* with no noticeable price effect near the elastic English/Welsh border. Where supply cannot respond, subsidy becomes price.
- **The landlord estate pipeline.** The English Private Landlord Survey 2024: the median individual landlord is 59; 64% are aged 55 or over; 36% are already retired; and the 17% of landlords with five or more properties account for 49% of tenancies (and are older still — 77% aged 55+) [7]. Buy-to-let is heavily an asset of the generation whose estates the scheme will process first.
- **The inheritance-backed top end.** Family assistance is involved in roughly half of under-35 purchases, making the "Bank of Mum and Dad" equivalent to a top-ten mortgage lender ([`../01-problem/housing.md`](../01-problem/housing.md)); above the entry level, expected inheritances also collateralise trading up and guarantor lending.

### 2.2 Transmission channels under the scheme

| Channel | Direction for prices | Segment hit |
|---|---|---|
| ~890k × £50k arriving annually as (partial) deposits | Up | Entry level, especially supply-constrained cities (Help-to-Buy logic) |
| Estate dispersal: houses sold to pay the charge rather than transmitted | Down | Family-home and top-end segments, over a mortality cycle |
| Elderly downsizing once dying-in-the-big-house loses its tax reward | Down | Large family homes ([savings-consumption.md](savings-consumption.md) §4) |
| BTL portfolios broken up at death (no passive-landlord deferral — [`../02-proposal/carveouts.md`](../02-proposal/carveouts.md)) | Down for prices, up for owner-occupier supply; rental supply falls | Ex-rental terraces and flats — classic first-buyer stock |
| Loss of inheritance-backed purchasing power (gifted deposits above threshold, expectation-collateralised lending) | Down | Mid-prime and prime, where BoMaD money sets marginal prices |
| Regional divergence: the endowment is worth most where houses cost least ([endowment-effects.md](endowment-effects.md) §2) | Up in cheap regions, down in inheritance-dense prime London/SE | Compresses the regional spread |

### 2.3 Bull and bear

**Bull case (prices supported or rising — especially entry level).** The endowment is a permanent, credit-multiplied demand injection: £50k as a deposit can unlock several times that in mortgage lending, and in supply-inelastic markets the Help-to-Buy evidence says sellers capture much of it. Meanwhile the BTL unwind cuts *rental* supply, pushing rents up and making ownership more attractive at any price; and estate-driven supply arrives only at mortality speed, a trickle against an annual demand wave that arrives every year from commencement. On this reading entry-level prices rise persistently and the affordability gain is partly illusory.

**Bear case (prices fall).** Supply arrives on three fronts at once — estate sales, earlier downsizing, landlord-estate dispersal — concentrated in exactly the stock first-time buyers and upsizers want; the top of the market simultaneously loses its dynastic marginal buyer and its inheritance-backed lending; and the endowment, unlike Help-to-Buy, is not housing-earmarked (education, debt, business uses leak demand away). Filtering transmits top-end softness down the chain; the national index drifts down even as transactions boom.

**Net judgement (speculation — confidence: medium on the rotation, low on the national level).** Both cases are partly right about different segments: the defensible central view remains the rotation — entry-level firm (partial capitalisation of the endowment is conceded, per [endowment-effects.md](endowment-effects.md) §5), top-end soft, the ratio compressing, national average roughly flat and still dominated by planning and credit policy. The honest inoculation line: this policy buys *turnover and access*, not cheapness ([`../14-communications/inoculation.md`](../14-communications/inoculation.md)).

**Distribution.** Winners: first-time buyers without parental wealth (deposit equalised), upsizing families (more large homes on the market), tenants who become owners of ex-BTL stock. Losers: prime-market owners and their agents; heirs who would have received houses intact; renters during the BTL transition if rental supply falls faster than tenants exit to ownership — a real, honest cost flagged for monitoring; non-endowed cohorts during phase-in ([`../02-proposal/endowment-delivery.md`](../02-proposal/endowment-delivery.md) §6 taper).

## 3. Prime and luxury property

**Anchors.** Prime central London is already running the natural experiment: values were 21.2% below their June 2014 peak by early 2025 (average PCL home ~£4.6m), with Savills attributing continued falls to the non-dom abolition and the raised stamp-duty surcharge [8]; Knight Frank's cross-asset table puts PCL at −4.3% over 2025 and −21.0% over ten years [9]. A decade of tax changes aimed at internationally mobile dynastic wealth has produced a persistent, orderly derating — not a crash, not nothing.

**Channels.** The top-end market depends unusually heavily on buyers purchasing *generational* assets: the £10m townhouse is bought partly because it can be passed on. The 100% band removes that succession value entirely; estate dispersal then adds supply of precisely these properties; principal-residence deferral ([`../02-proposal/carveouts.md`](../02-proposal/carveouts.md) §3) protects occupying widows, not absentee heirs.

**Bull case.** International buyers with pre-arrival wealth still buy: the regime taxes transmission, not acquisition, and the inbound-wealth design is deliberately neutral-to-welcoming ([`../08-capital-flight/inbound-wealth.md`](../08-capital-flight/inbound-wealth.md)) — an arriving fortune's first UK purchase is unaffected, and the non-dom episode shows demand thins rather than vanishes. Much of the dynastic premium has *already* been squeezed out by the 2014–25 derating, so the marginal repricing left for this scheme is smaller than the headline rate suggests; global scarcity value and amenity value of trophy London persists independent of succession.

**Bear case.** Strong, and stronger than for any other housing segment. The situs trap makes UK trophy property a *terminus* asset — every heir, resident or not, is caught on it ([`../08-capital-flight/inbound-wealth.md`](../08-capital-flight/inbound-wealth.md) §2) — so the rational international dynast discounts UK prime by the expected terminal charge; domestic dynastic demand is removed outright; and estate dispersal supplies the same segment. The observed PCL elasticity to far milder measures (non-dom, SDLT) is the empirical warning.

**Net judgement (speculation — confidence: medium-high that prime falls materially; magnitude low confidence).** Down, more than any other housing segment, over a decade-plus horizon; the one real offset is that prices have partly pre-adjusted. This is a feature, not a bug — the segment's derating is the dynastic premium leaving the market.

**Distribution.** Winners: buyers of formerly-prime stock (including conversion to flats), the state fund receiving in-specie property, boroughs gaining resident owners over lockbox vacancies. Losers: incumbent PCL owners, prime developers and agents, and — honestly — IHT receipts if prime values fall before the regime's first estates mature.

Above the prime tier sits the billionaire-only stock — chateaux, country estates, private islands — whose carrying costs prevent repricing to any lower-wealth buyer: the deep treatment is [trophy-assets.md](trophy-assets.md).

## 4. Farmland — the underappreciated pro-farmer argument

UK farmland persistently trades at prices yielding well under 2% on agricultural returns — average English farm rents of £174–253 per hectare (2024/25) against average GB sale prices of about £31,200 per hectare imply a gross rental yield below 1% [3] — because Agricultural Property Relief makes land a near-perfect IHT shelter: buy land, farm it nominally, pass it tax-free. APR demand from investors and estate-planners is capitalised into the price, which:

- prices working farmers and new entrants out of land they could service from farming income;
- rewards "land banking" over production;
- transfers the relief's fiscal cost to landowners as a capital gain, not to farming as support.

Remove the shelter (the proposal replaces APR with deferred payment for genuine working farms — [`../02-proposal/carveouts.md`](../02-proposal/carveouts.md)) and farmland prices should fall toward agricultural-yield value. **Losers: land bankers, institutional shelter-buyers, estates.** **Winners: tenant farmers, new entrants, county-council smallholdings, anyone who farms for a living.** The 2024–25 UK APR-reform protests framed relief restriction as anti-farmer; the second-order analysis says sustained relief is what makes farms unaffordable to farmers. This argument is underused and belongs in [`../06-palatability/framing.md`](../06-palatability/framing.md) and [`../14-communications/audience-messaging.md`](../14-communications/audience-messaging.md).

Confidence: medium–high on direction; magnitude depends on how much of the price is relief capitalisation versus amenity and development-option value (unverified — a researchable number).

### 4.1 The 2024–26 natural experiment

The "researchable number" above now has live evidence. Labour's October 2024 Budget proposed capping 100% APR/BPR at £1m per estate (50% relief — an effective 20% rate — above); farmland values across England and Wales, which peaked at £9,351/acre in Q3 2024, fell 5.1% over calendar 2025 to £8,696/acre — the first fall of over 5% in a calendar year since 2016 — with Knight Frank's head of farm sales attributing the stall directly to tax expectations, and noting that the government's partial U-turn (announced 23 December 2025: allowance raised to £2.5m, transferable between spouses to £5m per couple, effective 6 April 2026, ~1,100 estates expected to pay more) "calmed" the market and reduced the chance of forced-sale supply in 2026 [9][10]. Read as an event study, this is the relief-capitalisation channel operating in real time: a *prospective, partial* narrowing of the shelter — affecting perhaps a thousand estates a year — moved the national average price ~5% and froze turnover *before commencement*; the concession partially reversed it. Forward-looking repricing on announcement, exactly as §11 predicts.

### 4.2 How much of the price is shelter?

The demand being removed is documented, not hypothesised: the IFS notes farmland prices partly reflect demand from IHT avoiders [11], and CenTax finds nearly a third of estates claiming agricultural/business relief look like passive investors in farmland rather than farmers [12]. The arithmetic bound runs like this: at a sub-1% gross rental yield [3], farmland would need to fall by well over half to price off agricultural returns alone; nobody expects that, because amenity value, development-option value, and subsidy capitalisation are real and survive the scheme. **Speculation (confidence: low on the point estimate, medium on the range): full shelter removal implies price falls of roughly 20–40% from the 2024 peak, phased over the transition** — the 2025 experiment delivered ~7% from peak on a reform perhaps a tenth as large as this proposal, which is directionally consistent with the lower half of that range once the U-turn's reversal is accounted for.

### 4.3 What a 20–40% fall would mean

- **Incumbent balance sheets (the bear inside the bull).** Farm borrowing is secured on land; a large fall squeezes loan-to-value covenants and wipes out the retirement plans of owners who treated land as a pension. The deferral machinery ([`../02-proposal/carveouts.md`](../02-proposal/carveouts.md) §6) prevents forced sales at death, but cannot prevent the paper-wealth loss. Any honest advocacy concedes this: the policy transfers value from incumbent landowners (including genuine farmers) to entrants.
- **New entrants and expanding working farms (the bull).** Every pound off the price is a pound off the entry barrier the current regime built. At yield-anchored prices, land can once again be serviced from farming income rather than bought as a shelter.
- **Tenant farmers.** Under the working-farm test, absentee-owned land let as a passive rentier asset gets no deferral ([`../02-proposal/carveouts.md`](../02-proposal/carveouts.md) §6) — so landlord estates sell, and sitting tenants become the natural buyers at post-shelter prices. Risk case: churn and termination of tenancies during dispersal; protective sequencing (tenant right-of-first-refusal on estate sales) is worth adopting and is flagged for [`../07-implementation/`](../07-implementation/README.md).
- **County farms and public smallholdings.** Cheaper land is the binding input for the county-farm ladder; falling prices make replenishing public letting estates affordable again. Qualitative — no verified sizing.

**Net judgement (speculation — confidence: high on direction, low-medium on the 20–40% magnitude).** Down substantially, front-loaded on announcement (forward-looking premium) with a long tail as estates mature. Winners: working and tenant farmers, new entrants, county farms, food production over land banking. Losers: land bankers, institutional shelter-buyers, recent debt-financed purchasers, and retiring owner-occupier farmers counting on shelter-inflated exit values — the last group being the politically sympathetic face of the loss, to be engaged honestly ([`../13-movement/messengers.md`](../13-movement/messengers.md)).

## 5. City versus rural land

No verified evidence speaks directly to this; the entry is structured speculation throughout, anchored only in the mechanics above.

**Channels.** The endowment is spatially unbound capital: £50k is a ~17% deposit on the median England home but a third or more of a house in the cheapest regions — the grant is worth most where houses cost least ([endowment-effects.md](endowment-effects.md) §2). Estate dispersal simultaneously releases rural land and housing (farm consolidation unwinding, estate cottages sold separately), and the farmland repricing of §4 lowers the cost base of rural life and enterprise.

**Bull case for rural and cheaper regions.** A cohort arriving at 25 with a deposit *anywhere* is freed from the parental-housing-wealth geography that currently tethers young buyers to family equity; remote and hybrid work make the cheap-region option exercisable; falling land prices compound it. Result: a measurable young-household flow toward small towns and rural regions — a quiet levelling-up channel no spending programme has achieved.

**Bear case.** Agglomeration economics is untouched: the jobs, wage premia, and matching markets stay urban, and the young move to cities for careers, not deposits — a deposit subsidy does not relocate a labour market. Remote work has already been partially priced into rural markets. And the rural gentry assets the scheme disperses were held by the same class now buying back in via amenity demand.

**Net judgement (speculation — confidence: low-medium).** Mild convergence: the urban-rural and London-rest price gaps compress at the margin — mostly via the denominator (prime/urban softness, §§2–3) rather than a rural boom. Winners: cheaper regions' sellers and local economies, mobile non-graduates. Losers: none sharply; the effect is a gradient change, not a shock.

## 6. Education as an asset class

The substitute-inheritance question: if bequests above threshold are blocked, does the money flow into school fees and the human-capital arms race instead? [`../07-implementation/human-capital.md`](../07-implementation/human-capital.md) concedes the channel honestly — this entry prices it.

**Anchors.** The UK is again running the experiment: 20% VAT has applied to private school fees since 1 January 2025, expected by the Treasury to raise £1.725bn/year, with an official prediction of ~35,000 pupils (<0.5% of the pupil population) moving sectors [13]. The first post-VAT census (ISC, June 2026) shows pupil numbers down 3.5% year-on-year to ~526,600 — roughly 30,000 below the pre-VAT level, with the key intake years (Year 1, Year 3, Year 7, Year 12) down 5.6–6.6% [14]. Two lessons: demand for private schooling is price-elastic at the margin (the "they'll pay anything" story is false for the median private-school family), and taxing the channel is politically survivable.

**Channels.** Blocked above-threshold bequests raise the relative attractiveness of untaxable transfers, and education is the largest one: the design deliberately exempts education and maintenance to age 25 as consumption, not receipts ([`../07-implementation/boundary-problem.md`](../07-implementation/boundary-problem.md) via [human-capital.md](../07-implementation/human-capital.md)). Elite school and university places are positional goods with near-fixed supply — the Help-to-Buy logic of §2 in a different market: subsidised (here, tax-favoured-by-omission) demand against inelastic supply becomes price.

**Bear case for the scheme.** Wealthy parents redirect the blocked bequest into fees, tutoring, and donations; elite-tier fees inflate ahead of general inflation as dynasties bid for fixed places; the arms race Markovits describes intensifies, and advantage is laundered through credentials instead of capital — harder to see and impossible to tax. The scheme, on this reading, changes the *currency* of dynasty, not its existence.

**Bull case.** Three answers, in ascending strength. First, the VAT experiment shows the fee-payer base is thinner and more price-sensitive than the arms-race story assumes — a 3.5% exit on a partial pass-through, concentrated in new intakes [13][14]. Second, human capital must be re-earned each generation: the fee buys an opportunity the child must still convert through the labour market, where income tax already waits — it cannot compound while the heir sleeps ([`../07-implementation/human-capital.md`](../07-implementation/human-capital.md)). Third, the endowment universalises the launchpad the fees were buying: every 25-year-old gets the capital floor, and the £500k allowance still passes on a genuinely generous cash start on top of any education.

**Net judgement (speculation — confidence: medium).** Elite-tier fee inflation is a likely and conceded side-effect; total private-school demand may nonetheless *shrink* as the mass-affluent fee base (whose fees were partly funded by expected inheritances) thins. The honest formulation for the manifesto: **the scheme redirects the arms race from capital to cultivation; it does not end it — and cultivation is the version that must be re-earned.** Winners: state-educated endowment recipients (relative position), the Exchequer (VAT precedent). Losers: elite-school fee payers facing dynastic bidding, mid-tier private schools losing their marginal customer.

## 7. Equities

- **Dispersal of concentrated stakes.** Founding-family blocks liquidate over a generational cycle instead of compounding; free float rises; ownership disperses toward institutions, pension funds, and (via the endowment) the young. Price impact of block disposals is small in deep listed markets, larger for illiquid mid-caps; the deferred-payment machinery and optional state equity stake in [`../02-proposal/carveouts.md`](../02-proposal/carveouts.md) exist precisely to prevent forced fire-sales.
- **The AIM distortion unwinds.** UK AIM shares currently trade with a premium capitalising Business Property Relief — portfolios are marketed explicitly as "IHT solutions"; the OTS concluded that for third-party investors in AIM-traded shares the relief "is not necessary" to prevent business break-up, and the IFS puts the cost of relief on AIM shares at around £1.1bn a year [2]. Removing relief-driven demand deflates that premium: unwelcome for incumbent holders, welcome for price integrity.
- **Governance effect** (control, not price): covered in [dynasties-and-power.md](dynasties-and-power.md).

### 7.1 Ownership rotation, not level collapse

**Anchors.** The UK equity market is already an object lesson in ownership rotation without price collapse: overseas investors held a record 58.8% of UK quoted shares at end-2024 (up from 57.7% in 2022), UK individuals held 11.6% (£295bn, itself a record high), and the total market was £2.54tn [15]; insurance companies and pension funds — 45.7% combined in 1997 — held just 4.2% by 2022, a record low [15]. The largest ownership migration in the market's history moved *who holds* far more than *what it's worth*.

**Channels under the scheme.** Sellers: annuitising and consumption-smoothing wealthy elderly ([savings-consumption.md](savings-consumption.md) §§2–3), estates liquidating above-threshold portfolios, AIM-premium unwinding. Buyers and new permanent holders: insurance annuity reserves (invested, long-duration), the state endowment fund taking receipts in-specie ([`../02-proposal/constitutional-design.md`](../02-proposal/constitutional-design.md)), charitable foundations retaining diversified endowments ([charitable-giving.md](charitable-giving.md)), and 25-year-olds investing part of the grant.

**Bull case.** The rotation channels are near-symmetric: every seller's counterpart (insurer, state fund, foundation) is a *more* patient holder than a consuming heir; the state fund partially re-domesticates ownership after the pension exodus; deep global markets absorb UK-scale flows without price impact ([macro.md](macro.md) §1: composition changes, level roughly stable).

**Bear case.** The flow-through is not frictionless: mid-cap and AIM illiquidity means dispersal moves prices there (the AIM premium unwind is certain and intended); elderly sell-down is front-loaded while endowment-fund accumulation ramps slowly ([`../15-transition/revenue-bridge.md`](../15-transition/revenue-bridge.md)); and a UK-only scheme adds a marginal reason for globally mobile listings to domicile elsewhere.

**Net judgement (speculation — confidence: high on rotation, medium on neutral level).** [macro.md](macro.md)'s line holds: large change in composition and ownership, small change in level, concentrated exceptions in AIM and illiquid mid-caps. Winners: market governance, free float, patient-capital institutions. Losers: dynastic blockholders, IHT-portfolio managers, AIM incumbents.

## 8. Art, collectibles, and classic cars

Current UK conditional-exemption and acceptance-in-lieu schemes let heritage assets pass untaxed against public-access undertakings. Under the proposal, estates holding art above the threshold sell, donate to museums, or settle in-specie. Predictions: supply to the market rises at the masterpiece tier, softening prices; museum acquisitions via in-specie settlement rise substantially (a quiet cultural-policy win); the art market's role as an opaque store of dynastic value ([`../07-implementation/digital-and-novel-assets.md`](../07-implementation/digital-and-novel-assets.md) covers valuation games) shrinks. Thin markets mean price effects here are the largest and most visible of any asset class — and the least socially costly.

### 8.1 The deep dive

**Channels.** Three compounding bear channels: (i) estate liquidations into structurally thin markets, where a single trophy collection can be a year's auction supply; (ii) the narrowed conditional exemption ([`../02-proposal/carveouts.md`](../02-proposal/carveouts.md) §7) removes the pass-untaxed route for all but genuinely pre-eminent, publicly accessible works; (iii) valuation discipline — insurance-value cross-checks and statutory appraisal ([`../07-implementation/valuation.md`](../07-implementation/valuation.md)) kill the undervaluation games that currently let chattels slip through estates at fractions of market value, raising the effective tax on holding art to die with it.

**Bull case.** The marginal buyer of most art is global, and a UK regime does not move global demand; museum and charity absorption (acceptance-in-lieu expanded into routine in-specie settlement) provides a price-insensitive buyer of last resort at the top tier; and the consumption tier may *strengthen* — a die-with-zero culture among the wealthy elderly ([savings-consumption.md](savings-consumption.md) §3) buys the classic car to drive it, not to bequeath it. Collectibles that are consumption goods rather than dynastic stores hold up.

**Bear case.** Concentrated exactly where the money is: dynastic-store demand (art as an unregistered, portable, opaque vault for generational value) is removed from a market that thrives on it; estate supply is compulsory and lumpy; and the same enforcement that closes valuation games removes the discretion that made the market's opacity valuable.

**Net judgement (speculation — confidence: medium-high at the trophy/dynastic tier, low elsewhere).** Down hard at the tier priced as dynastic store; roughly flat at the passion-and-consumption tier; museums are the clearest winners of any entry in this file. Losers: trophy-asset holders, auction houses' estate-planning desks, the freeport economy.

For what physically happens to grand collections — and to the rest of the billionaire-only asset tier (yachts, estates, islands, jets, clubs) — see [trophy-assets.md](trophy-assets.md), including the museum-capacity constraint on the acceptance-in-lieu channel.

## 9. Gilts and fixed income

**Anchors.** UK annuity demand is already reviving from its post-2015 trough: 2025 individual annuity premiums hit £7.4bn, the highest since pension freedoms were announced in 2014, with purchases using pots over £500k up 54% and buyers aged 70+ up 8% [16]. Annuity reserves are invested in long-duration assets — the wealthy elderly buying longevity insurance are, at one remove, buying gilts.

**Channels.** The scheme turbocharges the annuitisation channel: above the threshold, an annuity strictly dominates holding assets taxed at 100% at death ([savings-consumption.md](savings-consumption.md) §2), so insurer demand for long gilts and matching assets rises structurally. Against that, the transition bridge may require issuance while receipts ramp ([`../15-transition/revenue-bridge.md`](../15-transition/revenue-bridge.md)), and the state fund's in-specie receipts arrive mostly as equities and property, not bonds.

**Bull case (for gilt prices / lower long yields).** A new, permanent, regulation-driven class of long-duration demand from annuity reserves — a partial reversal of the DB-pension de-risking bid that compressed yields in the 2000s–2010s; plus improved steady-state fiscal arithmetic ([macro.md](macro.md) §3) supporting the sovereign curve.

**Bear case.** Bridge-period issuance meets a market already saturated with duration supply; any credibility wobble around a radical fiscal reform prices into gilts first; and the annuity bid, while real, is small against a multi-trillion gilt market — the affected population is thin ([savings-consumption.md](savings-consumption.md) §2's own caution).

**Net judgement (speculation — confidence: medium on direction, magnitudes small).** Net long-duration demand modestly positive in steady state; two-way and noisy in transition. Nothing here moves policy rates materially ([macro.md](macro.md) §3). Winners: annuitants (better rates from deeper markets), long-duration issuers. Losers: no sharp class; this is the quietest entry in the file.

## 10. Crypto and portable stores of value

**Channels.** A 100% transmission tax raises the return to *unseeable* stores of value — the classic avoidance channel — while the enforcement stack simultaneously raises their cost: CARF automatic exchange (46 jurisdictions by 2027, 29 more by 2028, the US by 2029), AML choke points at every regulated ramp, and the suspended-receipt presumption that freezes "lost key" estates under automated on-chain watch ([`../07-implementation/digital-and-novel-assets.md`](../07-implementation/digital-and-novel-assets.md)).

**Bull case (for crypto demand/prices).** Every wealth-transfer regime in history has driven some demand into portable, concealable assets — this one, at 100%, maximally so; self-custody plus inheritance-by-seed-phrase is the modern bearer bond, and a determined minority will pay a premium for it. If many jurisdictions adopted similar regimes, the aggregate flight bid could be non-trivial.

**Bear case.** The enforcement design converts hidden crypto into *frozen* crypto: it can be held but not moved, banked, or spent at scale without surfacing (the "not an inheritance in any meaningful sense" equilibrium — [`../07-implementation/digital-and-novel-assets.md`](../07-implementation/digital-and-novel-assets.md)); an enforcement-risk discount, not a premium, attaches to coins with dirty ledger provenance; and a UK-scale scheme is a rounding error in a globally priced asset.

**Net judgement (speculation — confidence: low in both directions, honestly).** Domestic avoidance demand: real, bounded, and shrinking as CARF coverage completes. Global price effect of a UK adoption: negligible. The entry matters for enforcement design, not for asset-price forecasting. Winners/losers: compliance industry up; heirs holding unprovenanced coins locked out of the useful economy.

## 11. Transition versus steady state

| Phase | Dynamics |
|---|---|
| Announcement window | Gifting wave within thresholds; attempts to front-run via trusts and emigration — see [`../15-transition/announcement-effects.md`](../15-transition/announcement-effects.md), [`../07-implementation/gifts-and-timing.md`](../07-implementation/gifts-and-timing.md), [`../08-capital-flight/evidence.md`](../08-capital-flight/evidence.md) |
| First decade | Grandfathering ([`../15-transition/grandfathering.md`](../15-transition/grandfathering.md)) damps the cliff; estate-driven supply builds gradually as covered deaths occur; relief premia (AIM, APR) deflate quickly since they are forward-looking |
| Steady state | Turnover permanently higher; prices settle at levels reflecting use-value rather than shelter-value; the endowment's demand effect fully phased in |

Note the asymmetry: *relief-capitalisation effects (farmland, AIM) arrive fast* — prices reprice on enactment, not on deaths — while *turnover effects (housing supply) arrive slowly* over a mortality cycle. Communications should not promise quick housing results ([`../14-communications/inoculation.md`](../14-communications/inoculation.md)).

**The transition spike, honestly.** The pre-commencement window will be the noisiest asset-price environment the policy ever produces, and most of the noise will be meaningless. Forward-looking premia (AIM, farmland, prime property) reprice on announcement day — the 2025 farmland episode delivered a 5% national fall and a frozen market off a reform a fraction of this scheme's size [9][10]; the gifting-and-restructuring rush ([`../15-transition/announcement-effects.md`](../15-transition/announcement-effects.md)) spikes turnover in everything divisible; thin markets (art, estates, trophy property) gap rather than drift; and every price move will be claimed by both sides as verdict. None of it is steady-state evidence: transition prices measure the rush, not the destination. The design answer is to pre-publish this paragraph's content — expected repricings, expected turnover spike, monitoring indicators — *before* announcement, so that the spike reads as forecast rather than unravelling ([`../14-communications/inoculation.md`](../14-communications/inoculation.md)); the fiscal answer is that the bridge arithmetic already budgets for it ([`../15-transition/revenue-bridge.md`](../15-transition/revenue-bridge.md)).
