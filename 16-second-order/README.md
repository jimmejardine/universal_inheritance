# 16 — Second-Order Effects

**Executive summary.** This section is honest futures analysis, not advocacy: it maps what actually changes in a society in the years and decades after a 100% inheritance tax above a £500k/$750k lifetime threshold, paired with the universal endowment, takes effect. Some effects are welcome and central to the case; some are unwelcome and must be managed; several are genuinely ambiguous, and this section's credibility depends on saying so.

## Method and stance

The rest of this project argues *for* the policy ([`../03-case-for/`](../03-case-for/)) and against its objections ([`../09-objections/`](../09-objections/)). This section changes register. It assumes adoption and asks: what happens next? Each file states the mechanism, cites the relevant empirical literature (elasticities, natural experiments, historical analogues), gives a direction and a confidence level, and flags where credible analysts could reach opposite conclusions. Quantitative claims have been checked against primary sources and cited where confirmed; the small number that could not be confirmed are marked "(unverified)".

Three disciplines applied throughout:

1. **Behavioural responses are assumed, not wished away.** People re-optimise around a 100% marginal rate more aggressively than around any lower rate.
2. **Transition dynamics are separated from steady state.** The first decade looks very different from year forty; [`../15-transition/announcement-effects.md`](../15-transition/announcement-effects.md) covers the announcement window itself.
3. **Welcome/unwelcome is judged against the policy's own goals** (breaking dynastic accumulation, universalising capital) — not against a preference for no change.

## The three headline judgements

1. **The policy converts dynastic wealth into three streams — public revenue, philanthropic capital, and market turnover — not one.** The charitable channel is large; anyone presenting the policy as a pure revenue machine is overclaiming. The case that this is still a categorical improvement rests on the end of hereditary *control*, argued in [charitable-giving.md](charitable-giving.md).
2. **The most electorally salient second-order effect is asset prices**, especially housing and farmland, where the direction is favourable to the policy's constituency but the magnitude is genuinely uncertain — see [asset-prices.md](asset-prices.md).
3. **Power re-concentrates along new axes.** The honest long-run claim is not "no elites" but "no elites by birthright alone" — see [dynasties-and-power.md](dynasties-and-power.md) and the state-power risk in [`../04-case-against/state-failure.md`](../04-case-against/state-failure.md).
