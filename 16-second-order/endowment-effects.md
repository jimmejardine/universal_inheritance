# What 25-Year-Olds Actually Do With £50,000

**Executive summary.** The direct evidence on universal five-figure capital grants at 25 is thin — no country has run one — so this file triangulates from the nearest natural experiments: the GI Bill and veteran land grants, lottery windfalls, unconditional cash-transfer RCTs, the UK Child Trust Fund, and baby-bond modelling. The triangulation supports mostly productive use (housing deposits, education, debt clearance, some business formation), but two failure modes must be engaged seriously: predation on recipients, and capitalisation of the grant into starter-home prices.

## 1. The evidence base, honestly graded

| Evidence source | What it was | Relevance to a £50k grant at 25 | Key lesson |
|---|---|---|---|
| GI Bill (1944–) | Education finance + loan guarantees for veterans | High — the great historical capital-grant natural experiment | Mass capital access at scale transformed education, homeownership, and arguably built the mid-century middle class; delivery design (accredited uses, guarantees) shaped outcomes (to verify magnitudes) [1] |
| Homestead Acts / veteran land grants | Capital-in-kind (land) to ordinary citizens | Medium — in-kind, different era | Broad capital distribution is administrable and durable; fraud and speculation absorbed a real share — guardrails matter [2] |
| UK Child Trust Fund | ~£250–£500 state seed, maturing at 18 | Medium — universal but tiny | Sums too small to shape outcomes; ~£1.7bn unclaimed across ~750k dormant accounts (NAO 2023) — age 18 and passive delivery failed; hence age 25 and active claiming in [`../02-proposal/endowment.md`](../02-proposal/endowment.md) [3] |
| Lottery windfall studies (Imbens et al.; Cesarini et al.) | Random unearned lump sums | Medium–high for behavioural response | Moderate windfalls are mostly saved/invested; labour-supply reduction real but small per pound ([heirs-and-work.md](heirs-and-work.md)); no dissipation wave [4] |
| GiveDirectly lump-sum RCTs | Unconditional transfers, Kenya | Medium — different income context | Lump sums outperform streams for investment (durables, business assets); no systematic vice spending [5] |
| Baby-bond modelling (Hamilton & Darity; Urban Institute projections) | Means-scaled birth grants maturing at 18 | High for design logic | Modelled to close a large share of the US black–white *median* wealth gap (to verify) [6]; enacted in Connecticut 2023 — first administrative data arriving 2040s |
| Alaska PFD | Annual dividend stream | Low–medium — stream, not stock | Universal unconditional payments are politically durable; no employment collapse [7] |

The honest summary: nothing maps one-to-one, but every adjacent experiment points the same way — recipients of unconditional capital mostly use it on legible life-building, and the failure cases are concentrated in *delivery design*, not recipient character.

## 2. Expected uses

Plausible central allocation for UK 25-year-olds (judgement, not data — flagged as such): housing deposits the largest single use, followed by education/retraining, high-interest debt clearance (an unambiguous balance-sheet gain), business formation for a minority, and partial retention as savings. £50k is a 10%+ deposit on the median UK house (~£290k, to verify), full undergraduate maintenance, or genuine seed capital — the calibration argument in [`../02-proposal/endowment.md`](../02-proposal/endowment.md) §3. Heterogeneity will be sharp: graduates in high-cost cities buy housing access; non-graduates in low-cost regions can clear debt *and* capitalise a trade or business — regionally, the grant is worth most where houses cost least, a quietly equalising feature.

## 3. Labour supply and education

From the lottery literature's marginal propensity to earn (~−0.1 per unit of unearned income, to verify [4]), a one-off £50k implies a small, temporary reduction in labour supply — weeks-to-months equivalent, not careers — partly composed of *productive* withdrawal: education, training, job search, relocation. The Finnish basic-income and Alaska evidence finds no meaningful employment decline from far larger cumulative transfers [7]. Verdict: slightly down in hours, up in human-capital investment; net ambiguous and small.

## 4. Failure mode one: predation

A known, dated, universal payment creates the most predictable scam surface ever designed. Expect:

- **Advance-fee and "unlock early" schemes** — factoring companies offering discounted cash now against the age-25 claim, the structured-settlement-purchase industry's playbook.
- **Mis-selling** at the claim date: high-fee investment products, crypto schemes, franchise frauds targeting 25th birthdays.
- **Intra-family capture** — pressure to hand the grant to parents or partners; coercive-control cases.

Design responses (to be reflected in [`../02-proposal/endowment.md`](../02-proposal/endowment.md) and [`../07-implementation/administration.md`](../07-implementation/administration.md)): statutory non-assignability of the pre-25 claim (void against factoring, as with state pensions); a cooling-off default holding the balance in a low-cost state fund until actively directed; regulated-advice vouchers at claim; FCA/CFPB enforcement priority in the first years. None of this is speculative — the CTF generated a small ecosystem of fee-harvesting providers even at £500 scale [3].

## 5. Failure mode two: does a universal £50k just raise house prices?

The serious objection. Demand-side housing subsidies capitalise into prices where supply is inelastic: UK Help-to-Buy evidence found prices of eligible new-builds rose materially — in supply-constrained areas close to the subsidy's value (to verify) [8]. If every 25-year-old arrives with £50k, sellers of starter homes capture part of it. Partial answers, in order of strength:

1. **The grant is capital, not housing-earmarked.** Unlike Help-to-Buy it does not tie to property purchase; education, debt, and business uses leak demand away from housing, muting capitalisation relative to a housing-specific subsidy.
2. **The same policy releases supply at the top.** Estate sales and elderly downsizing add stock ([asset-prices.md](asset-prices.md) §2) while the endowment adds demand — the package is closer to demand-and-supply-neutral than the endowment viewed alone.
3. **Cohort flow is gradual.** ~700k recipients/year against ~1.2m annual UK housing transactions (to verify) — a phased demand shift, not a wall of money.
4. **Residual honesty:** partial capitalisation at the entry level is still likely, and the full answer is supply policy, which this proposal cannot substitute for. Say so — the inoculation strategy in [`../14-communications/inoculation.md`](../14-communications/inoculation.md) depends on conceding this early. A staged-drawdown variant (e.g. half at 25, half at 35) would further damp the effect and is flagged as a fallback in [`../02-proposal/endowment.md`](../02-proposal/endowment.md).

## 6. Monitoring indicators

- Use-of-funds survey panel from the first cohort (deposit/education/debt/business/savings shares).
- Starter-home price index in high- vs low-supply-elasticity areas (difference-in-difference on the phase-in).
- Complaints and enforcement data on endowment-targeted financial products.
- Business-formation and training-enrolment rates at ages 25–27 versus pre-policy cohorts.
