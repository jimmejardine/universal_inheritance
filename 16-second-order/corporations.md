# Corporations: The Immortal Owner Problem

**Executive summary.** Corporations never die, so a tax triggered by death and gift looks, at first sight, like an open invitation to warehouse dynastic wealth inside immortal entities — and, one step further, to convert firms into guilds that pay favoured heirs "salaries" that are really bequests. This file answers the three forms of that challenge: the look-through principle means corporate immortality shelters nothing (shares are assets on a mortal owner's ledger); the genuine escape vectors — retained earnings, ownerless foundations, purpose trusts, offshore layers — are each either self-defeating, deliberately permitted under guardrails, or already policed by machinery specified elsewhere in this repo; and the guild scenario founders on the fact that disguised inheritance-by-salary is the most visible and most heavily taxed avoidance route available, while genuinely closed hiring is competitively punished in exactly the way the heir-CEO literature documents.

## Findings

- **A corporation cannot inherit and cannot bequeath.** The tax never needed a corporate death event: it bites when *ownership* — shares, partnership interests, membership rights — transfers gratuitously to a natural person, and every corporation is ultimately owned by mortal people, foundations, or the state ([`../02-proposal/mechanism.md`](../02-proposal/mechanism.md) §1).
- **Retained earnings are not an escape.** Profit compounding inside a firm accrues pound-for-pound to share value, and the shares are taxed at full market value when they pass; deferral inside the wrapper changes the tax *date*, not the amount — the same non-result as gifting early ([`../09-objections/avoidance.md`](../09-objections/avoidance.md)).
- **Ownerless structures are the serious vector, and the repo already splits them in two:** genuine enterprise foundations (Bosch, Zeiss, Novo Nordisk — performance-neutral on the Danish evidence [6]) are a *permitted and arguably welcome* destination under the charitable guardrails; sham "orphan" structures that preserve family enjoyment are recharacterised as trust-equivalents and taxed ([`../07-implementation/trusts-and-vehicles.md`](../07-implementation/trusts-and-vehicles.md) §2, [`../02-proposal/carveouts.md`](../02-proposal/carveouts.md) §4).
- **The tax is not an antitrust substitute and should never claim to be.** It removes *hereditary* control of corporations; corporate *scale and market power* remain competition law's job — the two are siblings, per the antitrust analogy that opens the repo ([`../00-capitalism/README.md`](../00-capitalism/README.md)).
- **Steady-state equity ownership (reasoned speculation):** dynastic family blocks give way to a mix of dispersed heirs, institutional investors, enterprise and charitable foundations, employee-ownership trusts, and a bounded, non-voting, self-liquidating state endowment stake — a *more* dispersed register than today's, not a nationalised one. The verified UK baseline is already largely non-dynastic: individuals held 11.6% of UK quoted shares at end-2024, overseas investors 58.8% [1].
- **The guild scenario:** paying the founder's child £2m a year is income, taxed at roughly 47% at UK top marginal rates, visible in payroll data, and — above the arm's-length band — recharacterised onto the heir's lifetime ledger under the market-rate test ([`../07-implementation/nepotism-and-networks.md`](../07-implementation/nepotism-and-networks.md) §1). It is the worst-taxed, best-lit avoidance route in the whole design.
- **The honest residual is the one already conceded:** networks, internships, and the surname on the CV are untaxable ([`../07-implementation/human-capital.md`](../07-implementation/human-capital.md)). Historical guilds gated trades by *family plus capital*; the scheme removes the capital gate (the endowment funds entry) and leaves the network gate standing.

## 1. The look-through principle: corporations are owned, and owners die

The objection's premise — "corporations are immortal, so they become the wealthiest individuals and never pay" — mistakes what the tax is a tax *on*. It is not a tax on holding wealth or on being wealthy; corporations can grow without limit under this design, exactly as living people can ([`../00-capitalism/what-this-is-not.md`](../00-capitalism/what-this-is-not.md)). It is a tax on the **gratuitous transfer of alienable value to natural persons**. A corporation never receives a bequest; its *shares* are bequeathed, and shares are assets on some mortal owner's ledger like any other asset ([`../02-proposal/mechanism.md`](../02-proposal/mechanism.md) §1).

So the corporate form changes the *wrapper* of what an heir receives, never whether receipt occurs:

| What the family holds | What the heir receives at transfer | Tax treatment |
|---|---|---|
| Direct shareholding | Shares at market value | Receipt on the ledger; 100% above headroom |
| Holding company over the operating firm | Holding-company shares | Identical — the interposed layer adds a valuation exercise, not an exemption |
| Family investment company with growth shares | Value shifted between share classes | Value-shift rules + provisional attribution ([`../07-implementation/trusts-and-vehicles.md`](../07-implementation/trusts-and-vehicles.md) §3) |
| Trust or foundation holding the shares | Distributions, or deemed receipts on 10-year anniversaries | Look-through: distribution charge + periodic charge ([`../07-implementation/trusts-and-vehicles.md`](../07-implementation/trusts-and-vehicles.md) §§1–2) |

The trust-and-foundation machinery generalises to every corporate form because it was built on the same principle: **substance over form, attribution to the persons who benefit**. Where no individual ever formally "receives" anything but a family enjoys corporate wealth indefinitely, the statutory trust-equivalent checklist (gratuitous contribution + discretionary benefit + retained family influence) recharacterises the structure and taxes it as a trust, with grantor-trust and CFC-style attribution as the drafted-law precedents [7]. The corporation is immortal; its owners' deaths are the tax points; and an entity engineered to have no owners at all is precisely what §2(b) below and the trust-equivalent rules exist to catch.

## 2. The escape vectors, honestly

Four routes deserve a straight answer rather than the summary table.

**(a) Retained earnings: compounding inside the firm.** A family company that never pays dividends compounds pre-transfer wealth inside the wrapper, untouched by any receipts event for decades. True — and irrelevant to the eventual bill, because retained earnings accrue to the value of the shares, and the shares pass at market value. What retention *does* buy is deferral plus a valuation contest: unlisted shares are appraised, not marked, and minority-stake and marketability discounts are the real leakage — which is why the valuation discipline (statutory methodology, capped discounts, mandatory review above £2m) lives in [`../07-implementation/valuation.md`](../07-implementation/valuation.md) and why the state-equity option exists to resolve disputes in kind ([`../02-proposal/mechanism.md`](../02-proposal/mechanism.md) §4). Deferral itself is not avoidance under a lifetime-receipts base: the ledger waits.

**(b) Ownerless entities: the foundation-held firm.** The strongest version of the objection: transfer the company to a foundation and there is never again an owner to die. The repo's answer, developed in [`../16-second-order/dynasties-and-power.md`](dynasties-and-power.md) §1 and [`charitable-giving.md`](charitable-giving.md), is to split the vector in two. The *genuine* enterprise foundation — Robert Bosch Stiftung holding ~94% of Bosch's capital, the Carl-Zeiss-Stiftung as sole shareholder of Zeiss and Schott, the Novo Nordisk Foundation's control structure — is a permitted destination: the Danish evidence (Thomsen et al.) finds foundation-owned firms perform comparably to conventionally owned ones with longer horizons [6], and a foundation-held firm satisfies the policy's actual goal, because nobody can consume, bequeath, or collateralise the endowment — wealth persists, *heritable private control* ends ([`charitable-giving.md`](charitable-giving.md) §6). The *sham* version — IKEA's Stichting architecture is the textbook exhibit — fails the control test (family board majority, family-adjacent benefit, material family employment) and is recharacterised and taxed as a private vehicle ([`../02-proposal/carveouts.md`](../02-proposal/carveouts.md) §4). The residual cost — a larger foundation sector with its own executive elite — is owned honestly in [`charitable-giving.md`](charitable-giving.md) §5, and answered with payout floors and sunset rules, not denial.

**(c) Perpetual purpose trusts and "corporate philanthropy."** Patagonia's 2022 restructuring is the live template: the Chouinard family moved 100% of the company into a purpose trust (2% of stock, all the votes, family-guided) and a §501(c)(4) collective (98% of stock, non-voting, receiving the dividends for environmental purposes) [8]. Under this proposal such a structure is analysed limb by limb: the non-voting economic interest dedicated to public benefit is a charitable transfer (exempt, subject to the anti-cycling rule and payout floor); the family-guided voting trust is exactly the retained-influence pattern the trust-equivalent checklist and control test exist to price — enduring family *stewardship* of a purpose is permitted, enduring family *benefit* is a taxable receipt when it flows and a deemed receipt when it warehouses. The design intent: a founder may bind a company to a purpose forever; what cannot be done is dress dynastic enjoyment in purpose-trust clothing.

**(d) The offshore-entity layer.** Interposing a BVI holding company or a Liechtenstein *Anstalt* between the family and the assets is a concealment strategy, not a legal-form strategy, and it is met with the enforcement stack rather than new doctrine: CRS automatic exchange (116 jurisdictions, 171m+ accounts exchanged in 2024), beneficial-ownership registers, registration-or-blockage for structures touching UK/US assets, and the anchor rule that a resident recipient of any benefit from an unregistered structure owes the tax personally with the burden of proof reversed ([`../07-implementation/trusts-and-vehicles.md`](../07-implementation/trusts-and-vehicles.md) §4, [`../08-capital-flight/international-coordination.md`](../08-capital-flight/international-coordination.md)). The honest gap noted there remains: the March 2025 US rollback of Corporate Transparency Act reporting reopened the *domestic* US shell-company blind spot [9] — a live enforcement asymmetry, not a conceptual defeat.

## 3. What this tax is not: the antitrust boundary

A boundary statement the manifesto should make explicitly, because critics will otherwise demand the tax solve a problem it was never aimed at. Under this scheme, corporations can and will remain enormous; institutional ownership can and will remain concentrated in index funds and insurers; monopoly power, network effects, and market dominance are all untouched. **That is the division of labour, not a failure.** The repo's framing device is that this tax is competition law applied to time — antitrust keeps the game open *between firms*, the inheritance tax keeps it open *between generations* ([`../00-capitalism/README.md`](../00-capitalism/README.md)). Each instrument attacks the concentration the other cannot reach: antitrust cannot stop a founder bequeathing a controlling block to an untested heir, and this tax cannot stop a firm cornering a market. What the tax removes from the corporate world is specific and important: the *hereditary* transmission of corporate control — the founder's grandchild governing by birthright ([`dynasties-and-power.md`](dynasties-and-power.md) §1). What it deliberately leaves for governance and competition policy is everything else, including the Berle–Means manager-capture problem that heir succession's demise actually *amplifies* ([`dynasties-and-power.md`](dynasties-and-power.md) §5).

## 4. The steady-state equity split — reasoned speculation

**Labelled as such: this section is projection from the design, not measurement.** The verified baseline first. UK quoted equity is already mostly non-dynastic: at end-2024, overseas investors held a record 58.8% of UK quoted shares, UK individuals 11.6% (£295bn, itself a record value; the share was 28.2% in 1981), banks 3.6%, and insurance and pension funds jointly just 4.2% at the last full sectoral breakdown (end-2022) — down from 45.7% in 1997 [1][2]. The dynastic problem the tax targets is therefore concentrated not in the FTSE register but in **founder blocks within listed firms and in the private-company universe** — where family control is the norm and where succession events are decisive.

Projected shifts over one to two mortality cycles, mechanism by mechanism:

| Ownership class | Direction | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| Dynastic family blocks (listed and private) | Down, decisively | Each succession event disperses above-threshold holdings to market, foundation, state fund, or many small heirs ([`dynasties-and-power.md`](dynasties-and-power.md) §1) |
| Dispersed individual heirs (each ≤ £500k) | Up | Per-recipient threshold makes splitting a controlling block among many recipients the *encouraged* route ([`../09-objections/avoidance.md`](../09-objections/avoidance.md)) |
| Institutional investors (insurers, pension funds, index funds) | Up | Estate sales into the market; annuitisation channels late-life wealth into insurance reserves invested in long assets ([`savings-consumption.md`](savings-consumption.md) §2) |
| Enterprise and charitable foundations | Up, materially | The charitable-giving surge is one of this section's most confident predictions; the Nordic enterprise-foundation model is the performance-neutral template ([`charitable-giving.md`](charitable-giving.md), [6]) |
| Employee-ownership trusts | Up | EOTs supply a non-dynastic, non-PE succession buyer — ~1,300 UK employee-owned businesses by end-2022 from a 2014 standing start [10]; the natural exit for first-generation owners who will not bequeath |
| National Endowment Fund (state) | Up then flat, bounded | Non-voting preferred stakes taken in lieu of cash from illiquid receipts ([`../02-proposal/mechanism.md`](../02-proposal/mechanism.md) §4, [`../15-transition/revenue-bridge.md`](../15-transition/revenue-bridge.md)) |

**Why the state stake is not creeping nationalisation.** The fund's equity holdings are bounded by design on four sides: they are **non-voting** preferred stock (no control rights, by statute); they arise only as a *payment mechanism* for illiquid receipts, not from any acquisition mandate; they are **self-liquidating** — each stake carries a statutory buy-back option at audited valuation ([`../02-proposal/carveouts.md`](../02-proposal/carveouts.md) §5) and the fund's statutory purpose is to *sell down* holdings to pay £50k endowments, so its natural trajectory is churn, not accumulation; and governance follows the Norwegian GPFG separation of political mandate from operational management, with voting-rights neutrality [11]. A fund whose legal obligation is to convert stakes into cash for 25-year-olds is structurally the opposite of a nationalisation programme — though the repo's honest register requires saying that this is the design's *intent*, that [`../04-case-against/state-failure.md`](../04-case-against/state-failure.md) steelmans the drift risk, and that the fund's scale in the 10–30-year window is the point of maximum governance stress ([`dynasties-and-power.md`](dynasties-and-power.md) §6).

The one-sentence steady state, judgement labelled: **a corporate register that looks more like today's — institutional, dispersed, professionally managed — with the family-block layer largely replaced by foundations, employees, and many small holders, and a modest transient state layer passing through; less dynastic than today, and no more state-owned in the long run.**

## 5. The guild question: hiring as disguised inheritance

The strongest of the three challenges, because it needs no exotic structure: the firm simply *employs* the people it wishes to enrich. Would firms become guilds — admitting favoured entrants at above-market pay as inheritance in monthly instalments?

**5.1 The wage channel is taxed, visible, and already policed.** Paying the founder's child £2m a year for a £150k job is not a free transfer; it is income, taxed at the UK additional rate plus NI (a marginal rate of roughly 47%), reported through PAYE/RTI, and deducted against corporation tax only if defensible. Compare the alternatives open to an avoider and the wage route is the *worst on offer*: a lifetime gift is at least private until declared; a salary is declared monthly by the payer. The design then closes the remaining gap with the market-rate test specified in [`../07-implementation/nepotism-and-networks.md`](../07-implementation/nepotism-and-networks.md) §1 — extended here, not duplicated: connected-person compensation in family-controlled firms above the arm's-length band is recharacterised as a **taxable receipt on the heir's ledger**, consuming threshold and then taxed at 100%, on top of the income tax already paid on the defensible portion. The machinery is not hypothetical: US closely-held-firm tax law has policed exactly this line for decades under IRC §162(a)(1)'s "reasonable compensation" doctrine (the *Exacto Spring* independent-investor test) [12], and UK law under the wholly-and-exclusively rule (*Copeman v William Flood & Sons*) [13] — both currently deployed to stop *understatement* or excessive family wages for corporation-tax reasons, and both demonstrating that arm's-length banding of related-party pay is administrable. Guild wages are, in short, a route on which the avoider pays twice and is seen doing it.

**5.2 Full guild closure is competitively self-defeating.** A firm that systematically hires heirs of the favoured over the ablest available candidates is running a deliberate talent handicap, and the verified succession literature prices it: family-CEO successions cause operating profitability to fall by at least four percentage points relative to professional successions (Bennedsen et al., Danish registry data), and heir-led firms underperform on profitability and market-to-book (Pérez-González) [4][5]. That evidence concerns the top job, but the mechanism — positions allocated by birth rather than contest produce worse occupants — generalises, and competitive product and capital markets punish it. Firms facing competition cannot afford to be guilds; the guild equilibrium requires that competition be suppressed. Which is the historical point:

**5.3 What guilds actually were.** The economic history is unambiguous that guilds were not spontaneous market clubs but **legally enforced monopolies**: Ogilvie's survey concludes they existed to extract rents by restricting entry to trades — with masters' sons enjoying privileged admission — and survived for centuries "not because they were efficient but because they served the distributional interests of powerful groups," sustained by exclusive privileges purchased from political elites [3]. Two implications for this proposal. First, guild formation is downstream of *monopoly protection*, and modern competition law — the sibling instrument of §3 — is precisely the standing prohibition on the legal privileges guilds needed. Second, the historical guild gate was double: family *and* capital (the mastership fee, the workshop, the stock). The scheme demolishes the capital half of the gate from both ends — heirs above the threshold lose the family balance sheet, and every 25-year-old gains £50k of entry capital ([`../02-proposal/endowment.md`](../02-proposal/endowment.md)) — while the family-network half persists in the weakened form of §5.4.

**5.4 The honest residual.** What survives all of the above is what [`../07-implementation/human-capital.md`](../07-implementation/human-capital.md) and [`../07-implementation/nepotism-and-networks.md`](../07-implementation/nepotism-and-networks.md) §4 already concede: the internship secured over lunch, the CV read because of the surname, the elite professional network as untaxable inheritance. A firm can lawfully hire the well-connected at *market* rates forever, and no receipts tax reaches that. The mitigations are the ones argued there — network advantage decays without capital behind it, converts to salary the income tax already meets, and now competes against endowment-funded entrants — and the repo does not claim more.

**Verdict (judgement).** The guild scenario is a real behavioural margin but a contained one: the wage route is the most visible and most heavily taxed disguise available, with recharacterisation machinery that has run for decades in two jurisdictions; systematic closed hiring is competitively self-punishing wherever antitrust keeps markets open; and what genuinely escapes — networks and polish — is the same untaxable residual the repo concedes everywhere else, now facing better-capitalised competition from below. Corporations under this scheme trend toward *less* guild-like composition than today's, because today's version of the guild — the family-controlled block installing heirs at will — is the specific thing the tax dismantles.

## Honest limits

- **Private-company valuation is the load-bearing wall.** Everything in §2(a) assumes appraisal discipline holds against minority-discount and marketability games; the repo's answer is [`../07-implementation/valuation.md`](../07-implementation/valuation.md), but sustained litigation pressure on unlisted-share valuation is where this file's claims would erode first.
- **The steady-state ownership mix in §4 is reasoned speculation.** No jurisdiction has run this experiment; the ONS baseline is verified but every projected direction is model-free inference from the design. The foundation share in particular could be much larger than sketched ([`charitable-giving.md`](charitable-giving.md) §2's high scenario), which changes the character of the outcome.
- **The arm's-length band has a soft edge.** Reasonable-compensation doctrine works case by case; a statutory band by role and sector may prove infeasible, leaving §5.1 dependent on audit intensity ([`../07-implementation/nepotism-and-networks.md`](../07-implementation/nepotism-and-networks.md), research gaps). Equity-based pay (founders granting heirs carried interest or cheap options) is a harder variant now owned by [`../07-implementation/buy-borrow-die.md`](../07-implementation/buy-borrow-die.md) §3: receipt at grant at the market value of the right, §409A-grade appraisal discipline, and detection through the ERS employer feed.
- **The antitrust boundary cuts both ways.** If competition policy weakens, §5.2's discipline weakens with it: in genuinely monopolised sectors, guild behaviour is affordable. The tax's corporate-power story is only as good as the sibling instrument it relies on.
- **The US domestic-shell gap** (CTA rollback, 2025) is a real, current hole in the §2(d) enforcement stack for the US variant [9].
