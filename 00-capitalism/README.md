# 00 — The Premise: We Come to Save Capitalism

**Executive summary.** This project is written by strong supporters of market capitalism, and the 100% inheritance tax it proposes is a *pro-capitalist* intervention: it removes the one internal mechanism — untaxed dynastic transmission — capable of converting the most productive economic system ever devised into a hereditary order that destroys both its output and its public consent. Read this section first; everything else in the repository assumes its stance.

## The stance, stated plainly

We are not reluctant capitalists, tactical capitalists, or capitalists-until-a-better-idea-arrives. Market capitalism — private property, free exchange, price signals, competitive entry, profit and loss — is the greatest engine of human prosperity ever built, and the evidence for that claim is overwhelming ([what-capitalism-gets-right.md](what-capitalism-gets-right.md)). The proposal in this repository exists to *preserve* that engine, not to replace it. Nothing in the design touches a single market mechanism: no price control, no wealth levy on the living, no state ownership, no cap on what anyone may earn, build, or keep for life ([what-this-is-not.md](what-this-is-not.md)).

What the proposal removes is the one transaction inside the market system that is not a market transaction at all: the untested, unpriced, unearned transfer of large capital stocks to heirs ([markets-vs-dynasties.md](markets-vs-dynasties.md)).

## The syllogism

The whole argument of this section compresses to six steps:

1. **Capitalism is the greatest engine of prosperity ever built.** Living standards, longevity, poverty reduction, innovation — no rival system approaches its record ([what-capitalism-gets-right.md](what-capitalism-gets-right.md)).
2. **Its legitimacy and its function both depend on rewards tracking contribution.** Functionally, prices and profits allocate capital well only when they respond to performance; morally, the public consents to unequal outcomes only while they appear earned ([../01-problem/meritocracy-legitimacy.md](../01-problem/meritocracy-legitimacy.md)).
3. **Untaxed dynastic inheritance decouples reward from contribution — and compounds.** The heir receives without creating; when *r > g*, the decoupled stock grows faster than the earned economy around it ([../03-case-for/piketty-r-vs-g.md](../03-case-for/piketty-r-vs-g.md)).
4. **Left alone, this converts market capitalism into hereditary capitalism.** Capital allocated by birth rather than by contest; markets closed by incumbents; the entrepreneur replaced by the heir ([markets-vs-dynasties.md](markets-vs-dynasties.md)).
5. **Hereditary capitalism destroys both the system's output and its public consent.** The economic, political, and legitimacy channels of that destruction are traced in [the-self-destruction-thesis.md](the-self-destruction-thesis.md), with the damage evidence in [../01-problem/](../01-problem/README.md).
6. **Therefore the most pro-capitalist policy available is to break dynastic transmission while leaving every market mechanism untouched.** That is precisely what a recipient-based 100% tax above a generous threshold does ([../02-proposal/mechanism.md](../02-proposal/mechanism.md)).

Reject any step and the conclusion falls; each step has its own file or section carrying the evidence. We believe every step holds.

## The antitrust analogy

Nobody calls competition law anti-capitalist. When a monopolist buys up rivals, corners supply, and closes the market behind it, the state intervenes — not against the market but *for* it, because the market's own winners, left unchecked, will end the competition that made them. Antitrust defends the game against the winning player's attempt to stop the game.

Inheritance is the same problem one generation deferred. A fortune fairly won in open competition becomes, at the moment of transmission, a position never competed for — held by someone who never entered the contest, compounding across generations into exactly the entrenched, market-closing concentration that antitrust exists to prevent ([../01-problem/political-capture.md](../01-problem/political-capture.md), [../16-second-order/dynasties-and-power.md](../16-second-order/dynasties-and-power.md)). A 100% inheritance tax above a generous threshold is competition law applied to time: it keeps the game open between generations as antitrust keeps it open between firms. The claim that it is anti-capitalist should sound as strange as the claim that the Sherman Act was.

## Who this section is for

The sceptical centre-right reader — the person whose first reflex on hearing "100% inheritance tax" is *this is confiscation dressed up*. This section asks that reader to test one proposition: that everything they value about capitalism (reward for enterprise, open competition, dispersed power, prosperity) is threatened by dynastic accumulation, not by its removal — and that the thinkers who saw this first were capitalism's greatest friends, from Adam Smith to Carnegie to Buffett ([capitalist-supporters.md](capitalist-supporters.md)). The calm statement of what the proposal is *not* — not a wealth tax, not socialism, not anti-family — is [what-this-is-not.md](what-this-is-not.md).

A note on sincerity, because the sceptical reader will probe for it. The pro-capitalist framing here is not a rhetorical wrapper chosen for a hostile audience; it is the project's actual position, and the rest of the repository is consistent with it. The proposal contains no wealth tax, no income-tax rise, no state acquisition of assets, and no cap on lifetime earnings or holdings; its intellectual lineage is Smith–Mill–Carnegie–Roosevelt, not Marx ([../05-history/README.md](../05-history/README.md)); its strongest objections are steelmanned rather than strawmanned ([../04-case-against/README.md](../04-case-against/README.md)); and its redistribution mechanism creates seven hundred thousand new private capital-holders a year rather than a larger state (figure to verify — [../02-proposal/endowment.md](../02-proposal/endowment.md)). A movement that wanted to end capitalism would have written a different repository.

## Reading order

Within this section, the files build in sequence and are best read in the order listed: first the inventory of what is being defended ([what-capitalism-gets-right.md](what-capitalism-gets-right.md)), then the conceptual distinction that carries the whole argument ([markets-vs-dynasties.md](markets-vs-dynasties.md)) with its historical deepening ([feudal-remnant.md](feudal-remnant.md)), then the mechanism of collapse ([the-self-destruction-thesis.md](the-self-destruction-thesis.md)), then the authorities ([capitalist-supporters.md](capitalist-supporters.md)), then the boundaries ([what-this-is-not.md](what-this-is-not.md)). A reader persuaded by the premise should proceed to the damage evidence in [../01-problem/](../01-problem/README.md) and the design in [../02-proposal/](../02-proposal/README.md); a reader unpersuaded should go straight to the steelmen in [../04-case-against/](../04-case-against/README.md) and judge whether their side of the argument has been handled honestly — that test, more than any positive claim, is what this project stakes its credibility on.

In the eventual manifesto this section is the opening chapter: the stance must be established before the diagnosis, because every subsequent claim reads differently — and lands on different audiences — depending on whether the reader believes the authors want capitalism repaired or replaced ([../06-palatability/framing.md](../06-palatability/framing.md), [../14-communications/narratives.md](../14-communications/narratives.md)).
