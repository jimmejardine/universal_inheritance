# Capitalist Supporters of Inheritance Scepticism

**Executive summary.** Scepticism about inherited wealth is not a socialist import into capitalism but a continuous native tradition running from Adam Smith through Bentham, Mill, Carnegie, and Theodore Roosevelt to Buffett, Bill Gates Sr., and the Giving Pledge — the system's founders, richest practitioners, and staunchest theorists. This file organises that lineage, states the two great pro-inheritance counter-authorities (Hayek and Friedman) fairly, and shows why their arguments — aimed at estate confiscation by the state — do not reach a recipient-capped, universally redistributed design.

## The lineage, organised

| Jeremy Bentham (1795) | Utilitarian architect of legal-liberal reform | Escheat over taxation: windfalls to distant heirs are the least-burdensome revenue source ever identified — "supply without burthen" | [../05-history/mill-1848.md](../05-history/mill-1848.md) |

The pattern is diagnostic. These are not the system's critics; they are its founders (Smith), its philosophers (Bentham, the Mills), its most successful practitioners (Carnegie, Buffett, the Pledge signatories), and its political guardians (Roosevelt). Each reached inheritance scepticism *from* capitalist premises: Smith because entails throttled land markets; Mill because desert attaches to earning; Carnegie because heirs are harmed and fortunes are socially co-produced; Roosevelt because republics cannot coexist with hereditary economic aristocracy; Buffett because the "ovarian lottery" allocates nothing efficiently. The socialist tradition arrived at the same target later and for different reasons — the capitalist lineage is older ([../05-history/README.md](../05-history/README.md)).

## Three entries developed

**Smith.** The *Wealth of Nations* attack on entails is not incidental moralising but load-bearing economics: laws that bound estates to bloodlines in perpetuity kept land — the era's dominant capital — off the market, in the hands of owners selected by birth rather than by willingness to improve it. Smith's charge that entails rest on "the most absurd of all suppositions," that the dead should govern the living's property arrangements (paraphrase; exact wording to verify against Book III, ch. 2), is this project's argument in eighteenth-century dress: dynastic transmission is a *market restraint*, and removing it is deregulation, not confiscation. The American founders read him and legislated accordingly ([../03-case-for/democratic-renewal.md](../03-case-for/democratic-renewal.md)).

**Carnegie.** "The Gospel of Wealth" supplies the practitioner's twin arguments no theorist could: that the man who dies rich "dies disgraced" because the fortune was socially co-produced and owed back, and that great bequests injure the recipients — the observation behind the modern "Carnegie effect" literature on heirs' labour-force withdrawal ([../05-history/carnegie-1889.md](../05-history/carnegie-1889.md), [../16-second-order/heirs-and-work.md](../16-second-order/heirs-and-work.md)). Carnegie endorsed graduated death duties as the state's proper backstop where private virtue failed — the exact structural role this proposal assigns the tax.

**Roosevelt.** TR's case was neither fiscal nor egalitarian but *constitutional*: fortunes "swollen beyond all healthy limits" and transmitted intact would recreate in economic form the hereditary aristocracy the republic was founded to escape (phrases to verify — [../05-history/roosevelt-1906.md](../05-history/roosevelt-1906.md)). He is the direct ancestor of this project's claim that transfer taxation is order-maintenance for a free polity, and the standing proof that the position belongs naturally on the political right ([../12-political-adoption/right-parties.md](../12-political-adoption/right-parties.md)).

## The other side, stated fairly: Hayek and Friedman

Honesty requires the two heaviest pro-market authorities who defended inheritance, at full strength:

**Hayek** (*The Constitution of Liberty*, 1960, ch. 6 — to verify) argued that the family is a legitimate steward of capital and of the cultural transmission of standards; that passing on material advantage is continuous with passing on values and education; and that channelling parental ambition into wealth transfer is *less* socially harmful than channelling it into nepotistic placement of heirs into positions of power. Inequality of starting points, on his account, is part of the price of a free society, and the alternative — state allocation of starts — is worse.

**Friedman** (*Capitalism and Freedom*, 1962, ch. 10 — to verify) argued that the distinction between inherited property and inherited talent is ethically arbitrary — no one deserves their genes either; that a person is entitled to dispose of what they have legitimately earned, and the freedom to give is part of the freedom to use; and that attempts to equalise outcomes destroy the incentive structure freedom requires.

These are serious arguments and this project does not caricature them ([../04-case-against/liberty-autonomy.md](../04-case-against/liberty-autonomy.md) carries the full steelman).

## The answer

Three responses, in ascending order of force:

1. **Both were answering a different proposal.** Hayek and Friedman wrote against estate confiscation *to the state* — the mid-century model in which death duties fed general government expenditure. The design here is receipt-capping with universal private redistribution: every pound above the cap becomes private capital in the hands of young private citizens ([../02-proposal/endowment.md](../02-proposal/endowment.md)), and assets sell to private buyers, not to ministries ([what-this-is-not.md](what-this-is-not.md)). The state is a conduit, not a destination. The "state allocation of starts" Hayek feared is exactly what the design avoids: the endowment is unconditional, uniform, and unallocated.
2. **Hayek's nepotism trade-off cuts the other way at scale.** Below the threshold — the family home, education, a genuine start — his stewardship argument wins, and the design concedes it: £500k per child flows untaxed ([../02-proposal/thresholds.md](../02-proposal/thresholds.md), [../07-implementation/boundary-problem.md](../07-implementation/boundary-problem.md)). Above it, "family stewardship" means multi-generational command of firm-scale capital by untested stewards — the documented underperformance case ([markets-vs-dynasties.md](markets-vs-dynasties.md)), not the transmission of standards.
3. **Friedman's own commitments strain against dynasty.** Friedman championed equality of opportunity against equality of outcome, school vouchers to break accident-of-birth schooling, and the negative income tax as a floor without behavioural strings (readings to verify). A compounding hereditary capital aristocracy fails his opportunity test more comprehensively than any policy he attacked; and his genes-and-property parity argument, taken seriously, proves too much — no one proposes redistributing talent *because it cannot be done*, whereas financial inheritance is the one inherited advantage a liberal order *can* neutralise without touching persons ([../03-case-for/ethical.md](../03-case-for/ethical.md), [../07-implementation/human-capital.md](../07-implementation/human-capital.md)).

Note also what both concede: neither defends inheritance as *earned* by the heir. The desert claim — the moral core of capitalism's self-justification — is surrendered even by inheritance's best defenders; their case rests entirely on the giver's liberty and the family's function, which the threshold and lifetime-freedom design substantially honours.

## The modern revealed preference

The most striking twenty-first-century evidence is behavioural. The people who best understand large fortunes — those who built them — increasingly decline to transmit them. Buffett has pledged over 99% of his wealth away from his heirs (to verify); the Giving Pledge institutionalised the same choice across hundreds of founders; Gates Sr. organised billionaires *against* the repeal of the tax on their own estates [3][4]. When the arch-capitalists voluntarily do to their own fortunes roughly what the proposal would require of everyone's, the claim that the policy is anti-capitalist loses its last footing. What the Pledge does by private virtue, the proposal does by public rule — with the proceeds flowing not to foundations chosen by the wealthy but to every citizen equally ([../03-case-for/equal-start.md](../03-case-for/equal-start.md), [../16-second-order/charitable-giving.md](../16-second-order/charitable-giving.md)).

## Verification checklist

- Smith's "manifest absurdity" passage on entails, *Wealth of Nations* Book III, ch. 2 — exact wording and target.
- Hayek, *The Constitution of Liberty* ch. 6 and Friedman, *Capitalism and Freedom* ch. 10 — confirm chapters and reconstruct arguments against the text.
- Buffett quotations ("ovarian lottery"; "enough so they can do anything…" — the latter often attributed via *Fortune*, 1986) and the >99% pledge figure.
- Giving Pledge signatory count (≈240 as of mid-2020s).
- Gates Sr. / Chuck Collins, *Wealth and Our Commonwealth* (2002) — the "society's investment" argument.
- Friedman's voucher and negative-income-tax positions as opportunity-egalitarian commitments.
