# Markets vs Dynasties

**Executive summary.** "Capitalism" names two systems that share private property and little else: *market* capitalism, in which capital is continuously contested and rewards track value creation, and *dynastic* capitalism, in which capital is entrenched by birth and rewards track descent. This file draws the distinction precisely, shows that inheritance is the one market-free transaction at the heart of the market system, and states what the proposal removes — that transaction alone — and what it leaves untouched: everything else.

## Two systems, one name

The public argument about inheritance tax is confused because both sides say "capitalism" and mean different things. Distinguish:

| Dimension | Market capitalism | Dynastic capitalism |
|---|---|---|
| What is rewarded | Value creation — serving customers, building firms, taking risks that pay | Birth — presence in the right will |
| How capital is allocated | Contested: capital flows to whoever uses it best, policed by profit and loss | Entrenched: capital stays where it landed, policed by estate lawyers |
| Attitude to competition | Fuel — the mechanism that makes the system work | Threat — the force that erodes the position |
| The entrepreneur's status | Hero of the system; its central figure | An exit event; the dynasty's founder, never repeated |
| Churn at the top | Schumpeterian turnover — fortunes rise and fall with performance | Buddenbrooks persistence — the same families, defended not created |
| Time horizon of privilege | One competitive cycle; must be re-earned | Generations; compounds by default |
| Legitimacy claim | "I built this" — verifiable against the market record | "My grandfather built this" — a claim about someone else |

Both systems can exist under identical company law, identical property law, identical tax on income. The switch between them is thrown by one variable: whether large capital stocks pass intact across generations. Where they do, market capitalism drifts into dynastic capitalism as a matter of arithmetic — the inherited share of total wealth rises, and with it the share of capital allocated by the right-hand column ([../01-problem/great-wealth-transfer.md](../01-problem/great-wealth-transfer.md), [../03-case-for/piketty-r-vs-g.md](../03-case-for/piketty-r-vs-g.md)).

## Inheritance is the one market-free transaction in the market system

Consider what every ordinary capitalist transaction requires. A sale requires a price both sides accept. An investment requires a pitch that beats competing pitches. A wage requires work someone values. A profit requires customers who keep choosing you. Each transfer of resources passes a test administered by other people who are free to say no.

Now consider a large bequest. There is **no price** — the heir pays nothing. There is **no competition** — no rival heir could have won the capital by better performance. There is **no contribution test** — the heir need never have created a pound of value, and the transfer proceeds identically whether they have or not. It is, structurally, the allocation mechanism of feudalism operating in the middle of a market economy: resources assigned by blood, exempted from every test the system applies to everyone else's money. The claim is historical, not rhetorical — [feudal-remnant.md](feudal-remnant.md) traces liberalism's demolition of every other hereditary institution and finds this one alone still standing.

This is not a moral flourish; it is a definitional observation. The one large transaction capitalism never subjects to market discipline is the one this proposal taxes. Everything the market *does* discipline, the proposal leaves alone.

## The heir: capitalism's only unaccountable capital allocator

Market capitalism's boast is that no one commands capital without answering for it. The founder answers to customers and investors; the CEO to the board; the fund manager to clients; even the state borrower answers to the bond market. The heir alone commands large capital having passed no test and owing no account — selected by a mechanism (the will) that cannot respond to performance even in principle, since its author is dead.

The predictable result is measurable. Heir-controlled capital underperforms: second-generation-managed family firms show productivity and profitability declines relative to professionally managed peers, and inherited-CEO appointments are associated with significant performance deterioration (effect sizes and studies collected in [../16-second-order/dynasties-and-power.md](../16-second-order/dynasties-and-power.md) and [../01-problem/declining-dynamism.md](../01-problem/declining-dynamism.md); to verify against [Pérez-González 2006](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/aer.96.5.1559) and [Bennedsen et al. 2007](https://academic.oup.com/qje/article-abstract/122/2/647/1942108)). A capitalist who cared only about allocative efficiency — nothing about fairness — would still flag the heir as the system's weakest allocator.

## Rentier drift: the dynastic equilibrium

Dynastic capital does not merely underperform; it *changes what it holds*. A living founder's capital sits in the venture they understand and drive. Heirs, rationally, redeploy toward what requires no founder: land, rental property, index claims on other people's firms, monopoly positions and collectables — assets whose returns are owner-skill-independent and therefore transmit without decay. Across generations, dynastic wealth migrates from creating to collecting, from equity in the new to rent from the old. This is the rentier drift documented in [../01-problem/rentier-economy.md](../01-problem/rentier-economy.md), and it is the *equilibrium* of dynastic capitalism, not an accident: rent is the only income stream that survives the death of talent.

The macro consequence is a rising share of national income flowing to passive ownership and a falling share to work and enterprise — the precise inversion of what the market system is *for* ([../01-problem/asset-price-feedback.md](../01-problem/asset-price-feedback.md)).

## What the proposal removes — and the longer list of what it doesn't

The design ([../02-proposal/mechanism.md](../02-proposal/mechanism.md)) targets the dynastic transaction and nothing adjacent to it:

| Untouched | Removed |
|---|---|
| Every price, wage, and profit | — |
| Every property right of every living person | — |
| The founder's ownership and control of everything they build, for their entire life | — |
| Consumption, philanthropy, reinvestment at any scale | — |
| Generous provision to each child — £500k/$750k per recipient, tax-free ([../02-proposal/thresholds.md](../02-proposal/thresholds.md)) | — |
| — | Transfers *above* that threshold to any single recipient: the dynastic increment only |

The founder keeps and controls everything for life. They may spend it, give it to good causes, plough it back into the firm, or spread it across many recipients Mill-fashion ([../05-history/mill-1848.md](../05-history/mill-1848.md)). What they may not do is the one thing that is not a market act: install an untested successor atop a compounding fortune. The proceeds then fund the *opposite* of dynasty — a universal endowment giving every 25-year-old contestant capital of their own ([../02-proposal/endowment.md](../02-proposal/endowment.md), [../03-case-for/equal-start.md](../03-case-for/equal-start.md)).

## The Buddenbrooks pattern and the founder era

The two columns are visible in the historical record as alternating eras. Periods of high churn — the US in the decades after its founders abolished entail and primogeniture, the post-war decades when 70–80% transfer taxes were live, the first internet generation — put founders atop the wealth distribution: new names, new industries, fortunes traceable to identifiable acts of creation. Periods of low transfer taxation produce the opposite signature: the [*Buddenbrooks*](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/107272/buddenbrooks-by-thomas-mann-translated-by-john-edwards-introduction-by-t-j-reed/) pattern (Mann's chronicle of a merchant dynasty's generational decay), in which the same names persist while the underlying enterprise decays into rent collection. The diagnostic statistic is the inherited share of top wealth — rising again across the OECD after its mid-century trough, with 2023 reportedly the first modern year in which new billionaire wealth came more from inheritance than from founding ([UBS billionaire-ambitions report](https://www.ubs.com/global/en/media/display-page-ndp/en-20231130-great-wealth-transfer.html), to verify; series owned by [../01-problem/wealth-concentration.md](../01-problem/wealth-concentration.md)).

The point for a pro-capitalist reader: the founder era is not the natural state to which markets return if left alone. It was, where it existed, partly the *product* of dynastic-break policy — and it is the era every admirer of capitalism actually admires ([../03-case-for/historical-precedents-of-success.md](../03-case-for/historical-precedents-of-success.md)).

## An objection anticipated: "the founder built it *for* the heir"

The strongest counter to this file concedes every column of the table but relocates the value: the dynasty is not a bug in the founder's incentive but its content — people build empires *in order to* hand them on, so breaking transmission breaks the building ([../04-case-against/bequest-motive.md](../04-case-against/bequest-motive.md) carries the steelman). Three short answers, developed fully elsewhere: the empirical bequest-motive literature finds the effect far weaker than folk intuition assumes, with large fractions of terminal wealth accidental or precautionary ([../03-case-for/optimal-tax-theory.md](../03-case-for/optimal-tax-theory.md)); the design leaves the motive substantially intact — a founder can still make every child a millionaire, fund a name-bearing foundation, and control the enterprise until death ([../02-proposal/thresholds.md](../02-proposal/thresholds.md)); and the motive's residual, dynastic-scale component is precisely the demand for the right-hand column — a preference the system can no more indulge than it indulges the monopolist's preference for no competitors.

## The choice, honestly framed

The status quo is not "capitalism with low taxes." It is a slow, compounding transfer of the economy's commanding heights from the left-hand column to the right — from market capitalism to dynastic capitalism — proceeding at the speed of *r > g* and now accelerating into the largest wealth transfer in history ([../01-problem/great-wealth-transfer.md](../01-problem/great-wealth-transfer.md)). One column is the system this project's authors admire ([what-capitalism-gets-right.md](what-capitalism-gets-right.md)); the other is the system that ends it ([the-self-destruction-thesis.md](the-self-destruction-thesis.md)). The proposal is a choice of columns. Defenders of inheritance must argue for the right-hand one on its merits — and its merits are the table above.
