# The Self-Destruction Thesis

**Executive summary.** Untaxed dynastic inheritance is the mechanism by which capitalism, left alone, dismantles itself — through three channels: economic (capital allocated by birth compounds and underperforms), political (dynastic wealth buys the rules and closes the markets), and legitimacy (a generation that cannot earn what others inherit withdraws its consent, and what replaces market capitalism is worse). The thesis is not anti-capitalist critique; its authors are Schumpeter, the mid-century democracies that taxed estates at 70–80% to head off revolution, and — read honestly — Marx, whose prophecy the tax is designed to refute.

## The claim

Market systems have failure modes, and the deepest is internal: the system's outputs, accumulating and transmitting untaxed, gradually overwrite the system's mechanisms. Nothing external needs to attack capitalism for it to end; it only needs its winners' capital to escape the performance tests described in [what-capitalism-gets-right.md](what-capitalism-gets-right.md) — which is what inheritance does, at scale, automatically ([markets-vs-dynasties.md](markets-vs-dynasties.md)). Three channels carry the damage.

## Channel A — Economic: the patrimonial drift

When the return on capital exceeds the growth rate (*r > g*), existing fortunes compound faster than the earned economy expands, and the inherited share of total wealth rises mechanically — Piketty's central result, with inheritance flows in France and the UK tracking back toward Belle Époque levels ([../03-case-for/piketty-r-vs-g.md](../03-case-for/piketty-r-vs-g.md), [../05-history/piketty.md](../05-history/piketty.md)). Two aggravations make the drift a productivity problem, not merely a distributional one:

- **Capital allocated by birth underperforms.** Heir-managed firms and heir-directed portfolios measurably lag professionally contested capital; the startup rate falls as incumbent, defended capital crowds the field ([../01-problem/declining-dynamism.md](../01-problem/declining-dynamism.md), [../16-second-order/dynasties-and-power.md](../16-second-order/dynasties-and-power.md)).
- **Rentier lock-in.** Dynastic capital migrates toward skill-free, defensible income — land, housing, monopoly rents — pushing asset prices beyond labour income's reach and shifting national income from work and enterprise to passive ownership ([../01-problem/rentier-economy.md](../01-problem/rentier-economy.md), [../01-problem/asset-price-feedback.md](../01-problem/asset-price-feedback.md), [../01-problem/housing.md](../01-problem/housing.md)).

The endpoint of channel A is an economy that still *looks* capitalist — private property, stock exchanges, contracts — but allocates its commanding heights by descent: high concentration, low churn, low growth.

## Channel B — Political: from market capitalism to cronyism

Concentrated dynastic wealth does not sit politically idle; defending an inherited position is cheaper and more reliable than competing, so dynastic capital rationally invests in rule-making — lobbying, political finance, regulatory capture, media ownership ([../01-problem/political-capture.md](../01-problem/political-capture.md)). Each success makes markets less contestable: licensing walls, favourable tax carve-outs, zoning that protects asset values, competition authorities defanged. The system slides from capitalism (profit through serving customers) to cronyism (profit through owning the referee) — and cronyism is what most people who say they hate "capitalism" actually hate. Channel B is self-reinforcing: captured rules protect the fortunes that fund the capture ([../01-problem/README.md](../01-problem/README.md), thread 4). It is also the channel that destroys capitalism's *political* gift — dispersed power — from within.

## Channel C — Legitimacy: the withdrawal of consent

Capitalism is the only economic system that runs on a moral claim ordinary people can check: effort and contribution are rewarded. When the largest fortunes visibly attach to birth rather than contribution — when a young worker calculates that no achievable salary will ever match a classmate's inheritance — the claim fails audit, and the system's *consent* fails with it ([../01-problem/meritocracy-legitimacy.md](../01-problem/meritocracy-legitimacy.md), [../01-problem/social-trust-and-fatalism.md](../01-problem/social-trust-and-fatalism.md)). Surplus credentialed aspirants locked out of asset ownership supply the officer class of every anti-system movement ([../01-problem/elite-overproduction.md](../01-problem/elite-overproduction.md)).

The crucial point for a pro-capitalist reader: **the backlash does not produce a better capitalism.** Historically, delegitimised market orders are succeeded by populism (which keeps the cronyism and adds caprice) or socialism (which abolishes the mechanisms along with the dynasties). Both destroy what [what-capitalism-gets-right.md](what-capitalism-gets-right.md) inventories. Defenders of untaxed inheritance are not defending capitalism against its enemies; they are manufacturing its enemies.

## The prophets inside the tent

The self-destruction thesis is not an import from capitalism's critics. Its strongest statements come from within:

| Prophet | The prediction | Standing |
|---|---|---|
| Joseph Schumpeter, *Capitalism, Socialism and Democracy* (1942) | Capitalism's very success erodes the institutions and social attitudes that sustain it; it would be destroyed not by failure but by the by-products of triumph — including the hardening of its winners into a protective, entrepreneurial-no-longer class | Capitalism's greatest theorist of innovation |
| Karl Marx | Treated honestly: the theorist who *counted on* exactly this dynamic — capital concentrating, immiseration and delegitimation proceeding, until expropriation. His mechanism was real even where his remedy was catastrophic | The adversary whose forecast the tax exists to falsify |
| The mid-century democracies | US top estate rate 77% (1941–76); UK death duties near 80%; enacted by broadly capitalist legislatures, in part explicitly as system-preservation against revolutionary and fascist alternatives (motive and rates to verify — [../05-history/us-estate-tax.md](../05-history/us-estate-tax.md), [../05-history/uk-estate-duty.md](../05-history/uk-estate-duty.md)) | Practitioners, not theorists: they ran the remedy and capitalism thrived |
| Theodore Roosevelt (1906–10) | Hereditary fortunes as a menace to republican government; the inheritance tax as the anti-aristocratic instrument ([../05-history/roosevelt-1906.md](../05-history/roosevelt-1906.md)) | Republican president, trust-buster, no socialist |

Marx deserves the honest paragraph. His economics failed on wages, on the falling rate of profit, on the immiseration of workers under market growth — the Great Enrichment refuted him empirically. But his political sociology of *untreated* capital concentration — wealth begetting power begetting delegitimation begetting rupture — describes channels A–C uncomfortably well. The choice he denied was possible is precisely the one this proposal takes: keep the market engine, break the dynastic transmission. Every generation that inherits little and builds much is a standing refutation of historical materialism. A 100% inheritance tax is, among other things, the most anti-Marxist policy available: it removes the mechanism his prophecy requires.

## How the channels compound

The three channels are not parallel; they cascade. Channel A concentrates and entrenches the capital; channel B converts the concentration into rule-making power that protects it from correction — including from the transfer taxes that would interrupt the cycle, which is exactly what the post-1970s dismantling of estate taxation looks like from this vantage ([../05-history/abolitions.md](../05-history/abolitions.md), [../10-opposition/astroturf-and-lobbying.md](../10-opposition/astroturf-and-lobbying.md)); channel C then converts the visible unfairness into political combustibility. Each channel forecloses the gentler remedies for the others: captured politics (B) blocks the reform that would restore legitimacy (C), and lost legitimacy (C) discredits the market institutions (A) that a reform would need to preserve. This is why the intervention must come at the transmission point rather than downstream, and why waiting is not neutral — the coming transfer wave ([../01-problem/great-wealth-transfer.md](../01-problem/great-wealth-transfer.md)) runs all three channels at historically unprecedented volume.

The timeline matters to the political argument. Channel C moves fastest: legitimacy is a stock of belief, and the polling and social-trust series suggest it is being drawn down within a single generation ([../01-problem/social-trust-and-fatalism.md](../01-problem/social-trust-and-fatalism.md), [../06-palatability/polling.md](../06-palatability/polling.md)). Reformers therefore have less time than the economic series alone imply — but also a live constituency: the case in [../12-political-adoption/wedge-dynamics.md](../12-political-adoption/wedge-dynamics.md) is that the first mainstream party to treat dynastic drift as capitalism's problem, rather than capitalism as the problem, captures the anti-system energy for a pro-system repair.

## The pattern: capitalism saved from itself, repeatedly

The system's actual history is a sequence of pro-market interventions against its own failure modes, each denounced as anti-capitalist at the time and each now uncontroversial infrastructure:

| Intervention | Failure mode it removed | Verdict of history |
|---|---|---|
| Antitrust (Sherman 1890 onward) | Winners ending competition | Constitutive of markets, not opposed to them |
| Central banking as lender of last resort | Self-amplifying panics | Standard equipment of every capitalist economy |
| Deposit insurance (US 1933 onward) | Bank runs destroying sound institutions | Ended the run as a routine event (to verify) |
| Securities regulation and audited accounts | Fraud destroying the trust markets price on | The precondition of deep capital markets |
| Seatbelts-and-safety-rules class of interventions | Markets externalising lethal costs | Bought legitimacy at trivial efficiency cost |

Each entry follows the same grammar: identify the internal dynamic by which the system consumes itself; remove that dynamic surgically; leave every other mechanism running. The recipient-based inheritance tax is the next entry — the same grammar applied to the transmission of fortunes ([../03-case-for/historical-precedents-of-success.md](../03-case-for/historical-precedents-of-success.md), [../03-case-for/meritocratic-legitimacy.md](../03-case-for/meritocratic-legitimacy.md)). The burden this file leaves with the sceptic is symmetrical: to explain why dynastic accumulation — unlike monopoly, panic, and fraud — is the one self-destructive dynamic capitalism should be left to die of.

## What would falsify the thesis

A senior-analyst claim should state its own disconfirmers. The thesis weakens if: (a) inherited shares of top wealth stabilise or fall without policy change — i.e. fortunes dissipate naturally within two generations at the top of the distribution, as the "shirtsleeves to shirtsleeves" folk theory holds (the surname-persistence evidence currently runs hard the other way — see [../01-problem/wealth-concentration.md](../01-problem/wealth-concentration.md) and the debate treatment in [../09-objections/self-correcting.md](../09-objections/self-correcting.md) — but the great-transfer decade will test it); (b) heir-allocated capital is shown to perform no worse than contested capital once selection effects are handled; or (c) legitimacy measures prove insensitive to inherited concentration specifically, as opposed to inequality generally ([../01-problem/social-trust-and-fatalism.md](../01-problem/social-trust-and-fatalism.md)). Each is an empirical question with an owner-file tracking it; none currently looks likely to rescue the null.

## Verification checklist

- Schumpeter's argument in Part II of *Capitalism, Socialism and Democracy* — confirm the "destroyed by its success" framing and the obsolescence-of-the-entrepreneur passages.
- The system-preservation motive for 1930s–40s estate taxation (legislative record, FDR's 1935 tax message "inherited economic power" language — to verify).
- US 77% top estate rate dates (1941–1976) and UK ~80% peak death-duty rates.
- FDIC's effect on bank-run frequency; Sherman Act dating.
