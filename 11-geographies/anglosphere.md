# The Anglosphere Beyond the UK: US, Canada, Australia, New Zealand

**Executive summary.** Outside the UK, the Anglosphere presents a shell (the US federal estate tax, alive but exempting all but a few thousand estates a year), a stealth half-model (Canada's deemed disposition at death), and two outright absences (Australia since 1979, New Zealand since 1992 — to verify) in societies that still tell themselves egalitarian stories. None is a near-term candidate for the full proposal, but the US matters disproportionately: it houses the Carnegie–Roosevelt intellectual lineage, the world's deepest philanthropy culture, and the fortunes whose taxation would legitimise everyone else's.

## United States: the shell and the lineage

**The shell.** The federal estate tax survives at a 40% rate but with an exemption in the $13–15m-per-person range (doubled by TCJA 2017 and extended since — to verify current figure), portable between spouses, with stepped-up basis wiping accrued gains at death. Well under 0.1% of deaths incur it (to verify — [`../05-history/us-estate-tax.md`](../05-history/us-estate-tax.md)). Grantor-trust and valuation-discount engineering (GRATs, IDGTs, FLPs) further hollow what remains ([`../07-implementation/trusts-and-vehicles.md`](../07-implementation/trusts-and-vehicles.md)). The 2001–2010 "death tax" repeal campaign remains the canonical study in framing defeat ([`../06-palatability/framing.md`](../06-palatability/framing.md), [`../10-opposition/attack-playbook.md`](../10-opposition/attack-playbook.md)).

**Constitutional position.** Better than commonly assumed: the estate tax was upheld as an indirect excise not subject to apportionment (*Knowlton v. Moore* 1900 on legacies; *New York Trust Co. v. Eisner* 1921 — to verify), and an accessions-type tax on *receipts* would argue for the same characterisation or for treatment as income under the Sixteenth Amendment. The apportionment cloud that hangs over wealth taxes does not straightforwardly cover transfer taxes — but a maximalist Supreme Court is an unpriced risk ([`../10-opposition/legal-challenges.md`](../10-opposition/legal-challenges.md)).

**Why it still matters.** Polarised institutions (Senate, filibuster, judiciary — C4=1) make near-term adoption implausible; yet the US holds unique assets: the native intellectual lineage (Carnegie's *Gospel of Wealth*, TR's 1906 call — [`../05-history/carnegie-1889.md`](../05-history/carnegie-1889.md), [`../05-history/roosevelt-1906.md`](../05-history/roosevelt-1906.md)); the strongest charitable-giving culture, which a 100% regime with a charity exemption would supercharge ([`../16-second-order/charitable-giving.md`](../16-second-order/charitable-giving.md)); billionaire signatories of the Giving Pledge as ready-made messengers ([`../13-movement/messengers.md`](../13-movement/messengers.md)); and the largest single share of the great wealth transfer ([crisis-windows.md](crisis-windows.md)). "Baby bonds" proposals (Booker; Hamilton–Darity) are the live US stepping-stone for the endowment half ([`../12-political-adoption/stepping-stones.md`](../12-political-adoption/stepping-stones.md)).

## Canada: the underrated half-model

Canada abolished estate taxation in 1972 but replaced it with **deemed disposition at death** — accrued capital gains are taxed as if assets were sold, before heirs take a fresh basis ([`../05-history/abolitions.md`](../05-history/abolitions.md) corrects the "abolition" label). This is an underrated partial model:

- It concedes the principle that **death is a taxable event** — the rhetorical wall other no-tax countries hide behind is already breached.
- It taxes the *gain*, not the *transfer*: the principal passes untouched, principal residences are exempt, and spousal rollovers defer. A dynasty transmitting a diversified portfolio pays once on accrued gains and never on the corpus.
- Upgrade path: deemed disposition plus a recipient-based receipts tax above the lifetime threshold is an architecturally clean combination — Canada could adopt this proposal *without repealing anything*, layering the receipts ledger on top.

Politics: majoritarian-ish parliament, high administrative capacity, extreme housing salience (Toronto/Vancouver), but proximity to a zero-tax US border and a strong "brain drain" reflex make unilateral movement unlikely before US or Commonwealth cover exists ([`../08-capital-flight/exit-options.md`](../08-capital-flight/exit-options.md)).

## Australia: the cautionary tale that occasionally stirs

Australia's 1977–79 cascade (Queensland defects, all states follow, federal duty falls) is this project's canonical federal failure mode ([adoption-criteria.md](adoption-criteria.md) C10; [`../05-history/abolitions.md`](../05-history/abolitions.md)). Since then: the 2010 Henry Review canvassed a bequest tax and was disowned within days (to verify); Labor's mild 2019 franking/negative-gearing package was successfully attacked as a phantom "death tax" it did not contain — evidence of how contaminated the ground is ([`../14-communications/inoculation.md`](../14-communications/inoculation.md)). Yet Australia also has the assets: superannuation gives every citizen an owned capital account (endowment-adjacent infrastructure), housing anger is ferocious, and the tax would be federal this time. Long-window candidate; near-term untouchable.

## New Zealand: clean slate, small sail

Estate duty effectively ended 1992, gift duty 2011 (to verify); there is no CGT either — NZ taxes wealth transfer not at all, an outlier even in the Anglosphere. Favourable: a genuinely majoritarian unicameral parliament (fastest legislative path in the OECD after the UK), egalitarian self-image, severe housing crisis. Unfavourable: no infrastructure, no live debate, a small open economy across a free-movement ditch from zero-tax Australia. NZ adopts, if ever, as a fast follower inside an Anglosphere or OECD frame — though its institutional nimbleness makes it a conceivable *pilot* jurisdiction ([`../15-transition/pilots.md`](../15-transition/pilots.md)).

## Analytic judgement

Rank within the group: **Canada > NZ > Australia > US** for feasibility-of-path, but **US first** for strategic value — an American move would end the tax-competition argument globally ([`../08-capital-flight/game-theory.md`](../08-capital-flight/game-theory.md)), while a Canadian or Kiwi move proves administrability in a settler-society context the Nordics and Ireland cannot. The common Anglosphere lesson is negative space: societies with egalitarian self-images but no living transfer tax show that *absence is self-reinforcing* — each cohort that inherits untaxed becomes a constituency against introduction. This is the strongest argument for urgency anywhere a tax still exists ([uk.md](uk.md)).
