# The Anglosphere Beyond the UK: US, Canada, Australia, New Zealand

**Executive summary.** Outside the UK, the Anglosphere presents a shell (the US federal estate tax, alive but exempting all but a few thousand estates a year), a stealth half-model (Canada's deemed disposition at death), and two outright absences (Australia since 1979, New Zealand since 1992 [6]) in societies that still tell themselves egalitarian stories. None is a near-term candidate for the full proposal, but the US matters disproportionately: it houses the Carnegie–Roosevelt intellectual lineage, the world's deepest philanthropy culture, and the fortunes whose taxation would legitimise everyone else's.

## United States: the shell and the lineage

**The shell.** The federal estate tax survives at a 40% rate but with a $15m-per-person exemption from 2026 ($13.99m in 2025; doubled by TCJA 2017, then set permanently at $15m — indexed from 2027 — by the 2025 One Big Beautiful Bill Act) [1][2], portable between spouses, with stepped-up basis wiping accrued gains at death. Well under 0.1% of deaths incur it — 0.08% in 2019, before the exemption rose further [3] ([`../05-history/us-estate-tax.md`](../05-history/us-estate-tax.md)). Grantor-trust and valuation-discount engineering (GRATs, IDGTs, FLPs) further hollow what remains ([`../07-implementation/trusts-and-vehicles.md`](../07-implementation/trusts-and-vehicles.md)). The 2001–2010 "death tax" repeal campaign remains the canonical study in framing defeat ([`../06-palatability/framing.md`](../06-palatability/framing.md), [`../10-opposition/attack-playbook.md`](../10-opposition/attack-playbook.md)).

**Constitutional position.** Better than commonly assumed: the estate tax was upheld as an indirect excise not subject to apportionment (*Knowlton v. Moore* 1900 on legacies; *New York Trust Co. v. Eisner*, 256 U.S. 345, 1921 [4]), and an accessions-type tax on *receipts* would argue for the same characterisation or for treatment as income under the Sixteenth Amendment. The apportionment cloud that hangs over wealth taxes does not straightforwardly cover transfer taxes — but a maximalist Supreme Court is an unpriced risk ([`../10-opposition/legal-challenges.md`](../10-opposition/legal-challenges.md)).

**Why it still matters.** Polarised institutions (Senate, filibuster, judiciary — C4=1) make near-term adoption implausible; yet the US holds unique assets: the native intellectual lineage (Carnegie's *Gospel of Wealth*, TR's 1906 call — [`../05-history/carnegie-1889.md`](../05-history/carnegie-1889.md), [`../05-history/roosevelt-1906.md`](../05-history/roosevelt-1906.md)); the strongest charitable-giving culture, which a 100% regime with a charity exemption would supercharge ([`../16-second-order/charitable-giving.md`](../16-second-order/charitable-giving.md)); billionaire signatories of the Giving Pledge as ready-made messengers ([`../13-movement/messengers.md`](../13-movement/messengers.md)); and the largest single share of the great wealth transfer ([crisis-windows.md](crisis-windows.md)). "Baby bonds" proposals (Booker; Hamilton–Darity) are the live US stepping-stone for the endowment half ([`../12-political-adoption/stepping-stones.md`](../12-political-adoption/stepping-stones.md)).

### Will the United States ever do this?

The question deserves a direct answer, because the scattered pieces of it — capture-curve analysis, legislative mechanics, target politicians, estate-tax history — all live in different files. Consolidated:

**1. The honest base case: follower market, not first mover.** Veto-point saturation (C4 = 1 above) is the scorecard's expression of what the procedural analysis spells out — filibuster on regular order, Byrd-rule surgery and structural sunset risk on reconciliation, a property-rights-receptive judiciary, and fifty-state trust-situs leakage ([`../15-transition/legislative-path-us.md`](../15-transition/legislative-path-us.md)). Worse, the blocking power is not static: every measured wealth-to-power channel — campaign money, platforms, courts, think-tank infrastructure, direct politician-billionaire fusion — shows a rising US trajectory, and the closing-window verdict is that the US is already well along the capture curve and *may need a crisis-scale opening* to legislate confiscatory transfer taxation at all ([`../13-movement/closing-window.md`](../13-movement/closing-window.md) §1, §5). That is the base case, stated without cosmetics: the US adopts late, after the model is proven elsewhere.

**2. Why "crisis-scale window" does not mean "never."** The US precedent for legislating against entrenched wealth is not weak — it is uniquely strong. Within twenty-five years the country ratified the 16th Amendment (February 1913, dislodging a "Millionaires' Club" Senate that had blocked the income tax for two decades and required constitutional surgery to correct), enacted the permanent federal estate tax (Revenue Act of 1916), and drove the top rate to 70% in the Revenue Act of 1935 — 77% by 1941, where it stood essentially untouched for thirty-five years under three Republican and four Democratic presidencies ([`../05-history/us-estate-tax.md`](../05-history/us-estate-tax.md)). Each of those windows looked impossible the decade before it opened; the historical calibration in [`../13-movement/closing-window.md`](../13-movement/closing-window.md) §3 finds US reform windows opening and delivering within 2–15 years of sustained agitation. The country that ran a 77% top estate rate for a generation is not constitutionally allergic to this tax; it is cyclically available to it.

**3. The assets, present tense.** No other improbable adopter holds anything like this inventory:

- **The pairing already drafted as law.** The Booker–Pressley American Opportunity Accounts Act (S.441, 118th) is a baby-bonds endowment "fully paid for" by restoring the estate tax to 2009 levels — the tax→endowment architecture of this proposal, in miniature, in bill text ([`../12-political-adoption/target-politicians.md`](../12-political-adoption/target-politicians.md)).
- **A ready vehicle.** Batchelder's inheritance-as-income design gives the US a drafted, Congress-familiar accessions-tax route that is arguably *more* constitutionally robust than the estate tax it would replace ([`../15-transition/legislative-path-us.md`](../15-transition/legislative-path-us.md)).
- **Settled constitutional law.** Transfer taxes have been upheld as excises since *Knowlton* (1900) and *Eisner* (1921) [4], with the Sixteenth Amendment as a second line of defence for a receipts basis — subject to the unpriced maximalist-Court risk that *Moore* (2024) left open [11] ([`../10-opposition/legal-challenges.md`](../10-opposition/legal-challenges.md)).
- **The native lineage, with living heirs.** Carnegie and TR founded the argument ([`../05-history/carnegie-1889.md`](../05-history/carnegie-1889.md), [`../05-history/roosevelt-1906.md`](../05-history/roosevelt-1906.md)); Gates Sr.'s 2001 Responsible Wealth "Call to Preserve the Estate Tax" gathered 1,000+ wealthy signatories with Buffett making the public case — the proof-of-concept for argument-against-interest campaigning ([`../13-movement/heir-defectors.md`](../13-movement/heir-defectors.md)).
- **Delivery proof.** Connecticut's baby-bonds programme has been running since July 2023, ~33,000 children enrolled [8] — the endowment half is not theoretical in the US; it is operating.
- **Movement infrastructure at industrial scale.** ActBlue processed $1.55bn from ~7 million unique small-dollar donors in Q3 2024 alone ([`../13-movement/paid-acquisition.md`](../13-movement/paid-acquisition.md)) — no other candidate country has a comparable funding rail for a citizen campaign.

**4. The strategy consequence.** Because the base case is follower-market and the assets are real, the US play is preparation, not persuasion-to-lead: the path runs through the UK/Netherlands demonstration decade and the international floor ([`../15-transition/sequencing.md`](../15-transition/sequencing.md), Phase 4), while interim US work banks what current politics allows — defending and restoring estate-tax parameters, expanding state baby bonds, mainstreaming the Batchelder inheritance-as-income discourse, and recruiting from what is plausibly the world's largest heir-defector ecosystem (judgement — Responsible Wealth, Patriotic Millionaires, Resource Generation, and the Giving Pledge are all US-anchored; [`../13-movement/heir-defectors.md`](../13-movement/heir-defectors.md), [`../12-political-adoption/stepping-stones.md`](../12-political-adoption/stepping-stones.md)). The direction-of-travel headwind, stated honestly: the 2025 OBBBA made the $15m-per-person exemption permanent [2], and 41 Republican senators moved for outright repeal in 2023 ([`../12-political-adoption/target-politicians.md`](../12-political-adoption/target-politicians.md)) — the near-term momentum runs *against* the tax, which is exactly why the interim rungs are defensive.

**5. Verdict.** Unlikely to lead, likely to follow, decisive when it does — a US adoption ends global tax competition ([`../08-capital-flight/game-theory.md`](../08-capital-flight/game-theory.md), [README.md](README.md)).

## Canada: the underrated half-model

Canada abolished estate taxation in 1972 but replaced it with **deemed disposition at death** — accrued capital gains are taxed as if assets were sold, before heirs take a fresh basis ([`../05-history/abolitions.md`](../05-history/abolitions.md) corrects the "abolition" label). This is an underrated partial model:

- It concedes the principle that **death is a taxable event** — the rhetorical wall other no-tax countries hide behind is already breached.
- It taxes the *gain*, not the *transfer*: the principal passes untouched, principal residences are exempt, and spousal rollovers defer. A dynasty transmitting a diversified portfolio pays once on accrued gains and never on the corpus.
- Upgrade path: deemed disposition plus a recipient-based receipts tax above the lifetime threshold is an architecturally clean combination — Canada could adopt this proposal *without repealing anything*, layering the receipts ledger on top.

Politics: majoritarian-ish parliament, high administrative capacity, extreme housing salience (Toronto/Vancouver), but proximity to a zero-tax US border and a strong "brain drain" reflex make unilateral movement unlikely before US or Commonwealth cover exists ([`../08-capital-flight/exit-options.md`](../08-capital-flight/exit-options.md)).

## Australia: the cautionary tale that occasionally stirs

Australia's 1977–79 cascade (Queensland defects, all states follow, federal duty falls) is this project's canonical federal failure mode ([adoption-criteria.md](adoption-criteria.md) C10; [`../05-history/abolitions.md`](../05-history/abolitions.md)). Since then: the 2010 Henry Review canvassed a bequest tax as a matter for public deliberation, and the Rudd government ruled one out "at any stage" in the response it published alongside the review on 2 May 2010 [5]; Labor's mild 2019 franking/negative-gearing package was successfully attacked as a phantom "death tax" it did not contain — evidence of how contaminated the ground is ([`../14-communications/inoculation.md`](../14-communications/inoculation.md)). Yet Australia also has the assets: superannuation gives every citizen an owned capital account (endowment-adjacent infrastructure), housing anger is ferocious, and the tax would be federal this time. Long-window candidate; near-term untouchable.

## New Zealand: clean slate, small sail

Estate duty was abolished for deaths on or after 17 December 1992, gift duty for dispositions from 1 October 2011 [6][7]; there is no general CGT either — NZ taxes wealth transfer not at all, an outlier even in the Anglosphere. Favourable: a genuinely majoritarian unicameral parliament (fastest legislative path in the OECD after the UK), egalitarian self-image, severe housing crisis. Unfavourable: no infrastructure, no live debate, a small open economy across a free-movement ditch from zero-tax Australia. NZ adopts, if ever, as a fast follower inside an Anglosphere or OECD frame — though its institutional nimbleness makes it a conceivable *pilot* jurisdiction ([`../15-transition/pilots.md`](../15-transition/pilots.md)).

## Shared Anglosphere dynamics

Four cross-cutting features make the Anglosphere a single strategic theatre rather than four separate campaigns:

- **A common media and framing market.** The "death tax" coinage crossed from US repeal politics into Australian and British usage within years; attack lines, poll findings, and counter-frames all travel ([`../14-communications/naming-and-language.md`](../14-communications/naming-and-language.md)). A framing victory in one country is immediately available in the others — in both directions.
- **The common-law trust.** The most sophisticated avoidance technology in the world is an Anglosphere export, and every Anglosphere regime would face the same discretionary-trust, offshore-trust, and dynasty-trust problems ([`../07-implementation/trusts-and-vehicles.md`](../07-implementation/trusts-and-vehicles.md)). US perpetual dynasty trusts in South Dakota and Nevada are the frontier case.
- **Homeownership as identity.** All four societies built mass-middle-class politics on the owned home, which makes threshold design ([`../02-proposal/thresholds.md`](../02-proposal/thresholds.md)) more electorally sensitive than in renter-normal continental Europe — and makes the young renter cohort's exclusion correspondingly more explosive.
- **Free movement between them.** CANZUK mobility and the US draw mean each country's wealthy have culturally frictionless exits into the others; unilateral adoption anywhere in the group sharpens the case for group-level coordination ([`../08-capital-flight/exit-options.md`](../08-capital-flight/exit-options.md)).

## Stepping stones by country

| Country | Nearest live stepping stone | Next plausible step |
|---|---|---|
| US | Baby-bonds bills (Booker's American Opportunity Accounts Act; Connecticut's live state programme [8]); estate-tax exemption fights | Exemption reduction + grantor-trust and step-up repair; endowment via baby-bonds expansion |
| Canada | Deemed disposition already in place; recurring capital-gains inclusion-rate fights | Receipts register layered over deemed disposition; principal-residence gain inclusion above a cap |
| Australia | Superannuation death-benefit taxation debates; Productivity Commission wealth-transfer research (2021) [9] | CGT on inherited assets (ending the step-up analogue); then a federal receipts tax |
| NZ | Recurrent CGT/wealth-tax commission proposals (Tax Working Group 2019; its CGT recommendation was rejected in April 2019 [10]) | Any capital taxation at all; NZ must build the *category* before the instrument |

## What to watch

- **US:** the estate-tax exemption's statutory trajectory at each budget-reconciliation cycle (the 2025 OBBBA fixed it at $15m from 2026, permanent and indexed [2]); progress of baby-bonds legislation federally and in states (Connecticut's programme, running since July 2023, as the live pilot [8]); any Supreme Court taxation ruling touching the excise/direct-tax boundary (*Moore v. United States*, 2024, upheld the mandatory repatriation tax narrowly and left the wealth-tax question open [11]).
- **Canada:** capital-gains inclusion-rate politics as the proxy war for taxing wealth transfer; provincial housing politics pushing federal parties toward generational offers.
- **Australia:** whether the Productivity Commission's 2021 wealth-transfer research [9] resurfaces in any government review; superannuation death-benefit tax changes as the thin end.
- **NZ:** any re-run of the Tax Working Group's capital-taxation recommendations under a future Labour-led government.
- **Group-level:** CANZUK-adjacent mobility agreements, which raise the value of coordinated adoption before individual moves.
- **Media:** whether any major Anglosphere outlet breaks from the "death tax" frame during great-wealth-transfer coverage — the leading indicator that the framing market is contestable ([`../14-communications/narratives.md`](../14-communications/narratives.md)).
- **Trusts:** state or federal movement against perpetual dynasty trusts, the quiet enabler of Anglosphere wealth persistence ([`../16-second-order/dynasties-and-power.md`](../16-second-order/dynasties-and-power.md)).

## Analytic judgement

Rank within the group: **Canada > NZ > Australia > US** for feasibility-of-path, but **US first** for strategic value — an American move would end the tax-competition argument globally ([`../08-capital-flight/game-theory.md`](../08-capital-flight/game-theory.md)), while a Canadian or Kiwi move proves administrability in a settler-society context the Nordics and Ireland cannot. The common Anglosphere lesson is negative space: societies with egalitarian self-images but no living transfer tax show that *absence is self-reinforcing* — each cohort that inherits untaxed becomes a constituency against introduction. This is the strongest argument for urgency anywhere a tax still exists ([uk.md](uk.md)).

A UK move would put the question to all four within a decade: shared language, shared media, and shared policy networks make the Anglosphere the fastest contagion path once any member breaks the seal.
