# Adoption Criteria: What Makes a Society Likely to Enact This

**Executive summary.** Whether a country adopts a 100%-above-threshold inheritance tax with a universal endowment is predicted far better by institutional structure — tax-administration capacity, existing transfer-tax infrastructure, majoritarian legislative power, constitutional openness, and national-level tax assignment — than by egalitarian values, which the abolition record shows are weakly and even perversely correlated with retention (see [`../05-history/abolitions.md`](../05-history/abolitions.md)). This file defines ten criteria and a scorecard method applied consistently across every country file in this section.

## The ten criteria

### C1. State and tax-administration capacity

The proposal demands a Central Register of Receipts, lifetime gift aggregation, professional valuation at scale, and trust look-through ([`../02-proposal/mechanism.md`](../02-proposal/mechanism.md), [`../07-implementation/administration.md`](../07-implementation/administration.md)). Countries with pre-filled income-tax returns, population registers, and land registries with near-universal coverage (Nordics, Korea, Japan, Netherlands, UK) can run this; countries with fragmented or low-trust administration cannot, whatever their politics.

### C2. Social trust and tax morale

High-trust societies tolerate high visible taxation because citizens believe others also pay and the state delivers. Low tax morale converts any inheritance tax into a compliance war. Caveat: Sweden and Norway rank near the top of every trust index and still abolished — trust is necessary for *operation*, not sufficient for *adoption*.

### C3. Existing transfer-tax infrastructure

It is far easier to raise the rate and restructure the base of a functioning inheritance tax than to introduce one from zero. A live tax means valuation practice, case law, professional familiarity, and a revenue baseline already exist. Ireland's Capital Acquisitions Tax is the strongest asset here (recipient-based, lifetime-cumulative — see [ireland.md](ireland.md)); Australia, New Zealand, Canada, Sweden, and Norway would all face the much harder politics of *reintroduction*.

### C4. Majoritarian vs consensus institutions

A unitary parliamentary system with single-party majorities (UK, New Zealand) can legislate radical fiscal change in one budget. Consensus systems — coalition government, strong bicameralism, referendums, constitutional courts — give concentrated wealth multiple veto points. Switzerland is the limiting case: the 2015 federal inheritance-tax initiative died by referendum ([unlikely-and-havens.md](unlikely-and-havens.md)). Veto-point counting (Tsebelis-style) is the operative logic here.

### C5. Egalitarian norms

Public belief that large fortunes are unearned and that life chances should not depend on birth. Matters for coalition-building ([`../13-movement/coalitions.md`](../13-movement/coalitions.md)) — but see the paradox below.

### C6. Housing-crisis salience among the young

Where a generation is locked out of ownership and inheritance visibly determines who buys, the endowment half of the proposal ([`../02-proposal/endowment.md`](../02-proposal/endowment.md)) acquires a ready-made constituency. The UK, Ireland, Korea, the Netherlands, and Australasian cities score highest; see [`../01-problem/housing.md`](../01-problem/housing.md).

### C7. Exposure to the great wealth transfer

Countries where post-war cohorts hold historically large housing and pension wealth now passing to heirs — making inheritance politically visible on a scale not seen since 1914 (magnitudes in [crisis-windows.md](crisis-windows.md), to verify). High exposure cuts both ways: more revenue and salience, but also more voters expecting a bequest ([`../12-political-adoption/electoral-arithmetic.md`](../12-political-adoption/electoral-arithmetic.md)).

### C8. Crisis windows

Major redistributive fiscal change historically clusters around shocks — war finance, depression, post-crisis consolidation ([crisis-windows.md](crisis-windows.md), [`../05-history/`](../05-history/README.md)). Score reflects proximity of plausible windows: fiscal crunch from ageing, post-crisis consolidation needs, electoral tipping points.

### C9. Constitutional property protections

Some constitutions protect not merely property but *inheritance itself*. Germany's Art. 14(1) Grundgesetz guarantees "property and the right of inheritance" (*das Eigentum und das Erbrecht werden gewährleistet*), and the Bundesverfassungsgericht has repeatedly policed inheritance-tax design on this basis (to verify scope — see [continental-europe.md](continental-europe.md)). France's Conseil constitutionnel has struck down levies as "confiscatory." The UK, with no codified constitution, has no equivalent blocker. This criterion is a **gate**: a hard constitutional bar cannot be legislated around, only amended around.

### C10. Federal vs unitary tax assignment

Bequest taxation assigned to competing sub-national units self-destructs: Australia's 1977–79 cascade, begun by Queensland and complete within three years, is the canonical failure mode ([`../05-history/abolitions.md`](../05-history/abolitions.md)); Spain's regional bonifications are the live re-run ([continental-europe.md](continental-europe.md)). The tax must sit at national level, ideally under a multilateral floor ([`../08-capital-flight/international-coordination.md`](../08-capital-flight/international-coordination.md)).

## Scorecard method

Each country file scores its subject 0–5 on each criterion:

| Score | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 5 | Strongly favourable; no meaningful obstacle on this dimension |
| 4 | Favourable with minor friction |
| 3 | Mixed; obstacle real but navigable |
| 2 | Unfavourable; would require significant political or legal work |
| 1 | Severely unfavourable; near-blocking |
| 0 | Blocking as things stand |

Rules of application:

- **Additive with two gates.** Total score (max 50) drives the ranking in [README.md](README.md), *except* that a 0–1 on C9 (constitution) or C10 (federal assignment, where no national fix is available) caps the country's rating at "unlikely" regardless of total. Germany illustrates the C9 gate; pre-federal-fix US state action illustrates C10.
- **Score the variant, not the status quo.** The question is adoptability of *this* design (recipient-based, thresholded, endowment-paired), not sympathy for existing estate taxes. Korea's live debate about moving to a recipient basis (to verify) raises its C3 score even while its government debates rate *cuts*.
- **Direction of travel noted qualitatively.** A country cutting its inheritance tax scores the same on infrastructure (the machinery exists) but the country file must flag momentum.
- **Ordinal, not cardinal.** Scores are structured analyst judgement for comparison across this section, not measurements. Every load-bearing factual claim behind a score is flagged (to verify) in the country file.

## The paradox to hold in mind

The societies that best fit the *values* criteria — Sweden, Norway, Austria, Canada, Australia, New Zealand — are precisely the ones that abolished or never rebuilt their transfer taxes, while the highest surviving rates sit in Korea, Japan, and France, none of them egalitarian by Nordic standards. The abolition record ([`../05-history/abolitions.md`](../05-history/abolitions.md)) shows why: badly designed taxes died of design failure wherever they stood, and small open economies felt the exit threat most acutely ([`../08-capital-flight/evidence.md`](../08-capital-flight/evidence.md)). The practical inference for this whole section: **weight C1, C3, C4, C9, and C10 above C2 and C5.** Institutions and infrastructure predict adoption; values predict, at most, durability once adopted.
