# Korea and Japan: The High-Rate Incumbents

**Executive summary.** South Korea and Japan operate the highest inheritance-tax rates in the OECD (top statutory rates around 50–55%, to verify) with accepted state capacity and, in Korea's case, the world's most aggressive enforcement against controlling-shareholder dynasties — making them the countries that have travelled furthest toward this proposal's rate, though not its structure. Both prove that heavy inheritance taxation can coexist with advanced capitalism and strong family norms; neither is currently trending toward a 100% rate, and Korea's live reform debate is about *cutting*.

## Korea: dynasty politics as the engine

### The Samsung saga as the world's largest inheritance-tax event

The death of Lee Kun-hee (2020) produced an inheritance-tax assessment on the family reported around ₩12 trillion — roughly $10bn, described as the largest such bill ever levied anywhere (to verify) — paid in instalments partly through share sales and art donations. Add the earlier merger-and-succession manoeuvres that put Lee Jae-yong in court, and Korea offers the clearest real-world demonstration that (a) very large fortunes *can* be made to pay at scale, and (b) the attempt to avoid succession tax is itself what corrupts — the governance abuses prosecuted in the chaebol cases were substantially succession-tax avoidance schemes ([`../01-problem/political-capture.md`](../01-problem/political-capture.md), [`../16-second-order/dynasties-and-power.md`](../16-second-order/dynasties-and-power.md)).

### Structure and sentiment

- Top statutory rate 50%, with a surcharge on controlling ("largest shareholder") stakes pushing effective top rates toward ~60% (to verify) — the only regime that explicitly taxes *control premia*, an anti-dynastic instrument this proposal should study.
- Public sentiment is strongly anti-dynastic: the "gold spoon / dirt spoon" (*geumsujeo/heuksujeo*) class discourse maps exactly onto [`../01-problem/meritocracy-legitimacy.md`](../01-problem/meritocracy-legitimacy.md).
- **Counter-current:** the mid-2020s policy debate ran toward *reduction* — proposals to cut the top rate to ~40% and to convert from an estate basis to a recipient-acquisition basis (to verify). The conversion half is convergence with this proposal's structure; the rate half is retreat. Business lobbies argue the current burden forces firm sales to private equity or triggers relocation ([`../04-case-against/family-business.md`](../04-case-against/family-business.md)).

### Confucian family norms cut both ways

Obligation to transmit the family enterprise intact fuels resistance; but the same ethic supplies the *anti*-dynastic argument's force — unearned third-generation control violates the meritocratic-examination tradition, and chaebol heirs' scandals (the "nut rage" genre) are read as moral failure of inherited position. Korean public opinion simultaneously supports taxing chaebol succession heavily and worries about "excessive" burdens on ordinary firms — the same split the threshold design in [`../02-proposal/thresholds.md`](../02-proposal/thresholds.md) is built to exploit.

## Japan: capacity plus demography

- Top rate 55% above ¥600m per statutory heir; the 2015 reform *broadened* the base by cutting the basic deduction, raising the share of deaths incurring tax from roughly 4% to 8–9% (to verify) — a rare rich-democracy example of inheritance-tax *expansion* passing without major backlash.
- The Japanese computation (aggregate estate notionally divided among statutory heirs, tax computed per share) is already half-way to a recipient basis.
- The dankai generation's savings — a household financial-asset stock around ¥2,000tn (to verify) — is transferring now; the state actively runs early-gifting incentive schemes to accelerate it, showing transfer timing is already a live policy lever ([`../07-implementation/gifts-and-timing.md`](../07-implementation/gifts-and-timing.md)).
- Obstacles: consensus politics without a radical-reform tradition in peacetime; weak housing grievance; an endowment would compete with acute pension/care spending claims; and low-salience politics — inheritance tax is accepted, not contested, and nobody is campaigning to raise it.

## Analytic judgement

Korea is the more probable adopter of something *recognisably* like this proposal — its anti-dynastic politics could, under a reformist government and a chaebol scandal window ([crisis-windows.md](crisis-windows.md)), push a recipient-based restructure upward rather than downward, and its threshold politics would follow the same "protect ordinary families, cap dynasties" line as [`../02-proposal/`](../02-proposal/README.md). Japan is the more probable *quiet convergence* case: incremental base-broadening and rate creep under fiscal pressure, without manifesto politics. Neither will pair the tax with a universal endowment unaided — that idea has no local lineage, and exporting it is a communications task ([`../14-communications/audience-messaging.md`](../14-communications/audience-messaging.md)).
