# The Unlikely and the Havens

**Executive summary.** Switzerland, Singapore, the Gulf states, Monaco, and the smaller haven jurisdictions will not adopt this proposal in any foreseeable window — by referendum verdict, by business model, or by constitutional identity — and the campaign should say so plainly rather than pretend universality. Their significance is systematically overrated: havens matter only to the degree that big economies leave the doors open, and the proposal's dual-nexus, exit-tail, and coordination architecture ([`../02-proposal/avoidance.md`](../02-proposal/avoidance.md), [`../08-capital-flight/international-coordination.md`](../08-capital-flight/international-coordination.md)) is designed precisely so that a handful of refusers cannot veto everyone else.

## Switzerland: the referendum verdict

Switzerland is the definitive consensus-institution case. Inheritance taxation is cantonal; most cantons exempt direct descendants entirely, and cantons compete on the margin (to verify current cantonal map). The decisive datum: the 2015 federal popular initiative "Millionen-Erbschaften besteuern" — a 20% federal estate tax above CHF 2m, earmarked for the pension system — was rejected by roughly 71% of voters and every canton (to verify). A 2025 JUSO initiative for a 50% tax on very large estates fared comparably badly (to verify). Two readings matter:

1. **The institutional reading.** Even a moderate rate with a high threshold and popular earmarking cannot pass a wealth-holding, referendum-based polity where cantonal treasuries and the private-banking sector campaign against it. This is C4 = 0: the veto is the median voter herself, primed by an asset-management ecosystem.
2. **The campaign reading.** Opponents will cite 2015 as "the people rejected this." The honest answer: Switzerland is the *most* wealth-defensive institutional environment on earth, its vote generalises to nowhere with majoritarian institutions, and even there the initiative put the question on the national agenda ([`../14-communications/debate-toolkit.md`](../14-communications/debate-toolkit.md)).

Switzerland's other role is as destination: the Kamprad precedent ([nordics.md](nordics.md)), lump-sum taxation (*Pauschalbesteuerung*) for wealthy foreigners, and forfait deals make it the default European exit option — the pivotal chair in [`../08-capital-flight/exit-options.md`](../08-capital-flight/exit-options.md).

## Singapore, the Gulf, Monaco: refusal as business model

- **Singapore** abolished estate duty in 2008, explicitly to attract wealth-management flows (to verify); its family-office regime (13O/13U incentives) actively recruits the fortunes other countries might tax. State capacity is world-class; the *choice* is the point. Domestic inequality politics (HDB-vs-landed divide) occasionally surfaces wealth-tax talk from establishment economists (to verify), but transfer taxation would contradict the national development model.
- **Gulf states (UAE foremost)** run zero-personal-tax models with golden-visa recruitment of the globally wealthy; Sharia forced-heirship rules govern succession for Muslims, an entirely different legal frame. Adoption probability is effectively zero and no campaign resource should be spent pretending otherwise.
- **Monaco, Liechtenstein, Channel Islands, Caribbean territories** are constitutionally *constituted* as havens; several are dependencies of larger states (UK, France), which is the actual pressure point — haven policy is metropolitan policy at one remove ([uk.md](uk.md), [`../08-capital-flight/international-coordination.md`](../08-capital-flight/international-coordination.md)).

## Why their non-adoption matters less than assumed

The instinctive objection — "the money will just go to Monaco" — misreads where enforcement power lives. Four structural points, argued fully in [`../08-capital-flight/`](../08-capital-flight/README.md):

1. **The tax follows persons and situs, not flags.** Under the dual-nexus rule ([`../02-proposal/avoidance.md`](../02-proposal/avoidance.md)), transfers to a resident of an adopting country are taxable wherever the assets sit, and domestic real property is taxable whoever inherits it. A haven helps only if the *recipient* genuinely relocates their life — and the 10-year exit tail prices that.
2. **Havens hold accounts, not lives.** The great wealth transfer's assets are overwhelmingly housing, pensions, and operating businesses physically inside the big economies ([crisis-windows.md](crisis-windows.md)); these cannot be moved to Singapore, only their owners can, and observed migration elasticities of the wealthy are modest ([`../08-capital-flight/evidence.md`](../08-capital-flight/evidence.md), to verify).
3. **The CRS/FATCA precedent.** Automatic exchange of financial-account information was imposed *on* the havens by G20 market power within a decade of being dismissed as utopian; a transfer-tax floor rides the same rails ([`../08-capital-flight/international-coordination.md`](../08-capital-flight/international-coordination.md)).
4. **Defection has a natural ceiling.** A haven absorbing exiled dynasties imports their political capture problem ([`../16-second-order/dynasties-and-power.md`](../16-second-order/dynasties-and-power.md)); jurisdictions selling stability have historically capped how much fugitive wealth they welcome when reputational cost rises (to verify against post-2022 sanctions-era behaviour).

The honest residual: a world where the US refuses ([anglosphere.md](anglosphere.md)) is a world where the haven question stays live, because the largest fortunes have a large domestic-market refuge. Haven-proofing is downstream of big-economy coordination, not of haven conversion.

## Analytic judgement

Write these jurisdictions off as adopters and price them in as adversaries: expect Swiss private banking, Singaporean family-office promoters, and dependency governments to fund the international opposition case ([`../10-opposition/astroturf-and-lobbying.md`](../10-opposition/astroturf-and-lobbying.md)). The campaign posture is containment — dual nexus, exit tails, situs rules, metropolitan pressure on dependencies, and a G20-level floor — rather than persuasion. One reframing worth deploying: every generation of tax history has had its refusers, and the refusers did not stop the income tax, VAT, or CRS from becoming the world's norm; they merely arrived last.
