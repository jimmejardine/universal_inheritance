# Continental Europe: High Rates, Hard Constraints

**Executive summary.** Continental Europe contains both the highest nominal inheritance-tax rates outside East Asia (France's 45–60%, to verify) and the hardest legal blockers anywhere (Germany's constitutional inheritance guarantee), plus the clearest running natural experiments in sub-national tax competition (Spain's regional race to zero, Belgium's regional divergence). The region's collective importance is less as a set of individual first movers than as the place where an EU-level floor would have to be forged — the coordination prize discussed in [`../08-capital-flight/international-coordination.md`](../08-capital-flight/international-coordination.md).

## France: the intellectual home with a legal ceiling

France is where the modern intellectual case lives — Piketty's *Capital and Ideology* proposal (progressive inheritance tax funding a ~€120,000 grant at 25) is essentially this project's design with a progressive schedule ([`../05-history/piketty.md`](../05-history/piketty.md)), and the 2021 Blanchard–Tirole commission recommended moving to a lifetime recipient basis (to verify). The existing tax is already heavy: 45% top rate in the direct line above ~€1.8m, 60% between non-relatives, with a 15-year gift-recall rule that approximates lifetime cumulation (all to verify).

Three constraints:

1. **The Conseil constitutionnel's "confiscatory" doctrine.** The 2012 censure of the 75% income-tax tranche established that levies can be unconstitutional as confiscatory (to verify decision no. 2012-662 DC). A 100% marginal rate invites the same challenge; the defence — that a *threshold-protected receipts* tax is not confiscatory of anything the recipient earned — needs French constitutional drafting work ([`../10-opposition/legal-challenges.md`](../10-opposition/legal-challenges.md)).
2. **Inheritance tax is spectacularly unpopular in France** — polling regularly shows large majorities favouring cuts even among left voters (to verify) — despite, or because of, its high rates and low thresholds. France is the cautionary case for [`../06-palatability/polling.md`](../06-palatability/polling.md): high rates without a visible benefit or generous threshold generate resentment, not egalitarian consolidation.
3. **The réserve héréditaire** (forced heirship) embeds inheritance as a family *right* in the Civil Code — a cultural-legal grain the proposal cuts against.

## Germany: the Article 14 gate and the Mittelstand hollowing

Art. 14(1) GG guarantees "property and the right of inheritance." The BVerfG has read this as protecting the *institution* of inheritance while permitting substantial taxation — but the boundary is unmapped at anything like a 100% marginal rate, and most commentary treats confiscatory-level transfer taxation as requiring constitutional amendment (to verify against the 1995 and 2006 valuation rulings and 2014 §13a/b decision). This is the C9 gate case in [adoption-criteria.md](adoption-criteria.md).

The live German story is the mirror image of abolition: the tax survived but was hollowed. Business-succession exemptions (§§13a–13b ErbStG) let very large enterprise transfers pass at effective rates near zero if jobs are notionally preserved; the BVerfG struck the regime down as unequal in December 2014, and the 2016 "fix" preserved most of the relief. Result (to verify against official statistics): the largest German inheritances face *lower* effective rates than mid-sized ones — the inverse-incidence pathology of [`../05-history/abolitions.md`](../05-history/abolitions.md) operating inside a surviving tax. Germany matters to the global case as proof that Mittelstand-style reliefs, once granted, are politically irrevocable and fiscally bottomless — the reason [`../02-proposal/carveouts.md`](../02-proposal/carveouts.md) offers deferral, never exemption.

## Netherlands, Belgium, Spain: the natural experiments

- **Netherlands.** Recipient-based *erfbelasting* (10–40% by kinship and amount, to verify), excellent administration, no constitutional review of statutes (Art. 120 Grondwet), acute housing crisis among the young, and a live domestic debate over the business-succession relief (BOR) mirroring Germany's. On pure structure the Netherlands scores as continental Europe's most adoptable state; coalition politics is the brake. An underrated candidate — flagged accordingly in [README.md](README.md).
- **Belgium.** Inheritance tax is regionalised (Flanders, Wallonia, Brussels set rates); rates are high by OECD standards but planning around them (gift regimes, the erstwhile "kaasroute" via Dutch notaries, closed 2020 — to verify) is a national sport. Demonstrates C10 friction inside even a small federation.
- **Spain.** The clearest running rerun of Australia 1977–79 ([adoption-criteria.md](adoption-criteria.md), C10): inheritance tax is devolved, and regions led by Madrid have introduced 99%+ *bonificaciones* for close relatives, pulling wealthy residents and pressuring other regions to follow (to verify). Spain is the live exhibit that sub-national assignment destroys transfer taxation — cite it whenever the US-states or Swiss-cantonal route is proposed ([`../08-capital-flight/game-theory.md`](../08-capital-flight/game-theory.md)).

## Regional analytic judgement

No continental state is a plausible unilateral first mover: France is legally and politically boxed, Germany is constitutionally gated, the Benelux and Iberian systems are structurally exposed or fragmented. But the region holds two strategic assets. First, the **Netherlands** as a dark-horse adopter within a coalition-agreement window. Second — decisive — the **EU as the coordination venue**: a directive-level or enhanced-cooperation floor on transfer taxation would simultaneously solve the Nordic exit fear ([nordics.md](nordics.md)), the Spanish/Belgian internal races, and the Irish small-economy constraint ([ireland.md](ireland.md)). The realistic continental path is not any capital adopting alone; it is Paris-plus-Berlin sponsoring a floor that others implement — with Germany implementing at whatever ceiling Art. 14 allows and the endowment carried by ordinary budget law.
