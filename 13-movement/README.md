# 13 — Movement: Building the Organisation That Carries the Idea

**Executive summary.** This section is the organising doctrine for getting from the current position — one manifesto, zero members — to a policy community that political parties must reckon with. It covers the causal theory of change, who to recruit and how, who should speak, what to build, who pays, who to ally with, and which historical movements to copy or avoid.

## The honest starting position

As of writing, this project is a research corpus and nothing else. There is no organisation, no funder, no membership list, no named spokesperson. That is not a weakness to conceal; it is the normal starting condition of every idea that later became policy. The neoliberals in 1947 were a few dozen academics at a Swiss hotel; the UBI advocates of 2010 were a mailing list. The question this section answers is not "how does a movement win?" but the prior question: **how does a document become a movement at all?**

The discipline this imposes is sequencing. Almost every recommendation in sections [06-palatability](../06-palatability/README.md), [12-political-adoption](../12-political-adoption/README.md), and [14-communications](../14-communications/README.md) presupposes capacities — credibility, messengers, money, allies — that do not yet exist and must be built in a specific order. This section is the build order.

## Scope boundary

This section is **organising doctrine**: recruitment, structure, money, alliances. It deliberately excludes message content — what to say, in which words, to which audience — which lives in [14-communications](../14-communications/README.md). Where the two meet (e.g. which messenger delivers which message), this section owns the *who* and 14 owns the *what*.

## Three organising judgements that shape everything downstream

1. **Ideas travel through institutions, not through virality.** The precedents ([precedents.md](precedents.md)) are consistent: durable policy change came from patient institutional infrastructure (think-tanks, fellowships, journals, chapters), not from moments of attention. Build for a 15–25 year horizon; treat viral moments as recruitment surges to be captured, not as the strategy.
2. **The defector is worth more than the believer.** A tax lawyer who explains avoidance from the inside, a founder who says "I don't want my children to inherit a dynasty," a farmer who supports the carve-out design — each is worth hundreds of already-convinced progressives, because they neutralise the strongest attacks catalogued in [10-opposition](../10-opposition/README.md). Recruitment ([recruitment.md](recruitment.md)) and messenger doctrine ([messengers.md](messengers.md)) are built around defectors.
3. **The beneficiary constituency must be organised, not merely polled.** The universal endowment ([02-proposal/endowment.md](../02-proposal/endowment.md)) gives this policy something wealth taxes lacked: millions of concrete winners. The movement's job is to make those winners *identifiable to themselves* — the failure to do this is what killed otherwise-popular wealth-tax campaigns ([precedents.md](precedents.md)).
