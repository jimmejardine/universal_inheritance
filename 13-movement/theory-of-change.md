# Theory of Change: From Manifesto to Entrenched Law

**Executive summary.** The proposal reaches the statute book through a six-stage causal chain — research corpus → credible institutional voice → salience events → coalition → party adoption → legislation → entrenchment — and each stage has explicit, falsifiable milestones. The chain requires both an inside track (elite persuasion) and an outside track (public pressure), because inheritance taxation is precisely the kind of policy that each track alone has historically failed to deliver.

## The causal chain

| Stage | What it is | Time horizon (indicative) | Primary owner |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1. Research corpus | This repository matured into citable, verified work | Years 0–2 | Core writers ([recruitment.md](recruitment.md)) |
| 2. Credible institutional voice | A named programme or institute that journalists and officials can call | Years 1–4 | [organisational-forms.md](organisational-forms.md) |
| 3. Salience events | Moments when inherited wealth dominates news cycles, met with a ready answer | Opportunistic, years 2+ | Communications ([14-communications/narratives.md](../14-communications/narratives.md)) |
| 4. Coalition | Adjacent movements signed up to a shared minimum demand | Years 3–8 | [coalitions.md](coalitions.md) |
| 5. Party adoption | At least one major party carries a recognisable version | Years 5–15 | [12-political-adoption/](../12-political-adoption/README.md) |
| 6. Legislation | An enacted first step on the ladder | Years 8–20 | [15-transition/](../15-transition/README.md) |
| 7. Entrenchment | The policy survives its first hostile government | Years 10–25 | [10-opposition/sabotage-after-passage.md](../10-opposition/sabotage-after-passage.md) |

Stages overlap; the numbering is causal, not strictly chronological. Stage 3 (salience) is the only stage the movement cannot manufacture on demand — the Great Wealth Transfer, a dynastic scandal, a fiscal crisis ([11-geographies/crisis-windows.md](../11-geographies/crisis-windows.md)) — so the doctrine is *preparedness*: when the window opens, the research, the spokesperson, and the demand must already exist. This is Milton Friedman's much-quoted observation that in a crisis, the actions taken depend on "the ideas that are lying around" — the movement's job is to do the lying around.

## Why this policy needs both tracks

**Inside track (elite persuasion).** Convince economists, tax professionals, Treasury officials, editorial boards, and party policy units that the proposal is technically sound and fiscally attractive. Necessary because inheritance-tax design is genuinely technical — the difference between a workable recipient-based lifetime tax ([02-proposal/mechanism.md](../02-proposal/mechanism.md)) and an unworkable one is invisible to the public but decisive to the officials who write the bill.

**Outside track (public pressure).** Build a visible constituency — endowment beneficiaries, priced-out young voters, coalition partners — that makes adoption electorally attractive and abandonment electorally costly.

**Why neither suffices alone:**

- *Inside track alone fails* because inheritance tax is the most unpopular tax in Western polling ([06-palatability/polling.md](../06-palatability/polling.md)). No official will champion, and no party will adopt, a technically elegant proposal that reads as electoral poison. Carbon pricing is the cautionary case: near-unanimous economist endorsement, technocratic adoption, then repeated public backlash because no mass base defended it ([precedents.md](precedents.md)).
- *Outside track alone fails* because a movement without technical depth is destroyed at the committee stage. The opponent's playbook ([10-opposition/attack-playbook.md](../10-opposition/attack-playbook.md)) is to concede the sentiment and attack the mechanism — valuation, liquidity, farms, avoidance. Every answer must already exist in [07-implementation/](../07-implementation/README.md) and be deliverable by credentialed insiders.

## Long-game templates

### The neoliberal precedent (Mont Pelerin → Thatcher/Reagan)

The most-studied case of a fringe idea capturing governing parties. Key transferable features: (1) a founding intellectual society (Mont Pelerin, 1947) that maintained doctrine across decades of irrelevance; (2) a deliberately built ecology of second-hand dealers in ideas — IEA (1955), Heritage (1973), and dozens of national think-tanks — translating academic work into policy briefs; (3) patient funder relationships (Fisher, Coors, Scaife — to verify specifics) measured in decades; (4) a prepared programme ready when the 1970s stagflation crisis discredited the incumbent consensus. Elapsed time from founding society to governing power: roughly 30 years. The honest lesson: this was almost entirely *inside-track*, and it worked because its beneficiaries (business, finance) were already organised and funded. Our beneficiaries are not, which is why we cannot simply run the same play — we must add the outside track.

### The UBI precedent

A useful modern template because it started from a comparably unpopular, "utopian" position. Its respectability cascade: academic lineage rehabilitated → pilot programmes (Finland, Stockton — to verify details) generated news-friendly evidence → tech-elite validators normalised it in centrist media → a presidential campaign (Yang 2020) made it a household term. Elapsed time from fringe to mainstream-debatable: roughly 15 years. Cautionary note: UBI achieved *familiarity* without *legislation* — it lacked a concrete, costed demand and a legislative pathway. Our design must not repeat that: the demand is specific (£500k threshold, £50k endowment — [02-proposal/thresholds.md](../02-proposal/thresholds.md)) and the pathway is drafted ([15-transition/legislative-path-uk.md](../15-transition/legislative-path-uk.md)).

## Falsifiable milestones by stage

A theory of change that cannot fail is a faith. Each stage below has tests; if a test fails on schedule, the strategy — not the calendar — should change.

| Stage | Milestone (pass) | Falsifier (fail signal) |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Corpus | All quantitative claims verified; at least one external economist willing to cite the work | After 2 years, no credentialed academic will associate with it |
| 2. Voice | A named programme with staff; media cite it by name; officials request briefings | Repeated pitch cycles to think-tanks produce no host ([organisational-forms.md](organisational-forms.md)) |
| 3. Salience | During a relevant news event, the programme is quoted as the recognised proponent | Salience events pass with the movement unmentioned twice running |
| 4. Coalition | Three or more independent organisations publicly endorse a shared minimum demand | Adjacent movements decline alliance on any terms ([coalitions.md](coalitions.md)) |
| 5. Party adoption | A recognisable version appears in a major-party platform or serious leadership campaign | A decade of engagement with policy units yields no adoption by any faction ([12-political-adoption/party-incentives.md](../12-political-adoption/party-incentives.md)) |
| 6. Legislation | A first-step bill (see stepping-stones, [12-political-adoption/stepping-stones.md](../12-political-adoption/stepping-stones.md)) is enacted | Adopted policy is diluted below recognisability before passage |
| 7. Entrenchment | Policy survives one full hostile-government term; endowment recipients defend it | Repeal or hollowing-out within first hostile term ([10-opposition/sabotage-after-passage.md](../10-opposition/sabotage-after-passage.md)) |

## Strategic implications

1. **Front-load credibility, not attention.** Stages 1–2 are cheap and fully within current control. Publicity before credibility invites the attack playbook against an undefended position.
2. **The endowment is the outside track's engine.** Public pressure needs beneficiaries who can name what they gain. Every outside-track activity should make the £50k/$60k endowment the lead ([00-executive-summary.md](../00-executive-summary.md) design choice).
3. **Plan for the founder to become irrelevant.** A 25-year chain must survive the exit of every current participant. Governance and succession are treated in [organisational-forms.md](organisational-forms.md); over-reliance on a single charismatic founder is a listed movement-killer in [precedents.md](precedents.md).
4. **Entrenchment is designed now, not later.** Grandfathering, sequencing, and beneficiary lock-in ([15-transition/grandfathering.md](../15-transition/grandfathering.md), [16-second-order/endowment-effects.md](../16-second-order/endowment-effects.md)) determine whether stage 7 is winnable, and they constrain stage 6 drafting.
