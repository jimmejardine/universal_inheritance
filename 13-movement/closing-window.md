# The Closing Window: How Much Time Is Left?

**Executive summary.** Billionaire wealth has compounded at 7.5% a year in real terms since 1987 against 1.4% growth in global income per adult — an arithmetic that roughly doubles the top stratum's relative weight every twelve years — and a growing share of that weight is being converted into *blocking* power: campaign finance, media and platform ownership, judicial pipelines, and direct occupancy of government. The honest reading of the evidence is that the United States is already well along the capture curve while the United Kingdom's window remains open but is narrowing measurably — which, given that this movement's own causal chain ([theory-of-change.md](theory-of-change.md)) needs 10–25 years to run, makes the case for starting now arithmetic rather than rhetorical.

## Findings

- The wealth-to-power conversion is documented, not hypothetical: the 2024 US cycle saw a record ~$4.5bn in outside spending [3], with the top 100 donors supplying 16% of *all* federal fundraising and one individual (Elon Musk) giving over $290m [2][4][5]; in the UK, the share of private political donations from £1m+ givers rose from 1% in 2015 to 35% in 2024 — a 35-fold concentration in nine years [6].
- Blocking power now operates through at least seven mutually reinforcing channels — campaign money, legacy media, platforms, courts, think-tank/astroturf infrastructure, direct politician-billionaire fusion, and (emerging) AI — and every one of them shows a rising trajectory in the US; the UK shows the same direction on money and media but retains structural dampeners (spending caps, no paid TV advertising, Ofcom impartiality) [1][6][19].
- The arithmetic clock: at Zucman's verified rates (7.5%/yr real billionaire wealth growth vs 1.4% income growth — [../01-problem/wealth-concentration.md](../01-problem/wealth-concentration.md)), the billionaire stratum doubles its weight relative to society roughly every 12 years; billionaire wealth has already gone from ~3% to ~14% of world GDP since 1987 [1]. Political spending consumes well under 0.1% of that stock, so the binding constraint on blocking expenditure is norms and law, not resources — and each doubling relaxes it further.
- Historical calibration cuts both ways: reform windows have opened and delivered within 2–15 years (People's Budget 1909 → Parliament Act 1911; muckraking 1900s → 16th Amendment 1913 → estate tax 1916; Depression → 70% estate rate 1935) [1][21][22][23][24], but consolidation has closed windows just as fast — Russia's oligarchy locked in within ~5 years of loans-for-shares [26], Hungary's media system was captured within 8 years of Orbán's supermajority [25], and the Gilded Age "Millionaires' Club" Senate blocked correction for two decades until a constitutional amendment was required [24].
- **Judgement, clearly labelled:** on current trajectories the UK has roughly one to two decades of comparatively open window; the US may need a crisis-scale opening ([../11-geographies/crisis-windows.md](../11-geographies/crisis-windows.md)) to legislate against already-entrenched blocking power. Neither reading supports despair; both make delay the most expensive strategic choice available.

## 1. The mechanisms that convert wealth into blocking power

The general evidence that concentrated wealth buys policy outcomes is set out in [../01-problem/political-capture.md](../01-problem/political-capture.md) (Gilens–Page, Citizens United, lobbying, revolving door), and the full scholarly literature — responsiveness empirics, oligarchy theory, and the democratic-survival evidence — is surveyed in [../01-problem/wealth-and-democratic-stability.md](../01-problem/wealth-and-democratic-stability.md). This section is narrower: it tracks the specific channels through which the very top of the distribution can *block* transfer taxation, with a trajectory assessment for each.

| # | Mechanism | Strongest verified evidence | Trajectory |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Campaign finance (US) | Record ~$4.5bn outside spending in 2024; top 100 donors = 16% of all fundraising; top 10 individuals gave $599m (7%); Musk alone gave $291m+ — the largest single-donor outlay since at least 2010 [2][3][4][5] | Rising steeply since 2010 |
| 2 | Campaign finance (UK) | £1m+ donors' share of private donations: 1% (2015) → 35% (2024); 66% of 2023 private donations from 19 mega-donors; parties took almost £100m in 2024, the most expensive UK election ever [6][7] | Rising steeply; capped spending but uncapped donations |
| 3 | Media ownership | Three companies control 90% of UK national newspaper circulation [19]; the proprietors are themselves dynasts with a direct stake in this tax — full treatment in [../10-opposition/elite-media-capture.md](../10-opposition/elite-media-capture.md) | High plateau; print reach declining, agenda-setting persists |
| 4 | Platform ownership | X's algorithm shifted around Musk's 13 July 2024 Trump endorsement: his post views +138%, retweets +238%, with a measurable boost to Republican-leaning accounts (QUT analysis) [14][15]; Meta ended US third-party fact-checking in January 2025 and donated $1m to the Trump inaugural fund [13] | New since ~2022 and rising; no UK/EU equivalent yet at scale |
| 5 | Judicial capture (US) | The Federalist Society pipeline supplied Trump's 234 first-term judges including three Supreme Court justices from Leonard Leo's curated lists [8]; Leo's network received the largest known political-advocacy gift in US history — Barre Seid's $1.6bn transfer to the Marble Freedom Trust, structured to avoid ~$400m in tax [9] | Locked in for a generation: lifetime appointments, 6–3 court |
| 6 | Think-tank / astroturf saturation | The 2001 estate-tax repeal was a two-decade purchased-infrastructure campaign — catalogued in [../10-opposition/attack-playbook.md](../10-opposition/attack-playbook.md) and [../10-opposition/astroturf-and-lobbying.md](../10-opposition/astroturf-and-lobbying.md); Seid's $1.6bn shows the current funding scale of a single node [9] | Mature in US; expanding in UK (Tufton Street model) |
| 7 | Politician–billionaire fusion | The 2025 US cabinet is the richest in history — Forbes counts $7.5bn for the cabinet proper, more than double the 2019 record, with 12 billionaires across the administration versus a combined ~$118m for Biden's cabinet [10][11][12]; a majority of Congress are millionaires (median ~$1m+) [16] | Step-change in 2025; the blocker now sits inside the government |
| 8 | AI as concentration accelerator (emerging/contested) | IMF modelling: ~40% of global employment is AI-exposed, and in its scenarios capital-income and wealth inequality *always increase* with AI adoption, because gains accrue to holders of AI capital [17][18]; frontier-lab control sits with a handful of billionaire founders and mega-cap boards | Emerging; direction supported by IMF analysis, magnitude genuinely uncertain |

Three observations on the table, the first two evidenced, the third judgement:

1. **The channels reinforce each other.** Judicial capture protects campaign-finance deregulation (Citizens United is itself case law); platform ownership amplifies the media channel; cabinet fusion converts private preference directly into administrative action without needing the slower channels at all.
2. **The cost asymmetry favours blocking.** US billionaire political spending in 2024 — the most expensive cycle ever — consumed well under 0.1% of US billionaire wealth [1][2][3]. Blocking a tax that would bind at generational transfer is, for this stratum, close to free at any foreseeable scale of politics; the same is not true for the reform side, whose beneficiaries are unorganised ([theory-of-change.md](theory-of-change.md)).
3. **Judgement:** the binding constraint on blocking power today is legal and normative (donation caps, impartiality rules, disclosure), not financial. That is precisely why the watch-indicators in §4 track *rule changes* as closely as spending levels: the rules are what the money will be spent changing.

## 2. The arithmetic clock

The verified inputs, from Zucman's 2026 synthesis ([../01-problem/wealth-concentration.md](../01-problem/wealth-concentration.md)): the wealth of the global top 0.0001% (~3,000 billionaire households) has grown 7.5% a year in real terms since 1987, against 2.5% for global wealth per adult and 1.4% for global income per adult; the stratum's wealth has risen from about 3% to roughly 14% of world GDP over that period [1].

The implication is mechanical: a stock compounding at 7.5% against income growing at 1.4% doubles its *relative* weight every ~12 years (ln 2 ÷ ln(1.075/1.014) ≈ 11.9). Each doubling operates on both sides of the reform ledger at once — it raises the wealth at stake (the incentive to block) and the free cash available for blocking (the capacity), while the cost of politics grows far more slowly.

**Illustrative scenarios — arithmetic projections, not forecasts.** They assume the 1987–2026 growth differential persists or moderates as stated; asset-price reversals, war, or policy change would break any of them ([../11-geographies/crisis-windows.md](../11-geographies/crisis-windows.md) catalogues exactly such breaks).

| Scenario | Assumption | Billionaire wealth, % of world GDP | Relative doubling time |
|---|---|---|---|
| A. Continuation | 7.5% wealth growth vs 1.4% income growth persists | ~14% (2026) → ~28% (late 2030s) → ~56% (c. 2050) | ~12 years |
| B. Moderation | Wealth growth halves to ~5%; income growth unchanged | ~28% around mid-2040s | ~20 years |
| C. Zucman floor | A 2% annual minimum tax on the stratum, all else as A | Net compounding ~5.5%; doubling slows but does not stop | ~17 years |

Scenario C is the quietly important row: even the strongest annual-tax proposal currently in mainstream debate *slows* the clock without stopping it, because a 2% levy on a stock growing at 7.5% leaves a 4-point differential over income. Only taxation at the generational transfer — the point where the stock must change hands — truncates the stock itself. That is the movement's own thesis restated as a race condition: the instrument that stops the clock is the one the accumulating power will most fiercely block ([../03-case-for/wealth-tax-comparison.md](../03-case-for/wealth-tax-comparison.md), [../16-second-order/dynasties-and-power.md](../16-second-order/dynasties-and-power.md)).

Two qualifications, for honesty. First, wealth-to-power conversion is not linear or automatic — Gilens–Page-style influence operates through institutions that retain friction (courts, devolution, regulators, elections), and billionaires are not politically homogeneous ([../00-capitalism/capitalist-supporters.md](../00-capitalism/capitalist-supporters.md) documents defectors). Second, the 7.5% series is substantially equity-valuation-driven; a decade of poor asset returns would slow the clock without any policy at all. Neither qualification changes the direction, only the date.

## 3. Historical calibration: how fast windows open and close

### Windows that opened — and how long they took

| Case | Agitation begins | Enactment | Elapsed | What it took |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Progressive Era, US | Muckraking journalism from ~1902; TR's 1906 inheritance-tax message ([../05-history/roosevelt-1906.md](../05-history/roosevelt-1906.md)) | 16th Amendment ratified February 1913 [21]; federal estate tax, Revenue Act of 1916 ($50k exemption, 1–10%) [22] | ~10–14 years | A decade of salience-building, a constitutional amendment, and war-preparedness finance |
| People's Budget, UK | Budget introduced April 1909 (super-tax and higher death duties); vetoed by the House of Lords [23] | Budget passed April 1910 after a general election; Parliament Act August 1911 permanently broke the Lords' veto [23] | ~2.5 years | Two general elections fought explicitly on "peers versus people" |
| New Deal, US | Crash 1929; FDR inaugurated March 1933 | Revenue Act of 1935 raised the top estate rate from 60% to 70% (77% by 1941) [22] | ~3–6 years | A systemic crisis that had already broken the blocking coalition |

Zucman's own LSE framing draws the same lesson from the first two rows: the People's Budget was "vetoed by House of Lords → Parliament Act of 1911," the French Senate blocked the income tax until WWI, the US needed the 16th Amendment — progressive taxation has *always* had to defeat an entrenched blocking institution, and it has repeatedly done so; his closing slide is "Time for the UK to lead again!" [1]. The encouraging reading: blocking power comparable to today's has been beaten before, within a decade, by movements that arrived at the window prepared. The sobering reading: each victory required either a constitutional confrontation or a war/depression — peacetime persuasion alone has a thin record ([../11-geographies/crisis-windows.md](../11-geographies/crisis-windows.md) on Scheidel).

### Windows that closed — consolidation past democratic correction

- **The Gilded Age Senate.** By the 1890s the indirectly elected Senate was popularly known as the "Millionaires' Club" — seats effectively purchasable through state legislatures — and it functioned as a standing veto on economic reform; the income tax was struck down in 1895 and correction ultimately required the 17th Amendment (direct election, ratified 1913) after roughly two decades of blocked attempts [24]. Lesson: a captured *veto institution* can hold for 20+ years even against sustained majorities, and dislodging it required constitutional surgery plus the Progressive-Era press.
- **Russia, 1995–2000.** The loans-for-shares privatisations of 1995–96 handed a handful of connected businessmen controlling stakes in the commanding heights of the economy; within five years an oligarch class controlled a substantial share of Russian industry and the state–wealth relationship inverted into mutual capture, then subordination under Putin — democratic correction never arrived [26]. Lesson: the open-window phase can be startlingly short once wealth, media, and state office begin to fuse.
- **Hungary, 2010–2018.** Eight years after Orbán's supermajority, 476 pro-government media outlets were donated simultaneously into a single foundation (KESMA, November 2018), and the government decreed the merger a matter of "strategic national importance" to exempt it from competition review; KESMA-aligned media now account for roughly 40% of the news and public-affairs market's revenue [25]. Lesson: a modern EU democracy consolidated media control *by legal instrument* within two parliamentary terms — the mechanism was rule-change, not tanks.

**Disanalogies, stated honestly.** Russia had no rule-of-law tradition, no independent judiciary, and a one-off privatisation shock; Hungary is small, has weak counter-majoritarian institutions, and a constitution its government could rewrite unilaterally. The US and UK have deeper courts, federal/devolved structures, competitive elections, and residual independent media; neither is on Hungary's timetable. The transferable finding is narrower but still uncomfortable: **consolidation, where it happened, ran in 5–8 years — faster than any reform window on record opened** — and it proceeded through channels (media roll-ups, court-stacking, rule changes blessed as legal) that are visibly active in the US table in §1. The negative cases are warnings about *speed and mechanism*, not predictions of destination.

## 5. Verdict: how much time is left?

Differentiated by geography, and labelled throughout as judgement resting on the evidence above.

**United States: substantially far along the capture curve.** Post-2010 campaign finance is structurally open to unlimited wealth [2][3]; the judiciary is generationally locked [8][9]; the 2025 executive is the wealth stratum governing in person [10][11][12]; and the platform layer has demonstrated in-cycle partisan intervention [13][14][15]. Judgement: routine legislative adoption of confiscatory transfer taxation is unlikely without a crisis-scale window; the realistic US path is the stepping-stone ladder ([../15-transition/legislative-path-us.md](../15-transition/legislative-path-us.md)) plus preparedness for a Scheidel-type opening. The US case history (1913, 1916, 1935) shows capture at this level *has* been reversed — but never cheaply.

**United Kingdom: the window is open and narrowing.** The UK retains the structural dampeners the US lost — statutory campaign spending caps, no paid TV political advertising, Ofcom due impartiality, no Citizens United equivalent — and its billionaire class is comparatively small: roughly 200 families above £700m on Zucman's series [1]. But the direction of travel is unambiguous: mega-donor share up 35-fold in nine years [6], record election spending [7], and press ownership already at 90%/three-companies concentration [19]. Judgement: on current trajectory the UK has perhaps one to two decades before its political-finance environment resembles the US of the 2010s; the decisive tripwires are donation-cap reform (window holds or reopens) versus cap removal or platform-scale intervention in a UK election (window closes early).

**The strategic conclusion is arithmetic, not motivational.** The movement's own causal chain needs 10–25 years to run ([theory-of-change.md](theory-of-change.md)); the reform ladder needs 10–20 ([../15-transition/sequencing.md](../15-transition/sequencing.md)); and the UK window, on the judgement above, is of the same order. The margin between "in time" and "too late" is therefore roughly zero *if the chain starts now* and negative if it starts later — while every 12 years of delay doubles the opposing resources (§2). Urgency here is not a rhetorical device; it is the schedule constraint that makes Phase 0 ([../15-transition/sequencing.md](../15-transition/sequencing.md)) — buildable today, without any window — the correct immediate act. The same clock also supplies the offer to the mainstream voter: this is the last comparatively cheap decade in which the feudal-remnant correction ([../00-capitalism/feudal-remnant.md](../00-capitalism/feudal-remnant.md)) can be legislated by ordinary democratic means, as an act of system maintenance rather than crisis response ([../03-case-for/democratic-renewal.md](../03-case-for/democratic-renewal.md)).

## Honest limits

- **The clock is a growth-differential extrapolation.** The 7.5% series is 39 years of data dominated by an equity bull era; sustained lower asset returns would stretch every doubling time in §2. The scenarios are labelled illustrative because they are.
- **Wealth-to-power conversion has no agreed exchange rate.** We can measure spending, ownership, and appointments; we cannot measure "blocking power" directly, and the political-influence literature (Gilens–Page and its critics — [../01-problem/political-capture.md](../01-problem/political-capture.md)) disputes magnitude, not direction.
- **The negative cases are imperfect analogies.** Russia and Hungary demonstrate speed and mechanism, not inevitability; the US and UK have institutional friction those states lacked. Equally, the positive cases all involved crisis or constitutional confrontation — there is no clean historical example of peacetime, pre-crisis adoption of the kind this project ultimately seeks.
- **AI as an accelerator is direction-supported, magnitude-unknown.** The IMF's "capital and wealth inequality always increase" result is model-based [17]; frontier-lab concentration is observable but its political translation is, so far, inference.
- **The verdict dates ("one to two decades") are judgement**, offered because refusing to estimate would be less honest than estimating with stated error bars. The dashboard in §4 exists precisely so the estimate can be updated or falsified rather than defended.
