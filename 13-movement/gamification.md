# Gamification: The Scoreboard That Makes Politicians Want to Jump On

**Executive summary.** This file answers one strategic question: what citizen action, visible in what form, makes supporting the proposal an *opportunity* for a politician rather than a risk — and how are both citizens and politicians given a game worth playing? The verified template is the most effective politician-gamification device in tax politics, Grover Norquist's Taxpayer Protection Pledge — public, binary, named, scoreboarded, and enforced at the ballot [1][2] — inverted here into a positive-sum **Universal Inheritance Pledge**, tracked on a public constituency dashboard whose UK design precedents (the Parliament petitions constituency data, TheyWorkForYou) already exist and already work [3][4][5].

## Key points

- **The politician mechanic is a tiered public pledge.** Norquist's pledge shows the mechanics: a short written commitment, signed by name, published in a database, treated as binding for the duration of office, with defection publicly visible — at peak, 238 House members and 41 senators had signed [1][2]. Our inversion is tiered (briefing → statement → step-2 vote pledge → full stepping-stones pledge), keyed to [../12-political-adoption/stepping-stones.md](../12-political-adoption/stepping-stones.md), and positive-only: movers are celebrated, holdouts are never mocked ([../14-communications/narratives.md](../14-communications/narratives.md) villain rule).
- **The citizen action that moves politicians is verified constituent contact from their own seat, at visible volume.** The best evidence anywhere is US: across three Congressional Management Foundation staff surveys (2004, 2010, 2015), over 90% of congressional staff said in-person visits from constituents influence an undecided lawmaker, and staff prize district-impact information they almost never receive [6]. No equivalent UK statistic was verifiable; the UK claim is labelled judgement (§1.3).
- **The dashboard is a routing tool, not a trophy cabinet.** Its headline metrics are Tufekci-style *capacity* metrics — members, organisers, meetups, MP replies — never follower counts [7][8]; every mechanic exists to move a supporter one rung up [engagement-ladder.md](engagement-ladder.md), per the conversion doctrine of [digital-organising.md](digital-organising.md) §6.
- **The proven UK formats already exist.** petition.parliament.uk publishes per-petition signature data by parliamentary constituency (JSON), and Petition Map renders it as a constituency heat map [3][4]; mySociety's TheyWorkForYou publishes MP voting records, debates, and interests as the standing accountability layer [5]. The dashboard is these two formats fused and pointed at one issue (design judgement).
- **The dashboard is also the story.** "The map turning green" gives journalists a running horse-race and gives every MP a number attached to their own name and seat — the visible scoreboard the death-tax reframing needs ([../14-communications/narratives.md](../14-communications/narratives.md)); the bandwagon mechanism is Cialdini's social proof, with its campaign application labelled judgement [9].
- Except where cited, everything below is **game-design judgement** — mechanics chosen for consistency with [theory-of-change.md](theory-of-change.md) and the repo's organising doctrine, not established fact.

## 1. The politician mechanic: the Universal Inheritance Pledge

### 1.1 The precedent, verified

Americans for Tax Reform's Taxpayer Protection Pledge, created in 1986 with Reagan's endorsement, is a one-sentence written commitment to "oppose any and all tax increases," signed by candidates and incumbents, treated by ATR as binding for as long as the signer holds the office, and framed as a promise made to constituents rather than to ATR itself [1]. ATR maintains a public database of signers and non-signers; as of ATR's own current count, 44 senators, 194 House members, and 17 governors are signatories, with nearly 1,400 elected officials having signed since inception [1]. At its peak in the 112th Congress (2011–12), 238 House members and 41 senators had signed — a House majority, including all but a handful of Republicans [2].

Why it worked (analysis, building on the verified record): it converted a diffuse ideological posture into a **binary, public, named, permanent** commitment; it made the *list* the story, so every signing and every defection was news; and it attached a credible electoral consequence — a broken pledge was publicised to primary voters. The pledge is the most successful piece of politician gamification in modern tax politics, and it was built by the other side. The correct response is not admiration from a distance; it is inversion.

### 1.2 The inversion (design judgement)

The **Universal Inheritance Pledge** differs from Norquist's in three deliberate ways: it is *tiered* rather than binary (politicians climb, like supporters on [engagement-ladder.md](engagement-ladder.md)); it is keyed to *stepping-stones*, never the flagship — no politician is ever asked to carry the map ([../12-political-adoption/stepping-stones.md](../12-political-adoption/stepping-stones.md)); and it is enforced by *reward*, not punishment (§5 explains why the punishment mechanic is unavailable to us and would backfire).

| Tier | Commitment | Cost to the politician | Maps to |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0 — Open | Accepted a briefing; on record as "open to the evidence" | Near zero, deniable | Ask-ladder rung 1 ([../12-political-adoption/target-politicians.md](../12-political-adoption/target-politicians.md)) |
| 1 — Supportive | Public statement for at least one stepping-stone (e.g. relief hygiene, recipient basis) | A quote, on their own party's cover story | Ask-ladder rungs 2–3 |
| 2 — Vote pledge | Written pledge to vote for the recipient-basis switch (step 2) when a bill comes | The Norquist unit: binary, named, published | Ask-ladder rung 4–5 |
| 3 — Full pledge | Written pledge for steps 1–3 plus the pilot endowment | A platform, not a vote — the manifesto-adjacent tier | Ask-ladder rung 6 |

Each tier is honourable to rest on indefinitely — the same rule as the citizen ladder. The pledge text at every tier is drafted so the signer can truthfully say "this is not a 100% inheritance tax," because it is not (the deniability test of [../12-political-adoption/stepping-stones.md](../12-political-adoption/stepping-stones.md)).

### 1.3 What actually moves an MP (evidence, then judgement)

**The verified evidence is American.** The Congressional Management Foundation's *Citizen-Centric Advocacy* (2017), built on nine staff surveys 2004–16, found that across the 2004, 2010, and 2015 waves more than 90% of congressional staff said in-person visits from constituents can influence an undecided lawmaker, and that 91% of staff want district-impact information while only 9% regularly receive it [6]. The transferable core: **individualised contact from a legislator's own voters, carrying local impact detail, is the highest-value input a citizen can produce** — and it is scarce, so modest volumes register.

**The UK translation is judgement.** No equivalently clean survey of MPs' weighting of constituent correspondence was verifiable for this file; UK parliamentary staff guidance and MP-communication research exist but yield no citable headline statistic. The working assumptions, labelled: an MP's office distinguishes constituents from non-constituents at the sorting stage; template floods are discounted, personal letters are not; and a small number of individual letters on an issue an MP hears nothing about reads as a signal, because the base rate is near zero. This is why the movement's letter tool (the routing doctrine of [digital-organising.md](digital-organising.md) §1: postcode-in, assignment-out) enforces the discipline of [engagement-ladder.md](engagement-ladder.md) §2 — own constituency only, personal words required, stepping-stone asks keyed to the MP's party, a reply-demanding question at the end.

**The challenger dynamic (judgement).** The pledge's sharpest edge is competitive: it is offered to *candidates*, not only incumbents. One candidate in a constituency signing tier 1 creates a question the incumbent must answer; a challenger signing tier 2 in a marginal seat turns the dashboard's constituency page into a local story. Norquist's pledge spread the same way — through candidates, cycle by cycle, until non-signing became the anomaly [2]. And for sitting MPs there is a first-mover prestige asset that costs the movement nothing to confer: the first MP at each tier, in each party, is permanently recorded as such — the approach sequencing for who is offered that distinction first is [../12-political-adoption/target-politicians.md](../12-political-adoption/target-politicians.md)'s ask-ladder.

## 2. The public dashboard

Design judgement throughout; the precedents are verified.

### 2.1 The precedents

- **petition.parliament.uk** (launched July 2015; 10,000 signatures compel a government response, 100,000 qualify for a debate) publishes each petition's signatures **by parliamentary constituency** as open JSON [3]; Unboxed's Petition Map renders exactly this as a constituency heat map with per-seat counts and the sitting MP's name and party on hover [4]. The format is proven, familiar to UK journalists, and legible to MPs — every MP already understands "how many of *my* constituents signed."
- **TheyWorkForYou** (mySociety, charity no. 1076346) publishes MPs' voting records, debate contributions, and registered interests, and its sibling WriteToThem routes citizen letters to the correct representative [5]. It is the standing proof that a public, per-MP accountability page is an accepted piece of UK civic furniture, not an act of aggression.

### 2.2 The components (design judgement)

| Component | What it shows | The game it creates |
|---|---|---|
| Constituency heat map | Supporters per constituency (aggregate count only — §5 data rules), shaded toward milestones | "Turn your constituency green"; constituency leagues (§3) |
| Completion bars | Progress toward named per-seat milestones (100 → 500 → 1,000 supporters) | Near-milestone seats mobilise hardest — the closing-the-bar effect |
| MP scoreboard | Each MP's public record verbatim (votes, quotes, pledge tier, whether they reply to letters) — TheyWorkForYou discipline: record, not commentary | First-mover prestige; the challenger question; a page every journalist can cite |
| Milestone unlocks | "This constituency hit 1,000 → funded town-hall event + local press release" | Converts online counts into offline rooms — the online-to-offline ramp of [digital-organising.md](digital-organising.md) §3 |
| The flywheel counter | "This month's outreach funded by N small donors" — live | Makes the funding loop visible and participatory ([paid-acquisition.md](paid-acquisition.md), [funding.md](funding.md) transparency doctrine) |
| Open data | Aggregate counts published as open JSON, petition-site style [3] | Third parties build their own maps; the data outlives the site |

Two hard rules. **The MP scoreboard celebrates movement and never mocks stasis**: an MP page shows what they have said and done, in their words, with tier badges for commitments made — there is no red column, no "worst MPs" list, no shame index. The villain of every story is the dynastic system, never a person ([../14-communications/narratives.md](../14-communications/narratives.md) rule 3); an MP publicly humiliated is an MP committed against you (§5). **All published numbers are aggregate**: never a supporter's name, never sub-constituency granularity — the ICO data rules of [digital-organising.md](digital-organising.md) §7 govern the whole build (§5).

### 2.3 What the dashboard is for

The dashboard is a **routing tool wearing a scoreboard costume**. Its private function (the movement's own view) is [recruitment.md](recruitment.md)'s metrics: rung transitions, conversion rates between digital actions, which constituencies are one organiser short of a chapter. Its public function is to make the movement's growth *legible to politicians as electoral information*: a specific, checkable number of their own constituents, climbing. The moment those two functions conflict — when a public number could be inflated at the cost of real capacity — the private function wins (§5, Goodhart).

## 3. Citizen mechanics, rung by rung

Every mechanic below is design judgement, and every one obeys a single rule from [digital-organising.md](digital-organising.md) §6: **a mechanic must move a person up the ladder of [engagement-ladder.md](engagement-ladder.md), or it is a vanity metric and is cut.** Tufekci's distinction is the test — movements fail when their visible *signal* outruns their organised *capacity* [7][8] — so the dashboard's headline numbers are capacity numbers (members, organisers, meetups held, MP replies logged), never followers or impressions.

| Mechanic | Ladder rung it serves | How it converts upward |
|---|---|---|
| Referral tree ("you recruited N; your branch is M deep") | 0→2 | Every share carries a join link; the tree is visible only to its owner — recruitment as a personal craft, Ice-Bucket nomination logic ([digital-organising.md](digital-organising.md) §5) |
| Action streaks (monthly action kept alive) | 2→4 | The streak's menu *is* rungs 2–4: stay subscribed, keep the £3 donation, write the letter — a lapsed streak triggers a human check-in, not a nag |
| MP-letter counter (per constituency, verified senders only) | 4 | The routing tool of [digital-organising.md](digital-organising.md): postcode-in, party-keyed stepping-stone template out, reply logged on the MP scoreboard |
| Rung badges (public profile shows highest rung held) | all | Makes the ladder itself the status game; "hosted 3 dinners" outranks "shared 300 posts" by design |
| Constituency league (seats ranked by supporters per elector) | 5–6 | League position unlocks the §2 milestones — town hall, local press moment, chapter kit |
| Team & chapter leaderboards (meetups held, letters generated, new members) | 8–10 | Measured on Han's organising outputs — capacity built, successors developed [10] — not attendance alone |

The anti-pattern list is as important as the mechanics: no points for follows, no leaderboard for shares, no "top poster" — every one of those rewards signal production and trains supporters to stop at rung 0 [7]. Descending remains honourable ([engagement-ladder.md](engagement-ladder.md) rule 2): badges are held, not forfeited, and a paused streak carries no public mark.

## 4. The narrative function: the dashboard is the story

A movement at stage 2–3 of [theory-of-change.md](theory-of-change.md) has a structural media problem: policy arguments are not news twice. A scoreboard is news every time it moves. The dashboard converts the movement's growth into the one story format political journalism never tires of — the horse race: which constituency turned green this month, which MP moved a tier, which party leads on pledges, how close seat X is to its milestone. Each of those is a local-press story with a local number and a named local MP in it (design judgement, but the petition site's constituency map already functions exactly this way for journalists during viral petitions [3][4]).

For the framing war, the scoreboard does something arguments cannot: it *shows* the death-tax frame being replaced rather than asserting it ([../14-communications/narratives.md](../14-communications/narratives.md), [../14-communications/naming-and-language.md](../14-communications/naming-and-language.md)). The mechanism is social proof — Cialdini's finding that people resolve uncertainty by copying what visibly many others are doing [9]. Its application here is labelled judgement: a rising, checkable count of neighbours-who-signed is the strongest available "people like me support this" signal, and for politicians the same signal reads as electoral cover. Bandwagons are built from evidence of a bandwagon; the dashboard manufactures that evidence honestly, or not at all (§5).

## 5. Honest limits

- **The slacktivism trap is the design's own failure mode.** A gamified layer, badly built, industrialises exactly what Tufekci warns against: maximal signal, minimal capacity [7][8]. The §3 conversion-first rule and capacity-only headline metrics are the mitigation; the falsifier is measurable — if dashboard numbers rise for two years while ring-3 conversion and meetup counts stall ([recruitment.md](recruitment.md) metrics), the game layer is producing spectators and must be rebuilt.
- **Political-opinion data is special-category data.** A supporter count by constituency is, at the individual level, a database of political opinions tied to location — squarely inside the ICO's political-campaigning guidance ([digital-organising.md](digital-organising.md) §7) [11]. Hard rules: explicit consent at sign-up for constituency-level counting; publication in aggregate only; no per-person map layer, ever; no data sharing with parties; deletion honoured on request. A movement proposing a state receipts register cannot survive being careless with its own register of supporters.
- **Brigading and fake sign-ups (judgement).** A public scoreboard invites inflation — by enthusiasts, by pranksters, and by opponents seeking a "fake numbers" exposé ([../10-opposition/astroturf-and-lobbying.md](../10-opposition/astroturf-and-lobbying.md) documents the accusation environment). Defences: email/postcode verification before a supporter counts, rate limiting, anomaly monitoring, periodic third-party audits of the published totals, and a published methodology note. The movement's numbers must be boringly defensible, because its entire critique of the other side is manufactured grassroots.
- **Scoreboard backfire.** Norquist's enforcement arm — primary punishment — is unavailable to a movement at this stage and undesirable at any stage: a publicly shamed MP acquires a personal incentive to dig in, and "campaign harasses MP" is the opposition's dream headline. Mitigation is structural, not tonal: the public layer records and celebrates only; pressure (letter volume, challenger questions) travels privately and locally. The map shows green; it never shows enemies.
- **Goodhart's law applies to every number above.** Any metric made a target — letters sent, streaks kept, pledges signed — will be gamed, first by our own supporters. Mitigations: rotate milestone metrics, audit quality behind quantity (a letter counted is a letter a human verified was personal), and keep the movement's internal success measures (rung transitions, chapters, MP *replies* rather than letters) distinct from the public game.
- **Evidence limits.** The CMF findings are US congressional staff surveys, not UK MP evidence [6]; the UK translation in §1.3 is unverified judgement. The Norquist precedent is verified as to mechanics and scale [1][2], but its effectiveness owes much to a primary system the UK lacks; the inversion's effectiveness is untested. Cialdini's social proof is established psychology [9]; its campaign-dashboard application here is extrapolation. No dashboard of this design has yet been run for a single-issue UK policy movement — the nearest formats [3][4][5] are proofs of legibility, not of persuasion.
