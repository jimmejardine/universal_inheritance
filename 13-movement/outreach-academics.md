# Outreach Map: Academics

**Executive summary.** The scholarly infrastructure this proposal needs — empirics, design review, credentialed validators, survey instruments, a student pipeline — already exists in three clusters: the economists of wealth-transfer taxation, the philosophers of inheritance and property-owning democracy, and the historians and sociologists of inherited wealth. This file maps the named individuals whose *published record* makes them worth approaching, what each has actually said, and the specific first ask — with the standing caveat that inclusion here means their work is relevant, **not** that any of them endorses this project.

**Key findings**

- The closest published cousins of the full architecture are already on the record: Piketty's endowment-at-25 funded by steep transfer taxes ([../05-history/piketty.md](../05-history/piketty.md)); Batchelder's US inheritance tax with a lifetime exemption as low as $500k [12]; and the French Conseil d'analyse économique note co-authored by Landais and Stantcheva proposing taxation of *lifetime inheritance flows received* plus a "capital guarantee for all" [8] — a receipts-plus-endowment design from a body that advises the French Prime Minister.
- The UK evidence base is institutionally concentrated: Advani and Summers (CenTax) on IHT reliefs and avoidance [9][10], Sturrock (IFS) on the coming inheritance wave and its mobility effects [11] — these institutions trade on neutrality, so the ask is analysis and scrutiny, never endorsement.
- The philosophers supply what economists cannot: Halliday's book-length case that inherited wealth entrenches "economic segregation" [21], Robeyns's limitarianism [22], and the Rawlsian property-owning-democracy school (White, O'Neill, Jackson) whose "citizen's inheritance" is this proposal's philosophical name [23][24][26].
- The two most valuable people to engage may be sceptics: Waldenström (inheritance flows are equalising in relative terms; transfer taxes raise little and distort much [15][16][17]) and Kopczuk (avoidance and administrative cost are first-order, not footnotes [19][20]). A design that survives their published critiques is a design that survives parliament.
- Every receptivity judgement beyond the published record is explicitly labelled **(judgement)**; nothing in this file should be read as a claim that any listed individual supports a 100% rate, this project, or any campaign.

## Engagement doctrine

### What academics can and cannot give a movement

| Can give | Cannot give (and should not be asked for) |
|---|---|
| **Credibility**: the Meade→Mirrlees→Atkinson→OECD chain ([../05-history/atkinson.md](../05-history/atkinson.md)) is the standing rebuttal to "crank economics" — living scholars extend it | Campaigning: an academic on a barricade converts expertise into partisanship and destroys the asset |
| **Design review**: costings, base definitions, behavioural-response estimates that survive Treasury scrutiny ([../12-political-adoption/policy-entrepreneurs.md](../12-political-adoption/policy-entrepreneurs.md), pre-positioning checklist) | Message discipline: they will publicly disagree with the 100% rate, the threshold, each other — that dissent *is* the credibility |
| **Instruments**: Stantcheva-style survey experiments are the difference between guessing and knowing what moves opinion [6][7] | Endorsement of a manifesto: institutions like IFS and CenTax are neutral by charter; asking is a category error |
| **Students**: PhD topics, MSc dissertations, RA projects — the twenty-year pipeline of specialists this policy will need in the 2040s | Speed: academic timescales are years; a movement needing a quote by Friday should call a think-tank instead |
| **Convening**: seminars and workshops that make the topic normal inside departments before it is normal outside them | |

### How to approach

1. **Cite their work accurately or not at all.** The fastest way to be deleted is to attribute to a scholar a position they do not hold. Every approach letter should quote their actual published words — this file's citation discipline is the template.
2. **Small concrete asks first.** "Would you review our revenue note's treatment of your elasticity estimate?" opens doors that "will you endorse our movement?" closes permanently. The ask ladder: comment on a technical note → speak at a seminar → advise on design → co-author → (rarely, and only by their initiative) public support.
3. **Offer data and questions, not slogans.** The proposal generates genuinely open research questions — endowment behavioural effects, receipts-ledger administration, threshold incidence — that are publishable in their own right. That is the currency academics trade in.
4. **Engage the sceptics first-class.** Waldenström and Kopczuk are listed here with the same respect as the allies because a proposal that has *not* been through their published objections is not ready ([../09-objections/README.md](../09-objections/README.md)). Fair characterisation of sceptics is also the movement's insurance against the charge of motivated scholarship.
5. **Protect their neutrality; it is the product.** An IFS or CenTax analysis is valuable *because* those institutions do not campaign. The movement's job is to make the policy analysable, then stand back.

### The credential-shield function

In conversation and under attack, "a Clark medallist studies exactly this," "the IFS projects inheritances will halve mobility gains," and "the French Prime Minister's own advisory council proposed taxing lifetime receipts" do defensive work no activist voice can ([messengers.md](messengers.md), academic-economists row; talking-point deployment in [../14-communications/narratives.md](../14-communications/narratives.md)). The shield only works if the underlying citations are exact — which is why this file verifies before it lists.

### The non-endorsement rule

**Listing here implies no endorsement by any named individual.** This is a map of whose published work bears on the proposal, drawn entirely from the public record; alignment labels characterise that record, not private views or willingness to engage; anything beyond the record is marked (judgement). Any public use of a scholar's name requires their explicit consent to the specific use — the disclosure-first and vetting doctrines of [messengers.md](messengers.md) apply to academic names too.
