# Organisational Forms: What to Build, in What Order

**Executive summary.** The build order is: a research programme hosted inside an existing think-tank (cheapest, fastest route to credibility), then a dedicated institute, then a legally separate campaign organisation, then local chapters — with charity-law constraints dictating the split between educational and political vehicles. Governance must be designed from day one against the two standard failure modes, founder-capture and donor-capture, and the mature ecosystem deliberately pairs a respectable insider institution with an independent radical flank.

## The build sequence

| Phase | Vehicle | What it does | Prerequisite | Approx. cost profile |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Research programme inside an existing think-tank | Verifies and publishes the corpus under a credible masthead | A host willing to adopt the programme; one funded fellow | One salary + overhead |
| 2 | Dedicated institute | Owns the agenda; convenes; briefs officials and media | Phase-1 track record; multi-year funding ([funding.md](funding.md)) | Small staff, office-optional |
| 3 | Campaign organisation | Public persuasion, lobbying, electoral-adjacent work | A demand worth campaigning for; coalition partners ([coalitions.md](coalitions.md)) | Comms + organising staff |
| 4 | Local chapters | Ring-3 advocate infrastructure ([recruitment.md](recruitment.md)) | Campaign org to charter and support them | Volunteer-led, small grants |

### Phase 1: the hosted programme

Renting credibility is an order of magnitude cheaper than building it. A programme on inheritance and universal endowments inside an established institute inherits its host's press contacts, peer-review norms, and official access on day one. Candidate hosts (UK): Resolution Foundation, IPPR, Demos, the Fairness Foundation, or a university centre such as LSE's International Inequalities Institute; (US): Roosevelt Institute, Washington Center for Equitable Growth (fit and openness to verify). Note the precedent: the concrete UK proposal closest to ours — a £10,000 citizen's inheritance — came from exactly this form, a Resolution Foundation commission output (to verify details; see [05-history/ackerman-alstott.md](../05-history/ackerman-alstott.md) and [05-history/atkinson.md](../05-history/atkinson.md) for the intellectual lineage).

Trade-off: the host's brand caps the radicalism of what can be published. A hosted programme can normalise "reform inheritance taxation and fund a citizen's endowment"; it likely cannot lead with "100%". That is acceptable in phase 1 — see the Overton sequencing in [14-communications/overton-strategies.md](../14-communications/overton-strategies.md) and stepping-stone logic in [12-political-adoption/stepping-stones.md](../12-political-adoption/stepping-stones.md).

### Phase 2: the dedicated institute

Triggered when the hosted programme hits the host's ceiling — topics it won't touch, positions it won't take, or ownership disputes over the agenda. The institute's assets are its publications, its convening power (an annual conference on inheritance and opportunity), and its availability: journalists and committees need a phone number that answers on this topic specifically ([theory-of-change.md](theory-of-change.md), stage 2).

### Phases 3–4: campaign and chapters

The campaign organisation exists because the institute legally and reputationally cannot campaign (below). Chapters exist because party adoption ([12-political-adoption/electoral-arithmetic.md](../12-political-adoption/electoral-arithmetic.md)) ultimately requires demonstrable constituency-level presence, and because volunteers retained by community outlast volunteers retained by content ([recruitment.md](recruitment.md)).

## Charity-law constraints

These constraints are load-bearing for structure; get counsel before incorporating anything (all specifics to verify with current guidance).

| Jurisdiction | Vehicle | Can do | Cannot do |
|---|---|---|---|
| UK | Registered charity (Charity Commission) | Research, education, policy analysis; some campaigning if ancillary to charitable purposes (CC9 guidance) | Political purposes as its *raison d'être*; party-political support |
| UK | Non-charitable company / unincorporated campaign | Unrestricted campaigning; regulated election spending (Electoral Commission rules) | Tax reliefs of charity status |
| US | 501(c)(3) | Research, education; insubstantial lobbying (or 501(h) election limits) | Any electoral intervention; substantial lobbying |
| US | 501(c)(4) | Unlimited lobbying; some electoral activity | Tax-deductible donations |

Implication: the mature US structure is the standard paired (c)(3)/(c)(4); the UK equivalent is charity + campaign company. The research institute and the campaign must be **separate legal entities with real governance separation** — a sham separation is itself an attack vector ([10-opposition/legal-challenges.md](../10-opposition/legal-challenges.md), [10-opposition/astroturf-and-lobbying.md](../10-opposition/astroturf-and-lobbying.md)).

## Positioning: single-issue vs broad-inequality

| | Single-issue (inheritance/endowment only) | Broad inequality org |
|---|---|---|
| Message discipline | High — one demand, repeated | Low — diluted across causes |
| Coalition breadth | Narrower but cleaner terms ([coalitions.md](coalitions.md)) | Wider but mission competes with allies |
| Attack surface | Small; hard to caricature | Inherits every left-coded position |
| Funder pool | Narrower | Wider |

**Judgement:** single-issue, firmly. The precedents ([precedents.md](precedents.md)) show concrete-demand discipline ($15 minimum wage, marriage equality) beating diffuse-cause breadth, and the policy's centre-right crossover potential ([06-palatability/README.md](../06-palatability/README.md)) survives only if the organisation is not a general progressive front. Breadth is achieved through coalition, not mission creep.

## Governance against capture

**Founder-capture** (the organisation becomes one person's vehicle and inherits their liabilities and blind spots):

- Fixed founder terms on the board; a named succession plan from year one.
- Editorial authority vested in a research director distinct from the founder.
- The corpus openly licensed, so doctrine survives any individual's exit ([theory-of-change.md](theory-of-change.md), implication 3).

**Donor-capture** (the agenda drifts toward what funders will pay for):

- Funder-diversity caps and irrevocable-gift preferences per [funding.md](funding.md).
- Published funding above a threshold; a written gift-acceptance policy including a "no editorial input" clause.
- Board seats never sold: donation and governance strictly decoupled.

## The respectability + radical flank model

Mature movements run two organisations deliberately: a credentialed insider institution officials can safely cite, and an independent, louder flank that shifts the perceived centre. Research on radical-flank effects suggests a (non-violent) radical flank can *increase* support for moderate factions by making them look reasonable (evidence mixed and context-dependent — to verify against current social-movement literature). Historical pattern: abolition, suffrage, civil rights, and climate (Greenpeace/EJF vs. green think-tanks) all exhibit the pairing.

Doctrine:

1. The institute never disowns the flank's *goal*, only distances itself from tactics as needed.
2. No shared funding, staff, or governance — the separation must be genuine, or both are discredited together ([10-opposition/astroturf-and-lobbying.md](../10-opposition/astroturf-and-lobbying.md)).
3. The flank's demand should be *this manifesto's actual position* (100% above the threshold), letting the institute's stepping-stone proposals ([12-political-adoption/stepping-stones.md](../12-political-adoption/stepping-stones.md)) occupy the reasonable middle.
4. Do not manufacture the flank. If no independent radical energy exists, that is information about movement maturity, not a gap to astroturf.
