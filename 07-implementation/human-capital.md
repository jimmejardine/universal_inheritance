# Human Capital: The Inheritance You Cannot Tax

**Executive summary.** The largest transfers many wealthy parents make are not financial: elite schooling, tutoring, curated childhoods, and the dispositions that convert into top-university places and top-decile earnings. This file is honest that a receipts tax cannot and should not reach these transfers, explains why the options for taxing them were considered and rejected, and argues that the financial tax remains decisive because it removes the compounding *capital* that converts educational advantage into dynastic wealth.

## The problem stated

Daniel Markovits's *The Meritocracy Trap* documents the modern mechanism: wealthy families no longer primarily transmit estates, they transmit *human capital* — sustained, expensive, deliberate investment in children that shows up as credentials and high labour income rather than bequests [1]. Annette Lareau's ethnographic work ("concerted cultivation") shows the transmission starts long before school fees [2]. If the proposal taxed every financial transfer at 100% above threshold while leaving this channel untouched, would dynasties simply migrate into it? Partly, yes — and that must be conceded up front rather than discovered by critics. See [`../01-problem/mobility.md`](../01-problem/mobility.md) for the mobility evidence and [`../01-problem/meritocracy-legitimacy.md`](../01-problem/meritocracy-legitimacy.md) for why credential-laundered advantage corrodes legitimacy in a distinctive way.

## Options considered and rejected

| Option | Why rejected |
|---|---|
| Tax private school / tutoring fees as taxable receipts by the child | Paternalism (taxing parents for educating children is politically and ethically toxic); valuation chaos (what is the "gift element" of a school?); timing absurdity (the ledger charge lands on a minor); pushes spending into untraceable forms (home tutoring, relocation to good catchments) |
| Tax the *donor* on educational spending above a cap | Same paternalism; trivially avoided via consumption relabelling (family "travel", "enrichment"); punishes education specifically among all luxury consumption, which is backwards |
| Impute a capitalised value of elite education into the heir's lifetime ledger | Unvaluable ex ante; the return is contingent on the child's own effort; confuses opportunity with outcome |
| Ban or cap private education (Finland-style integration) | A real policy debate, but a *different* policy — bundling it would sink the tax; see complementary-policy note in §3 |
| Tax parental time, networks, bedtime stories | Listed only to mark the reductio: no serious version exists |

The common failure across all five: educational spending is **consumption from the parent's standpoint and opportunity from the child's** — it does not hand the child an alienable asset. A receipts tax is a tax on alienable value received. The boundary drawn in [boundary-problem.md](boundary-problem.md) §2 therefore exempts education and maintenance to age 25 deliberately, not as an oversight.

## Why the financial tax still does the decisive work

The honest concession is narrower than it first appears, for three reasons.

**1. Human capital does not compound across generations by itself.** The Markovits mechanism has two stages: (i) expensive cultivation produces high labour income; (ii) high labour income is saved into capital and *transferred*, funding the next generation's cultivation plus a growing financial cushion. The receipts tax severs stage (ii) above the threshold. Each generation can still be given an excellent start, but must convert it into position through its own labour income; the fortune cannot ratchet. A dynasty of well-educated professionals who each earn their own money is not the pathology this project targets — the pathology is capital that grows while its holders sleep ([`../03-case-for/piketty-r-vs-g.md`](../03-case-for/piketty-r-vs-g.md)).

**2. Human capital is risky and non-transferable in exactly the way financial capital is not.** A trust fund pays out regardless of the heir's talent or effort; a school place does not. Taxing the unconditional channel while leaving the conditional one shifts the composition of advantage toward forms that still require the heir to perform — which is precisely the meritocratic-legitimacy improvement argued in [`../03-case-for/meritocratic-legitimacy.md`](../03-case-for/meritocratic-legitimacy.md). Advantage that must pass through a labour market is also advantage the state already taxes annually, via income tax.

**3. The endowment attacks the same gap from below.** The universal £50k/$60k at 25 ([`../02-proposal/endowment.md`](../02-proposal/endowment.md)) does not equalise childhoods, but it equalises the *capital account at adulthood's start* — the deposit, the training course, the buffer that lets a graduate take the good-but-risky job. The relevant comparison is not "perfect equality" but the status quo, where the wealthy child gets both the cultivation *and* the capital.

## What the proposal does not claim

- It does not claim to equalise opportunity. Cultivation gaps, network gaps ([nepotism-and-networks.md](nepotism-and-networks.md)), and neighbourhood gaps survive it.
- It does not claim schooling reform is unnecessary — admissions reform, school funding equalisation, and early-years investment are complementary policies argued for by the same evidence base [1][3], but they are **outside this proposal's scope** and must not be bundled into it. A tax proposal that arrives with an education-nationalisation rider hands opponents the "slippery slope" attack pre-written ([`../09-objections/slippery-slope.md`](../09-objections/slippery-slope.md)).
- It does not claim the untaxable remainder is small. It claims the remainder is *self-limiting* (must be re-earned each generation) where financial inheritance is *self-compounding* — and that taxing the compounding channel is therefore the highest-leverage single intervention available.

## The communication line

The attack will be: "the rich will just buy schools and connections instead, so you punish savers and spare the sharp-elbowed." The response, developed for use in [`../14-communications/debate-toolkit.md`](../14-communications/debate-toolkit.md): *we tax what can be handed over; what cannot be handed over must be earned, and earning is not the problem we are solving.* A tax is not obliged to solve every channel of advantage to be worth having — income tax does not capture leisure, yet nobody calls that a reason to abolish it.

## Research gaps

- Quantitative decomposition of intergenerational persistence into financial-transfer vs human-capital channels (Chetty et al. mobility data; UK cohort studies — to verify shares).
- Behavioural evidence on whether transfer taxes shift parental spending toward education (Scandinavian natural experiments, if any).
- Whether educational-spending disclosure (not taxation) above a high floor is worth the intrusion, purely for research and audit-risk purposes.
