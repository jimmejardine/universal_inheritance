# Why Some Countries Abolished Their Inheritance Taxes

**Executive summary.** Between 1972 and 2014 a cluster of wealthy democracies dismantled or hollowed out their inheritance and estate taxes, producing the most-cited empirical challenge to any renewed proposal for taxing bequests. The abolitions were driven less by a coherent ideological turn than by a recurring cocktail of negligible revenue yield, valuation and liquidity scandals (especially around family firms and farms), high-profile emigration by wealthy citizens, and the tax's political visibility being radically disproportionate to its fiscal weight [1][2][3].

## Headline findings

- In nearly every abolition episode, revenue from the inheritance or estate tax had fallen below 0.5% of total tax receipts, removing the fiscal cost of repeal as a political obstacle [1][2][4].
- Valuation asymmetries — especially between listed financial assets (marked to market) and real estate or unlisted family businesses (often assessed at stale book values) — eroded both horizontal equity and constitutional defensibility, most starkly in Austria [5].
- Highly visible tax-exile cases (Kamprad, the Rausings, Borg, Edberg in Sweden) converted an abstract tax into a narrative about capital flight and national humiliation [1][6].
- Cross-party or left-led abolition was common: Sweden's repeal passed under a Social Democratic government, and Norway's reform built on analysis originally commissioned during a centre-left coalition [1][3].
- Family-business and farm succession lobbies — often with cross-class sympathy — were decisive in Sweden, Norway, and Australia [1][3][7].
- Threshold drift onto middle-class housing wealth, unaccompanied by indexation, turned a "rich person's tax" into a tax on ordinary heirs in the Swedish and (to a lesser extent) Norwegian cases [1][4].
- Canada's 1972 reform is frequently miscatalogued as abolition; it substituted deemed-disposition capital-gains-at-death, which captures accrued gains but exempts the transferred principal [8].
- Australia's abolition was a textbook tax-competition cascade between sub-national jurisdictions, not a considered federal policy choice [7].
- Russia's 2005 repeal fits a distinct pattern of post-Soviet tax simplification under an executive aligned with concentrated wealth, rather than the OECD-style dynamics elsewhere [9].
- The OECD's 2021 review finds that in every abolishing country, administrative complexity and avoidance were invoked as justifications, though it concludes these were "often overstated" relative to yield [2].

## Country cases

### Sweden (2004)

Sweden's inheritance and gift tax was abolished unanimously by the Riksdag in December 2004, taking effect 1 January 2005, under Göran Persson's Social Democratic government [1][6]. The tax in its final form had progressive rates reaching 30% with a low spouse/child threshold (SEK 70,000 until a 2003 increase to SEK 300,000), and raised roughly 0.2–0.3% of total tax revenue in its final years [1]. Henrekson and Waldenström attribute abolition to a compound failure: nominal thresholds not tracking a thirty-year house-price boom, so heirs of ordinary Stockholm flats owed tax; punitive treatment of closely-held firms relative to listed equity; and a sequence of emigration scandals (Ingvar Kamprad's 1973 move to Switzerland, the Rausing family's relocation to the UK, tennis players Björn Borg and Stefan Edberg) that framed the tax as self-harming [1][6]. The death of Anna Lindh's husband in 2004, triggering a tax bill on the family home, reportedly cemented Social Democratic support [1].

### Norway (2014)

Norway's *arveavgift* was abolished effective 1 January 2014 by Erna Solberg's Conservative–Progress coalition, though the groundwork was laid by an expert committee under the preceding Stoltenberg government [3][4]. Rates had been 6–15% depending on kinship and amount, with a NOK 470,000 threshold per recipient. Revenue in the last full year was approximately NOK 2 billion, under 0.2% of mainland tax receipts [4]. Arguments centred on valuation distortions for unlisted family firms (which had enjoyed a 60% discount rule widely criticised as arbitrary), liquidity problems in generational farm and fishing-quota transfers, and administrative cost relative to yield [3]. Finansdepartementet's own analysis conceded the tax's weak revenue performance; opposition from Labour was muted, and no subsequent government has proposed reinstatement.

### Austria (2008)

The Austrian *Erbschafts- und Schenkungssteuer* lapsed on 1 August 2008 after the Verfassungsgerichtshof, in decisions G 54/06 (7 March 2007) on inheritance and G 23/07 on gift tax, struck down the valuation provisions as unconstitutional [5]. Real estate was assessed at *Einheitswerte* dating from 1973, producing effective tax bases a fraction of market value and violating the equality principle relative to financial assets. The Court gave parliament until 31 July 2008 to remedy the defect; the SPÖ–ÖVP grand coalition allowed the deadline to pass. Revenue had been roughly €150 million, under 0.2% of federal tax receipts [2][5]. The episode illustrates how unreformed cadastral valuations can destroy a tax by judicial route, without any organised repeal campaign.

### Canada (1972)

Canada is frequently but inaccurately listed as having "abolished" inheritance tax. Following the 1966 Carter Commission report, the 1971 tax reform (effective 1 January 1972) repealed the federal Estate Tax Act (1958) and replaced it with deemed disposition of capital property at death, taxing accrued but unrealised gains as income to the deceased [8]. Provincial succession duties were then phased out through the 1970s, with Quebec last in 1985. The substantive shift was from taxing the transferred stock of wealth to taxing the embedded capital gain; principal-residence exemption, spousal rollovers, and the basis step-up for heirs substantially narrow the base relative to a classical estate tax [8]. Revenue comparisons across the regimes are therefore not apples-to-apples.

### Australia (1979)

Australia's abolition was a sub-national tax-competition cascade rather than a deliberative federal reform. Queensland premier Joh Bjelke-Petersen abolished state death duties effective 1 January 1977, explicitly courting elderly interstate migration; within two years every other state had followed, and the Fraser Coalition government abolished the federal estate duty effective 1 July 1979 [7]. Federal revenue had been modest — around 1% of Commonwealth receipts at its 1940s peak, falling below 0.5% by the mid-1970s [7] (exact late-1970s shares vary across sources and should be confirmed against Australian Treasury historical tables). Subsequent work by the Productivity Commission and academic historians treats the episode as a canonical demonstration of the "race to the bottom" problem when bequest taxation is assigned to competing sub-national jurisdictions.

### Russia (2005)

Russia's inheritance and gift tax was repealed by Federal Law No. 78-FZ of 1 July 2005, signed by President Putin and effective 1 January 2006 [9]. The pre-repeal regime levied progressive rates up to 40% on estates above a low multiple of the minimum wage, but compliance was weak and yield negligible — reliable figures are difficult to isolate in Federal Tax Service data, and the precise pre-abolition share of revenue is not well documented in English-language sources. Repeal was presented domestically as simplification benefiting ordinary families, consistent with the 2001 flat-tax reform's rhetorical template. In substance it fits the broader pattern of Putin-era tax policy leaving concentrated post-privatisation wealth largely untouched by transfer taxation [9]. This case is less comparable to the OECD cluster and should be weighted accordingly in cross-country inference.

## The common failure pattern — how these conditions came to exist

The abolitions were not, at root, verdicts on the *principle* of taxing inheritance. They were verdicts on a recurring family of *design* errors that made the tax simultaneously easy for the wealthy to escape and hard for the middle class to avoid. Eight errors recur across the cases above and should be treated as the inheritance-tax equivalent of anti-patterns.

1. **Un-indexed nominal thresholds.** The Swedish threshold stood at SEK 70,000 for decades while Stockholm house prices multiplied; by the 2000s the tax hit heirs of ordinary flats. The same drift is visible in the pre-2014 Norwegian regime. A threshold fixed in nominal terms is a time-bomb: house-price and wage inflation will eventually turn any "tax on the rich" into a tax on homeowners' children.
2. **Estate-based rather than recipient-based.** Every abolished tax fell on the estate (the deceased's last act) rather than the heir. This is what permits the "death tax" framing. A recipient-based tax, which scales with what an individual *receives* rather than what the decedent *leaves*, is harder to attack rhetorically and rewards wide distribution.
3. **Ad-hoc family-business and farm rules.** Norway's 60% valuation discount and Austria's 1973-vintage *Einheitswerte* were both attempts to soften the liquidity problem for illiquid productive assets. Both produced arbitrary, avoidance-prone, and constitutionally fragile regimes. The absence of a *principled* mechanism (deferred payment at statutory interest; state equity stake; long-horizon operating-asset allowance) was fatal.
4. **Weak anti-avoidance spine, mobile wealth.** The truly rich could emigrate (Kamprad, Rausing), restructure into trusts, or give during life. The merely comfortable could not afford such advice and paid the nominal rate. Any tax whose incidence is inversely correlated with wealth above a threshold will eventually lose its legitimacy.
5. **Sub-national assignment with exit options.** Australia's cascade is the canonical case. Any tax assigned to jurisdictions that compete for residents will be abolished the moment one jurisdiction defects. Bequest taxation must sit at the national level at minimum; ideally it is internationally coordinated (the 2021 OECD global-minimum-tax agreement is the template).
6. **Unhypothecated revenue; asymmetric political economy.** Abolished taxes fed general revenue, producing a political equilibrium in which the cost was visible (grieving families, family-farm auctions) and the benefit was abstract (a line item in the treasury). Without a visible, individual beneficiary, the burden/benefit asymmetry is a standing vulnerability.
7. **Fragile valuation regimes.** Austria lost its tax entirely because valuation rules were frozen in 1973 and violated the constitutional equality principle. Any inheritance-tax design that relies on stale cadastral values, book values, or jurisdiction-specific assessment practices is one successful legal challenge away from collapse.
8. **Fiscal triviality as permission structure.** In every OECD case the tax raised under 0.5% of receipts before abolition. Government could afford to let it go. Designs that shrink over time — via eroding thresholds, expanding reliefs, and rising avoidance — eventually reach the point where abolition is politically cheap.

Two meta-points bind these errors together. *First*, most were not introduced at enactment — they developed over decades of piecemeal amendment, exemption-layering, and nominal drift. The taxes died by erosion, not by attack. *Second*, the erosion was a predictable consequence of the original political economy: dynastic wealth had the motive, patience, and resources to lobby for reliefs, while diffuse beneficiaries (the population at large) did not. Any successor regime must anticipate the same forces and build in automatic defences.

## How future policy can rescue this

The design specified in [02-proposal/](../02-proposal/README.md) is explicitly drawn to avoid the eight errors above. Mapping each:

| Historical error | Design response in this project's proposal |
|---|---|
| Un-indexed thresholds | Automatic annual indexation to wage growth or national median house price, not CPI |
| Estate-based framing | Recipient-based, lifetime-cumulative tax on what each individual receives |
| Ad-hoc business/farm rules | Principled deferral: heirs of productive businesses pay over ≥25 years at statutory interest, with a state equity option for illiquid cases |
| Weak anti-avoidance | Worldwide taxation on transfers to residents; exit tax on emigration; trust look-through; inter-vivos gifts count against the lifetime threshold |
| Sub-national competition | National-level only; pursue an OECD-style multilateral floor analogous to the 2021 global minimum tax |
| Unhypothecated revenue | Proceeds fund a visible universal endowment at ~25, making the benefit individual and concrete |
| Fragile valuation | Professional appraisal with rebuttable presumption for real estate; mark-to-market for listed assets; clear statutory treatment of IP and private equity; no reliance on historical cadastral values |
| Fiscal triviality | Broad base and high rate above threshold make the tax materially distributive even as it remains distributional-primary rather than revenue-primary |

Beyond these technical fixes, two political design principles follow from the historical record.

**First, build in automatic defences against erosion.** The abolitions document a four-decade pattern of lobbying for reliefs and carve-outs that hollowed out each tax. A credible future regime must index thresholds automatically, require affirmative legislative action to add new reliefs (with sunset clauses), and publish annual effective-rate reports broken down by estate size so erosion is visible before it becomes fatal.

**Second, pair the tax indivisibly with the universal endowment.** The single consistent feature of the abolished regimes was that they had no visible individual beneficiary. The universal endowment converts the political-economy asymmetry: abolition now means taking away a concrete £50,000 (or whatever the figure) from every twenty-something, not merely stopping an abstract Treasury receipt. This is not cosmetic; it is the structural defence against the Swedish/Norwegian/Australian pattern repeating.

The abolition record is the strongest empirical argument against naive inheritance-tax revival. It is correspondingly the strongest *design* argument for a recipient-based, indexed, hypothecated regime with a robust anti-avoidance spine — a design nowhere yet fully implemented.
