# The Family Business and Farm Objection

**Executive summary.** The strongest objection to any confiscatory inheritance tax is that it forces the sale of illiquid productive assets — farms, workshops, mid-sized firms — and thereby hands them to private equity and corporate consolidators, achieving the exact opposite of the policy's anti-concentration aim. This file states that case at full strength, including the German *Mittelstand* stewardship argument and the documented failure of carve-outs on both sides of the Atlantic, before assessing what the empirical succession literature and the proposal's deferral/state-equity design actually leave standing.

## The objection at full strength

### 1. Illiquidity and forced sale

A working farm or operating business worth £20m may generate £400k of annual free cash flow and hold almost no liquid reserves. A tax bill of many millions, due on receipt, cannot be paid from operations. The heir's options are: sell the whole business, sell a controlling stake, or leverage the balance sheet to destruction. Every one of these outcomes destroys precisely the thing the estate embodied — a going concern with embedded local employment, supplier relationships, and tacit knowledge. The tax is levied on paper wealth but collected from real operations. No other asset class presents this mismatch so acutely: shares can be sold in slices; a dairy herd, a foundry, or a family engineering firm cannot.

### 2. The consolidation perversity

Who buys a distress-sold family firm? Not another family — they face the same tax on their own eventual succession. The natural buyers are private-equity funds, listed corporates, and institutional aggregators: entities that never die and therefore never pay the tax. A 100% inheritance tax on family capital, with no answer to the liquidity problem, operates as a *subsidy to immortal capital*. Farmland consolidates into institutional portfolios; the *Mittelstand* firm becomes a bolt-on acquisition; the high street veterinary practice joins a roll-up. The policy that set out to break dynastic concentration instead accelerates the concentration of the corporate form — arguably a worse form, since a dynasty at least has a local name and reputational skin in the game. This is not hypothetical: critics of the UK's 2024 Agricultural Property Relief reform made exactly this argument, and the televised farm-auction imagery it produced is the standard opening move against any death duty (to verify specific coverage claims).

### 3. The stewardship case for family ownership

The strongest positive version of the objection comes from the German-speaking world. The *Mittelstand* literature — and the "patient capital" strand of comparative capitalism research (Hall & Soskice's *Varieties of Capitalism*; work on family-firm investment horizons) — argues that family ownership is not merely tolerable but *superior* for a class of firms:

| Claimed advantage | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| Long investment horizons | Owners answer to grandchildren, not quarterly earnings calls |
| Employment stability | Documented lower layoff propensity in downturns among family firms (to verify) |
| Regional rootedness | Headquarters and reinvestment stay in the founding town |
| Reputational capital | The family name on the gate disciplines quality and conduct |
| Resistance to short-termist finance | No exit-seeking shareholders forcing sale or leverage |

On this account, intergenerational transmission is not a bug of family capitalism but its engine: the *expectation* of handing over is what motivates the patient behaviour. Sever the chain and you convert every family firm into a one-generation venture with an implicit exit date — managed, rationally, for extraction rather than stewardship.

### 4. Reliance interests

Existing owners made decades of decisions — reinvesting rather than distributing, declining acquisition offers, training a successor child — under a legal regime that promised transmissibility (UK Business Property Relief has offered 100% relief on unquoted trading businesses since 1992). A confiscatory tax imposed on those settled expectations is retroactive in substance even if prospective in form. This is a genuine rule-of-law concern, not special pleading; it is the reason [`../15-transition/grandfathering.md`](../15-transition/grandfathering.md) exists.

### 5. Carve-outs fail in both directions

The obvious answer — exempt operating businesses — has been tried, and the record is that relief regimes are gamed in *both* directions:

| Regime | Failure direction | What happened |
|---|---|---|
| UK BPR | Too generous | AIM-share portfolios and recharacterised property companies marketed explicitly as IHT shelters; relief flowed to passive wealth, not working firms [4] |
| UK APR | Too generous | Farmland prices inflated by non-farming purchasers buying tax shelter; genuine farmers priced out of expansion [5] |
| US QFOBI (IRC §2057) | Too narrow | So hedged with tests that only ~415 estates claimed it in its peak year; heavily litigated; repealed 2004 [6] |
| German business relief | Too generous | Struck down by the Constitutional Court in 2014 as unconstitutionally favourable to large enterprises [7] |

The steelman's conclusion: there is no stable middle. A relief tight enough to exclude shelters excludes real businesses; a relief wide enough to protect real businesses becomes the avoidance route that guts the tax ([`../02-proposal/avoidance.md`](../02-proposal/avoidance.md) documents this as the deepest cause of prior abolitions). Either way, the family-business boundary is where the policy dies.

## What the evidence actually shows

The objection assumes that heir succession is worth protecting on efficiency grounds. The empirical literature is strikingly hostile to that assumption:

- **Pérez-González (2006, *AER*)** finds that US firms passing the CEO role to a family heir underperform those hiring outside managers, with the deficit concentrated in heirs who attended less selective colleges (to verify magnitudes).
- **Bennedsen, Nielsen, Pérez-González & Wolfenzon (2007, *QJE*)**, using the gender of the first-born child as an instrument in Danish data, find family-CEO successions cause substantial declines in firm profitability relative to unrelated-CEO successions (to verify magnitudes).
- **Bloom & Van Reenen's** World Management Survey work consistently finds primogeniture-managed family firms score among the worst on management practices across countries — family *ownership* with professional management scores far better [10].
- The patient-capital advantages in the table above attach mainly to family *ownership and values*, not to family *management by birthright*; the literatures are routinely conflated by lobby groups.

Two honest qualifications. First, this evidence concerns heir *management*, whereas the tax bites heir *ownership*; a policy defence built on the management literature must explain why passive heir ownership is also objectionable (the answer is in [`../01-problem/rentier-economy.md`](../01-problem/rentier-economy.md) and [`../03-case-for/meritocratic-legitimacy.md`](../03-case-for/meritocratic-legitimacy.md), not in these studies). Second, forced-sale horror stories under *existing* estate taxes are rarer than the rhetoric implies — US Treasury and CBO analyses repeatedly found very few farm estates with genuine liquidity crises (to verify) — but existing taxes have rates of 40% with wide reliefs; a 100% rate cannot borrow that comfort without argument.

## The proposal's answer, and what it costs

The design response ([`../02-proposal/carveouts.md`](../02-proposal/carveouts.md), §5–6) refuses the exemption route entirely: tax crystallises in full, but *payment* is deferred up to 30 years at gilts + 1%, secured on business equity, with an optional non-voting state preferred-equity stake and a 20-year claw-back. The business is never forced to sale; the tax is never forgiven. This dissolves the both-directions gaming problem — there is no relief to shelter into — and answers the liquidity mismatch directly. Hard boundary cases (the heir who half-works in the firm; the farm that is 60% development land) are worked through in [`../07-implementation/hard-cases.md`](../07-implementation/hard-cases.md) and [`../07-implementation/boundary-problem.md`](../07-implementation/boundary-problem.md).

The honest accounting of what this answer costs:

| Cost | Nature |
|---|---|
| State as reluctant shareholder | The Treasury accumulates minority preferred stakes in thousands of private firms it has no wish to hold, must value periodically, and must eventually exit without market disruption — a standing Hayekian knowledge problem ([`state-failure.md`](state-failure.md)) |
| 30-year administration | Each deferral is a three-decade credit relationship: monitoring, re-certification every 5 years, claw-back litigation. Administrative capacity is a real constraint ([`../07-implementation/administration.md`](../07-implementation/administration.md)) |
| Leverage drag | A firm carrying a secured tax charge invests and borrows less freely than an unencumbered one; the stewardship advantages above are attenuated even where the firm survives |
| Succession-incentive loss | The heir who must ultimately pay full value has weaker reasons to take over at all; more firms will be sold *voluntarily* at the first generation — which the proposal must own as an intended consequence, not deny |

## Verdict

What survives of the objection: the liquidity mismatch is real and the carve-out history is a genuine graveyard — any version of this policy without the deferral architecture deserves to fail, and would. The consolidation perversity is the objection's sharpest point and is only *partly* answered: deferral prevents forced sales, but the tax still tilts long-run ownership away from families toward institutions, and the proposal accepts that tilt deliberately, on the evidence that birthright management underperforms and on the anti-dynastic grounds of the whole project. What does not survive: the claim that family transmission is efficiency-enhancing in general (the succession literature points the other way), and the claim that no administrable middle exists (deferral-with-security is administrable — Ireland and the UK instalment options are working precedents [8] — though not cheap). Residual standing: a country adopting this policy will have visibly fewer century-old family firms in 2075. The proposal's position is that it will also have visibly more new ones; that claim is assessed in [`../16-second-order/heirs-and-work.md`](../16-second-order/heirs-and-work.md) and [`../03-case-for/economic-efficiency.md`](../03-case-for/economic-efficiency.md).
