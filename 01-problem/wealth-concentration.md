# Wealth Concentration

**Executive summary.** Wealth concentration in the United States, United Kingdom, and continental Europe has risen substantially since the late 1970s, with the gains accruing disproportionately to the top 1% and, above all, to the top 0.1%. Inherited wealth now accounts for roughly half of large fortunes and a rising share of aggregate household wealth, a trend most national wealth-transfer taxes have not arrested.

## Key findings

- In the United States, the top 1% share of household net worth rose from roughly 22–25% in the late 1970s to about 30–31% by 2024 on the Federal Reserve's Distributional Financial Accounts (DFA), while the top 0.1% share roughly doubled over the same period [1][2].
- The top 10% US wealth share sits near 67–71% depending on series (DFA vs. SCF vs. WID capitalization estimates), versus roughly 63–65% in 1989 [1][2][3].
- In the UK, the ONS Wealth and Assets Survey puts the top 10% share of total household wealth at about 57% (April 2018–March 2020 wave), with the top 1% holding around 23% — lower than the US but rising since the series began in 2006–08 [4].
- Across the euro area, the ECB Household Finance and Consumption Survey (2021 wave) reports a top 10% wealth share of roughly 56%, with Germany (59–63%) and Austria notably more concentrated than France, Italy, or Spain [5].
- World Inequality Database series constructed by Piketty, Saez, Zucman and Chancel show a clear post-1980 U-shape in top shares for the US and UK; continental Europe shows a milder but directionally similar rise [3][6].
- Among the Forbes 400, the share classifying as fully "self-made" (category 10 on Forbes' own 1–10 scale) has trended down in recent years; UBS's 2023 *Billionaire Ambitions Report* found that, for the first time since tracking began, heirs inherited more wealth ($150.8bn) than the year's new self-made billionaires created ($140.7bn) [7][8].
- Inter-vivos gifts are a large and growing share of the transfer flow: the IFS estimates inheritances received by those born in the 1980s will be roughly twice (as a share of lifetime income) those received by the 1960s cohort, and UK gifts currently run on the order of a quarter to a third of the bequest flow, though HMRC data on lifetime gifts is acknowledged to be incomplete [9][10].
- The very top is pulling away from the merely rich: Zucman's 2026 synthesis of the new international research puts real growth of the wealth of the global top 0.0001% (~3,000 households — the world's dollar billionaires) at **7.5% a year since 1987**, against 2.5% for global wealth per adult and 1.4% for global income per adult; on his long-run US series, the wealth of the top 0.00001% (the 19 richest households today; the 4 richest in 1913) relative to national income has returned to its Gilded Age range [21].
- The same research documents that tax systems turn *regressive* at the very top: across France, the Netherlands, Italy, the US, Sweden and Norway, billionaires' average personal tax rates fall below every other group's as a share of pre-tax income — equivalent to roughly 0–0.5% of their wealth a year, even in the countries that ran annual wealth taxes [21][22]. The policy response this finding has generated — Zucman's 2% minimum-tax floor — is engaged directly in [`../03-case-for/wealth-tax-comparison.md`](../03-case-for/wealth-tax-comparison.md) §5.
- Evidence that abolishing estate/inheritance taxes directly raised top wealth shares is suggestive but not clean: Sweden (abolished 2004) and Norway (abolished 2014) both saw top-1% shares continue rising post-repeal, but contemporaneous asset-price effects and capital-income changes make causal attribution contested [11][12].

## Detailed analysis

Top-end wealth shares differ across sources because of methodology (survey vs. tax-capitalization vs. estate multiplier) and unit of observation (adult vs. household). The table below uses the most recent official release from each jurisdiction.

| Jurisdiction | Source (year) | Top 10% | Top 1% | Top 0.1% |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| United States | Fed DFA (Q4 2024) | ~67% | ~30% | ~14% [1] |
| United States | SCF (2022) | ~71% | ~30% | n/a (suppressed) [2] |
| United States | WID (2022) | ~71% | ~35% | ~19% [3] |
| United Kingdom | ONS WAS (2018–20) | ~57% | ~23% | n/a [4] |
| United Kingdom | WID (2022) | ~57% | ~21% | ~9% [3] |
| Euro area | ECB HFCS (2021) | ~56% | ~18% | n/a [5] |
| France | WID (2022) | ~59% | ~27% | ~11% [3] |
| Germany | WID (2022) | ~63% | ~30% | ~14% [3] |

Three caveats apply. First, survey-based series (SCF, WAS, HFCS) under-capture the very top because billionaires are rarely sampled and may under-report; tax-capitalized series (Saez-Zucman; WID) generally report higher top shares. Second, the top 0.1% figures in WID for Europe are extrapolated from Forbes-type rich-lists above a threshold and carry wider error bars. Third, UK and euro-area series are shorter, so post-1980 comparisons rely on WID's historical reconstructions rather than continuous official data.

**Inherited vs. self-made at the top.** Forbes has published its 1–10 "self-made score" for the Forbes 400 annually since 2014; the median score has drifted down, and the share of list members who inherited the bulk of their fortune has risen from roughly a quarter in the mid-2010s toward a third more recently, though Forbes itself cautions the scoring is judgement-based [7]. UBS's *Billionaire Ambitions Report 2023* documented the first inheritance-dominant year in its nine-year series and projected roughly $5.2 trillion in billionaire wealth transfer over the next 20–30 years [8]. Academic work by Alvaredo, Garbinti, and Piketty shows the inherited share of aggregate wealth in France rising from about 45% in the 1970s to roughly 60% by the 2010s [13].

**Transfer flows.** The IFS's work on UK inheritance projects that for cohorts born in the 1980s, inheritances will equal about 14% of lifetime non-inheritance income at the median, compared with roughly 8% for the 1960s cohort [9]. The annual UK flow of inheritances has roughly doubled relative to national income — Atkinson's long-run series puts it at 4.8% of national income in 1977, rising to 8.2% by 2006 [14] — and Resolution Foundation finds inheritances doubled again in nominal terms over the two decades to 2015–16, passing £100bn, with a further doubling projected over the following two decades [20]. In the US, the SCF's transfer module and Federal Reserve analyses indicate that only about 20–25% of households report ever receiving an inheritance or substantial gift, but conditional amounts are highly skewed toward the top wealth decile [15].

**The billionaire stratum specifically.** The growth-rate differential is the *r > g* dynamic at its purest: a stock compounding at 7.5% a year real against income growing at 1.4% doubles its relative weight roughly every twelve years, and — taxed at 0–0.5% of wealth annually [21][22] — faces nothing between itself and hereditary permanence except transfer taxation. This ~3,000-household stratum is precisely the population whose transmission the proposal caps; the diagnosis is shared with Zucman's billionaire-minimum-tax campaign, though the instruments differ ([`../03-case-for/wealth-tax-comparison.md`](../03-case-for/wealth-tax-comparison.md) §5).

**Did weakening inheritance taxes matter?** Sweden abolished its inheritance tax in 2004 and its wealth tax in 2007; Norway abolished inheritance tax in 2014; Austria abolished it in 2008. In each, top-1% wealth shares continued rising, but so did equity valuations, and no difference-in-differences study has convincingly isolated the repeal effect from global capital-market trends [11][12]. The honest reading is that cross-country evidence is consistent with, but does not prove, a concentrating effect.

**Does wealth dissipate by itself? The persistence evidence.** The folk theory — "shirtsleeves to shirtsleeves in three generations" — holds that dynasties self-destruct through division, incompetence, and spending, making transfer taxation redundant. The long-run evidence contradicts it. Clark's surname studies across England, Sweden, the US, China, and Japan find intergenerational status persistence of roughly 0.7–0.8 once measured at the surname-group level — implying reversion to the mean over ten to fifteen generations, not three [16]. Clark & Cummins, tracking rare English surnames through probate records 1858–2012, find wealth persistence across at least five generations with no sign of accelerated decay at the top [17]. Barone & Mocetti link Florentine tax records across six centuries: the surnames atop the 1427 catasto remain significantly over-represented among the city's top earners in 2011 [18]. Three structural points complete the answer: the *aggregate* inherited share can rise even while individual families churn (and is rising — see above); the trust and family-office industry exists precisely to engineer against dissipation, with perpetual "dynasty trusts" now available in the US states that have abolished the rule against perpetuities as applied to trust interests — Sitkoff & Schanzenbach's empirical study found abolition raised a state's reported trust assets by roughly $6bn (about 20%) on average through 2003 [19]; and the visible mid-century dynastic decay the proverb feeds on occurred under 70–80% estate taxation and wartime capital destruction — evidence of policy working, not of nature self-correcting. The debate-facing version of this argument is [`../09-objections/self-correcting.md`](../09-objections/self-correcting.md).
