# 01 — The Problem: Ailments of Late-Stage Capitalism

**Executive summary.** Six overlapping pathologies — runaway wealth concentration, declining social mobility, a generational housing crisis, political capture by concentrated wealth, the erosion of meritocratic legitimacy, and the rise of a rentier economy — share a common structural root: the compounding, tax-thin transmission of capital stocks across generations. Six deepening chapters extend the diagnosis in time (the coming transfer wave), in mechanism (declining dynamism, the asset-price feedback loop), in consequence (elite overproduction, social fatalism), and in scholarship (the wealth-and-democratic-stability literature survey); together the twelve files constitute the empirical case that inheritance, not income, is the highest-leverage point of intervention.

## The six ailments

| # | Chapter | Headline finding |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | [Wealth concentration](wealth-concentration.md) | Top 1% US wealth share ~30%, up from ~22–25% in late 1970s; UBS reports 2023 was the first year heirs inherited more billionaire wealth than new founders created |
| 2 | [Social mobility](mobility.md) | US/UK income elasticity ≈ 0.47–0.50 vs Denmark ≈ 0.15; wealth elasticity is roughly double the income elasticity everywhere measured |
| 3 | [Housing and the generational gap](housing.md) | US median first-time buyer age rose from 28 (1991) to 38 (2024); UK home-ownership among 25–34s fell from ~51% (late 1980s) to ~28% (mid-2010s) |
| 4 | [Political capture by concentrated wealth](political-capture.md) | Gilens & Page: median-voter preferences have near-zero independent effect on US policy once elite preferences are controlled for |
| 5 | [Meritocratic legitimacy](meritocracy-legitimacy.md) | US trust in government: ~73% (1958) → mid-teens (2020s); belief "hard work is rewarded" moving toward luck/connections pole |
| 6 | [The rentier economy](rentier-economy.md) | US labour share ~64% (1970) → ~57% (2019); Rognlie's decomposition attributes nearly all the gain to housing |

## The argument that threads them

A conventional framing treats these as six separate problems demanding six separate policies — wealth taxes for concentration, education for mobility, supply reform for housing, campaign-finance reform for capture, and so on. The framing this project advances is different: these are six surface expressions of a single underlying dynamic, in which returns increasingly accrue to ownership of existing assets, those assets transmit intact across generations, and the transmission is largely untaxed.

Four structural observations tie the chapters together:

1. **Piketty's *r > g*.** When the return on capital exceeds growth, the wealth distribution does not self-correct from income flows; only transfer taxation does. See [wealth-concentration.md](wealth-concentration.md).
2. **Wealth is more heritable than income.** Parent–child wealth elasticities are roughly double parent–child income elasticities in every country with microdata. This means income-focused mobility policies systematically under-shoot. See [mobility.md](mobility.md).
3. **The rentier drift concentrates the heritability.** Returns to patents, rental property, and monopoly positions are owner-skill-independent and therefore transmit without decay — unlike a surgeon's practice or a founder-led business. See [rentier-economy.md](rentier-economy.md).
4. **Concentrated wealth buys the rules that preserve it.** The political-capture literature suggests this is not a stable equilibrium but a reinforcing one: wealth concentration funds the political action that protects it from redistribution. See [political-capture.md](political-capture.md).

The deepening chapters add two further threads: the dynamic is **accelerating** — the largest wealth transfer in history crests over 2030–2050, hardening today's income inequality into permanent inherited stratification ([great-wealth-transfer.md](great-wealth-transfer.md), [asset-price-feedback.md](asset-price-feedback.md)) — and it is **corrosive beyond distribution**, degrading business formation ([declining-dynamism.md](declining-dynamism.md)), political stability ([elite-overproduction.md](elite-overproduction.md)), and the belief that effort pays at all ([social-trust-and-fatalism.md](social-trust-and-fatalism.md)).

## Why inheritance is the leverage point

Given the above, inheritance is the *specific moment* at which dynastic concentration is reproduced. Every other policy lever — income tax, wealth tax, property tax, antitrust — either taxes stocks continuously (politically fragile, high avoidance) or taxes flows (does not scale with the stock transmitted). A recipient-based inheritance tax scales with exactly the quantity this section has identified as problematic, and imposes its deadweight loss on a party (the deceased) incapable of behavioural distortion.

This is why every serious historical proposal — Paine, Mill, Carnegie, Roosevelt, Atkinson, Ackerman, Piketty — converges on inheritance as the point of intervention. The empirical case in this section is the warrant for that convergence.

## Outstanding research gaps

- A UK-equivalent to the Bhutta et al. (2022) Fed decomposition of top-decile wealth into inherited vs. self-accumulated.
- Cross-country evidence on whether inheritance-tax weakening correlated with top-share increases, controlling for asset-price cycles.
- Quantitative micro-evidence on the inheritance-to-homeownership transmission channel (Resolution Foundation has the best existing work but it is UK-only).
- A unified decomposition of the post-1980 labour-share decline by rent source, following Rognlie but extended to monopoly and IP rents.
