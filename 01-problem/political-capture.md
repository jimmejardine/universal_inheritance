# Political Capture by Concentrated Wealth

**Executive summary.** Across multiple democracies, a small cohort of wealthy individuals and organised interests exerts influence on policy outcomes that is disproportionate to their numbers, operating through campaign finance, lobbying, media ownership, and revolving-door networks. The empirical record — while contested on magnitude and mechanism — is consistent enough that mainstream institutions (Electoral Commission, OpenSecrets, Reuters Institute, Transparency International) now treat concentrated political money as a first-order governance risk [1][2][3][4].

## Key findings

- In a dataset of 1,779 US policy questions (1981–2002), Gilens and Page found that the preferences of economic elites and organised business groups had substantial, statistically significant effects on policy, while the preferences of the median voter had a near-zero independent effect once elite preferences were controlled for [1]. The authors describe their results as consistent with "Economic-Elite Domination" and "Biased Pluralism," not with majoritarian democracy.
- US federal election spending has become markedly more concentrated at the top since the Supreme Court's 2010 *Citizens United* decision and the subsequent *SpeechNow.org v. FEC* ruling, which together enabled unlimited independent expenditures and the rise of Super PACs. OpenSecrets data show outside spending rising from roughly hundreds of millions per cycle pre-2010 to several billion dollars by 2020, with a disproportionate share traceable to a few hundred mega-donors [2][5].
- The Brennan Center has documented that in recent federal cycles, the top 100 donors to Super PACs have, in some cycles, contributed more than the combined small-dollar donations of millions of ordinary Americans — the exact ratio varies by cycle and should be checked against the most recent Brennan publication [5].
- UK political donations show similar (though smaller-scale) concentration. Electoral Commission registers and Transparency International UK analyses have repeatedly found that a double-digit share of major-party funding in recent cycles comes from a small number of high-net-worth individuals, property developers, and hedge-fund principals [3][6].
- US lobbying expenditure reported under the Lobbying Disclosure Act has hovered around $3.5–4.3 billion per year in the last decade per OpenSecrets, with finance, pharmaceuticals, and tech consistently among the top spenders; the true figure is widely considered an undercount because of "shadow lobbying" outside disclosure thresholds [2][7].
- Revolving-door movement between Congress, federal agencies, and K Street is extensively documented by OpenSecrets' revolving-door database and by Transparency International's integrity work; estimates that a majority of retiring members of Congress subsequently register as lobbyists or take lobbying-adjacent roles are widely cited, though precise shares vary by methodology [2][7].
- Media ownership in most mature democracies is concentrated. The Reuters Institute's annual *Digital News Report* and UK regulator Ofcom have documented high concentration in UK print and broadcast reach, and the Media Reform Coalition has repeatedly found that a handful of companies control a majority of UK national newspaper circulation and a large share of online news reach [4][8][9]. The US picture is similar for local TV (Sinclair, Nexstar) and increasingly for digital distribution.
- Multiple peer-reviewed studies — Bartels' *Unequal Democracy*, Gilens' *Affluence and Influence*, and Hacker and Pierson's *Winner-Take-All Politics* — find that where the preferences of high-income Americans diverge from those of the median voter, enacted policy tracks the high-income preferences substantially more closely [1][10][11].
- Dynastic and founder-wealth political networks have left a traceable footprint: the Koch-affiliated Americans for Prosperity network (documented by Theda Skocpol and colleagues), Mercer family backing of Cambridge Analytica-era Republican campaigns, and large individual outlays by Peter Thiel and Elon Musk in the 2022 and 2024 US cycles are all verifiable via FEC filings and OpenSecrets [2][12]. In the UK, hedge-fund principals including Michael Hintze and Crispin Odey (historically) have been among the largest single donors to the Conservative Party per Electoral Commission returns [3][6].

## Detailed analysis

The strongest single piece of academic evidence is Gilens and Page (2014). Their contribution is methodological: by regressing policy outcomes on the preferences of different income groups and interest-group alignments simultaneously, they isolate independent effects. The headline — that median-voter preferences have no statistically distinguishable independent effect once elite preferences are controlled for — has been challenged (notably by Enns 2015 and Branham, Soroka, and Wlezien 2017, who argue elite and median preferences correlate more than the original paper implies). The robust residual finding, even under critics' reanalyses, is that elite influence is at minimum co-equal with, and often exceeds, median influence [1][10].

On money: the post-*Citizens United* environment has not so much created concentration as removed the ceiling on it. OpenSecrets' longitudinal outside-spending series shows a step-change after 2010 [2]. The mechanism is not straightforward vote-buying but rather agenda-setting, candidate-selection (primary challenges funded by aligned Super PACs), and the structuring of post-office career incentives via the revolving door [7][11].

In the UK, the regulated cap on spending and the ban on paid TV political advertising limits some US-style dynamics, but Electoral Commission data show donation concentration has risen, particularly via unincorporated associations and through large single gifts to leadership campaigns [3][6]. Transparency International UK has flagged loopholes around shell-company donations and the "VIP lane" procurement patterns during COVID-19 as evidence of access-based influence [6].

Media is the third vector. Ofcom's news-consumption surveys and the Media Reform Coalition's ownership reports document that a small number of proprietors — historically Murdoch, Rothermere, and the Barclay family in the UK — reach a majority of the adult news-consuming public [8][9]. The Reuters Institute *Digital News Report* adds the cross-national comparator and shows the UK is an outlier in concentration among Western democracies [4]. Ownership effects on coverage are harder to prove causally but are documented in work by Martin and Yurukoglu (2017) on Fox News and in various content analyses of UK tabloid endorsements.

Taken together, the evidence does not require conspiracy. It describes a structural equilibrium in which concentrated wealth buys disproportionate access, agenda-setting, and narrative reach — and in which policy outputs, measured over decades, track elite preferences more tightly than median ones.
