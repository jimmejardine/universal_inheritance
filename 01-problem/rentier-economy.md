# The Rentier Economy

**Executive summary.** Since around 1980, a rising share of income in advanced economies has accrued to owners of existing assets — land, housing, intellectual property, financial claims, and dominant market positions — rather than to labour or to new productive investment. This "rentier drift" is empirically well-documented if contested at the margin, and it is highly consequential for inheritance policy because rents, unlike entrepreneurial returns, are detachable from the skill of the owner and thus pass intact across generations.

## Key findings

- The global labour share of income fell roughly 5 percentage points between 1980 and the late 2010s, with the US nonfarm business labour share dropping from about 64% in 1970 to roughly 57% by 2016 [1][2]. The ILO estimates a decline of about 4 percentage points across G20 advanced economies from 2004–2017 [3].
- Karabarbounis & Neiman (2014) documented the decline in 42 of 59 countries studied and attributed much of it to the falling relative price of investment goods, though subsequent work has challenged both the magnitude and the mechanism [1].
- Matthew Rognlie (2015) showed that virtually *all* of the net rise in the capital share identified by Piketty is attributable to **housing**; excluding housing, the non-housing capital share is roughly flat since 1948 [4]. This is the most important caveat: the story is less "capital beats labour" and more "landowners and asset-holders beat both" [4].
- De Loecker, Eeckhout & Unger (2020) estimate US average markups rose from about 21% above marginal cost in 1980 to roughly 61% by 2016, with the rise concentrated in the upper tail — implying a substantial reallocation from labour and consumer surplus into monopoly rents [5].
- Brett Christophers identifies seven overlapping rentier sectors in the UK — financial, natural-resource, land, IP, platform, infrastructure, and contract/outsourcing rents — and argues they collectively dominate the post-1979 British growth model [6]. Mazzucato develops a parallel critique of how national accounts conflate value *extraction* with value *creation* [7].
- Institutional investors owned roughly 3% of US single-family rentals after the 2008 foreclosure wave enabled firms like Invitation Homes (Blackstone) and American Homes 4 Rent to assemble large portfolios; the share is higher (15–25%+) in targeted Sun Belt metros such as Atlanta, Charlotte, and Phoenix [8][9]. Desmond's work links concentrated rental ownership to higher eviction rates and rent extraction from low-income tenants [10].
- Charges for the use of intellectual property are now a material component of services trade: the US earned roughly $128bn in IP charges receipts in 2022 versus about $51bn paid — a persistent and growing surplus, reflecting the increasing share of corporate value tied to patents, trademarks, and copyrights rather than tangible capital [11][12].
- Rentier income is structurally more heritable than entrepreneurial income. A patent portfolio, a rental building, an index-fund position, or a municipal-bond coupon stream requires no heir-specific skill to continue generating returns — unlike a founder-operated business, whose value typically decays on succession. This asymmetry is the core reason rentier drift strengthens the normative case for taxing transfers rather than flows.

## Detailed analysis

The labour-share literature converges on a decline but disagrees on scale and source. Three adjustments matter. **First**, proprietors' income must be split between labour and capital; conventional splits understate labour share. **Second**, depreciation has risen faster than gross capital income, so the *net* capital share has risen less than the gross [4]. **Third**, Rognlie's housing correction is decisive: stripping out owner-occupied and rental housing imputations leaves little secular trend [4]. The implication is not that the labour-share story is wrong, but that it is more accurately framed as a **rent-share** story.

Labour share trajectory (approximate, percentage points of gross value added):

| Economy | ~1970 | ~1990 | ~2010 | ~2019 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| United States (nonfarm business) | 64 | 63 | 58 | 57 [2] |
| United Kingdom | 71 | 69 | 65 | 65 [3] |
| Germany | 71 | 67 | 62 | 63 [3] |
| Japan | 73 | 69 | 61 | 60 [3] |
| OECD average | ~66 | ~64 | ~60 | ~59 [3] |

*Figures are approximate and sensitive to methodology — proprietor-income treatment, self-employment adjustment, and housing inclusion each move the number by 1–3 points.*

Within the non-labour share, four rent components stand out. **Housing**: Rognlie's decomposition plus rising metropolitan land values indicate housing is the single largest driver of measured capital-share gains [4]. **Monopoly markups**: De Loecker et al. find the markup distribution has fattened in its upper tail, consistent with winner-take-most dynamics in tech, pharma, and platform industries; Basu and others have disputed the methodology, but even conservative estimates show meaningful markup growth [5]. **IP**: OECD and WIPO data show royalty and licensing flows rising faster than GDP in most advanced economies, with the US, UK, Netherlands, and Switzerland as net exporters of IP rents [11][12]. **Financial rents**: Philippon has shown US financial-sector unit costs barely fell from 1900 to 2010 despite IT, suggesting rent rather than productivity [13]; Christophers extends this to asset-management fees and infrastructure concessions [6].

The institutional-landlord phenomenon is smaller in aggregate than the discourse suggests — single corporate owners hold a low-single-digit share of the US SFR stock — but concentrated geographically and important as a leading indicator of housing's transformation from consumption good into a yield-bearing asset class [8][9]. The ownership form matters: corporate rentier claims are perpetual and liquid, whereas a family landlord's holdings historically dispersed on death.

**The inheritance link.** The rentier drift redirects flow income toward stocks of existing wealth. Because those stocks — unlike a surgeon's practice or a chef's restaurant — generate returns independent of owner effort, they transmit across generations with minimal decay. A confiscatory tax on transfers (above a per-recipient threshold) therefore falls disproportionately on exactly the income category that has grown fastest and that contributes least to current production, while leaving entrepreneurial returns taxable under the ordinary regime during the earner's lifetime.
