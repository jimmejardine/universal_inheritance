# Housing and the Generational Gap

**Executive summary.** Housing has shifted over four decades from a durable consumption good bought in one's twenties on a single income into a capital asset that is increasingly inherited or deposit-gifted rather than earned. The result is that where you live, when you buy, and whether you own at all are now determined less by your labour-market position than by the property wealth of your parents — the clearest mechanism by which capitalism in the UK and US now transmits advantage across generations.

## Key findings

- UK house prices rose from roughly 4× average earnings in 1997 to around 8× in 2022, with the ratio peaking above 9× in London and the South East; the long-run average before the late 1990s was closer to 4× [1][2].
- In the US, the Case-Shiller Home Price Index has grown substantially faster than median household income since the mid-1980s; the price-to-income ratio on a national basis roughly doubled between 1985 and the early 2020s, though it remains below UK levels [3][4].
- The median age of a UK first-time buyer rose from around 29 in the mid-1990s to 34 in 2022–23 according to English Housing Survey data, with first-time buyers in London now typically in their mid-to-late thirties [5][6].
- In the US, the National Association of Realtors reported the median first-time buyer age reached an all-time high of 38 in 2024, up from 28 in 1991 — the largest generational shift in the series [7].
- Legal & General's "Bank of Mum and Dad" research has consistently found that parental/family help funds a very large share of UK first-time purchases; recent iterations estimate family assistance is involved in roughly half of purchases by under-35s, with lending volumes that would make BoMaD equivalent to a top-ten UK mortgage lender in some years [8].
- Resolution Foundation finds that owner-occupation among those aged 25–34 in the UK fell from around 51% in the late 1980s to roughly 28% by the mid-2010s, partially recovering since; boomers at the same age were roughly twice as likely to own [9][10].
- US Federal Reserve Survey of Consumer Finances data show households headed by under-35s held a smaller share of national wealth in 2019 than the same age group did in 1989, with housing equity the single largest component of the gap; millennials reached age 40 holding materially less real housing wealth than boomers did at 40 [11][12].
- The IFS and Resolution Foundation both conclude that housing is the dominant driver of the post-2008 intergenerational wealth divergence in the UK — house-price growth, not pension accumulation or labour income, explains the bulk of the widening gap between cohorts born in the 1950s and those born in the 1980s [10][13].
- ONS estimates from the Wealth and Assets Survey put net property wealth at approximately 36% of total household wealth in Great Britain (2018–2020), and HMRC inheritance-tax statistics show residential property is consistently the largest single asset class in taxable estates, typically around a third to 40% of estate value [14][15].

## Detailed analysis

The defining UK datapoint is the Nationwide / ONS house-price-to-earnings ratio. Prior to financial liberalisation in the 1980s, the ratio hovered in a 3–5× band. By 2007 it had crossed 6× nationally; by the early 2020s it stood near 8×, with London at 9–12× depending on the measure [1][2]. The US trajectory is less dramatic in absolute terms but structurally similar: Case-Shiller real prices roughly doubled between 1987 and 2022 while real median household income rose by perhaps a third [3][4].

| Decade | UK median first-time buyer age | UK house price / earnings ratio (approx.) | US median first-time buyer age |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1980s | ~27–28 (uncertain, pre-EHS) | 3.5–5 (peaked 1989) | ~28–29 |
| 1990s | ~29 | 3–4 (trough mid-90s) | ~30 |
| 2000s | ~30–31 | 5–7 | ~31–32 |
| 2010s | ~32 | 6–8 | ~32–33 |
| 2020–24 | 34 (2022–23) | ~8 nationally, ~9+ London | 38 (2024, NAR) |

The deposit channel is where inheritance becomes structurally important. Legal & General's BoMaD work and Savills research consistently find that a majority of first-time purchases by under-30s in high-price UK regions involve some family transfer, whether a gift, a loan, or co-signing [8]. Resolution Foundation's inheritance work shows these transfers are themselves stratified: the children of home-owning parents receive them; the children of renters overwhelmingly do not. Their projections suggest that by the 2030s, inheritances will roughly double as a share of lifetime income for typical recipients, and the single biggest determinant of whether a young person owns their home will be whether their parents did [10][16].

The generational arithmetic compounds. Resolution Foundation's Intergenerational Audit documents that a typical millennial reached age 30 with housing wealth far below what a boomer held at 30, while the IFS's work on wealth inequality shows that inherited wealth is becoming a larger share of lifetime resources for cohorts born since 1980 and that housing is the dominant asset involved [10][13]. On the US side, Fed Distributional Financial Accounts show baby boomers held roughly 21% of US household wealth at median age 35, versus single-digit shares for millennials at comparable ages; real estate is the largest contributor to that gap among non-wealthy households [12].

The inheritance loop closes here. HMRC inheritance-tax receipts statistics show residential property is the largest single asset class in taxable UK estates, and ONS Wealth and Assets data confirm property is around a third of all household wealth — and a far larger share for the median estate, which holds little financial wealth outside pensions [14][15]. What is passed down, therefore, is overwhelmingly the house. The policy implication for a manifesto is direct: any regime that does not tax housing inheritance heavily leaves the main conveyor belt of unearned advantage running untouched.
