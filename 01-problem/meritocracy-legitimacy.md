# Meritocratic Legitimacy

**Executive summary.** Across Anglo-American and most OECD democracies, institutional trust and belief that "hard work is rewarded" have drifted downward since the 1970s, even as the top of the wealth distribution has become measurably more dynastic. The academic debate is genuinely contested — Sandel and Markovits argue meritocracy is both empirically hollow and psychologically corrosive, while Wooldridge defends it as historically emancipatory — but the polling evidence that public belief in fair reward has weakened is robust.

## Key findings

- US trust in the federal government "to do what is right" has fallen from roughly 73% in 1958 and a late-1960s peak near 77% to low-to-mid-teens readings for most of the 2010s–2020s, per Pew Research's long-running trust series [1].
- The Edelman Trust Barometer's 2020–2024 waves consistently report majorities in most surveyed countries agreeing with "the system is failing me" and identifying inequality/unfairness as core drivers of distrust; Edelman's 2020 and 2023 reports framed this explicitly as a "trust in capitalism" problem [2].
- British Social Attitudes has tracked a long decline in the view that government "can be trusted to place the nation's needs above party" — roughly 38% in 1986 to single digits by the late 2010s — and a parallel rise in agreement that "there is one law for the rich and another for the poor" [3].
- OECD's *Trust in Government* series (2013, 2021, 2024) finds fewer than half of citizens across OECD-27 trust national government, with perceived fairness of public institutions a stronger predictor of trust than GDP or service delivery [4].
- World Values Survey waves show persistent cross-national divergence on "hard work brings a better life" vs. "success is determined by luck/connections"; the US and UK have both moved toward the luck/connections pole since the 1990s, though levels remain higher than in much of continental Europe [5].
- Pew Global Attitudes (2020, 2022) reports median majorities in advanced economies saying the economic system "needs major changes or complete reform"; in the US, ~70% in 2020 agreed [6].
- On who actually sits at the top: Forbes 400 analyses by United for a Fair Economy and academic updates find the *share* of self-made entrants has risen since the 1980s, but a persistent ~25–30% inherited their place and a further large share started from substantial family capital [7]. UBS's *Billionaire Ambitions Report* (2023, 2024) flagged the onset of the largest intergenerational wealth transfer on record, with heirs' numbers and wealth growing faster than self-made billionaires' for the first time in the report's history [8].
- Bhutta, Bricker, Dettling et al. (Federal Reserve, 2022) estimate that inheritances and inter-vivos transfers account for a substantial and growing share of wealth differences, particularly at the top and along racial lines — consistent with a dynastic, not purely meritocratic, wealth structure [9].
- Inglehart and Norris's *Cultural Backlash* (2019) and Rodrik's work on the globalisation backlash both document statistical associations between perceived economic unfairness / status threat and populist vote share, though neither isolates *inherited wealth* as a causal channel — the link to heirs specifically should be treated as suggestive, not established [10][11].

## Detailed analysis

**Trust trajectories.** The Pew series is the cleanest long arc: a collapse through Vietnam/Watergate, a partial Reagan-era recovery, a brief post-9/11 spike, then a secular decline to historically low levels [1]. BSA and Eurobarometer show shallower but directionally similar patterns; OECD's composite indicators since 2006 show trust plateauing below 50% in most member states and correlating more strongly with perceived integrity and fairness than with economic output [3][4]. Edelman's commercial barometer is methodologically weaker (online panels, client-facing framing) but useful as a consistent 2001-onward time series; its recurring finding that respondents view "the system" as rigged in favour of elites has been stable across business cycles [2].

**Fairness and mobility beliefs.** WVS and Pew Global both find that Americans' historically exceptional belief in effort-based mobility has converged downward toward European levels since roughly 2000 [5][6]. Alesina, Stantcheva and Teso (2018, *AER*) showed experimentally that Americans *overestimate* mobility while Europeans underestimate it — relevant because it implies US legitimacy rests on a belief that empirical data increasingly contradict.

**How meritocratic is the top?** Three data points converge. Forbes 400 decompositions find a non-trivial minority are pure heirs and a large majority benefited from above-average starting capital [7]. UBS's 2023 report was notable for being an industry (not advocacy) source flagging the heir-vs-founder crossover [8]. The Fed's Bhutta et al. paper provides the most rigorous microdata estimate that transfers — not labour income or entrepreneurship — explain a growing share of top-decile wealth [9].

**The academic debate.** Sandel's *Tyranny of Merit* (2020) argues meritocratic ideology produces hubris among winners and humiliation among losers, eroding solidarity; his empirical backbone is Chetty et al.'s mobility data and Case-Deaton's "deaths of despair" [12]. Markovits's *Meritocracy Trap* (2019) makes a narrower, more quantitative claim: educational and labour-market sorting have produced a self-reproducing elite whose "merit" is itself inherited human capital [13]. Wooldridge's *Aristocracy of Talent* (2021) concedes the dynastic drift but argues the solution is *more* meritocracy — stronger testing, less credentialism, narrower inheritance — not less [14]. The three largely agree on the facts and disagree on prescription.

**Link to populism.** Inglehart–Norris frame backlash as cultural-values-driven but note economic insecurity as an amplifier; Rodrik's account is more directly economic, tying trade-shock regions to populist vote shares [10][11]. Neither isolates inherited wealth specifically, so claims that visible dynasticism *causes* populist surges should be framed as plausible and consistent with the evidence, not demonstrated.

**Uncertainty flags.** Exact Forbes 400 self-made percentages vary by coder definitions; Edelman's methodology limits cross-decade comparability; WVS question wordings shifted between waves 5–7. Treat point estimates as directional.
