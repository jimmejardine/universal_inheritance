# Wealth Concentration and Democratic Stability: A Literature Survey

**Executive summary.** Across four bodies of scholarship — the responsiveness empirics, the theory of oligarchy, the inequality-and-democratic-survival literature, and the institutions literature on why capitalism itself needs constrained power — the weight of evidence supports a two-way dependency: extreme wealth concentration degrades democratic responsiveness and consolidation, while secure market capitalism depends on exactly the constrained, credible, inclusive political institutions that concentrated wealth erodes. The causal claims are contested at every step — this survey states the strongest critiques at full strength — but the literature converges on a structural conclusion the rest of this repo operationalises: hereditary concentration is the compounding term in the loop, and the transmission point is where the loop can be cut.

(This file is the consolidating survey. The Gilens–Page centrepiece and the money/media/lobbying mechanisms are documented in [political-capture.md](political-capture.md); the capture-speed evidence and Hungary/Russia negative cases in [../13-movement/closing-window.md](../13-movement/closing-window.md); the classical republican tradition in [../03-case-for/democratic-renewal.md](../03-case-for/democratic-renewal.md); the self-reinforcing loop as thesis in [../00-capitalism/the-self-destruction-thesis.md](../00-capitalism/the-self-destruction-thesis.md). This survey adds the scholarly map those files draw on, including the critiques they compress.)

## Key findings

- The core empirical result — US policy tracks the preferences of economic elites far more closely than those of the median voter (Gilens & Page 2014 [1]) — survives its critics only in bounded form: the "coincidental representation" rejoinder (Enns 2015 [2]; Bashir 2015 [3]; Branham, Soroka & Wlezien 2017 [4]) shows middle-class and elite preferences agree so often that outcomes usually satisfy both; the robust residual is that *where preferences diverge, the middle rarely prevails independently*.
- A systematic review of 1,163 responsiveness estimates across 25 studies (Elkjær & Klitgaard 2021 [7]) finds pro-rich differential responsiveness is the modal result — strongest in multivariate designs, and, unexpectedly, *stronger in European democracies than in the US* — while stressing that magnitude is heavily model-dependent.
- The wealthy are different in measurable, politically relevant ways: the SESA pilot survey of the top 1% (Page, Bartels & Seawright 2013 [9]) found them far more politically active than the public and sharply divergent on deficits, regulation and social spending; Bartels' *Unequal Democracy* [8] documents senators' responsiveness rising steeply with constituents' income; Cagé's *The Price of Democracy* [10] documents the same skew through campaign-finance and fiscal channels in France and comparatively.
- The theory of oligarchy (Winters [11]) identifies **wealth defence** as the one constant of oligarchic behaviour across three millennia, with modern "civil oligarchy" coexisting comfortably with elections so long as fortunes are left intact — a frame that predicts, rather than merely describes, organised resistance to transfer taxation (Hacker & Pierson [12]; Lindblom's structural-dependence mechanism [13], steelmanned in [../04-case-against/capital-flight.md](../04-case-against/capital-flight.md)).
- The survival evidence says inequality's danger is to democratic *consolidation*, not transition: Houle 2009 [16] finds inequality raises breakdown risk while not affecting democratisation; Boix [17] and Acemoglu & Robinson [14] supply the redistribution-threat theory; Scheidel's *Great Leveler* [19] is the darkest datum — historically, only violent shocks have compressed wealth at scale — and Scheve & Stasavage [18] show even the 20th-century egalitarian tax era rested on war-compensation arguments, not peacetime egalitarianism.
- The reverse arrow is at least as well established: secure property, deep capital markets and sustained growth historically required credible constraints on power (North & Weingast 1989 [22]; Olson [23]) and inclusive rather than extractive institutions (Acemoglu, Johnson & Robinson 2001 [24]; 2024 Nobel citation [25]) — with Rajan & Zingales' *Saving Capitalism from the Capitalists* [26] supplying the book-length version of this repo's own thesis: incumbents use the state to close the markets they climbed.
- The honest counter-case exists: Milanovic's "political capitalism" [27] (China; Singapore-adjacent) demonstrates decades of capitalist growth without democracy — but with property rights resting on discretionary forbearance rather than law, which is Winters' warring/sultanistic oligarchy in modern dress, not a refutation of the constraint argument.

## 1. The empirical core: does concentrated wealth reduce democratic responsiveness?

**The centrepiece and its bounds.** Gilens & Page (2014) regressed outcomes on 1,779 US policy questions (1981–2002) against the preferences of median-income citizens, the affluent (90th percentile), and organised interest groups simultaneously, and found the median's *independent* effect statistically indistinguishable from zero once elite preferences were controlled [1] — the finding [political-capture.md](political-capture.md) builds on. The critiques deserve full strength:

| Critique | Claim | Where it bites |
|---|---|---|
| Enns 2015, "Relative Policy Support and Coincidental Representation" (*Perspectives on Politics* 13:4) [2] | Middle- and high-income groups rank policies so similarly that policy ends up "about where we would expect" if the middle were represented — **coincidental representation**. Even on divergent issues, outcomes usually fall near the median's preference | Converts "the middle has no influence" into "the middle's influence is observationally hard to distinguish" |
| Bashir 2015 (*Research & Politics*) [3] | The Gilens–Page statistical design is "prone to underestimating the impact of citizens at the 50th percentile by a wide margin" (shown by simulation); on direct rich-vs-middle disagreements, average citizens got their preferred outcome roughly as often as elites | Attacks the near-zero coefficient itself, not just its interpretation |
| Branham, Soroka & Wlezien 2017 (*Political Science Quarterly*) [4] | The rich and middle agree on ~90% of the 1,779 items; on the 185 disagreements, the rich win only somewhat more often (53% vs 47%) | The headline "oligarchy" reading overstates how often preference divergence occurs at all |

Gilens' response (*Perspectives on Politics* 13:4, pp. 1065–71 [5]; popular version with Page in 2016 [6]) concedes the correlation but distinguishes *influence* from *outcomes*: when the middle wins, it wins as "coincident beneficiaries rather than causes of the victory" — representation that depends on happening to agree with the affluent is not political equality, and it predictably fails exactly where the classes part company (top tax rates, financial regulation, transfer taxation: the terrain of this project). The bounded post-debate consensus: elite influence is at minimum co-equal with and probably exceeds median influence; the median almost never prevails *against* the affluent; and the magnitude of the gap is sensitive to specification.

**The meta-analytic anchor.** Elkjær & Klitgaard's systematic review (2021) of 1,163 estimates from 25 studies finds the literature "collectively suggests political outcomes better reflect the preferences of the rich," with the differential starkest in multivariate models (the probability of finding extreme pro-rich bias rises from 8% to 59% when income groups are modelled simultaneously), remarkably uniform across policy domains — suggesting a generic driver rather than any single mechanism — and, strikingly, *stronger in comparative European studies than in the US* despite Europe's lower inequality [7]. That last finding matters for this UK-focused project: unequal responsiveness is not an American pathology that UK institutions are immune to.

**Who the affluent are and what they want.** Page, Bartels & Seawright's SESA pilot (2013) — the first systematic survey of the actual top 1% (Chicago-area respondents, mean wealth ~$14m) — found the wealthy vastly more politically active than the general public (a large majority making political contributions, initiating contacts with officials at rates ordinary citizens do not approach) and holding distinctively conservative economic preferences: budget deficits ranked as the top problem, less economic regulation, less redistribution and social spending than the public wants [9]. Bartels' *Unequal Democracy* (Princeton UP, 2nd ed. 2016) supplies the legislative-behaviour half: across multiple Congresses, senators' roll-call responsiveness rises steeply with constituent income, with the bottom third of the income distribution receiving little or no measurable weight [8]. Cagé's *The Price of Democracy* (Harvard UP, 2020) extends the account beyond the US: French and comparative political-finance systems channel disproportionate influence to large donors — with the regressive twist that tax reliefs on political giving mean "the poor end up subsidizing the political preferences of the rich" — and her remedy (democracy vouchers, strict donation caps) attacks the conversion channel this project's instrument bypasses by attacking the fortune's persistence instead [10].

## 2. The theory of oligarchy

**Winters.** *Oligarchy* (Cambridge UP, 2011) is the survey's theoretical spine: oligarchs are actors empowered by concentrated material resources, and across every case from classical Athens and Rome to Suharto's Indonesia and the modern US, their politics reduces to **wealth defence** — protecting the fortune from confiscation (property defence) and from taxation (income defence) [11]. His typology orders the cases: *warring* and *sultanistic* oligarchies defend wealth personally or through a strongman; *civil* oligarchies — the modern democratic condition — disarm and delegate defence to law, the tax bar, and the political-finance industry, coexisting contentedly with elections so long as the fisc leaves fortunes intact. Two implications carry the survey's weight: dynastic transmission is the *reproduction mechanism* of civil oligarchy (each inheritance renews the fortune and the defence industry around it — [../16-second-order/dynasties-and-power.md](../16-second-order/dynasties-and-power.md)), and the counterattack on any serious transfer tax is predictable from theory ([../10-opposition/README.md](../10-opposition/README.md) plans for it; [../03-case-for/democratic-renewal.md](../03-case-for/democratic-renewal.md) §5 makes the argument).

**Hacker & Pierson.** *Winner-Take-All Politics* (Simon & Schuster, 2010) supplies the mechanism study for the US case: the post-1970s surge of top-end inequality was not the weather of technology and trade but the harvest of **organised money** — business mobilisation, lobbying asymmetry, and policy *drift* (the deliberate non-updating of rules, estate-tax erosion included, as an invisible form of enactment) [12]. Its analytical contribution is to move the unit of analysis from elections to organised combat between persistent interests — which is why diffuse majorities lose to concentrated minorities even when polls side with the majority ([../10-opposition/attack-playbook.md](../10-opposition/attack-playbook.md) documents the 2001 estate-tax repeal as the type case).

**Lindblom.** The structural-dependence argument — capital need not lobby at all, because governments dependent on private investment discipline themselves in anticipation (*Politics and Markets*, 1977; "The Market as Prison," 1982) [13] — is the deepest version of the capture claim and is steelmanned where it bites hardest, in [../04-case-against/capital-flight.md](../04-case-against/capital-flight.md): it cannot be refuted in advance, only managed.

**The classical lineage, in one paragraph.** The proposition that oligarchy and popular government cannot share a polity is the oldest in political science: Aristotle's *Politics* holds that extreme wealth disparity produces masters and slaves rather than citizens, making the middle-class polity the stable form [28]; Machiavelli's *Discourses* identifies the *gentiluomini* — those who live idly off accumulated holdings — as fatal to republican life, because their resources buy partisans and convert public office into private instrument [29]. The full tradition (Harrington's "balance of dominion," the founders' abolition of entail and primogeniture, Tocqueville on inheritance law) is developed in [../03-case-for/democratic-renewal.md](../03-case-for/democratic-renewal.md) §§1–3 and is not duplicated here; the point for this survey is that the modern empirics of §1 are a quantification of a 2,300-year-old hypothesis, not a novelty of post-*Citizens United* America.

## 3. The stability evidence: inequality and democratic survival

**Theory: democracy as a bargain over redistribution.** Acemoglu & Robinson's *Economic Origins of Dictatorship and Democracy* (Cambridge UP, 2006) models democratisation as elites' credible concession under threat of revolt: since promises of future redistribution are not credible, elites extend the franchise — but *consolidation* fails where inequality makes democracy too expensive for elites (coups) or too tempting for majorities (radical redistribution triggering backlash) [14]. Boix's *Democracy and Redistribution* (Cambridge UP, 2003) reaches a sharper prediction from a similar engine: democracy emerges and survives where inequality is low and assets are mobile — where the rich have less to fear from the median voter and less ability to threaten exit [17]. Their later *The Narrow Corridor* (Penguin Press, 2019) generalises the frame: liberty lives only in the corridor where a capable state is matched by an organised society — the "shackled Leviathan" — and both despotism *and* capture by narrow elites are exits from the corridor [15].

**Evidence: consolidation, not transition.** Houle's "Inequality and Democracy" (*World Politics* 61:4, 2009) is the cleanest large-N result: across post-1960 regimes, inequality *harms consolidation* — raising the probability of democratic breakdown — while having no detectable net effect on transitions to democracy [16]. For this project the direction matters: the claim the evidence supports is not "inequality prevents democracy from being born" but "inequality kills democracies that exist," which is precisely the maintenance framing of [../03-case-for/democratic-renewal.md](../03-case-for/democratic-renewal.md) §9.

**The two dark data points, at full strength.**

- **Scheve & Stasavage**, *Taxing the Rich* (Princeton UP, 2016): the great egalitarian tax episodes — top income and estate rates of 70–90% — were *not* produced by democracy responding to inequality. Universal suffrage, in their data, barely moved top rates; mass-mobilisation war did, because conscription of bodies licensed the *compensatory* conscription of wealth. After ~1970 the compensatory argument lost its referent and top rates fell everywhere [18]. Taken seriously, this predicts that peacetime democracies do not enact confiscatory transfer taxation merely because inequality is high — the feasibility constraint this repo answers with a new compensatory offer (the universal endowment as "equal start," tested as an argument in [../06-palatability/framing.md](../06-palatability/framing.md)) rather than by disputing the history.
- **Scheidel**, *The Great Leveler* (Princeton UP, 2017): across recorded history, major compressions of wealth inequality came almost exclusively from the "Four Horsemen" — mass-mobilisation warfare, transformative revolution, state failure, lethal pandemics; peacetime politics has rarely if ever levelled at scale [19]. As an objection to this project it is the strongest on the shelf: it implies the proposal is asking history for something history has never granted. The honest reading, developed in [../11-geographies/crisis-windows.md](../11-geographies/crisis-windows.md), keeps it a constraint rather than a verdict — Scheidel measures outcomes, not attempts; the violent levellers worked through fiscal-political mechanisms (revenue need, broken coalitions, licensed ideas) that lesser shocks can reproduce; and his own concluding discussion of modern policy options is pessimistic but not fatalistic. The project's self-description follows: it is an attempt to build the peaceful leveller the historical record lacks, before the violent ones return.

**Backsliding and the populist channel.** The modern regress literature locates the mechanism inside elected governments: Haggard & Kaufman's *Backsliding* (Cambridge UP, 2021) traces democratic erosion through polarisation, party-system capture, and incremental legal derogations across 16 cases [21] — mechanisms, note honestly, that they root in polarisation rather than inequality directly. The link back to distribution runs through the grievance channel: Guriev & Papaioannou's *JEL* survey (2022, repo-verified in [../03-case-for/democratic-renewal.md](../03-case-for/democratic-renewal.md) §6) finds the populist wave driven substantially by economic insecurity and unfairness perceptions interacting with cultural backlash [20], and the elite-overproduction frame ([elite-overproduction.md](elite-overproduction.md)) supplies the counter-elite supply side. The two negative cases showing how fast consolidation outruns correction — Hungary's KESMA media roll-up (476 outlets into one pro-government foundation within eight years of supermajority) and Russia's loans-for-shares oligarchy (locked in within five) — are documented with sources in [../13-movement/closing-window.md](../13-movement/closing-window.md) §3 and are not re-cited here.

## 4. The reverse direction: why capitalism needs democracy

The author's framing question — stable democracy as "one of the necessary underpinnings of capitalism" — is its own literature, and it is the one that makes this project's pro-capitalist framing more than rhetoric.

**Credible commitment.** North & Weingast's "Constitutions and Commitment" (*Journal of Economic History* 49:4, 1989) is the founding document: England's post-1688 institutions — parliamentary control of taxation, independent courts — allowed the Crown to *credibly commit* not to confiscate, and lenders responded within a generation, with government borrowing capacity and private capital markets expanding dramatically [22]. The deep point: capital markets are not prior to constrained politics; they are its product. An investor's property is only as secure as the constraints on whoever could take it.

**The stationary bandit.** Olson's *Power and Prosperity* (Basic Books, 2000) supplies the general logic: even a self-interested autocrat — the "stationary bandit" — protects some production to have something to tax, but his time horizon is his life, his successor is unbound, and nothing constrains confiscation when it pays; durable prosperity therefore requires *market-augmenting government* — power constrained enough that contracts and property outlive the ruler's convenience [23]. Democracy with rule of law is the only known institutionalisation of that constraint at scale.

**Inclusive vs extractive institutions.** Acemoglu, Johnson & Robinson's "Colonial Origins" (*AER*, 2001) turned the claim into an identification strategy — settler-mortality-instrumented institutions explain large income differences, with extractive institutions (power concentrated, property insecure for the many) persistently poorer [24] — and the 2024 Nobel citation ("for studies of how institutions are formed and affect prosperity") canonised the framework: inclusive institutions that enforce property rights, protect democracy and limit corruption foster development; extractive ones, which concentrate power and resources in a small elite, stifle it [25]. Read against §§1–3, the loop closes: concentrated wealth pushes inclusive institutions toward extractive ones from within.

**The book-length version of this repo's thesis.** Rajan & Zingales, *Saving Capitalism from the Capitalists* (2003; Princeton UP pbk) [26]: free markets — especially financial markets, the entry ticket of the outsider — are the most fragile institution of capitalism, because *incumbents* are their natural enemies: those who have already climbed use political influence to pull the ladder up, restricting access to capital and competition. Their title is this project's §Channel-B argument ([../00-capitalism/the-self-destruction-thesis.md](../00-capitalism/the-self-destruction-thesis.md)) issued from inside Chicago-school finance, and the book belongs on the outreach map ([../13-movement/outreach-academics.md](../13-movement/outreach-academics.md)) for exactly that reason: it is the credentialed pro-market statement that defending capitalism requires disarming its winners.

**The honest counter-cases.** Two must be stated at strength. First, *authoritarian capitalism exists*: Milanovic's *Capitalism, Alone* (Harvard UP, 2019) classifies China as "political capitalism" — sustained capitalist growth legitimated by performance, with a deliberately unbound rule of law and a private sector held under discretionary control [27]; Singapore shows a softer variant. The rejoinder the literature itself supplies: in political capitalism property rights rest on personalist forbearance — the fortune exists at the ruler's pleasure (China's disciplining of its own billionaires is the running demonstration), which is Winters' pre-civil oligarchy rather than a stable alternative equilibrium, and the model's own theorists concede its corruption and succession problems are structural [27][11]. Second, *the classic liberal claim runs one way*: Friedman's *Capitalism and Freedom* (1962) argued capitalism is *necessary but not sufficient* for political freedom [30] — he did not claim democracy was necessary for capitalism, and Chile under Pinochet was his era's test case. The institutions literature above is, in effect, the empirical answer to Friedman's unasked converse: capitalism without constrained power stays extractive, stays shallow, or stays hostage — the strong form of "capitalism needs democracy" is really "capitalism at the frontier, over generations, needs credibly constrained power," and democracy under law is the only durable supplier so far observed.

## 5. Synthesis for this project (argument, so labelled)

The four literatures assemble into a loop, each arc independently evidenced even where the whole is not cleanly identified:

1. **Concentrated wealth → purchased politics.** §1's responsiveness gap plus the mechanism files ([political-capture.md](political-capture.md), [../13-movement/closing-window.md](../13-movement/closing-window.md)).
2. **Purchased politics → extractive drift.** Winters' wealth defence and Hacker–Pierson's drift converge with Rajan–Zingales: the rules are rewritten to protect incumbents — which degrades *both* democratic responsiveness and market contestability at once. Democratic decay and market sclerosis are the same disease observed from two windows.
3. **Extractive drift → instability.** Houle's consolidation result, the grievance-populism channel (Guriev–Papaioannou), and the backsliding mechanics (Haggard–Kaufman) describe the political price; Scheidel describes the historical endgame when the price is not paid voluntarily — the violent levellers.
4. **The compounding term is hereditary.** A one-generation fortune is a bid for influence; a transmitted fortune is an institution. At the verified 7.5%/yr real growth of top-stratum wealth against 1.4% income growth ([wealth-concentration.md](wealth-concentration.md), [../13-movement/closing-window.md](../13-movement/closing-window.md) §2 [31]), the stratum's relative weight doubles every ~12 years, and [../16-second-order/dynasties-and-power.md](../16-second-order/dynasties-and-power.md) maps what it buys.

The proposal's place in the loop follows: a 100% inheritance tax above a £500k per-recipient lifetime threshold, funding a £50k universal endowment, is aimed at the **transmission step, not the holding step** — it confiscates no living person's property, polices no one's speech, and caps no one's earnings; it interrupts the *institutionalisation* of the fortune at the one moment the holder is beyond deterrence and the heir has done nothing to be deterred from. Against Scheidel, it is the candidate peaceful leveller his history lacks: violence levelled by destroying capital; the transfer tax levels by *recycling* it into fifty thousand pounds of agency for every citizen at 25. Against Scheve–Stasavage, the endowment is the peacetime compensatory offer their history says confiscatory taxation requires. Against Winters, it is the anti-oligarchy instrument that needs no standing confrontation with living wealth — only a rule at the grave.

**What the literature does not establish**, stated plainly: (a) no clean causal identification runs from inequality to democratic breakdown — Houle's is a hazard correlation and the transitions literature is genuinely contested; (b) the coincidental-representation critique bounds the Gilens claim — most policy, most of the time, is not an oligarchic imposition, and the responsiveness gap binds mainly where elite and mass preferences diverge (though transfer taxation is exactly such an issue); (c) authoritarian capitalism is a real existence proof that markets can grow without democracy for decades — the claim defensible from the evidence is about security, contestability and duration, not possibility; (d) nothing in §§1–4 demonstrates that *this* instrument, at *these* parameters, restores responsiveness — that is the argued case of [../03-case-for/](../03-case-for/README.md), resting on the mechanism evidence, not a tested policy history.

## 6. Honest limits

- **Measurement and causality are contested throughout.** The responsiveness literature's magnitudes swing with model specification (Elkjær & Klitgaard's central finding); the survival literature's inequality measures (Gini vs capital share vs top shares) and regime codings vary across studies; none of it is experimental.
- **US-centricity.** The core §1 results are American; the comparative extensions (Elkjær & Klitgaard, Cagé) partially correct this but the UK-specific responsiveness evidence remains thin — a research gap this repo has flagged before ([README.md](README.md)).
- **Book-length works are characterised from publisher pages, journal records and serious reviews**, verified as such, not from full re-reads; page-level claims are avoided accordingly.
- **The survey is a map, not a meta-analysis.** It reports where the weight of the literature lies and where the critiques bite; it does not pool effect sizes, and a motivated sceptic could assemble a thinner but non-empty shelf pointing the other way (Bashir, Scheve–Stasavage, and Milanovic are on it, and are cited here at full strength deliberately).
