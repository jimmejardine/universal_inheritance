# Social Mobility

**Executive summary.** Intergenerational mobility in most rich democracies is low and has stagnated or declined for cohorts born since the 1970s, with the United States and United Kingdom among the least mobile of large developed economies. The picture is sharper still for wealth than for income: parental wealth predicts children's wealth roughly twice as strongly as parental income predicts children's earnings, implying that inherited *capital* — not just inherited human capital — is a principal driver of persistent economic position [1][4][7].

## Key findings

- The intergenerational income elasticity (IGE) — the share of a parent's income advantage transmitted to a child — is approximately 0.47 in the US and 0.50 in the UK, versus roughly 0.15 in Denmark and 0.19 in Canada, meaning it takes two to three additional generations for a low-income family in Nordic countries to approach mean income compared with five or more in the Anglophone economies [1][3].
- OECD's *A Broken Social Elevator?* (2018) estimates that in the average OECD country it would take 4.5 generations for a child born into the bottom decile to reach mean income; in France, Germany, and the US the figure is 6 generations, and 9 in Brazil [3].
- The "Great Gatsby curve" (Corak 2013; Krueger 2012) documents a strong positive cross-country correlation — roughly r ≈ 0.8 across ~20 rich countries — between the Gini coefficient of income inequality and the IGE: more unequal societies are, on measured evidence, less mobile [1][2].
- Chetty et al.'s "Fading American Dream" (2017) finds the share of US children earning more than their parents at age 30 (in real terms) fell from about 90% for those born in 1940 to roughly 50% for those born in 1984, with rising inequality accounting for the majority of the decline in counterfactual decompositions [4].
- Rank-rank mobility in the US has been roughly flat for cohorts born 1971–1993 (Chetty et al. 2014, 2017), but because the income distribution stretched, the *absolute* mobility decline above is consistent with stable *relative* mobility — a distinction often blurred in public debate [4][5].
- UK evidence (Blanden, Gregg, Machin) shows intergenerational income persistence *rose* between the 1958 and 1970 British birth cohorts (IGE moving from ~0.17 to ~0.29 in their specification), with educational expansion disproportionately benefiting richer families; more recent cohorts show little further improvement [6].
- Intergenerational wealth elasticities are markedly higher than income elasticities: Pfeffer & Killewald (2017, 2018), using the US PSID, estimate a parent–child wealth rank correlation of ~0.37 and a multigenerational (grandparent–grandchild) association that remains substantial after controlling for parental wealth, consistent with wealth transmission operating through channels beyond income [7][8].
- Charles & Hurst (2003) found a US intergenerational wealth elasticity of roughly 0.37; Boserup, Kopczuk & Kreiner (2016), using Danish administrative data, put the figure near 0.27 even in a high-mobility country — both well above the corresponding income elasticities in the same populations [8][9].
- Direct evidence on inheritances and mobility is thinner but suggestive: Elinder, Erixson & Waldenström (2018), using Swedish estate data, find inheritances *reduce* relative wealth inequality mechanically (they are larger in percentage terms for less wealthy heirs) but *increase* absolute inequality and strengthen intergenerational wealth persistence once expected inheritances are capitalised [10].
- **Uncertainty flag:** cross-country IGE comparisons are sensitive to sample age, measurement window (single year vs. multi-year parental income), and administrative vs. survey data; point estimates can shift by 0.05–0.10. Rankings between Nordic, Continental, and Anglophone blocs are nonetheless robust [1][3].

## Detailed analysis

Corak's (2013) meta-synthesis remains the canonical cross-country benchmark, subsequently extended by the OECD (2018) and national administrative-data studies. Approximate father–son income elasticities:

Two interpretive points matter for policy. First, the Great Gatsby curve is a *correlation*, not a causal identification; candidate mechanisms include differential educational investment, labour-market sorting, neighbourhood effects (Chetty & Hendren 2018), and direct wealth transfers. The most credible causal micro-evidence — Chetty & Hendren's movers design — attributes a substantial share of place-based mobility variation to childhood environment exposure, which is itself endogenous to family resources [5].

Second, the income-mobility literature systematically understates *economic* persistence because wealth is transmitted more strongly than earnings. Pfeffer & Killewald (2017) show that grandparents' wealth predicts grandchildren's wealth even conditional on parents' wealth, violating the first-order Markov assumption built into most income-mobility models and implying multi-generational dynastic effects [7]. Since roughly 35–45% of household wealth in France, Germany and the US is inherited rather than self-accumulated, a chapter-scale argument about inheritance is warranted by the empirics rather than merely consistent with them.

The inheritance-specific causal evidence is limited but converging. Elinder, Erixson & Waldenström (2018) exploit the 2004 Swedish inheritance-tax abolition and estate-register data; Nekoei & Seim (2023) use the same setting to show that inheritances reduce relative wealth inequality in the short run but that behavioural responses (saving, labour supply) partially offset this within a decade [10]. Their central finding is consistent with a 100%-threshold inheritance regime plausibly flattening the wealth distribution materially, though the magnitude depends on threshold design and behavioural response — neither of which has been directly observed at the 100% margin.
