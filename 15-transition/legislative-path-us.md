# Legislative Path: United States

**Executive summary.** The US is the hard case: the realistic vehicle is budget reconciliation, which brings Byrd-rule surgery and a structural sunset risk for which the 2001–2010 estate-tax farce is the cautionary tale, while the filibuster forecloses the ordinary route absent a sixty-vote consensus that does not exist. The genuine good news is constitutional: a recipient-based accessions tax is arguably *more* robust than the estate tax it replaces, because an inheritance receipt is defensibly income within the Sixteenth Amendment's reach — but the honest strategic assessment is that the US adopts late, after the model is proven elsewhere.

## The vehicle problem

| Route | Requirement | Viability |
|---|---|---|
| Regular order | 60 Senate votes (filibuster) | Not realistic for a confiscatory-rate transfer tax in any foreseeable Senate |
| Budget reconciliation | Simple majority; Byrd-rule compliance | The realistic vehicle — with structural costs below |
| Filibuster abolition/carve-out | Simple-majority rules change | Possible but exogenous to this policy; if it happens, regular order reopens |

**Reconciliation and the Byrd rule.** Reconciliation bills cannot contain provisions "extraneous" to the budget: no provision without direct fiscal effect, none whose fiscal effect is "merely incidental" to policy intent, and — critically — none increasing deficits beyond the budget window unless sunset. Consequences for this scheme:

- The **tax** itself is squarely budgetary and survives Byrd scrutiny.
- The **anti-avoidance and register architecture** is vulnerable clause-by-clause: purely regulatory provisions (reporting duties, valuation-panel structures) risk being ruled extraneous and stripped, leaving the rate without its spine. Mitigation: draft enforcement provisions with explicit revenue effects and route what falls out through Treasury regulation under existing authority ([`../07-implementation/administration.md`](../07-implementation/administration.md)).
- The **endowment** is spending and can ride reconciliation (mandatory-spending changes are core reconciliation content), but a scheme scored as deficit-increasing beyond the window must sunset — the exact trap that follows.

## The 2001–2010 sunset farce as cautionary tale

EGTRRA 2001, passed by reconciliation, phased the estate tax down through the decade, **repealed it entirely for calendar 2010, then resurrected it at 2001 parameters for 2011** — because the Byrd rule forbade the out-year deficit. The results are the canonical demonstration of why this scheme must not be enacted with an expiry date:

- A decade of planning chaos: estate plans drafted in triplicate against three possible regimes.
- The 2010 window itself: heirs of billionaires who died that year (George Steinbrenner is the always-cited case) passed estates entirely untaxed, and a genuinely macabre empirical literature examined death-timing responses around the boundaries (Kopczuk–Slemrod line of work; to verify specific estimates).
- The "resolution": a lame-duck 2010 deal, then permanence in 2012 at a high exemption — the sunset structurally favoured the tax's opponents, who needed only gridlock.

The lesson for transition design: **a known future discontinuity in transfer taxation converts the tax into a timing game** — the same phenomenon as the forestalling problem in [announcement-effects.md](announcement-effects.md), institutionalised for a decade. If reconciliation forces a sunset, the scheme must be scored deficit-neutral-or-better in the out-years so the sunset never attaches; this is a further argument for the revenue-first sequencing in [revenue-bridge.md](revenue-bridge.md) and for enacting the basis switch (revenue-neutral by design, [sequencing.md](sequencing.md)) before any rate escalation.

## Constitutional questions

**Direct-tax apportionment.** Article I requires "direct taxes" to be apportioned among states by population — a death sentence for any tax so classified, and the perennial threat hanging over federal wealth-tax proposals. The estate tax survived in *New York Trust Co. v. Eisner* (1921) and its lineage as an **excise on the transfer** at death, not a direct tax on property. An accessions tax has two independent lines of defence, making it arguably *more* robust than what it replaces:

1. **Excise theory.** If taxing the privilege of *transmitting* is an excise, taxing the event of *receiving* is at least equally so — the same transfer, viewed from the other end.
2. **Sixteenth Amendment theory.** An inheritance receipt is an accession to wealth, clearly realized, over which the recipient has complete dominion — the *Glenshaw Glass* definition of income. Current law *excludes* gifts and bequests from income by statute (§102 IRC), not by constitutional command; Congress could include them. The friction point is *Eisner v. Macomber*'s narrower "derived" income language, but the modern doctrine (and the taxation of prizes, windfalls, and treasure trove) supports treating gratuitous receipts as taxable income (to verify against the academic literature on accessions taxes — Andrews, Batchelder — which reaches this conclusion).

The design consequence: the US statute should be drafted **as an income-inclusion regime** (gratuitous receipts above the lifetime exclusion included in gross income at a 100% surtax-equivalent rate, with the coordination rules from [`../02-proposal/mechanism.md`](../02-proposal/mechanism.md) §7) rather than as a freestanding wealth levy — buying Sixteenth Amendment cover at the price of some drafting inelegance. Litigation will come regardless; the challenge map is [`../10-opposition/legal-challenges.md`](../10-opposition/legal-challenges.md).

## State–federal coordination

- **The tax must be exclusively federal.** State-level transfer taxes have been in competitive retreat since the 2001 repeal of the federal credit for state death taxes converted them from revenue-sharing into stand-alone burdens; most states abandoned theirs (to verify count). The Australian cascade logic ([`../05-history/abolitions.md`](../05-history/abolitions.md)) applies with full force among fifty mobile-resident states.
- **Conformity mechanics.** A restored federal credit (up to a percentage of the federal accession liability) would let states share revenue without inter-state competition — the pre-2001 architecture, resurrected deliberately.
- **Community-property and trust-situs divergence** (South Dakota/Nevada dynasty-trust industries) require federal look-through rules that do not depend on state trust law — treated in [`../07-implementation/trusts-and-vehicles.md`](../07-implementation/trusts-and-vehicles.md) and [grandfathering.md](grandfathering.md).
- **The endowment** is federally administered (SSA-style), avoiding fifty delivery systems; baby-bond pilots at state level ([pilots.md](pilots.md)) remain valuable as machinery tests.

## Honest assessment: the US adopts late

Every structural feature points the same way: supermajority chokepoints, constitutional litigation risk, a federal judiciary currently receptive to property-rights challenges, fifty-state coordination, and a political culture in which the 2001–2010 episode showed even the *existing* estate tax barely survives. The strategic conclusion, developed in [`../11-geographies/anglosphere.md`](../11-geographies/anglosphere.md) and [`../12-political-adoption/`](../12-political-adoption/README.md):

1. **The US is a follower market.** The proof-of-concept jurisdictions are elsewhere ([`../11-geographies/adoption-criteria.md`](../11-geographies/adoption-criteria.md)); US adoption becomes plausible only after a peer democracy runs the scheme visibly for a decade.
2. **US-specific preparation still starts now**: the income-inclusion drafting, the accessions-tax academic groundwork, baby-bond delivery pilots, and beneficial-ownership infrastructure (the Corporate Transparency Act's troubled rollout is the live front; to verify current status).
3. **Interim rungs exist inside current politics**: restoring estate-tax parameters, closing valuation-discount and grantor-trust loopholes, and Batchelder-style inheritance-income proposals are all Congress-familiar stepping stones ([`../12-political-adoption/stepping-stones.md`](../12-political-adoption/stepping-stones.md)).

The UK path ([legislative-path-uk.md](legislative-path-uk.md)) has no equivalent of any of these barriers; that asymmetry, more than any cultural claim, is why this project is UK-primary.
