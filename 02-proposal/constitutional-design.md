# Constitutional Design and Entrenchment

**Executive summary.** The historical record says this policy's greatest danger arrives *after* passage: not repeal by frontal assault but strangulation by threshold drift, carveout creep, fund raids, and administrative starvation ([`../10-opposition/sabotage-after-passage.md`](../10-opposition/sabotage-after-passage.md)). This file specifies the entrenchment architecture — statutory indexation formulae, a named National Endowment Fund with independent trustees on sovereign-fund precedents, radical transparency, and legal locks where constitutions permit — while being honest that in the UK the strongest lock available is not legal at all: it is the creation of 700,000 new stakeholders every year.

## Key commitments

- **Indexation in primary legislation**, as self-executing formulae — max(wage, median house-price) growth for the threshold, wage growth for the endowment — with no ministerial discretion to suspend by regulation (§2).
- A **National Endowment Fund (NEF)**: receipts in, endowments out, statutory board of guardians, published investment mandate, and a surplus rule releasing money to general revenue only after every endowment is funded (§3).
- **Annual transparency report** — receipts collected, distributional tables, endowments paid, avoidance estimates, cost-to-yield — produced independently of ministers (§4).
- **Legal locks used wherever available** — referendum or supermajority entrenchment in jurisdictions that allow it; Alaska's constitutionalised fund is the model — with no pretence they exist at UK national level (§5).
- **The political ratchet as the primary UK defence**: named accounts from birth, statements from 16, a taper cohort already paid — repeal must visibly expropriate identified young people (§6).
- **Constitutional accommodation designed in from the start** for Germany-type property/inheritance guarantees and US direct-tax doctrine, rather than litigated as an afterthought (§7; [`../10-opposition/legal-challenges.md`](../10-opposition/legal-challenges.md)).

## 1. The threat model

The sabotage catalogue (documented at [`../10-opposition/sabotage-after-passage.md`](../10-opposition/sabotage-after-passage.md)) reduces to five vectors, each with a historical fingerprint:

| Vector | Historical fingerprint | Structural counter here |
|---|---|---|
| Threshold/indexation drift | Swedish and Norwegian nominal thresholds silently tightened by house-price inflation ([`../05-history/abolitions.md`](../05-history/abolitions.md)) | Self-executing max-of-two formula in primary legislation (§2; [`thresholds.md`](thresholds.md) §5) |
| Carveout creep | UK BPR/APR expansion until the base hollowed out ([`carveouts.md`](carveouts.md)) | Closed statutory list of reliefs; new reliefs require primary legislation plus a published base-erosion estimate |
| Fund raiding / hypothecation decay | UK Road Fund raided by Churchill from 1926, hypothecation formally ended by the Finance Act 1936 [5]; National Insurance fund notionality | Separate legal person, guardians with fiduciary duty to beneficiaries, surplus-only release rule (§3) |
| Administrative starvation | US IRS audit rates fell from 0.9% to 0.25% over 2010–19 as funding was cut, with the steepest falls for the highest incomes [6] | Statutory funding floor tied to a published cost-to-yield corridor; transparency report flags breach (§4) |
| Rate/schedule salami-slicing | US estate-tax exemption ratcheted from $675k (2001) to $13.99m (2025), and $15m from 2026 [7] | The 0%/100% kink has no intermediate rates to slice; changes are legible or nothing |

## 2. Statutory indexation, not discretionary uprating

Discretionary uprating is deferred repeal: every year a minister must act, inaction tightens (or, for the endowment, shrinks) the policy, and inaction is the politically cheapest option. The design therefore writes the formulae into primary legislation as self-executing duties: the threshold moves each April by the greater of national wage growth and median house-price growth; the endowment moves by wage growth ([`thresholds.md`](thresholds.md) §5; [`endowment.md`](endowment.md)). Precedents cut both ways and are instructive: the UK state-pension triple lock has been kept by every government since its 2011 introduction — modified only once, for 2022/23 — because abolishing so popular a policy is judged politically prohibitive [8], while US federal minimum-wage non-indexation shows how a nominal figure decays when uprating needs fresh legislation each time. We put the ratchet on our side. Suspension requires primary legislation — a whipped, debated, headline vote — never a statutory instrument.

## 3. The National Endowment Fund

Hypothecation fails when it is an accounting fiction inside the consolidated fund. The NEF is instead a **separate legal entity**:

- **Inflows:** all receipts-tax revenue, plus deferral interest and state-equity dividends ([`carveouts.md`](carveouts.md) §5).
- **Outflows:** endowment payments, then — only when the year's cohort is fully funded and a statutory buffer (illustrative: one year's payments) is intact — surplus to general revenue, honouring the [README](README.md) commitment that the Treasury takes only the residual.
- **Governance:** a board of guardians with a fiduciary duty owed *to present and future endowment recipients*, appointed on staggered terms with cross-party confirmation; a published, boring investment mandate (global diversified, low-cost — the fund smooths timing, it does not run industrial policy); annual independent audit.

| Feature | Norway GPFG [1] | Alaska Permanent Fund [2] | NZ Superannuation Fund [3] | Proposed NEF |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Legal basis | Statute (Government Pension Fund Act 2005) | **State constitution** (art. IX §15, 1976 referendum) | Statute (2001) | Statute; constitutional where available |
| Manager | Norges Bank (NBIM) | APFC, independent board | Guardians, independent | Board of guardians |
| Spending rule | ~3% real-return fiscal rule (handlingsregelen) | Only earnings spendable; principal locked | Formula-driven withdrawals from 2030s | Endowments first, buffer, then surplus release |
| Withstood raids? | Yes — by cross-party norm, not law | Principal yes (constitutional); dividend contested (statutory) | Contributions suspended 2009–17 — the cautionary case | Design target: raid requires primary legislation against named beneficiaries |
| Transparency | Full holdings published | Annual reports, public board | Full disclosure | §4 regime |

The Norway/Alaska contrast is the key finding: Norway protects an unentrenched fund through elite consensus; Alaska protects an entrenched principal even through decades of fiscal stress — but its merely-statutory *dividend* gets fought over annually. Lesson: entrench what you can, and make what you cannot entrench universal and named (§6).

## 4. Transparency: the annual "who got what" report

Sunlight is an enforcement mechanism and a legitimacy mechanism at once. The NEF and revenue authority jointly publish, annually and independently of ministers (production regulated against the Code of Practice for Statistics by the Office for Statistics Regulation, which is independent of ministers [9]):

- Receipts collected, by band, region, and asset class; number of recipients crossing the threshold.
- Anonymised distributional tables — the aggregate "who got what" of inheritance itself, ending the statistical darkness in which transfer flows currently sit ([`revenue-model.md`](revenue-model.md) §1). Individual privacy is preserved; the *pattern* of dynastic transmission becomes public data.
- Endowments paid: cohort size, take-up, unclaimed balances, fraud losses ([`endowment-delivery.md`](endowment-delivery.md) §3).
- Estimated avoidance gap and cost-to-yield against the statutory corridor (§1).
- Register of reliefs with each one's annual base-erosion cost — making carveout creep measurable the year it starts.

Publication is a statutory duty with a named accountable officer; the report is the tripwire for every §1 vector.

## 5. Legal locks where constitutions allow

- **Referendum-entrenched jurisdictions.** Alaska's 1976 amendment shows a resource-windfall fund can be placed beyond ordinary legislative reach [2]. Where the scheme is adopted by referendum or in a state/canton with constitutional initiative, the fund's existence, the hypothecation, and the indexation formulae are the three clauses worth entrenching. Jurisdiction screening is at [`../11-geographies/adoption-criteria.md`](../11-geographies/adoption-criteria.md).
- **Supermajority repeal clauses.** Available in some constitutions; largely symbolic where absent from constitutional text, but even statutory "notwithstanding"-style declarations raise the political price of quiet amendment.
- **US federal.** No entrenchment of statutes is possible, and the tax itself must be framed to survive direct-tax apportionment doctrine: the estate tax was upheld as an excise on the transfer ([*Knowlton v. Moore*](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/178/41/), 1900), the gift tax likewise ([*Bromley v. McCaughn*](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/280/124/), 1929); a recipient-based accessions tax should be drafted squarely as an excise on the *receipt* of property, not a tax on ownership. Full litigation map: [`../10-opposition/legal-challenges.md`](../10-opposition/legal-challenges.md) and [`../15-transition/legislative-path-us.md`](../15-transition/legislative-path-us.md).
- **Germany.** [Art. 14(1) GG](https://www.gesetze-im-internet.de/gg/art_14.html) guarantees inheritance *as an institution* (*Erbrechtsgarantie*); the BVerfG polices both over-taxation and — as in the 2014 business-relief judgment ([`carveouts.md`](carveouts.md) §5) — over-generous relief. Accommodation strategy: the generous per-recipient threshold, unlimited spousal relief, and dispersal incentive are precisely the features that preserve the institution of inheritance for the family while capping dynastic accumulation; the argument is that the scheme regulates the *scale*, not the *existence*, of inheritance. This must be built into the legislative record from the start, not improvised in Karlsruhe. (Full proportionality analysis is a matter for German constitutional counsel; see [`../11-geographies/continental-europe.md`](../11-geographies/continental-europe.md).)

## 6. The UK problem — and the political ratchet

No UK Parliament can bind its successor; "constitutional statutes" enjoy at most protection from *implied* repeal ([*Thoburn*](https://www.bailii.org/ew/cases/EWHC/Admin/2002/195.html), 2002), which is worthless against an express repeal bill. The honest conclusion: **in the UK, entrenchment is political engineering, not law.** The design already contains the machinery:

1. **700,000 new stakeholders a year.** Each cohort paid is a constituency that will never vote to abolish its successors' grants; each cohort *approaching* 25 holds a statement with a number on it. Within one parliament, ~3.5m voters have been paid and ~3.5m more are within five years of payment (illustrative arithmetic from cohort size [4]).
2. **Named accounts from birth, statements from 16** ([`endowment-delivery.md`](endowment-delivery.md) §2). Repeal ceases to be the abolition of a tax and becomes the visible cancellation of identified individuals' balances — expropriation framing, running in the repealer's face.
3. **The taper cohort** (§6 of [`endowment-delivery.md`](endowment-delivery.md)) extends the stakeholder base backwards at commencement.
4. **The Alaska demonstration.** The PFD is statutory, was held subject to ordinary appropriation and gubernatorial veto in [*Wielechowski v. State*](https://law.justia.com/cases/alaska/supreme-court/2017/s-16558.html) (2017), and has still been paid every year since 1982 because no Alaskan politician survives attacking it [2]. The NHS occupies similar ground in Britain: legally ordinary, politically constitutional. That is the status the endowment is designed to reach — and the abolition literature says it reaches it quickly, because the benefit is universal, individual, and legible ([`endowment.md`](endowment.md) §8).
5. **Pre-commitment devices short of law:** manifesto pledges, a founding cross-party commission, and international coordination ([`avoidance.md`](avoidance.md) §7) all raise repeal costs at the margin; party-system dynamics are analysed in [`../12-political-adoption/wedge-dynamics.md`](../12-political-adoption/wedge-dynamics.md).
