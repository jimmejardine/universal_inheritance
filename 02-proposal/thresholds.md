# Thresholds and Indexation

**Executive summary.** A £500,000 / $750,000 per-recipient lifetime threshold, indexed annually to the greater of national wage growth or median house-price growth, is calibrated so that the overwhelming majority of heirs transmit and receive unaffected, while concentrated dynastic transfers face the 100% marginal rate. This document grounds the number in house-price and wage multiples, shows the distribution of inheritances received, estimates revenue, and defends the indexation rule against the nominal-erosion failure mode seen in abolished Nordic regimes.

## Key findings

- UK median house price is approximately £290,000 (ONS HPI, 2024), so a £500k threshold is ~1.7× median housing wealth — enough to fully absorb a typical inherited home in almost every region outside inner London [1].
- £500,000 equals ~14× UK median gross annual earnings of ~£35,000 (ONS ASHE 2023) [2]; $750,000 equals ~13× US median earnings of ~$59,000 (BLS) [3].
- Inheritance receipt is rare and typically small: only 4% of GB adults received an inheritance of £1,000+ over 2014–16, with a median receipt of £11,000 (ONS, 2018) [9]; among recipients, half got £10,000 or less and only 10% got over £100,000 (Resolution Foundation, *Passing on*, 2018) [4].
- On IFS lifetime projections for the 1980s-born (the highest-inheriting cohort yet), the median lifetime inheritance is ~£136,000, the top 25% exceed ~£284,000, and only the top 10% exceed ~£495,000 — for the 1960s-born the top-10% cut-off is ~£269,000 [5]. A £500k per-recipient threshold therefore sits at roughly the 90th percentile of projected receipts even for the 1980s cohort.
- Aggregate annual inheritance flow in the UK is projected to roughly double from ~£100bn to ~£200bn by the 2040s (IFS) [5]; the US SCF transfer module shows a similarly top-heavy distribution [6].
- CPI-only indexation would erode the threshold because UK house prices have outpaced CPI by ~1.5–2 percentage points annually over four decades (ONS) [1] — the same drift that neutered Sweden's and Norway's nominal thresholds before abolition (see `../05-history/abolitions.md`).
- A £500k threshold rewards distributive estate planning: four children inherit £2m tax-free; two inherit £1m tax-free.

## 1. Why a per-recipient threshold at all

A threshold (as opposed to a flat 100% rate on the first pound) exists because the policy target is dynastic wealth concentration, not ordinary family transmission of a home, savings, or a modest business share. A *recipient-based* threshold (vs. the UK's current estate-based nil-rate band) aligns the tax with the principle that the morally relevant quantity is what an individual receives unearned, not what a deceased person leaves. It also mechanically creates the incentive central to the design: break up large estates across more beneficiaries to avoid the 100% rate. An estate-based exemption produces no such incentive and privileges single-heir dynasties.

## 2. Why £500,000 / $750,000

Three anchors converge on this range:

**(a) Median house price.** UK median residential property: ~£290,000 (ONS HPI, 2024) [1]. A £500k threshold absorbs a typical home plus typical pension/savings wealth in one transfer. US median home sale price: ~$420,000 (NAR) [7]; $750,000 gives equivalent headroom given higher US housing dispersion.

**(b) Wage multiples.** £500,000 ≈ 14× UK median full-time earnings (ONS ASHE) [2]; $750,000 ≈ 13× US median earnings (BLS) [3]. Both are well above typical lifetime savings capacity for a median household, so the threshold cannot credibly be characterised as targeting the middle class.

**(c) Recipient distribution.** IFS projections put the 90th percentile of lifetime inheritance receipts for the 1980s-born at roughly £495,000 (median ~£136,000; 75th percentile ~£284,000), and lower for earlier cohorts (1960s-born 90th percentile: ~£269,000) [5]. Actual receipts to date are far smaller still: the median inheritance among 2014–16 recipients was £11,000 (ONS) [9]. A £500k lifetime threshold therefore leaves roughly 90% of even the highest-inheriting projected cohort entirely untaxed — and materially more of every earlier cohort — before any behavioural redistribution.

**Tradeoff variants.**

- *£250k (tighter):* captures roughly the top quarter of projected 1980s-born lifetime receipts (75th percentile ≈ £284,000) [5]; politically riskier because it bites into ordinary inherited homes in the South East.
- *£1m (looser):* sits well above the projected 90th percentile of receipts (~£495,000 for the 1980s-born [5]); the precise share of recipients above £1m is unpublished but plausibly only the top few per cent (unverified). Revenue and dynastic-break effect substantially weaker; acceptable only if paired with a larger citizen's endowment.

### Variant considered and declined: the descending-threshold ratchet

The most natural challenge to a fixed £500k launch is: *why not start where the opposition is smallest?* Open the 100% rate at £1bn — a defender class of a couple of hundred UK families ([`../03-case-for/wealth-tax-comparison.md`](../03-case-for/wealth-tax-comparison.md) §5) — then ratchet the threshold down by steps (£100m → £10m → £1m) over decades. The steelman is genuinely strong: it is the wedge-by-shrinking-defenders manoeuvre of [`../12-political-adoption/stepping-stones.md`](../12-political-adoption/stepping-stones.md) step 6 applied to the threshold axis; it mirrors the Zucman billionaire-first logic that put a 2% floor through the French National Assembly; and since flow is heavily top-concentrated (§4), the early revenue sacrifice looks tolerable. Its strongest form is a **single-act pre-legislated glide path** — one open parliamentary vote legislating the full descent to the £500k terminus on a fixed statutory schedule — which cures the deniability and repeated-vote objections to an open-ended decade-by-decade version.

The variant was evaluated and declined, for four reasons, each owned by the linked file:

1. **It institutionalises forestalling.** A published schedule of future threshold cuts is the "announced-but-uncommenced" pathology in recurring form: every household in the next band down receives a decade's notice to disperse and restructure, re-running the pre-announcement rush the transition machinery exists to prevent — at every rung ([`../15-transition/announcement-effects.md`](../15-transition/announcement-effects.md); the Australian death-timing evidence shows behaviour responds to *any* pre-announced discontinuity).
2. **It starves the endowment and delays entrenchment.** A £1bn threshold reaches on the order of 200 families and raises next to nothing for decades, so the universal endowment — the 700,000-stakeholders-a-year engine that makes the regime irreversible ([constitutional-design.md](constitutional-design.md) §6, [endowment.md](endowment.md)) — cannot launch at meaningful scale. The tax would spend its formative decades exhibiting exactly the "fiscal triviality as permission structure" that made the abolished regimes cheap to kill ([`../05-history/abolitions.md`](../05-history/abolitions.md), error #8).
3. **It validates the slippery-slope attack.** The design's core answer to "first them, then your house" is that the threshold is engineered to move only *upward* in real terms ([`../09-objections/slippery-slope.md`](../09-objections/slippery-slope.md)). A descending ratchet converts that rebuttal into a falsehood and hands opponents a published schedule of intended descent — "concealed radicalism" made checkable.
4. **It breaks the ledger's no-retroactivity doctrine.** Under lifetime cumulation ([mechanism.md](mechanism.md)), a recipient sheltered at £5m while the threshold stood at £1bn would, at the £1m rung, already be £4m "over" on money received and spent — taxable only by the retrospection the regime forswears ([`../15-transition/grandfathering.md`](../15-transition/grandfathering.md)), or forgiven at the cost of making each rung's descent largely symbolic for wealth already transferred.

**The resolution is that the design already takes the ratchet's benefit — on the other axis.** The sequencing ladder ([`../15-transition/sequencing.md`](../15-transition/sequencing.md)) holds the threshold fixed and legible at £500k from Phase 2 and ratchets the *rate* (rising schedule by tranche → 100%), so the first serious bite still lands on the largest receipts and the defender group still starts small — while the threshold promise stays true, the endowment funds from Phase 3, and no household is ever waiting for a scheduled future cut to reach it. The glide-path form remains the least-bad version of the variant should a future coalition insist on it; its residual costs (recurring forestalling at each step, a decades-late endowment) do not disappear, they are merely voted for honestly. *What would settle the empirics:* the missing micro-simulation of per-recipient flow shares above £1bn / £100m / £10m / £1m, which would quantify exactly how little each early rung raises (judgement pending that modelling).

### One formula, local numbers: the localisation doctrine

The architecture of this proposal is universal; its numbers are not. What travels across borders unchanged: the recipient basis, lifetime cumulation, the single 0/100 kink, the endowment pairing, and indexation to the maximum of wage and house-price growth (§5) — run, in each country, on that country's own statistical series. What never travels: the threshold and endowment amounts. Each country re-derives them from the same three national anchors used in §2:

- **(a) Dwelling price:** threshold ≈ 1.5–2× the national median (or, where no median is published, mean) dwelling price — enough to absorb a typical family home plus savings in a single transfer;
- **(b) Earnings:** threshold ≈ 12–15× median gross annual earnings — far above median lifetime savings capacity, so the "targets the middle class" attack fails arithmetically;
- **(c) Receipts distribution:** threshold ≈ P90 of lifetime inheritance receipts, where distributional data exist (the UK is unusual in having the IFS projections [5]; most countries must legislate on (a) and (b) and commission the microdata).

The endowment has landed at roughly 1–1.5× median gross annual earnings in every calibration to date (judgement: an observed regularity across the calibrations below, not a derived optimum).

**The EU question, answered explicitly.** For EU members, the architecture and the Phase-4 minimum-tax floor coordinate EU-wide ([`../08-capital-flight/international-coordination.md`](../08-capital-flight/international-coordination.md)); the numbers stay national. A single EU-wide threshold is the one harmonisation the design forbids: set where Dutch house prices demand, it would be toothless in lower-price member states; set where their anchors put it, it would be confiscatory of ordinary family homes in the high-price ones. Pillar Two's division of labour is the template — common base, common floor, national parameters. Free-movement mechanics — how one person's ledger travels across different national thresholds, and the CJEU case law the design must satisfy — are in [`../08-capital-flight/international-coordination.md`](../08-capital-flight/international-coordination.md) §"Free movement and the mobile ledger".

## 3. How many people would pay

**UK.** Only 4% of GB adults received an inheritance of £1,000+ in the two years to mid-2016, and the median receipt was £11,000 [9]; even on IFS lifetime projections for the 1980s-born, a fifth will inherit less than £10,000 [5]. IFS projections imply around 10% of the 1980s-born would cross £500k in lifetime cumulative receipts (top decile cut-off ~£495,000), with smaller shares for earlier cohorts [5]; precise figures *require modelling* on a cumulative lifetime basis rather than single-event basis.

**US.** The Federal Reserve SCF transfer module and CBO analysis show ~20–25% of households receive any transfer, with the top 1% of heirs receiving more than 40% of the aggregate [6][8]. Roughly the top 3–5% of recipients would exceed $750k cumulatively — again, exact figure *requires modelling*.

"Partially affected" (cross the threshold at all) therefore plausibly sits in the low-single-digit percent of recipients; "materially affected" (receive multiples of the threshold) is a fraction of that.

## 4. Revenue implications

UK aggregate annual inheritance-and-gift flow: ~£104bn currently, projected to ~2.4× (>£230bn) by the mid-2040s (Demos/Resolution Foundation; see [arithmetic.md](arithmetic.md) §1). If ~30–40% of aggregate flow sits above £500k per recipient (IFS distributional work [5]), the notional pre-behavioural base is ~£31–42bn/year today, ~£70–92bn at mid-2040s flows.

Actual revenue will be materially lower because: (i) business/farm deferral over 30 years smooths large receipts out of a single tax year (timing, not loss); (ii) spousal relief defers the base until wealth exits the marriage chain ([carveouts.md](carveouts.md) §1); (iii) behavioural response (lifetime giving, distribution across more recipients, charitable redirection) is exactly the intended outcome and will shrink the base. The consolidated waterfall, scenario compositions, and funding verdict are owned by [arithmetic.md](arithmetic.md): illustrative net receipts of **~£10–25bn/year on current flows** (central ~£13–15bn; pessimistic tail below £10bn), rising to **~£25–60bn/year at mid-2040s flows** — *requires HMRC/OBR micro-simulation* to refine ([arithmetic.md](arithmetic.md) §5). US equivalent using CBO/JCT methodology scales proportionally [8].

The policy's success metric is not revenue; it is the re-dispersion of dynastic wealth.

## 5. Indexation: max(wage growth, median house-price growth)

CPI indexation is inadequate because the goods the threshold must "cover" — housing wealth, pension pots, business equity — do not track consumer prices. UK house prices have outpaced CPI by ~1.5–2 pp/year over four decades (ONS HPI) [1]. Wage growth is the better floor because the threshold's legitimacy rests on its relation to lifetime earning capacity. Taking the maximum of the two prevents either a housing boom or a wage boom from silently tightening the policy.

This is precisely the failure mode documented in `../05-history/abolitions.md`: Swedish and Norwegian inheritance taxes lost political legitimacy partly because nominally-fixed thresholds captured ordinary middle-class homes as property prices rose, generating the coalition that ultimately abolished them.

## 7. Distributional framing

The per-recipient design has a deliberate incentive. Under a £500k threshold:

- An estate split equally between **4 children** transmits **£2,000,000** tax-free.
- An estate split between **2 children** transmits **£1,000,000** tax-free.
- An estate left to a **single heir** transmits **£500,000** tax-free; the remainder faces 100%.

Combined with the unconditional £50,000 citizen's endowment at 25, the policy ensures everyone starts with a floor while removing the ceiling-less transmission of dynastic fortunes. Wide distribution is rewarded; concentration is taxed away.
