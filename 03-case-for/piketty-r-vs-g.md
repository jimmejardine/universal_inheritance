# The Structural Case: Breaking the r > g Ratchet

**Executive summary.** When the after-tax return on capital persistently exceeds the growth rate of national income, the wealth-to-income ratio rises without bound and inherited fortunes mechanically outpace earned ones — a ratchet no income tax can unwind [1][2]. Inheritance taxation is the uniquely high-leverage intervention because it is the only instrument that acts on the *stock* at the one moment the holder can no longer re-optimise against it [1][8].

## Key findings

- Across 1700–2010, Piketty's long series shows r ≈ 4–5% and g ≈ 1–2% outside the anomalous 1914–1975 window; the postwar compression was a historical exception, not the steady state [1][3].
- Saez & Zucman reconstruct US wealth from capitalised income flows and find the top 0.1% share rising from ~7% in 1978 to ~22% by the mid-2010s — a pace consistent with r − g ≈ 2–3 pp compounding through inheritance [4].
- Kuhn, Schularick & Steins (2020) using the SCF+ find that race and portfolio composition amplify wealth divergence: equity-heavy top portfolios compound faster than housing-heavy middle portfolios, so r is not a single number but a distribution favouring incumbents [5].
- The *World Inequality Report 2022* confirms the pattern globally: the top 10% now capture 76% of wealth; the bottom 50% hold 2% [6].
- Rognlie (2015) correctly shows most of the measured capital-share rise is imputed housing rents — but housing *is* inherited, and the housing-concentration channel produces the same dynastic ratchet by a different route [7].
- Auten & Splinter (2024) reduce the measured US top-1% *income* share growth once transfers and tax units are reweighted — but their critique targets flows, not stocks; the Saez–Zucman *wealth* trajectory survives largely intact [8].
- A wealth tax is theoretically equivalent to an inheritance tax on an annuitised basis, but empirically brittle: valuation of private equity and art, liquidity crises forcing asset sales, and exit migration. France's ISF raised ~0.2% of GDP before repeal; estate taxes at comparable rates historically raised more [1][10].
- At r − g ≈ 2.5 pp and an intergenerational interval of ~30 years, a one-shot transfer tax of ~50% per generation roughly stabilises the wealth ratio; a 100%-above-threshold regime is structurally super-sufficient and therefore robust to avoidance slippage [1].

## 1. What r > g actually says

Piketty's claim is narrow and mechanical. Let β = W/Y be the capital/income ratio, s the savings rate, and g the growth rate of Y. In the Harrod–Domar–Solow steady state, β → s/g. If the rate of return r exceeds g, the capital share α = rβ rises, and because savings out of capital income exceed those out of labour income, wealth concentrates at the top of the distribution [1][2]. The common misreading is that r > g alone causes inequality — it does not; it causes *wealth/income* to rise. Distributional concentration requires the additional fact that savings rates rise with wealth, which the data confirm [4].

## 2. Empirical verification

Piketty & Zucman (2014) document β rising from ~250% to ~500–600% across rich economies since 1970 [3]. Saez & Zucman's capitalisation method, applied to US tax micro-data, shows top-0.1% wealth shares tripling over the same window [4]. Kuhn–Schularick–Steins add the portfolio-composition channel and confirm divergence is a *compounding* phenomenon, not a flow-income artefact [5]. The *WIR 2022* extends the finding globally and cross-sectionally [6]. The qualitative picture — rising β, rising top wealth shares, inheritance flows recovering toward 19th-century levels — is robust across methods.

## 3. Steelmanning the critiques

**Rognlie.** Most of the capital-share rise is imputed housing rent; net of housing, the non-housing capital share is roughly flat [7]. *Response:* housing is among the most heritable assets (primary residences pass at stepped-up basis in the US; owner-occupation is ~70% wealth-held in middle deciles); a housing-driven β rise is still a dynastic ratchet.

**Auten & Splinter.** Once unreported income, government transfers, and changing tax-unit definitions are accounted for, US top-1% income-share growth since 1960 is roughly half the Piketty–Saez figure [8]. *Response:* their critique is about *income flows*. The structural argument here is about *stocks*; the wealth series (Saez–Zucman, SCF+, Forbes-calibrated) is not substantially dented. Even halved, the trajectory is decisively upward.

**Acemoglu & Robinson.** Institutions, not mechanical r > g, drive inequality [9]. *Response:* accepted — but inheritance taxation *is* an institution, and the mechanical force they downweight is exactly what a well-designed institution must counteract.

## 4. Why inheritance is the high-leverage point

If r > g, redistributing labour income cannot stabilise W/Y: capital grows faster than the thing you are taxing. A wealth tax addresses the stock but suffers valuation, liquidity, and exit frictions [10]. An inheritance tax uniquely:

- acts on the *stock* (scales with the quantity that is actually diverging);
- fires at a *discrete transmission moment*, not continuously;
- imposes deadweight loss on a *decedent* who can no longer reallocate labour supply or consumption in response — the textbook efficient-taxation target [1];
- aligns with a normative baseline (equality of opportunity) that income taxes violate.

This is why Piketty himself, in *Capital and Ideology*, shifts emphasis from the annual wealth tax to a lifetime-receipt inheritance regime [2]. The full instrument-by-instrument comparison — equivalence arithmetic, the OECD abolition record, and the honest column of what a wealth tax does better — is [wealth-tax-comparison.md](wealth-tax-comparison.md).

## 5. Quantitative sensitivity

With r = 5%, g = 2%, β = 6, generation length 30 years, untaxed dynastic wealth multiplies by ≈ 2.4× per generation. A flat inheritance tax of ~58% exactly neutralises the ratchet at these parameters. Saez–Zucman's progressive wealth-tax simulations reach similar aggregate yields at 2–8% annual rates on large fortunes [4]. A 100%-above-threshold regime is calibrated to be sufficient at *any* plausible r − g ∈ [1%, 4%]; it is deliberately over-engineered because the cost of under-shooting (renewed compounding) is asymmetric with the cost of over-shooting (some foregone bequest utility above a lifetime threshold already several multiples of median lifetime earnings).

## 6. Political-economy feedback

r > g does not only produce wealth concentration; it produces the *political resources* that defend wealth concentration — lobbying, media ownership, foundation-funded epistemic infrastructure (see [`../01-problem/political-capture.md`](../01-problem/political-capture.md)). Each generational transmission both transfers capital and renews the political protection of the next transmission. Inheritance taxation intervenes at the one moment when the holder cannot lobby in response. This is the structural argument: breaking the ratchet requires a tax instrument that is itself unratchetable.
