# Wealth Tax vs This Proposal: The Dedicated Comparison

**Executive summary.** The proposal is routinely miscategorised as a wealth tax, and the two instruments are routinely treated as substitutes; both errors need answering. Categorically: a wealth tax assesses the *living owner's holdings annually*; this proposal assesses a *recipient's windfall once*, at the only moment capital changes hands without a market test — no living person's wealth is ever valued, levied, or diminished. In theory the instruments are annuitised equivalents (a 2% annual levy ≈ a 45% once-per-generation transfer tax); in practice the equivalence breaks entirely in the transfer tax's favour on efficiency, administration, migration, and political survival — the OECD's annual wealth taxes have died almost everywhere they were tried, and it was *wealth* taxes, not inheritance taxes, that produced the famous tax exiles. The honest column the other way: a wealth tax bites *now*, while a transfer tax concedes the holder's entire lifetime and waits a generation for full effect.

## Headline findings

- **Categorically distinct:** the tax event, the taxpayer, the valuation frequency, and the liquidity demand all differ. The wealth-holder never meets this tax; their heir meets it once ([`../00-capitalism/what-this-is-not.md`](../00-capitalism/what-this-is-not.md)).
- **The valuation asymmetry is decisive and usually understated.** The classic administrative case against wealth taxes — valuing every asset, every year, against a living owner's adversarial best — simply does not transfer. This design needs one valuation per generation, and at the one event where the legal system *already compels* a valuation: UK personal representatives must value the whole estate to obtain probate even when no tax is due [12], and the US values every inherited asset at death regardless of estate-tax liability, to set the heir's stepped-up capital-gains basis under IRC §1014 [13]. The marginal administrative demand is a rate attached to an existing event, not a new valuation apparatus (§2).
- **The annuitised equivalence** — (1 − τ)³⁰ per 30-year generation — makes annual rates of 1%, 2%, and 5% equivalent to one-shot transfer taxes of roughly 26%, 45%, and 79% respectively. The instruments aim at the same stock; everything else about them differs (§2).
- **The empirical record is one-sided:** OECD countries operating net wealth taxes fell from 12 in 1990 to 4 in 2017 [1]; France's ISF raised ~€4–5bn a year — roughly 0.2% of GDP — before conversion to a property-only tax in 2018 [2]; US estate and gift taxes at their mid-1930s peak raised $379m (1936) — nearly half the yield of the individual income tax that year [8].
- **The exile roster belongs to the wealth tax.** The emblematic departures that haunt this debate — Kamprad from Sweden, the Norwegian 2022–23 outflow — were responses to *annual levies on living holders*, exactly the design this proposal rejects; the retention proposition "the owner never gets a bill" is available only to the transfer design ([`../08-capital-flight/retention-attraction.md`](../08-capital-flight/retention-attraction.md), [`../08-capital-flight/evidence.md`](../08-capital-flight/evidence.md)).
- **Piketty himself moved** from the "utopian" global wealth tax of *Capital in the Twenty-First Century* to a lifetime-receipts inheritance regime as the lead instrument in *Capital and Ideology* ([piketty-r-vs-g.md](piketty-r-vs-g.md) §4, [`../05-history/piketty.md`](../05-history/piketty.md)) [3].
- **Zucman's billionaire minimum tax (2024–26) is engaged directly, not dismissed** (§5): the 2% *floor* on the effective taxation of the largest fortunes — put to the G20 in 2024, passed by the French National Assembly in February 2025, and carried to a mass audience by *We Need to Tax Billionaires* — is the strongest current version of the annual case and shares this project's diagnosis. The answer is complementarity with a boundary: on Zucman's own figures billionaire wealth compounds at 7.5% a year real, so a 2% floor slows the dynasty without ending the transmission; it is a candidate *bridge* instrument, not a substitute for the cap [14][15][16].
- **The honest disadvantage:** a transfer tax waits. Existing living concentrations — and the political power they exercise during the holding decades — are untouched until they transmit (§4). This is a real cost, accepted deliberately, and the transition literature owns it ([`../15-transition/revenue-bridge.md`](../15-transition/revenue-bridge.md)).

## 1. Why this is not a wealth tax

| Dimension | Annual net wealth tax | This proposal |
|---|---|---|
| Tax event | Existing, continuing ownership | Transfer of capital to a *new person* |
| Taxpayer | The living owner — usually the person who built or bought the assets | The recipient of a windfall, who has paid nothing and earned nothing on it ([`../09-objections/fairness.md`](../09-objections/fairness.md) master key) |
| Frequency | Every year, for life — 40–60 valuations of the same estate | Once per receipt; in practice once per generation |
| Valuation machinery required | An entirely new annual apparatus for every asset class | A rate attached to valuations the law already compels — UK probate [12] and US basis step-up at death [13] |
| What the wealthy person owes in life | An annual bill on everything they hold | **Nothing, ever** |
| Liquidity demand | Continuous — cash from potentially illiquid holdings every year | At one discrete moment, with 30-year deferral machinery for operating assets ([`../02-proposal/carveouts.md`](../02-proposal/carveouts.md)) |
| Behavioural surface | The owner re-optimises against it annually for decades: valuation games, wrappers, emigration | Fires when the donor can no longer re-optimise at all ([optimal-tax-theory.md](optimal-tax-theory.md)) |
| Relationship to markets | Taxes holdings the market allocated | Taxes the one large transaction the market never tested ([`../00-capitalism/markets-vs-dynasties.md`](../00-capitalism/markets-vs-dynasties.md)) |

The categorical point matters politically as much as technically: every "this is confiscation from the successful" attack presumes the wealth-holder is the taxpayer. Under this design the successful are never taxed by it — not on earning, holding, or spending — which is why [`../00-capitalism/what-this-is-not.md`](../00-capitalism/what-this-is-not.md) leads its boundary table with this row.

## 2. The theoretical equivalence, and where it breaks

On a blackboard the instruments converge: an annual levy τ_w compounds to a generational take of 1 − (1 − τ_w)ᵍᵉⁿ, so over ~30 years:

| Annual wealth tax | Equivalent one-shot generational transfer tax |
|---|---|
| 0.5% | ~14% |
| 1% | ~26% |
| 2% | ~45% |
| 5% | ~79% |

(Illustrative compounding, ignoring returns and behavioural response.) Note what this table implies both ways: the Warren/Saez–Zucman-style 2–6% top rates [4] are *generationally comparable* to the mid-century 70–80% estate taxes — and a 100%-above-threshold transfer tax is stronger than any politically imaginable annual rate. The instruments target the same object: the compounding dynastic stock of [piketty-r-vs-g.md](piketty-r-vs-g.md).

The equivalence breaks on four practical margins, all favouring the transfer design:

1. **Deadweight timing.** The wealth tax's burden falls on living decision-makers who respond — investment, domicile, structuring — every year. The transfer tax's marginal payer is a decedent who can no longer reallocate anything; the formal literature scores accidental and terminal bequests as a near-lump-sum base ([optimal-tax-theory.md](optimal-tax-theory.md)).
2. **Valuation.** The wealth tax must value private firms, art, and land *annually*, at the owner's adversarial best — 40–60 valuations of the same estate over a holding lifetime. This proposal values once per generation, at an event where valuation already legally occurs: probate in the UK, where the estate must be valued whether or not any tax is due [12], and death in the US, where §1014 steps up every heir's capital-gains basis to date-of-death value and so forces a valuation of every inherited asset even when no estate tax is payable [13]. A wealth tax builds a new annual valuation industry; this design attaches a rate to valuations the system already performs ([`../09-objections/practicality.md`](../09-objections/practicality.md), [`../07-implementation/valuation.md`](../07-implementation/valuation.md)).
3. **Liquidity.** Annual levies press recurring cash demands on illiquid holdings — a standing theme of the OECD abolition record [1]. (Germany's Constitutional Court struck down its wealth tax in 1995 on the adjacent valuation ground: real property assessed at stale 1964 cadastral values against market-valued financial assets violated equal treatment, and the tax has not been levied since 1997 [9].) The transfer design's deferral-or-state-equity machinery handles one discrete event.
4. **Migration.** An annual tax gives every wealthy resident a standing yearly reason to leave; the transfer design gives the living owner none — the entire retention analysis of [`../08-capital-flight/`](../08-capital-flight/README.md) §"retention-attraction" is built on this asymmetry.

## 3. The empirical record

The wealth tax has the worst survival record of any major tax instrument: twelve OECD members operated net wealth taxes in 1990; by 2017 only four did (France, Norway, Spain, Switzerland), and after France's 2018 conversion the annual, comprehensive form survived only in Switzerland, Norway, and Spain — plus Colombia, which made its wealth tax permanent in 2022 — each with heavy carve-outs [1][10]. Austria, Denmark, Germany, Finland, Iceland, Luxembourg, the Netherlands, Sweden, and France abolished or gutted theirs, citing the ratio of administrative and flight costs to a yield that rarely exceeded 0.2–1% of GDP [1][2]. France's ISF — the emblematic case — raised ~€4–5bn a year (roughly 0.2% of GDP) amid a running exile narrative before Macron converted it to a real-estate-only tax, the IFI, in 2018 [2]. The survivors are instructive rather than encouraging: Switzerland pairs low rates with the forfait regime for wealthy foreigners; Spain's regional 100% reliefs (Madrid) hollowed the base; Norway's 2022 rate rise produced the exit episode analysed in [`../08-capital-flight/evidence.md`](../08-capital-flight/evidence.md).

Scheuer and Slemrod's survey [5] states the professional consensus this project adopts: most of a wealth tax's goals are achievable at lower cost by taxing capital *transfers* and capital *income* well. Estate taxation, by contrast, operated at 70–80% top rates for decades in the US and UK while those economies led the world ([historical-precedents-of-success.md](historical-precedents-of-success.md)) — and its failures were failures of avoidance design, not of concept ([`../05-history/abolitions.md`](../05-history/abolitions.md)). One jurisdictional footnote favouring the transfer form in the US: estate taxation has been constitutionally settled since *Knowlton v. Moore* (1900), which upheld the federal death duty as an excise not subject to apportionment, while a federal net wealth tax faces an unresolved direct-tax apportionment question [6].

## 4. What a wealth tax does better — the honest column

- **It bites now.** A transfer tax leaves every existing living fortune untouched until it transmits — decades of compounding and of the political power the holding window confers ([`../01-problem/political-capture.md`](../01-problem/political-capture.md); the same concession made for inbound dynasts in [`../08-capital-flight/inbound-wealth.md`](../08-capital-flight/inbound-wealth.md) §3). The wealth tax addresses the stock in real time — and the strongest current instrument for this column is Zucman's minimum floor, engaged in §5.
- **Revenue is annual and smooth** from year one; the transfer design's revenue ramps with mortality and needs bridge instruments across the transition ([`../02-proposal/revenue-model.md`](../02-proposal/revenue-model.md) §5, [`../15-transition/revenue-bridge.md`](../15-transition/revenue-bridge.md)).
- **It reaches wealth that never transmits to persons** — capital parked in perpetual structures. (The transfer design's answer is look-through and periodic-charge rules for trusts and foundations, [`../07-implementation/trusts-and-vehicles.md`](../07-implementation/trusts-and-vehicles.md), but the wealth tax's claim here is simpler.)
- **Switzerland proves administrability** at low rates — the existence proof that the instrument is not impossible, merely expensive relative to yield [1][5]. The Swiss evidence cuts both ways, though: reported taxable wealth rises by at least 43% within six years of a 1-percentage-point cut in a canton's wealth-tax rate, with sizeable evasion and mobility components — a very large behavioural response for a tax on the stock [11].

Two mitigations keep this column from reopening the design: first, mortality is certain and the Great Wealth Transfer is already in motion — the "wait" is shorter than it sounds, ~£100bn/year UK flow now and doubling ([`../01-problem/great-wealth-transfer.md`](../01-problem/great-wealth-transfer.md)); second, a **one-off** transition levy (the UK Wealth Tax Commission's 2020 recommendation — one-off, not annual, precisely because of everything in §3 [7]) remains available as a bridge instrument without importing the annual tax's pathologies — an option weighed in [`../15-transition/revenue-bridge.md`](../15-transition/revenue-bridge.md), not part of the core design.

## 5. The billionaire minimum tax: the strongest current version of the annual case

Zucman's minimum effective tax — proposed to the Brazilian G20 presidency in June 2024 ($1bn+ wealth, a 2%-of-wealth minimum *effective* levy, ~3,000 taxpayers worldwide, an estimated $200–250bn a year) and broadened in *We Need to Tax Billionaires* (2025–26) to centimillionaires, with UK revenue put at ~£15bn a year — is deliberately not the classic annual wealth tax this file rejects, and deserves separate engagement [14][15][16]. Three design moves distinguish it: it is a **floor, not a levy** — "a billionaire who already pays the equivalent of 2% of their wealth in income tax would have no extra tax to pay" [16]; its population is a few thousand households, not every family with a farm or a firm; and its enforcement borrows Pillar Two's architecture — beneficial-ownership reporting plus rules letting participating states tax non-participants' undertaxed residents ([`../08-capital-flight/international-coordination.md`](../08-capital-flight/international-coordination.md)). It thereby escapes much of §3's record: no mass annual valuation, no middle-wealth liquidity crush. And its motivating evidence — billionaires' personal taxes running at 0–0.5% of wealth *even in the countries that operated wealth taxes* [16] — is the same regressive-at-the-top diagnosis this project runs on ([`../01-problem/wealth-concentration.md`](../01-problem/wealth-concentration.md)). It has real political momentum: a 2% floor passed the French National Assembly in February 2025 [15].

Zucman also states the sharpest version of the case *against* relying on inheritance taxation, and it must be answered rather than waved at: an annual floor, he argues, "more effectively safeguards progressivity at the top of the wealth distribution, because it ensures that the individuals with the highest ability to pay taxes cannot postpone taxation for years or decades" [16]. Three answers, in ascending order of importance:

1. **The postponement is bounded by mortality** — the one deadline no adviser extends. Under a lifetime-cumulative recipient basis with full gift integration ([`../02-proposal/mechanism.md`](../02-proposal/mechanism.md)), "postponing" selects *which year within one generation* the charge lands, never whether; and with the Great Wealth Transfer cresting, the bound is near-term, not hypothetical ([`../01-problem/great-wealth-transfer.md`](../01-problem/great-wealth-transfer.md)).
2. **The two instruments treat different diseases.** The minimum tax repairs the income tax's regressivity *now*; it does not touch transmission. On Zucman's own figures, top-0.0001% wealth has compounded at 7.5% a year in real terms since 1987 [14] — a 2% floor leaves the dynastic stock growing at roughly 5.5% a year and passing to heirs intact. It makes billionaires pay tax; it does not stop billion-pound fortunes becoming hereditary. Only a transmission cap does that — which is why his own blueprint concedes a progressive inheritance tax as a companion option [16].
3. **Where the floor is strongest — the holding window — is exactly where this design concedes** (§4). A Zucman-style floor is the best current instrument for the honest column's "it bites now" entry, and therefore the natural *external candidate for the transition period*, when this regime's revenue is immature and living concentrations sit untouched ([`../15-transition/revenue-bridge.md`](../15-transition/revenue-bridge.md)). The §6 caution governs the terms: run as a named, visibly time-limited bridge instrument, it complements; bundled permanently, it re-imports the annual form's politics and destroys the "the owner never gets a bill" retention proposition.

The relationship to state plainly in debate: **shared diagnosis, different disease, compatible medicine — in sequence, not in bundle.**

## 6. Why the project declines the hybrid

Piketty's *Capital and Ideology* package runs both taxes together ([`../05-history/piketty.md`](../05-history/piketty.md)); this project deliberately does not, for three reasons. The wealth tax would **destroy the retention proposition** — "live as richly as you like; you just can't found a dynasty" cannot be said by a regime billing the living annually ([`../08-capital-flight/retention-attraction.md`](../08-capital-flight/retention-attraction.md)). It would **import the abolition record** — attaching the OECD's least-survivable tax to the package hands the opposition a proven kill-path ([`../05-history/abolitions.md`](../05-history/abolitions.md)). And it would **validate the slippery-slope attack** — "first inheritance, then your savings" is currently false and answerable ([`../09-objections/slippery-slope.md`](../09-objections/slippery-slope.md)); a bundled wealth levy makes it true. The design principle, consistent across the repo ([`../07-implementation/human-capital.md`](../07-implementation/human-capital.md), [`../08-capital-flight/inbound-wealth.md`](../08-capital-flight/inbound-wealth.md)): adjacent instruments are named, not bundled.

## 7. Debate-room summary

| Challenge | Answer |
|---|---|
| "This is just a wealth tax" | No annual levy, no valuation of any living person's holdings, no bill to anyone who built anything — the tax exists only for the person *receiving* a fortune they didn't create (§1) |
| "Why not a wealth tax instead?" | Because it's the instrument with the worst survival record in the OECD, the one that actually caused the famous tax exiles, and the one that bills the living builder annually instead of the untested heir once (§§2–3) |
| "Saez–Zucman say wealth tax" | Their own annuitised arithmetic makes 2%/year ≈ 45%/generation — the transfer design reaches the same stock harder, with the deadweight loss parked on a decedent (§2); and Piketty moved our way [3] |
| "So the living rich escape entirely?" | For their lifetimes, deliberately, yes — that is the retention design; what never escapes is the transmission (§4 concedes the holding window honestly) |
| "Wealth taxes work in Switzerland" | At low rates, with the forfait, funding cantons — an existence proof of administrability, not of the yields or survivability the endowment requires [1][5] |
| "Valuing everything is an administrative nightmare" | It is — *annually*. That is the case against wealth taxes, and it does not transfer: this tax needs one valuation per generation, at the moment when probate and US basis rules already compel one today (§2) [12][13] |
| "Why not Zucman's 2% minimum tax instead?" | Shared diagnosis, different disease: the floor repairs the income tax's regressivity now, but on Zucman's own figures billionaire wealth compounds at 7.5%/yr — minus a 2% floor, the dynasty still grows and still passes intact. Run it, if at all, as a named transition instrument; the transmission cap is what ends the dynasty (§5) [14][16] |

## Research gaps

- Survey the post-*Moore v. United States* (2024) commentary on the direct-tax/apportionment question for a federal net wealth tax (*Knowlton*'s holding itself is confirmed) [6].
- Quantify the annuitised-equivalence table properly with returns and behavioural response (the naive compounding here overstates equivalence at high rates).
- Whether any jurisdiction has run a *one-off* wealth levy successfully in peacetime — the revenue-bridge option's evidence base [7]. (Japan's 1946–47 capital levy, discussed in [historical-precedents-of-success.md](historical-precedents-of-success.md) §2, is the nearest large-scale case, though under occupation.)
