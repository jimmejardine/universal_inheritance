# Friction and Anchors: What Actually Keeps Wealthy People in Place

**Executive summary.** The flight objection models the wealthy as portfolio capital; the evidence models them as people — late-career, married, business-owning "embedded elites" whose location is fixed by workforce, family, language, healthcare, courts, and status ecosystems that no haven can replicate. The decisive structural point is an asymmetry the folk model misses entirely: the *owner* can move but UK/US-situs assets cannot, and a recipient-based tax with dual nexus therefore requires donor emigration *and* heir emigration *and* asset re-situsing, sustained for decades — a compound improbability, not a single decision.

## Headline findings

- The migration literature's low average elasticities ([evidence.md](evidence.md)) are not anomalies; they are what embeddedness predicts. Young's administrative-data work finds US millionaires *less* migratory than the median taxpayer, and their moves dominated by non-tax motives [1].
- The anchors bind hardest at exactly the life-stage when inheritance-tax planning happens: the median wealth-holder contemplating bequest is elderly, and elasticity of relocation falls steeply with age — with the Moretti–Wilson over-65 billionaire finding as the exception that needs engagement ([evidence.md](evidence.md) §3) [2].
- Under the proposed dual nexus, personal emigration alone achieves nothing for UK-situs wealth; the escape path is a multi-actor, multi-decade project with visible taxable steps at each stage ([`../02-proposal/avoidance.md`](../02-proposal/avoidance.md)).

## The anchor inventory

| Anchor | Mechanism | Why havens can't substitute |
|---|---|---|
| **Operating business & workforce** | Founders' wealth is typically concentrated in one firm with a local workforce, suppliers, customers, and site-specific knowledge | The firm is the wealth; relocating it destroys value. Selling it to move crystallises tax and severs identity |
| **Family and grandchildren** | Adult children's careers, grandchildren's schools, ageing parents | The single most-cited relocation deterrent in wealth-manager surveys (to verify); grandchildren do not move to Monaco |
| **Language and culture** | Daily life, humour, media, friendship networks formed over decades | Havens offer expatriate enclaves, not belonging |
| **Healthcare** | Trusted specialists and continuity of care at the age when it matters most | Micro-jurisdictions import tertiary care; the anxious 75-year-old knows it |
| **Courts and property security** | Centuries-deep commercial law, enforceable contracts, independent judiciary | The UAE/Caribbean discount is precisely here; wealth ultimately depends on the legal system it sits in |
| **Status ecosystem** | Honours, boards, chairs, galas, philanthropy, club memberships, university connections — position built over a lifetime | Status is jurisdiction-specific and non-portable; a knighthood has no Dubai equivalent (see [retention-attraction.md](retention-attraction.md)) |
| **Physical safety & stability** | Political stability, personal security, low kidnap/extortion risk | Genuine in the core havens, but so is the fishbowl: small societies, no anonymity |
| **Ageing itself** | Relocation propensity declines sharply with age across all populations | The tax binds at death; the decision window closes as the anchors tighten |

The anchors compound: each is individually survivable, but the wealthy household that is simultaneously willing to leave its business, children, doctors, courts, and social position for a tax saving its members will never personally enjoy (the saving accrues to heirs) is rare — and the [evidence](evidence.md) suggests exactly that rarity.

## The situs asymmetry: the owner moves, the assets don't

The deepest flaw in the folk model is treating "the wealthy leave" as equivalent to "the wealth leaves." Under the proposal's dual nexus ([`../02-proposal/avoidance.md`](../02-proposal/avoidance.md) §5), tax applies if *either* the recipient is UK-resident *or* the property is UK-situated. Consider what each asset class actually does when its owner emigrates:

| Asset class | Can it follow the owner? | Situs consequence |
|---|---|---|
| UK land and buildings | No — physically fixed | Always in charge, whoever holds it, wherever they live |
| UK-registered company shares | No — situs at the register | In charge; re-registering means re-domiciling the company, with its own exit charges |
| UK operating business | Only by relocating operations | Workforce, premises, customer base resist; partial moves leave a taxable permanent establishment |
| UK pensions, gilts, bank deposits | Partially, by liquidation | Liquidation is visible, often taxable, and reported under CRS ([international-coordination.md](international-coordination.md)) |
| Portfolio securities (foreign) | Yes | The genuinely mobile fraction — but taxed anyway if any recipient is UK-resident |
| Art, crypto, bearer assets | Yes | The hard tail, handled by [`../07-implementation/digital-and-novel-assets.md`](../07-implementation/digital-and-novel-assets.md) and enforcement tools |

For the large UK fortunes the tax targets — concentrated in land, housing, and UK business equity (composition to verify against ONS wealth data) — the majority of the base is situs-anchored. Emigration converts the problem from "avoid the tax" into "liquidate and re-deploy the estate abroad," a project that takes years, pays transaction costs and capital-gains tax, appears in CRS data, and must be *completed* — a half-moved estate is a fully taxable one on its UK remainder.

## The compound improbability

Escaping the proposed design in full requires **all** of the following, jointly:

1. **The donor emigrates** — and does so more than 10 years before death (the exit tail — [exit-tax-design.md](exit-tax-design.md)), i.e. typically in their 60s or earlier, paying the deemed-receipt exit charge on unrealised accrual at departure.
2. **Every intended heir also emigrates** — because the tax is recipient-based, a single UK-resident child is a fully taxable recipient regardless of where the donor died. The heirs must also clear their own 10-year tails before receipt.
3. **The assets are re-sitused** — UK land sold, UK companies re-domiciled or sold, proceeds re-deployed offshore, with each step taxable and CRS-visible.
4. **The arrangement holds for decades** — no heir returns for career, marriage, or preference; no premature death inside a tail; no intervening gift to a resident family member.

Each step has independent probability well below one; the product is small. Compare the single-step escape available against the abolished regimes catalogued in [`../05-history/abolitions.md`](../05-history/abolitions.md) — Kamprad simply moved, once, alone, and Sweden's estate-based, no-tail, no-situs-backstop design let that single act suffice. The proposal is engineered so that no single act suffices.

## What friction does *not* do

Honesty requires the converse. Friction does not stop: (a) the young founder who builds abroad from the start — a real long-run formation-location margin, addressed in [retention-attraction.md](retention-attraction.md) and [game-theory.md](game-theory.md); (b) the ultra-mobile 0.01% with multi-jurisdictional lives already in place, for whom the anchors were cut long ago — the Moretti–Wilson population, requiring the [exit-tax-design.md](exit-tax-design.md) and coordination tools; (c) portable-asset concealment, an enforcement problem rather than a migration one ([`../07-implementation/enforcement.md`](../07-implementation/enforcement.md)). Friction is the first line of defence, not the whole army.

## Research gaps

- UK-specific decomposition of top-1% estate composition by situs-mobility class (ONS Wealth and Assets Survey; HMRC IHT statistics).
- Survey evidence on stated relocation deterrents among UK HNWIs (existing wealth-manager surveys are self-interested; independent replication needed).
- Age profile of actual UK tax-motivated emigrants post-2017 and post-2025 non-dom reforms (Advani et al. follow-up work).
