# Composition and Concealment: How Billionaire Wealth Is Actually Held, and Who Would See It Move

**Executive summary.** [confiscatory-stakes.md](confiscatory-stakes.md) assumes billionaire-tier families attempt structured escape and asks what attempting achieves; this file supplies the empirical layer under that analysis — what the top-tier balance sheet actually consists of, and, class by class, who would see a movement. The verified composition data (UBS, Altrata, Forbes methodology, the offshore-wealth literature) show that billionaire wealth is dominated by registered, titled, and publicly-disclosed asset forms — concentrated company equity above all — while the genuinely concealable classes (offshore cash, art, gold, crypto) are the smallest slice of the balance sheet and the most capacity-constrained. The "secret offshore move" at billionaire scale is therefore largely mythical: the realistic escape is the loud, slow, taxable liquidation the companion file models. What secrecy still genuinely buys — concealment of the already-liquid slice, where the top 0.01% demonstrably evade ~25% of their taxes — is stated at full strength as an enforcement problem, not argued away.

## Headline findings

- **The typical top-tier fortune is mostly a registered stake in the family's own company.** Forbes builds its billionaire valuations from public regulatory filings for listed stakes and comparable-company estimates for private ones [3] — the wealth is *measured* from disclosure documents because it *lives* in disclosed form. Altrata's UHNW allocation data put public equities at roughly 45–50% and private business holdings at roughly 25–30% of older-generation ultra-wealthy portfolios, with liquid assets (cash and fixed income) only ~10–15% [2].
- **Offshore holdings are real, large in aggregate, and violently concentrated — but small against the total.** Offshore household financial wealth is estimated at ~$13.25tn (2023), about 12.5% of world GDP [6], up from Zucman's original ~8%-of-household-financial-wealth / $7.6tn estimate [5]; roughly 50% of the offshore wealth uncovered in leaks and amnesties belongs to the richest 0.01% of households [7]. Set against ~$15.8tn of billionaire wealth alone [1] and ~$60tn of UHNW wealth [2], even the whole offshore stock could not house the world's top fortunes — and most of it is *reported* offshore wealth post-CRS, not hidden.
- **The visibility gradient runs the wrong way for the escaper.** The largest asset classes are the most watched: a listed founder-stake sale is public by construction (SEC Schedule 13D/G above 5%, Form 4 insider sales within two business days [8][9]; UK TR-1 notifications at 3% and every 1% thereafter [10]); private companies sit on Companies House with PSC registers; land sits on title registries. The genuinely opaque classes — freeport art (Geneva alone held ~1.2m works, ~$100bn [12]), gold, self-custodied crypto — are the thinnest slice, and even they leak: insurance schedules betray art values ([`../07-implementation/valuation.md`](../07-implementation/valuation.md) §4), and crypto is permanently public on-chain with CARF closing the off-ramps ([international-coordination.md](international-coordination.md)).
- **Family offices are the coordination layer, not a hiding place.** Deloitte counts ~8,030 single family offices managing ~$3.1tn (family wealth ~$5.5tn), projected to ~10,720 offices and ~$5.4tn AUM by 2030 [4] — professionally administered, bank-custodied, CRS-reportable structures. (The larger "$6–8tn" figures sometimes quoted were not verifiable and are not used.)
- **The honest counterweights, at full strength:** the already-liquid slice *can* be hidden, and at the top it demonstrably is — the ~25%-evasion finding [7] is this repo's standing concession ([`../09-objections/avoidance.md`](../09-objections/avoidance.md)); and visibility governs the *stock*, not the flow — multi-year sell-downs and derivative monetisation (prepaid variable forwards and their kin [13]) can convert visible equity into portable value slowly, and transfer economic exposure without moving title (§3).

## 1. The composition of billionaire wealth, verified

**(a) The concentrated stake.** UBS's Billionaire Ambitions Report 2025 puts global billionaire wealth at a record USD 15.8tn across nearly 3,000 individuals, of whom roughly 2,059 are self-made; 860 multi-generational billionaires oversee USD 4.7tn [1]. The report's sector decomposition (consumer/retail USD 3.1tn, financial services USD 2.3tn, industrials USD 1.7tn) is a decomposition of *operating-company* wealth: billionaire fortunes are counted by attributing companies to their owners. Forbes' methodology makes the same point operationally: public holdings are marked to market from SEC filings; private businesses are valued from revenue/profit estimates against listed comparables with a 10% liquidity discount, cross-checked against secondary-market pricing [3]. No verified source publishes a single "average % of billionaire net worth in the primary business holding" — that precise statistic does not exist in citable form and is not claimed here — but the structure of every major rich-list methodology is itself the evidence: the fortune *is* the stake, plus a residual.

For the UHNW tier generally, Altrata's allocation analysis (population ~510,810 individuals, ~$59.8tn, mid-2025) gives older-generation portfolios as roughly: public equities 45–50%, private business/direct private equity 25–30%, real estate 15–20%, liquid assets (cash and fixed income) 10–15%, alternatives including hedge funds, venture, crypto and art 5–10% [2]. These are article-grade approximations, not audited balance sheets, and billionaires are more concentrated in the core holding than the UHNW average — but the ordering is robust across sources: **registered equity first, titled property second, liquid and exotic assets a distant tail.** The often-cited older Wealth-X claim that liquid assets run to ~35% of UHNW net worth could not be verified in current Altrata editions and is not relied on.

**(b) The offshore stock.** Zucman's foundational estimate put ~8% of global household financial wealth — about $7.6tn — in tax havens [5]; the EU Tax Observatory / Skatteforsk Atlas of the Offshore World updates this to ~$13.25tn in 2023, about 12.5% of world GDP, of which roughly $3.55tn remains untaxed/unreported post-CRS [6]. Concentration at the top is the sharpest fact in the literature: in the Alstadsæter–Johannesen–Zucman leak-and-amnesty data, **about 50% of uncovered offshore wealth belongs to the richest 0.01% of households**, and that cohort evades about 25% of its taxes, against under 5% detected in random audits across the general population [7] (repo-verified in [`../09-objections/avoidance.md`](../09-objections/avoidance.md)). Both halves of that finding matter here: offshore wealth is a top-0.01% phenomenon — *and* it is bounded. Fifty percent of a ~$13tn stock, spread across the world's top 0.01% (hundreds of thousands of households), is a large evasion problem and a small share of top-tier balance sheets: the same households hold tens of trillions in registered onshore form.

**(c) UK-specific.** HMRC estate data show estates above £1m increasingly composed of securities rather than housing (repo-verified — [friction-and-anchors.md](friction-and-anchors.md) [5]); the situs-mobility decomposition of the £100m+ tiers remains the named micro-simulation task of [confiscatory-stakes.md](confiscatory-stakes.md) §2. Sunday Times Rich List sector tables were again not verifiable to this repo's citation standard in this pass and are not used.

## 2. The visibility gradient: what can move secretly

The flight debate treats "wealth" as an undifferentiated fluid. It is not. Each asset class has a specific set of watchers, and a movement is secret only if *every* watcher misses it.

**Listed founder stakes.** Disclosure is triple-layered and public. In the US, crossing 5% beneficial ownership requires a Schedule 13D within five business days (13G for passive investors), with material changes — including sales — amended within two business days [8]; insiders (officers, directors, >10% holders) report every transaction on Form 4 within two business days [9]. In the UK, DTR 5 requires TR-1 notification to the FCA and the issuer at 3% of voting rights and at every 1% threshold thereafter, up or down [10]. Below the thresholds sits the market itself: a block sale of a major stake moves price, hits the tape, and is reported by the exchange — the disposal of a billionaire's core holding is *public by construction* (judgement, but barely: it is what the disclosure regime exists to guarantee). There is no secret way to sell £5bn of a FTSE company.

**Private operating companies.** Domestically unconcealable: Companies House filings, the PSC (persons of significant control) register, and — for foreign entities holding UK property — the Register of Overseas Entities identify ownership; any genuine sale requires a buyer whose diligence, financing, and merger-control filings generate their own paper trail. A billionaire cannot quietly dispose of a business any more than quietly dispose of a listed stake; he can only dispose of it *slowly*.

**Titled property.** Land registries record every transfer; the UK looked through offshore envelopes over residential property in 2017 and now requires overseas-entity registration (repo-covered — [confiscatory-stakes.md](confiscatory-stakes.md) §2, [`../16-second-order/foreign-ownership.md`](../16-second-order/foreign-ownership.md)).

**Fund positions and custody accounts.** Here the *public* loses sight but the *state* does not. A transfer of an interest in a Cayman fund is invisible to journalists — but under the CRS, equity and debt interests in investment entities are themselves financial accounts, reportable by the entity to the holder's residence jurisdiction [11], and bank/custody accounts ride the same rails: 171m+ accounts covering ~EUR 13tn were exchanged in 2024 alone (repo-verified — [international-coordination.md](international-coordination.md) [1]). The visibility is one-eyed — the taxman sees, the public does not — which is exactly the eye the tax needs.

**Trusts and wrappers.** Look-through, registration-or-blockage, and recipient liability are the repo's standing machinery ([`../07-implementation/trusts-and-vehicles.md`](../07-implementation/trusts-and-vehicles.md)); TRS/FATCA/CRS controlling-person reporting supplies the data. A trust relocates administration, not visibility.

**Art, gold, freeports.** The genuinely opaque tier. The Geneva Freeport was estimated (2013) to hold ~1.2m artworks, with the facility's art valued near $100bn; Swiss regulation has since tightened — inventories identifying owners required from 2009, anti-money-laundering rules and a six-month customs storage limit from 2016 — but freeport contents remain undisclosed to any tax authority by default [12]. This is real concealment capacity. Its limits are equally real: the class is a single-digit share of top-tier portfolios [2]; it is illiquid and yields nothing; moving billions into it is itself a conspicuous market event; and the enforcement answer is cheap — owners insure what they hide, and the regime's insurance/collateral cross-check ([`../07-implementation/valuation.md`](../07-implementation/valuation.md) §4) plus provenance and auction databases turn the owner's own paperwork against the concealment.

**Crypto.** The visibility paradox: self-custodied crypto is the one major asset that can cross a border in a memorised passphrase — and every movement is recorded permanently on a public ledger. Pseudonymity is the only shield, and it fails at the fiat boundary: CARF extends CRS-style reporting to crypto intermediaries, with first exchanges from 2027 across 52 jurisdictions and 75 committed (repo-verified — [international-coordination.md](international-coordination.md) [7]; [`../07-implementation/digital-and-novel-assets.md`](../07-implementation/digital-and-novel-assets.md)). Concealment at scale requires never touching a regulated off-ramp again — a constraint that grows, not shrinks, with the amount concealed.

### The punchline table

Shares are illustrative, from the §1(a) allocation data (article-grade; billionaires more concentrated in the core stake than the UHNW average):

| Asset class | Share of typical top-tier balance sheet | Who sees a movement | Concealable? |
|---|---|---|---|
| Listed equity (incl. founder stake) | ~45–50% (higher for listed-company founders) | SEC 13D/G + Form 4 / FCA TR-1; the market tape; the press | **No — public by construction** |
| Private operating company | ~25–30% | Companies House/PSC, ROE, buyer diligence, competition filings | **No — domestically unconcealable** |
| Real estate | ~15–20% | Land registries; overseas-entity registers | **No** |
| Bank, custody, fund positions | ~10–15% | Public: no. Residence-state tax authority: yes (CRS, incl. investment-entity interests [11]) | **From the public, yes; from the state, no — absent evasion** |
| Art, gold, collectibles (freeports) | within the ~5–10% alternatives slice | Insurers, shippers, auction houses; otherwise nobody | **Yes — genuinely opaque; capacity- and liquidity-constrained** |
| Crypto (self-custodied) | small fraction of alternatives | Everyone (on-chain, permanently) and no one (pseudonymous); CARF at every off-ramp | **Pseudonymously, until first regulated touch** |

The gradient is almost exactly inverted from the folk model: **concealability is highest where the money is least, and lowest where the money is most.**

## 3. The synthesis for the flight analysis (labelled argument)

The following is this file's argument from the verified facts above, not itself a measured finding.

**The secret offshore move at billionaire scale is largely mythical.** Billionaire wealth is disproportionately held in the *most visible* forms capital takes — registered concentrated equity and titled assets, watched by statutory disclosure regimes, registries, markets, and counterparties. The genuinely concealable classes are the smallest slice of the balance sheet and cannot absorb top-tier fortunes without severe price and liquidity degradation — the asset-side twin of the absorption constraint in [confiscatory-stakes.md](confiscatory-stakes.md) §2b. It follows that the realistic top-tier escape is the one the companion file models: the **loud, slow, taxable conversion** — sell the stake (publicly), pay CGT, take paper, remove the people — not a midnight wire transfer. A billionaire's fortune cannot slip out of the country, because it is not the kind of thing that slips.

**What secrecy can still do — two concessions at full strength.** First, it can conceal the *already-liquid slice*: the AJZ finding is precisely that the top 0.01% successfully hid enough offshore to evade ~25% of their taxes in the pre-CRS era [7], and the untaxed offshore residual is still ~$3.55tn [6]. On a £1bn balance sheet with 10–15% liquid, that is a £100–150m concealment surface — material, criminal under this design (evasion, not avoidance), and an enforcement-resourcing problem of exactly the kind [`../07-implementation/enforcement.md`](../07-implementation/enforcement.md) prices, not a refutation of the design. Second, secrecy protects *new* wealth positioned offshore from formation — the founder who builds the next company abroad, outside the situs net from day one. That is the formation-margin residual already conceded in [game-theory.md](game-theory.md) and conceded again here: this file's visibility analysis applies to the existing stock and says nothing about wealth that never becomes UK-sitused.

**The stock/flow honesty paragraph.** Visibility applies to the stock; sophisticated restructuring works on the flow. A decade of gradual sell-downs — each tranche disclosed, none newsworthy — converts a visible stake into portable paper *visibly but boringly*, and the disclosure regime guarantees only that the conversion can be seen, not that it can be stopped: under this design it is legal, CGT-paying, and priced in [confiscatory-stakes.md](confiscatory-stakes.md) §1. Derivatives go further: a prepaid variable forward delivers most of a stake's value in cash up front while deferring gain recognition and leaving title (and the disclosure obligations) with the holder — the standard monetisation technique for concentrated founder positions, litigated at scale in *Estate of McKelvey* (Monster.com founder; $102.4m of constructive gain on extension) [13] — and cash-settled swaps can transfer economic exposure with, at most, a 13D line-item and no title movement [8]. The design's answer is the receipt definition plus substance-over-form: what the ledger taxes is value received, however wrapped ([`../02-proposal/avoidance.md`](../02-proposal/avoidance.md) §3A), and a derivative that delivers a stake's economics to an heir is a receipt of that value — the full loan-and-derivative treatment, including why buy-borrow-die itself collapses under the receipts design, is [`../07-implementation/buy-borrow-die.md`](../07-implementation/buy-borrow-die.md). The honest cost of that answer: it converts a registry-lookup enforcement problem into an economic-substance enforcement problem — the hardest kind, fought contract by contract against the best-advised taxpayers (§4).

**Enforcement prioritisation falls out of the table.** The §2 gradient is a map of where enforcement money buys revenue: near-zero marginal effort on the ~90% of the top-tier balance sheet that watches itself (registries, disclosure regimes, CRS feeds into the mesh), concentrated effort on the two genuine gaps — the liquid-slice evasion surface (audit, whistleblowers, no-limitation assessment) and derivative-based economic transfer (specialist financial-instruments capability). This line is now cross-referenced from [`../07-implementation/enforcement.md`](../07-implementation/enforcement.md).

## 4. Honest limits

- **Composition data is survey/estimate-grade.** Forbes and UBS estimate; Altrata's allocation ranges are article-grade approximations for the UHNW population, not billionaire-specific audited data; no citable source gives the average share of billionaire net worth in the primary holding, and the £100m+ UK tier decomposition remains an open micro-simulation input ([confiscatory-stakes.md](confiscatory-stakes.md) §7).
- **Offshore-stock estimates are inherently uncertain.** The method (anomalies in international investment positions) measures what hides from statistics; the $13.25tn and the ~50%/top-0.01% concentration are best-available estimates from leaks, amnesties, and macro gaps, not censuses [5][6][7].
- **Derivative-based economic transfer is the hardest enforcement frontier.** The substance-over-form answer is stated, not demonstrated: no jurisdiction has run receipt-recharacterisation of monetisation structures against a 100% incentive, and the SEC's own decision to leave most cash-settled derivatives outside beneficial ownership [8] shows how contested the visibility boundary is even for disclosure, let alone taxation.
- **None of this measures intent.** The visibility gradient says what *could* move secretly, not what would; the behavioural layer belongs to [confiscatory-stakes.md](confiscatory-stakes.md) and [evidence.md](evidence.md), and the §3 synthesis inherits all of their stated uncertainty.
