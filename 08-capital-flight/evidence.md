# The Empirical Evidence on Tax-Driven Migration of the Wealthy

**Executive summary.** The natural-experiment literature finds that tax-driven migration of the wealthy is real but small on average, highly concentrated among the young, foreign, and footloose, and largest in the one study most relevant to this project — Moretti and Wilson's work on billionaires and US state estate taxes, which is engaged directly below rather than buried. The honest reading is asymmetric: income-tax elasticities support the thesis strongly; death-tax elasticities are higher because decades of planning time are available — which is precisely why the proposed design taxes the *recipient* with a *dual nexus*, so that planning time helps the taxpayer less than folk intuition assumes.

## Headline findings

- The canonical US millionaire-migration studies (Young & Varner on New Jersey; Young, Varner, Lurie & Prisinzano on federal returns) find migration responses to top income-tax rises near zero — on the order of a low single-digit share of the affected population, with net revenue effects trivially small relative to the tax raised (figures to verify) [1][2].
- The high-elasticity findings all share a profile: *young, internationally famous, thin local attachments*. Kleven, Landais & Saez find near-unit elasticity for foreign footballers; Akcigit, Baslandze & Stantcheva find superstar *foreign* inventors highly mobile while domestic inventors barely move [4][5][6].
- The single most inconvenient study for this thesis is Moretti & Wilson (2023) on the Forbes 400: billionaires' state of residence responds meaningfully to state estate taxes, especially past age 65 — yet even there, the authors find that for almost all states the estate tax *still raises more than the migration loses* (to verify) [7].
- Wealth-tax episodes (Sweden pre-2007, Norway 2022–23, Spain's regional divergence) show visible flight where the tax was annual, poorly designed, or announced without exit rules; the Norwegian case is the modern cautionary tale but its media framing outran the data [8][9][10].
- Death taxes differ structurally from income taxes: the taxpayer has decades to plan. This cuts both ways — more time to emigrate, but also more time for anchors (business, family, ageing) to bind, and more time for a residency tail and situs rules to apply (see [friction-and-anchors.md](friction-and-anchors.md)).

## 1. Income-tax migration: the low-elasticity core

**New Jersey and California.** Young & Varner (2011) examined New Jersey's 2004 "millionaire tax" using administrative tax records and found semi-elasticities close to zero: the raised rate produced a barely detectable outflow against a large and stable millionaire population (to verify) [1]. Varner & Young's follow-up work on California's Proposition 30 top-rate rise and the 2005 mental-health surcharge reached similar conclusions [3]. Young et al. (2016), using thirteen years of federal returns covering effectively all US million-dollar earners, found millionaires *less* migratory than the general population; where they did move, the tax gradient explained a small fraction of moves, mostly to Florida specifically rather than to low-tax states generally [2]. Young's synthesis, *The Myth of Millionaire Tax Flight* (2017), coins the frame this project adopts: the rich are "embedded elites" — late-career, married, dual-earner, business-owning — not free-floating capital [3].

**Star scientists.** Moretti & Wilson (2017) is the strongest *income-tax* counter-finding: star scientists' location choices respond significantly to state personal and corporate tax differentials, with long-run elasticities that are economically meaningful (to verify) [4]. Note the population: individually recruited, nationally marketed professionals whose employer often pays relocation — closer to the footballer case than to the settled business owner.

## 2. The superstar studies: where elasticity is high

| Study | Population | Finding (directional; figures to verify) |
|---|---|---|
| Kleven, Landais & Saez (2013) [5] | European footballers | Foreign players' location highly tax-elastic (elasticity around 1); domestic players much less so |
| Kleven, Landais, Saez & Schultz (2014) [6] | High earners in Denmark | The preferential foreigner scheme roughly doubled the number of highly paid foreigners; elasticity for *foreigners* very high |
| Akcigit, Baslandze & Stantcheva (2016) [11] | Superstar inventors, 8 countries | Foreign superstar inventors strongly mobile; domestic inventors' elasticity an order of magnitude smaller |
| Kleven et al. (2020), JEP survey [12] | Synthesis | Migration elasticities "extremely high" for narrow footloose groups, low for the general wealthy population |

The pattern is consistent: mobility is a property of *life-stage and attachment*, not of wealth per se. An inheritance tax binds at exactly the life-stage — late age, settled family, illiquid business — where measured elasticity is lowest.

## 3. The estate-tax study that must be engaged: Moretti & Wilson on billionaires

Moretti & Wilson's study of the Forbes 400 and US state estate taxes (AEJ: Applied, 2023) found that after the 2001 federal reform made state estate taxes a real differential, billionaires became significantly more likely to reside in non-estate-tax states, with the effect concentrated among those aged 65+ — consistent with deliberate end-of-life tax planning (magnitudes to verify) [7]. Three points of honest engagement:

1. **It is the right population.** Billionaires are the target of this proposal, and they responded. This is the strongest empirical support for the flight objection anywhere in the literature and [`../04-case-against/capital-flight.md`](../04-case-against/capital-flight.md) is correct to lead with it.
2. **The treatment was uniquely escapable.** US states share language, law, currency, family proximity, and federal citizenship; moving from Connecticut to Florida costs almost none of the anchors catalogued in [friction-and-anchors.md](friction-and-anchors.md). International flight from a national tax is a categorically harder move — the Australian intra-federation cascade in [`../05-history/abolitions.md`](../05-history/abolitions.md) makes the same point. This is also why the proposal rejects sub-national assignment outright.
3. **Even so, the tax paid.** The authors' own cost-benefit finding is that for the large majority of states, retaining the estate tax was revenue-positive despite billionaire mobility, because one-off estate receipts from those who stay exceed the income-tax stream lost from those who leave (to verify) [7]. The inconvenient study, read to its conclusion, supports taxing anyway — and supports it more strongly for a national tax with an exit tail than for a state tax with none.

## 4. Wealth-tax episodes: Sweden, Norway, Spain, the UK non-dom reform

- **Sweden.** The annual wealth tax (abolished 2007) and the inheritance tax (2004) generated the celebrity-exile narrative — Kamprad, the Rausings — documented in [`../05-history/abolitions.md`](../05-history/abolitions.md). Henrekson & Waldenström's account attributes flight less to the rate than to the regime's asymmetries: closely-held firms punished, listed wealth favoured, no exit charge [8].
- **Norway 2022–23.** After the Støre government raised the wealth-tax rate and valuation bases, media reported dozens of wealthy Norwegians — including industrialist Kjell Inge Røkke — relocating to Switzerland, with widely circulated counts of 30–80+ movers (media figures; treat cautiously and verify against tax-registry data) [9]. Two lessons survive scrutiny: the movers were disproportionately the *most* mobile fraction of the top 0.01%, and Norway's exit-tax rules at announcement contained deferral features that made leaving cheap — subsequently tightened in 2024. The episode is a sequencing failure (rate before exit architecture), not proof of unmanageable elasticity; see [`../15-transition/announcement-effects.md`](../15-transition/announcement-effects.md).
- **Spain.** Regional wealth-tax divergence (Madrid's 100% credit) produced measurable internal relocation of declared residence — Agrawal, Foremny & Martínez-Toledano find substantial responses, again in an intra-national, zero-anchor-cost setting (to verify) [10].
- **UK non-doms.** Advani, Burgherr & Summers's work on the 2017 deemed-domicile reform found emigration responses among affected non-doms far smaller than lobby predictions — most stayed and paid (magnitudes to verify) [13]. Their caution against Henley & Partners-style "millionaire exodus" counts — which infer migration from social-media profiles and investment-migration industry data — applies with force to the 2024–25 non-dom abolition coverage; the industry producing the exodus statistics sells the exits [13].

## 5. Elasticities by tax type: the synthesis table

The literature is often cited as one undifferentiated "tax migration" finding. It is three distinct literatures with different populations, planning horizons, and policy levers:

| Tax type | Population studied | Directional elasticity (figures to verify) | Planning horizon | Principal design lever |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Top income tax (general) | US state millionaires [1][2] | Near zero | Annual; move must precede income | None needed; friction suffices |
| Top income tax (superstars) | Footballers, foreign high earners, inventors [5][6][11] | High (~1 for foreigners) | Short; career-portable | Not this proposal's population |
| Annual wealth tax | Swedish, Norwegian, Spanish episodes [8][9][10] | Moderate; concentrated in ultra-rich; high intra-nationally | Ongoing annual bill creates standing exit incentive | Exit tax + valuation design; or avoid annual wealth taxes entirely |
| State estate tax (intra-federal) | Forbes 400 billionaires [7] | Meaningful, esp. 65+ | Decades; zero-anchor-cost moves | National-level assignment (adopted by this proposal) |
| National death/receipts tax (international) | No direct estimate exists | Bounded above by the intra-federal case; likely far lower | Decades — but tails and situs rules consume the horizon | Dual nexus, 10-year tail, recipient basis |

Two readings of this table matter. *For the objection*: the bottom-right cell is empirically empty — no one has measured international migration response to a recipient-based national inheritance tax, so confident claims of mass flight are extrapolation, not evidence. *Against complacency*: the same emptiness means the proposal cannot claim measured safety either; it claims bounded risk, with the bounds set by the intra-federal worst case and the design levers listed.

## 6. Why death-tax elasticities differ — and what that implies

Death taxes allow decades of planning, so measured responsiveness among those who *do* plan (Moretti–Wilson's over-65 billionaires) is higher than income-tax studies suggest. But the same decades work for the tax authority under the proposed design: the [`../02-proposal/avoidance.md`](../02-proposal/avoidance.md) architecture (dual nexus, 10-year tail, deemed-receipt exit charge, lifetime gift aggregation) means successful escape requires the donor's early emigration *and* the heirs' emigration *and* asset re-situsing, sustained over decades without any intervening return, death, or transfer — the compound-improbability argument developed in [friction-and-anchors.md](friction-and-anchors.md). The elasticity that matters is not "will a billionaire move?" but "will an entire family system re-domicile itself and its assets for thirty years?" — and no study finds evidence on that harder question.

## Research gaps

- Commission or locate registry-based verification of the Norwegian 2022–23 outflow (Statistics Norway / Skatteetaten data) against the media counts.
- Obtain the precise elasticities and revenue cost-benefit tables from Moretti & Wilson (2023) and reproduce the calculation under a national tax with a 10-year tail.
- Advani et al.'s updated estimates on the 2025 UK non-dom abolition as data emerges — the nearest live experiment to this proposal's residence rules.
- No study measures *recipient-side* migration elasticity (heirs moving to escape an inheritance receipts tax); Ireland's CAT is the natural setting.
