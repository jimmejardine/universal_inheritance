# The Empirical Evidence on Tax-Driven Migration of the Wealthy

**Executive summary.** The natural-experiment literature finds that tax-driven migration of the wealthy is real but small on average, highly concentrated among the young, foreign, and footloose, and largest in the one study most relevant to this project — Moretti and Wilson's work on billionaires and US state estate taxes, which is engaged directly below rather than buried. The honest reading is asymmetric: income-tax elasticities support the thesis strongly; death-tax elasticities are higher because decades of planning time are available — which is precisely why the proposed design taxes the *recipient* with a *dual nexus*, so that planning time helps the taxpayer less than folk intuition assumes.

## Headline findings

- The canonical US millionaire-migration studies (Young & Varner on New Jersey; Young, Varner, Lurie & Prisinzano on federal returns) find migration responses to top income-tax rises near zero — semi-elasticities generally below 0.1 in New Jersey, and millionaires migrating between states at a *lower* annual rate (2.4%) than the general population (2.9%) — with net revenue effects trivially small relative to the roughly $1 billion a year the New Jersey tax raised [1][2].
- The high-elasticity findings all share a profile: *young, internationally famous, thin local attachments*. Kleven, Landais & Saez find near-unit elasticity for foreign footballers; Akcigit, Baslandze & Stantcheva find superstar *foreign* inventors highly mobile while domestic inventors barely move [4][5][6].
- The single most inconvenient study for this thesis is Moretti & Wilson (2023) on the Forbes 400: billionaires' state of residence responds strongly to state estate taxes — 35% of local billionaires leave estate-tax states, with sensitivity rising significantly with age — yet even there, the authors find that the revenue benefit of the estate tax *exceeds the cost of migration for the vast majority of states* [7].
- Wealth-tax episodes (Sweden pre-2007, Norway 2022–23, Spain's regional divergence) show visible flight where the tax was annual, poorly designed, or announced without exit rules; the Norwegian case is the modern cautionary tale but its media framing outran the data [8][9][10].
- Death taxes differ structurally from income taxes: the taxpayer has decades to plan. This cuts both ways — more time to emigrate, but also more time for anchors (business, family, ageing) to bind, and more time for a residency tail and situs rules to apply (see [friction-and-anchors.md](friction-and-anchors.md)).

## 1. Income-tax migration: the low-elasticity core

**New Jersey and California.** Young & Varner (2011) examined New Jersey's 2004 "millionaire tax" — a 2.6-percentage-point rise in the top rate, to 8.97% — using administrative tax records and found little responsiveness, with semi-elasticities generally below 0.1: a barely detectable outflow against a large and stable millionaire population, while the tax raised roughly $1 billion per year [1]. Varner & Young's follow-up work on California's Proposition 30 top-rate rise and the 2005 mental-health surcharge reached similar conclusions [3]. Young et al. (2016), using thirteen years of federal returns covering effectively all US million-dollar earners, found millionaires *less* migratory than the general population; where they did move, the tax gradient explained a small fraction of moves, mostly to Florida specifically rather than to low-tax states generally [2]. Young's synthesis, *The Myth of Millionaire Tax Flight* (2017), coins the frame this project adopts: the rich are "embedded elites" — late-career, married, dual-earner, business-owning — not free-floating capital [3].

**Star scientists.** Moretti & Wilson (2017) is the strongest *income-tax* counter-finding: star scientists' location choices respond significantly to state tax differentials, with long-run mobility elasticities of 1.8 with respect to personal income taxes and 1.9 with respect to state corporate income taxes [4]. Note the population: individually recruited, nationally marketed professionals whose employer often pays relocation — closer to the footballer case than to the settled business owner.

## 2. The superstar studies: where elasticity is high

| Study | Population | Finding |
|---|---|---|
| Kleven, Landais & Saez (2013) [5] | European footballers | Foreign players' location highly tax-elastic (elasticity to the net-of-tax rate around 1); domestic players far less so (around 0.15) |
| Kleven, Landais, Saez & Schultz (2014) [6] | High earners in Denmark | The preferential foreigner scheme doubled the number of highly paid foreigners relative to ineligible groups; elasticity for *foreigners* very high |
| Akcigit, Baslandze & Stantcheva (2016) [11] | Superstar inventors, 8 countries | Foreign superstar inventors strongly mobile (elasticity around 1); domestic inventors' elasticity around 0.03 |
| Kleven et al. (2020), JEP survey [12] | Synthesis | Elasticities vary greatly by population: high for high-income workers with little location-specific human capital, low for settled general populations |

The pattern is consistent: mobility is a property of *life-stage and attachment*, not of wealth per se. An inheritance tax binds at exactly the life-stage — late age, settled family, illiquid business — where measured elasticity is lowest.

## 3. The estate-tax study that must be engaged: Moretti & Wilson on billionaires

Moretti & Wilson's study of the Forbes 400 and US state estate taxes (AEJ: Economic Policy, 2023) found that after the 2001 federal reform made state estate taxes a real differential, billionaires became significantly more likely to reside in non-estate-tax states — 35% of local billionaires leave states with an estate tax — and their sensitivity to the estate tax increases significantly with age, consistent with deliberate end-of-life tax planning [7]. Three points of honest engagement:

1. **It is the right population.** Billionaires are the target of this proposal, and they responded. This is the strongest empirical support for the flight objection anywhere in the literature and [`../04-case-against/capital-flight.md`](../04-case-against/capital-flight.md) is correct to lead with it.
2. **The treatment was uniquely escapable.** US states share language, law, currency, family proximity, and federal citizenship; moving from Connecticut to Florida costs almost none of the anchors catalogued in [friction-and-anchors.md](friction-and-anchors.md). International flight from a national tax is a categorically harder move — the Australian intra-federation cascade in [`../05-history/abolitions.md`](../05-history/abolitions.md) makes the same point. This is also why the proposal rejects sub-national assignment outright.
3. **Even so, the tax paid.** The authors' own cost-benefit finding is that "the revenue benefit of an estate tax exceeds the cost for the vast majority of states" despite the tax-induced mobility causing a large reduction in the aggregate tax base: the one-off estate-tax receipts from billionaires who stay exceed the forgone income-tax revenue over the remaining lifetimes of those who leave [7]. The inconvenient study, read to its conclusion, supports taxing anyway — and supports it more strongly for a national tax with an exit tail than for a state tax with none.

## 4. Wealth-tax episodes: Sweden, Norway, Spain, the UK non-dom reform

- **Sweden.** The annual wealth tax (abolished 2007) and the inheritance tax (2004) generated the celebrity-exile narrative — Kamprad, the Rausings — documented in [`../05-history/abolitions.md`](../05-history/abolitions.md). Henrekson & Waldenström's account attributes flight less to the rate than to the regime's asymmetries: closely-held firms punished, listed wealth favoured, no exit charge [8].
- **Norway 2022–23.** After the Støre government raised the wealth-tax rate and valuation bases, wealthy Norwegians — including industrialist Kjell Inge Røkke — relocated to Switzerland: Finance Ministry data record 82 wealthy Norwegians with combined net wealth of about NOK 46 billion (~$4.3bn) leaving in 2022–23, more than 70 of them to Switzerland [9]. Two lessons survive scrutiny: the movers were disproportionately the *most* mobile fraction of the top 0.01%, and Norway's exit-tax rules at announcement contained deferral features that made leaving cheap — subsequently tightened in 2024. The episode is a sequencing failure (rate before exit architecture), not proof of unmanageable elasticity; see [`../15-transition/announcement-effects.md`](../15-transition/announcement-effects.md).
- **Spain.** Regional wealth-tax divergence (Madrid's 100% credit) produced measurable internal relocation of declared residence — Agrawal, Foremny & Martínez-Toledano find the stock of wealthy individuals in Madrid rose 10% relative to other regions within five years, and that mobility cost other regions personal-income-tax revenue about six times the direct wealth-tax losses — again in an intra-national, zero-anchor-cost setting [10].
- **UK non-doms.** Advani, Burgherr & Summers's work on the 2017 deemed-domicile reform found emigration responses among affected non-doms far smaller than lobby predictions — the emigration rate of affected long-stayers roughly doubled from about 5% to about 10%, while the average income tax they paid rose by over 150%: most stayed and paid [13]. Their caution against Henley & Partners-style "millionaire exodus" counts — which infer migration from social-media profiles and investment-migration industry data — applies with force to the 2024–25 non-dom abolition coverage; the industry producing the exodus statistics sells the exits [13].

## 5. Elasticities by tax type: the synthesis table

The literature is often cited as one undifferentiated "tax migration" finding. It is three distinct literatures with different populations, planning horizons, and policy levers:

| Tax type | Population studied | Directional elasticity | Planning horizon | Principal design lever |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Top income tax (general) | US state millionaires [1][2] | Near zero | Annual; move must precede income | None needed; friction suffices |
| Top income tax (superstars) | Footballers, foreign high earners, inventors [5][6][11] | High (~1 for foreigners) | Short; career-portable | Not this proposal's population |
| Annual wealth tax | Swedish, Norwegian, Spanish episodes [8][9][10] | Moderate; concentrated in ultra-rich; high intra-nationally | Ongoing annual bill creates standing exit incentive | Exit tax + valuation design; or avoid annual wealth taxes entirely |
| State estate tax (intra-federal) | Forbes 400 billionaires [7] | Large (35% of local billionaires leave), rising with age | Decades; zero-anchor-cost moves | National-level assignment (adopted by this proposal) |
| National death/receipts tax (international) | No direct estimate exists | Bounded above by the intra-federal case *at literature stakes* — a bound on the propensity to move against a c. 16–20% state levy, not on behaviour at a 100% rate (see below) | Decades — but tails and situs rules consume the horizon | Dual nexus, 10-year tail, recipient basis |

Two readings of this table matter. *For the objection*: the bottom-right cell is empirically empty — no one has measured international migration response to a recipient-based national inheritance tax, so confident claims of mass flight are extrapolation, not evidence. *Against complacency*: the same emptiness means the proposal cannot claim measured safety either; it claims bounded risk, with the bounds set by the intra-federal worst case and the design levers listed.

One qualification to the "bounded above" cell, added with [`../04-case-against/model-risk.md`](../04-case-against/model-risk.md) §1 in view: the intra-federal case bounds the *propensity to relocate* under literature-stakes incentives (top US state estate-tax rates run around 16–20%). At a 100% rate above threshold the incentive is not a point further along that curve — for a billionaire family the prize is the fortune itself — so the intra-federal bound governs the friction side of the comparison (international moves cost more than interstate ones), not the incentive side. For the billionaire and £100m tiers this section therefore abandons elasticity extrapolation altogether and substitutes a decision-model planning posture — assume attempted escape, and price what attempting achieves — developed in [confiscatory-stakes.md](confiscatory-stakes.md).

## 6. Why death-tax elasticities differ — and what that implies

Death taxes allow decades of planning, so measured responsiveness among those who *do* plan (Moretti–Wilson's older billionaires) is higher than income-tax studies suggest. But the same decades work for the tax authority under the proposed design: the [`../02-proposal/avoidance.md`](../02-proposal/avoidance.md) architecture (dual nexus, 10-year tail, deemed-receipt exit charge, lifetime gift aggregation) means successful escape requires the donor's early emigration *and* the heirs' emigration *and* asset re-situsing, sustained over decades without any intervening return, death, or transfer — the compound-improbability argument developed in [friction-and-anchors.md](friction-and-anchors.md). The elasticity that matters is not "will a billionaire move?" but "will an entire family system re-domicile itself and its assets for thirty years?" — and no study finds evidence on that harder question.

## Research gaps

- The Norwegian 2022–23 outflow count is now sourced to Finance Ministry data (82 movers) [9]; a full Statistics Norway / Skatteetaten registry analysis of the movers' wealth composition and subsequent tax paid remains to be located.
- Reproduce Moretti & Wilson's (2023) state-level cost-benefit calculation under a national tax with a 10-year tail.
- Advani et al.'s updated estimates on the 2025 UK non-dom abolition as data emerges — the nearest live experiment to this proposal's residence rules.
- No study measures *recipient-side* migration elasticity (heirs moving to escape an inheritance receipts tax); Ireland's CAT is the natural setting.
- No evidence of any kind exists on family-level escape *completion rates* at confiscatory stakes — the parameter the [confiscatory-stakes.md](confiscatory-stakes.md) scenarios span with 30/50/70% planning assumptions; the first adopter's administrative data will be the first measurement.
