# International Coordination: Closing the Exits Multilaterally

**Executive summary.** The infrastructure that made tax flight easy in the abolition era (1972–2014) has been substantially dismantled: FATCA and the Common Reporting Standard ended bank secrecy for over 100 jurisdictions, beneficial-ownership registers are spreading, and the OECD Pillar Two 15% corporate minimum proved that a multilateral tax *floor* — long dismissed as utopian — can be negotiated and implemented within a decade. The target architecture for this proposal is a minimum-inheritance-tax floor on the Pillar Two template, with a defined set of unilateral tools (dual nexus, exit tails, treaty saving clauses, defensive measures) that a first mover can deploy while the coalition assembles.

## Headline findings

- Automatic exchange of information under CRS now covers well over 100 jurisdictions — 116 have commenced exchanges, and in 2024 information was exchanged on over 171 million financial accounts covering nearly EUR 13 trillion in assets; the era in which an undeclared Swiss account was a practical inheritance-avoidance plan is over [1].
- Pillar Two is the proof of concept: ~140 Inclusive Framework members agreed a minimum effective corporate rate with a *defensive backstop* (the UTPR) that taxes the undertaxed profits of non-adopters — the same top-up logic transposes to inheritance [2].
- Zucman's 2024 report for the Brazilian G20 presidency proposing a coordinated minimum tax on billionaires put individual-wealth floors on the formal multilateral agenda for the first time — and the track has outlived the stalled G20 process: the campaign went mass-market with *We Need to Tax Billionaires* (2025–26, ~20 languages), and a 2% floor passed the French National Assembly in February 2025 [3]. The blueprint's enforcement mechanics — beneficial-ownership requirements in country-by-country reporting, plus rules letting participating countries tax non-participants' undertaxed individuals — are the UTPR logic applied to natural persons, i.e. exactly the architecture the minimum-inheritance-tax floor below requires [3].
- Estate/inheritance double-tax treaties are few (the UK has 10 inheritance-tax conventions; the US roughly 15 estate/gift tax treaties [6]), so the treaty-renegotiation burden that plagues income-tax reform barely exists here [4].
- Unilateral adoption is viable *before* coordination: the dual-nexus and trailing-tail design of [`../02-proposal/avoidance.md`](../02-proposal/avoidance.md) is built to operate alone, with coordination as an amplifier rather than a precondition.
- Inside the EU, differently-calibrated national thresholds ([`../02-proposal/thresholds.md`](../02-proposal/thresholds.md)) coexist with free movement because the *ledger* travels with the person while the *threshold* belongs to the taxing state: the CJEU has upheld the 10-year residence tail (*van Hilten-van der Heijden* [10]) and polices residence discrimination in allowances, not rates (*Barbier*, *Mattner*, *Welte* [8][9]) — a defect the per-recipient design cannot exhibit, because every heir gets the same threshold regardless of anyone's residence.

## The transparency stack, as it stands

| Layer | What it does | Status | Relevance to inheritance taxation |
|---|---|---|---|
| **FATCA (2010)** | US withholding compulsion forcing worldwide reporting of US persons' accounts | Operating since 2014 | Demonstrated that a large economy can *unilaterally* conscript global banks; the template for defensive measures [1] |
| **CRS (2014)** | Automatic exchange of financial-account data among 100+ jurisdictions | Operating; expanding to crypto via CARF | Makes offshore receipts visible to the recipient's residence jurisdiction — the exact data a receipts tax needs [1] |
| **[CARF](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2023/06/international-standards-for-automatic-exchange-of-information-in-tax-matters_ab3a23bc.html)** | Crypto-Asset Reporting Framework extending CRS to digital assets | 52 jurisdictions committed to first exchanges by 2027; 75 committed overall (2027–29 waves) [7] | Closes the most-cited novel-asset gap ([`../07-implementation/digital-and-novel-assets.md`](../07-implementation/digital-and-novel-assets.md)) |
| **Beneficial-ownership registers** | Identify natural persons behind companies and trusts | UK PSC register (2016); EU AMLD registers (CJEU limited *public* access 2022, restored for legitimate interest under AMLD6); US Corporate Transparency Act gutted in March 2025 — FinCEN's interim final rule exempts all US-formed entities and their beneficial owners, leaving only foreign-registered companies reporting [5] | Defeats the nominee/shell layer between donor and recipient — except in the US, where the March 2025 rollback reopens the domestic-shell gap [5] |
| **Pillar Two (2021)** | 15% minimum effective corporate tax with IIR/UTPR top-up mechanics | EU directive in force 2024 (22 of 27 member states had implemented the IIR and QDMTT by 2025); 40+ jurisdictions with enacted or draft legislation on BDO's tracker (December 2025); US participation unresolved [2] | The institutional proof and the mechanical template [2] |

The stack was built in fifteen years, mostly after 2008, mostly against confident predictions of impossibility — directly relevant to the equilibrium-shift argument in [game-theory.md](game-theory.md).

## Pillar Two as template: a minimum-inheritance-tax floor

The transposition is direct:

1. **Floor standard.** Participating jurisdictions commit to taxing inheritances/receipts above an agreed threshold at no less than an agreed minimum effective rate — a floor far below this proposal's 100% marginal rate; the floor's job is to eliminate *zero-tax* jurisdictions as planning destinations, not to harmonise rates.
2. **Top-up rule (IIR analogue).** Where a resident of a participating state receives from an estate routed through a non-participating jurisdiction at below the floor, the residence state taxes the difference. The proposal's dual nexus already does this unilaterally.
3. **Defensive backstop (UTPR analogue).** Participating states apply top-up charges to *situs assets* of estates organised from non-participating havens — the mechanism that gave Pillar Two its adoption cascade, because staying outside stopped protecting anyone's tax base.
4. **Information annex.** CRS extended from account balances to *transfers* — a dedicated schedule for cross-border gifts, bequests, and trust/foundation distributions, as specified in [`../02-proposal/avoidance.md`](../02-proposal/avoidance.md) §7.

Precedents for each element exist; what is missing is only the political assembly, which is the subject of [game-theory.md](game-theory.md) and [`../12-political-adoption/stepping-stones.md`](../12-political-adoption/stepping-stones.md).

## Institutional routes

| Route | Mechanics | Prospects |
|---|---|---|
| **OECD Inclusive Framework** | Extend the Pillar structure to personal transfer taxation | Natural home; slow; requires G7/G20 sponsorship — the Zucman/G20 billionaire-tax track is the opening [3] |
| **EU directive** | Direct-tax directives need unanimity; enhanced cooperation (9+ states) is the workaround, precedent-tested with the FTT attempt | Enhanced cooperation among willing members (plausibly the Franco-German-Spanish-Benelux core) is the realistic EU path |
| **[UN framework convention on tax](https://financing.desa.un.org/unfcitc)** | The 2023–24 UN tax convention process, driven by the Global South | Longer horizon; valuable as normative pressure and as a venue havens cannot veto |
| **Bilateral/plurilateral treaty** | A purpose-built minimum-inheritance-tax convention among a small coalition, open for accession | Fastest credible route: UK+EU-core+US covers the decisive share of inheritable wealth ([game-theory.md](game-theory.md)) |
| **Model-treaty update** | Saving clauses preserving exit tails; credit ordering for situs-state tax | Low-cost housekeeping a first mover does immediately ([exit-tax-design.md](exit-tax-design.md)) |

## What a unilateral adopter does meanwhile

Coordination is an amplifier, not a precondition. The first mover, alone, can:

1. **Operate the dual nexus** — residence or situs, either sufficient ([`../02-proposal/avoidance.md`](../02-proposal/avoidance.md) §5); no other state's consent required.
2. **Run the 10-year tail and exit charge** — domestic law, CJEU-compliant drafting per [exit-tax-design.md](exit-tax-design.md).
3. **Exploit CRS data already flowing** — the receipts ledger ([`../02-proposal/mechanism.md`](../02-proposal/mechanism.md)) consumes existing exchange streams.
4. **Apply defensive measures to blacklisted havens** — FATCA-style withholding on transfers routed via non-cooperative jurisdictions; the [EU non-cooperative-jurisdictions list](https://www.consilium.europa.eu/en/policies/eu-list-of-non-cooperative-jurisdictions/) supplies the vehicle.
5. **Condition market access** — the City of London/Wall Street lesson of FATCA: financial-centre gravity converts domestic reporting rules into global ones.
6. **Publish the accession treaty** — a ready-to-sign floor convention lowers the next adopter's cost and signals the cascade intent.

## Free movement and the mobile ledger: different thresholds inside one union

The localisation doctrine of [`../02-proposal/thresholds.md`](../02-proposal/thresholds.md) ("One formula, local numbers") sets each adopter's threshold from national anchors — £500k UK, €700k Netherlands — and forbids a single harmonised figure. Free movement then poses the obvious question: what happens to one person's lifetime ledger as they move between member states running different numbers? The answer is an allocation principle plus three pieces of machinery this file already owns (the tail, the floor, the credit cascade).

### The allocation principle: the ledger travels, the threshold does not

Three rules (design):

1. **The ledger travels with the person.** The Central Register records worldwide lifetime receipts regardless of where the recipient lived when each arrived. Moving never resets headroom — otherwise every large bequest would be preceded by a change of address.
2. **The threshold and rate belong to the taxing jurisdiction.** Whichever state has nexus over a receipt applies *its own* threshold to the recipient's cumulative worldwide total. There is no "portable" threshold and no pro-rating; the local number governs while the local claim exists.
3. **Connecting factors are the dual nexus of [`../02-proposal/avoidance.md`](../02-proposal/avoidance.md) §5, restated for a union:** the current residence state taxes worldwide receipts; the situs state taxes local assets ([`../16-second-order/foreign-ownership.md`](../16-second-order/foreign-ownership.md)); the former residence state retains a tail claim (§ below).

**Worked example.** Mira, a Dutch resident, receives €400,000 at 30. Against the Dutch €700,000 threshold: tax nil; ledger reads €400,000. At 33 she moves to France — the exit charge of [`../02-proposal/mechanism.md`](../02-proposal/mechanism.md) §3 is a deemed receipt only of the excess over residual headroom, and she has €300,000 of Dutch headroom left, so the exit event crystallises nothing. At 38, French-resident, she inherits €500,000. Her ledger now reads €900,000 *worldwide*; France applies the French threshold to that cumulative total (illustratively €600,000 — France's actual figure is deliberately not derived in this repo; [`../02-proposal/thresholds.md`](../02-proposal/thresholds.md) leaves it to French anchors): €300,000 of the new receipt is above the line and taxed at 100%. The Dutch 10-year fiction still claims her as a Dutch national within ten years of emigration; the Dutch computation (€900,000 − €700,000 = €200,000) is smaller than the French tax already paid, so after credit the Netherlands collects nothing. Note what moving did and did not do: it never reset her headroom, but it did change which threshold governs — she pays €100,000 more than if she had stayed in the Netherlands, and would have paid less had she moved somewhere with a higher threshold. That second possibility is the arbitrage the tail exists to police.

### Threshold arbitrage and the trailing tail

The play free movement invites is: establish residence in the highest-threshold member state *before* the big bequest lands. The N-year tail defeats it: the former residence state keeps computing its own worldwide charge for ten years after departure, crediting the new state's tax and collecting the difference — so relocating to a higher-threshold state yields nothing until a genuine decade has passed, at which point it is emigration, not planning ([exit-options.md](exit-options.md)). The load-bearing precedent is already verified in this repo: in ***van Hilten-van der Heijden* (C-513/03, 23 February 2006)** the CJEU upheld the Dutch rule taxing the estate of a Dutch national who dies within ten years of leaving as if she had never left, holding it is *not* a restriction on free movement of capital because emigrants are taxed identically to residents "while enjoying relief in respect of inheritance taxes levied by other States" [10] ([`../11-geographies/netherlands.md`](../11-geographies/netherlands.md) §5). That proviso is the design constraint: the tail survives EU law because it is a *residual* claim behind a credit, never a double charge. With every adopter running a tail and the Phase-4 floor removing zero-tax destinations, threshold shopping inside the union requires ten years of genuine relocation before the transfer — the same answer this file gives to extra-EU flight, delivered intra-EU by instruments the CJEU has already blessed.

### What EU law requires of the design

The free-movement case law constrains *how* a member state may tax inheritances, not *how much*:

- **Inheritances and gifts are capital movements.** Settled since ***Barbier* (C-364/01, 11 December 2003)**, where Dutch inheritance-duty rules valuing a non-resident's Dutch property less favourably than a resident's were struck down [8]; the Court has restated the classification in every transfer-tax case since (*Jäger*, *Mattner*, *Welte*) [8][9].
- **The vice is residence discrimination in allowances, not the rate.** ***Mattner* (C-510/08, 22 April 2010)** struck down Germany's €1,100 gift-tax allowance for transfers of German land between non-residents against €205,000 where a party was resident [9]; ***Welte* (C-181/12, 2013)** did the same for the inheritance allowance (€2,000 vs €500,000) [9] — the cases already carried by [`../10-opposition/legal-challenges.md`](../10-opposition/legal-challenges.md).
- **This design complies natively.** The per-recipient lifetime threshold is a single number identical whatever the residence of donor or heir: a Monaco-resident heir taxed on the situs nexus gets the full local threshold against her worldwide ledger, exactly as a local heir does. The Mattner/Welte defect — a smaller allowance for the non-resident case — is structurally impossible, and the 100% rate above the line is not itself a free-movement issue so long as it applies without regard to nationality or residence. (Honest corollary: crediting a non-resident's worldwide ledger requires cross-border receipt data — the CRS-transfer annex above is what makes the compliant design administrable.)

### The civil-law template: Brussels IV

The EU has coordinated *civil* cross-border succession since 17 August 2015: **Regulation 650/2012 ("Brussels IV")** gives jurisdiction to, and applies the law of, the member state of the deceased's **habitual residence at death** (Arts. 4, 21), with an election for the law of one's **nationality** (Art. 22) [11]. The tax layer proposed here mirrors an architecture the union already runs — one primary personal connecting factor, a nationality-linked exception, coordination without harmonising the substantive national rules. Three honesty notes: the Regulation *excludes* tax from its scope (Art. 1(1)), so it is a template, not a vehicle; the UK and Ireland never opted in and Denmark is not bound (recitals 82–83) [11]; and where Brussels IV picks *one* law to the exclusion of others, the tax layer deliberately runs *two* concurrent claims (residence + situs) plus a tail — so its coordination instrument is the credit, not exclusivity.

### Double-taxation mechanics: who credits whom

Two claimant patterns recur: (a) residence state + situs state on the same receipt; (b) old-residence tail + new residence. The credit cascade (design, consistent with [inbound-wealth.md](inbound-wealth.md)):

1. **Situs state first** — taxes local assets at its threshold and rate;
2. **Current residence state second** — taxes the worldwide receipt, crediting situs-state tax on the same assets;
3. **Tail state last** — computes its own worldwide charge, credits both, and collects only any excess.

The soft-law basis exists: **Commission Recommendation 2011/856/EU** (15 December 2011) already recommends essentially this relief ordering — situs-state priority, personal-link states crediting — for member states' inheritance taxes [12] ([`../11-geographies/continental-europe.md`](../11-geographies/continental-europe.md)). But it is non-binding, and bilateral relief is thin — the UK has all of 10 inheritance-tax conventions [6] — which is why *binding* credit ordering belongs in the Phase-4 floor convention (the "model-treaty update" row above; [exit-tax-design.md](exit-tax-design.md)). The honest residual until then: a genuinely mobile life gets a *blended* outcome — each receipt taxed under the threshold of whichever state holds the primary claim at that moment, credits preventing double charge but nothing guaranteeing the total equals what either state would have levied alone. That is the price of national calibration plus free movement, and it is bounded, not open-ended: the credits cap the liability at the highest single-state computation.

### Localisation and free movement are one principle

Different national thresholds are not a bug that free movement exposes; they are the localisation doctrine of [`../02-proposal/thresholds.md`](../02-proposal/thresholds.md) seen from the border. A threshold set for Dutch house prices and one set for Slovak house prices *must* diverge, or one of them is miscalibrated — the single EU-wide number is the one harmonisation the design forbids. What free movement then requires is precisely the machinery this file specifies anyway: the tail (so divergence cannot be arbitraged on a timescale shorter than a real life change), the floor (so no member state offers the zero option), and the credit cascade (so two legitimate claims never become two full charges). Pillar Two's division of labour once more: common base, common floor, national parameters — and top-up mechanics policing the seams.

## Sequencing with the domestic reform

Coordination workstreams should start *before* domestic enactment, in this order:

1. **At announcement:** publish the draft floor convention alongside the domestic bill — signalling that the regime is designed for company, not isolation, blunts the "lone lunatic" attack line ([`../10-opposition/media-narratives.md`](../10-opposition/media-narratives.md)).
2. **At enactment:** activate CRS-transfer-annex negotiations at the OECD and table the enhanced-cooperation proposal in the EU (for an EU adopter) or the treaty saving-clause programme (UK/US).
3. **Within the first parliament:** land at least one bilateral mutual-recognition agreement with a peer considering adoption — the first accession is the proof the cascade argument needs ([`../15-transition/sequencing.md`](../15-transition/sequencing.md)).

## Honest limits

Coordination has never been total and will not be here: some havens will hold out (their capacity limits are the mitigation — [exit-options.md](exit-options.md)); the US federal politics of joining any floor are volatile, as Pillar Two's US career shows; enforcement against determined concealment of portable assets remains an [`../07-implementation/enforcement.md`](../07-implementation/enforcement.md) problem. The claim defended here is narrower and sufficient: the trajectory of the last fifteen years runs entirely toward transparency and floors, the mechanical templates exist, and a design that *works unilaterally and improves multilaterally* is on the right side of that trajectory — the abolished regimes of [`../05-history/abolitions.md`](../05-history/abolitions.md) had neither property.
