# Game Theory: First Movers, Coalitions, and the Shifted Equilibrium

**Executive summary.** Tax competition is a strategic game, and the abolition wave of 1972–2014 was one equilibrium of it — small jurisdictions defecting, large ones racing down, capital arbitraging the gaps. This file argues the game's parameters have changed: transparency infrastructure and the Pillar Two precedent have raised the cost of defection, haven capacity is a natural brake on flight, and a coalition of just the UK, EU and US would cover the decisive majority of the world's inheritable wealth — making the relevant question not "can one country tax inheritance alone?" but "how small a coalition suffices, and how does a first mover survive until it forms?"

## Headline findings

- The classic race-to-the-bottom results (Zodrow–Mieszkowski, Oates) assume mobile capital and immobile bases competing on *rates* with no coordination technology; every one of those assumptions has weakened since 2008 [1][2].
- Australia 1977–79 is the canonical inheritance-tax defection cascade — but it occurred among *sub-national* units with zero migration cost, the limiting worst case, not the general one ([`../05-history/abolitions.md`](../05-history/abolitions.md)) [3].
- Pillar Two demonstrated the escape from the prisoner's dilemma: a defensive backstop (taxing the undertaxed profits of non-adopters) flips the payoff matrix so that *staying out* stops paying — the same mechanism transposes to inheritance ([international-coordination.md](international-coordination.md)) [4].
- Haven capacity is a natural brake: flight is congestible, so the marginal émigré faces rising prices and rationed entry, meaning aggregate flight is bounded regardless of individual elasticities ([exit-options.md](exit-options.md)).
- First-mover risk is real but front-loaded and manageable by sequencing (exit architecture before rate — [`../15-transition/sequencing.md`](../15-transition/sequencing.md)); first-mover *advantages* (agenda-setting, template-writing, revenue, endowment visibility) are durable.

## The classical game and why it fit 1972–2014

In the standard tax-competition model, jurisdictions set rates non-cooperatively over a mobile base; equilibrium rates are inefficiently low, and small jurisdictions rationally undercut large ones because their gain from imported base exceeds their loss on domestic base (Kanbur–Keen) [1][2]. The abolition era ran this script faithfully: Queensland defected for elderly migrants and every Australian state followed within two years; celebrity exiles gave Sweden's repeal its narrative; each abolition lowered the reputational and fiscal cost of the next. Three enabling conditions did the work: **secrecy** (offshore holdings were invisible), **no defensive technology** (nothing taxed the departed or their assets), and **fiscal triviality** (sub-0.5%-of-revenue yields made repeal cheap) — the eight design errors of [`../05-history/abolitions.md`](../05-history/abolitions.md) restated strategically.

## What changed after 2008

| Parameter | 1972–2014 | Now |
|---|---|---|
| Information | Bank secrecy; no exchange | FATCA, CRS 100+ jurisdictions, CARF, ownership registers [4] |
| Defensive technology | None deployed | Exit taxes CJEU-approved and ATAD-mandated; UTPR-style top-ups live in corporate tax ([exit-tax-design.md](exit-tax-design.md)) |
| Coordination precedent | "Tax harmonisation is utopian" | Pillar Two: ~140 members, implemented in the EU within three years of agreement [4] |
| Haven politics | Havens courting inflows | Portugal repealed NHR; EU killed golden passports; Swiss forfait contested by referendum ([exit-options.md](exit-options.md)) |
| Public salience | Inequality a fringe concern | Post-Piketty, post-2008 legitimacy politics; billionaire minima on the G20 agenda [5] |

The game has not been solved, but its payoff matrix has been visibly re-priced: defection (by individuals and by jurisdictions) is more observable, more punishable, and more politically costly than at any point in the abolition era.

## First-mover analysis

**Risks.** (1) *Announcement-window flight*: the mobile minority exits between announcement and the exit-architecture taking effect — the Norwegian error; mitigated by legislating the tail and exit charge *first*, ideally with effect from announcement day ([`../15-transition/announcement-effects.md`](../15-transition/announcement-effects.md)). (2) *Formation-location drift*: young founders build elsewhere over decades — bounded by the agglomeration goods analysis in [retention-attraction.md](retention-attraction.md), but the genuine long-run exposure. (3) *Narrative capture*: every departure becomes a front page regardless of fiscal materiality — a communications problem ([`../10-opposition/media-narratives.md`](../10-opposition/media-narratives.md)), and the abolition record shows it is the *politically* decisive one.

**Advantages.** (1) *Template-writing*: the first mover's statute becomes the multilateral draft, as US FATCA became CRS. (2) *Revenue and proof*: a working endowment funded by a working tax is the strongest recruitment tool for the coalition ([`../13-movement/precedents.md`](../13-movement/precedents.md)). (3) *Base retention via situs*: the dual nexus means even a lone adopter keeps taxing its land, housing stock, and registered companies — the majority of the domestic base — whatever the owners do ([friction-and-anchors.md](friction-and-anchors.md)). (4) *Selection effects*: a regime that deters only dynasts while welcoming builders may improve, not degrade, the composition of resident wealth ([retention-attraction.md](retention-attraction.md)).

**Net assessment.** A lone small open economy (the Australian-state case) should not move first. A large jurisdiction with situs-heavy wealth, financial-centre gravity, and CRS access — the UK is the archetype — can move first at bounded cost, provided sequencing discipline holds. See [`../11-geographies/adoption-criteria.md`](../11-geographies/adoption-criteria.md) for the jurisdiction-screening framework and [`../11-geographies/uk.md`](../11-geographies/uk.md) for the UK case.

## The small-coalition theorem

The coalition needed is smaller than intuition suggests, for three compounding reasons:

1. **Wealth concentration by geography.** The UK, EU and US together hold the large majority of the world's privately inheritable wealth held under strong rule of law (share to verify against Credit Suisse/UBS Global Wealth Report data) [6]. A three-party floor covers most of the base before any haven signs anything.
2. **Situs coverage.** What the coalition's residents own is largely *in* the coalition: its land, its listed companies, its funds. Dual-nexus rules make coalition-situs wealth taxable regardless of owner or heir residence, so the coalition's writ runs over its assets even for émigrés.
3. **Congestion pricing of the exits.** The havens jointly cannot house more than a trivial fraction of the affected population; as inflows rise, property prices, entry deposits, and domestic political resistance rise with them — Monaco's waiting lists, Singapore's 60% stamp duty, and Portugal's repeal are the congestion mechanism already operating ([exit-options.md](exit-options.md)). Flight is self-limiting in aggregate even where individually rational.

Formally: the abolition-era game was many competing jurisdictions and free exit; the available game is a three-player coordination problem with congestible outside options and a UTPR-style punishment technology. Coordination problems of that shape get solved — Pillar Two is the existence proof [4].

## Honest residuals

The equilibrium has shifted; it has not arrived. The US may be the coalition's laggard rather than its member for electoral cycles at a time ([`../12-political-adoption/electoral-arithmetic.md`](../12-political-adoption/electoral-arithmetic.md)); Pillar Two itself shows implementation wobble under US pressure (status to verify); a determined haven with rule-of-law credentials (Switzerland, Singapore) capturing even a small share of the *formation* margin is a multi-decade erosion risk; and game-theoretic optimism is not a substitute for the domestic anti-avoidance spine, which must carry the load alone in the meantime ([`../02-proposal/avoidance.md`](../02-proposal/avoidance.md)). The claim this section sustains is calibrated, not triumphal: capital flight is a bounded, priceable, design-addressable friction — a cost of the policy, not a refutation of it. That is all the thesis requires.

## Research gaps

- Quantify the UK+EU+US share of global inheritable private wealth and its situs composition (UBS/Credit Suisse GWR; WID.world).
- Model announcement-window flight under different sequencing assumptions (calibrate to the Norwegian and 2025 UK non-dom episodes).
- Literature review: empirical tests of Kanbur–Keen small-jurisdiction undercutting applied to personal rather than corporate bases.
- Track Pillar Two's post-2025 durability as the leading indicator for floor-treaty feasibility.
