# Co-optation: The Soft Kill, and the Acceptance Tests That Prevent It

**Executive summary.** The likeliest way this proposal dies is not defeat but embrace: a half-measure, offered warmly, that relieves the political pressure while conceding nothing that compounds — the "soft kill" that has defused wealth-reform movements for a century. This file documents the five standard co-optation plays with verified historical cases, and then does the one thing that defeats them: it pre-registers, before any counter-offer exists, the acceptance tests that distinguish a ratchet-step worth banking from a trap worth refusing.

## Key points

- **Co-optation is cheaper than confrontation, so expect it first.** Everything in [attack-playbook.md](attack-playbook.md) costs the opposition money and visibility; a half-loaf costs them a press release. The threat matrix in [README.md](README.md) brackets the hard attacks; this file covers the soft ones.
- **The plays are documented, not hypothetical:** elite philanthropy as the answer to redistribution (contested since the Walsh Commission called foundations "a menace to the welfare of society" in 1915 [1][2]); the non-binding pledge (8 of 22 deceased Giving Pledgers fulfilled their pledge; the original signatories' wealth has since grown 166% in real terms [4]); the commission that buries (Australia's Henry Review: 138 recommendations, roughly 3 implemented, the bequest-tax finding dropped [5][6]; the UK's OTS inheritance-tax review: "not to proceed" [7][8]); and the token concession that demobilises (the UK's December 2025 APR climbdown [9]).
- **The stepping-stones need acceptance tests.** [../12-political-adoption/stepping-stones.md](../12-political-adoption/stepping-stones.md) supplies the ratchet logic — each step must build infrastructure, constituency, principle, or binding force. This file turns those into four pass/fail tests (§3) and applies them to the five likeliest counter-offers (§4).
- **The pre-commitment is the point.** Movements split over half-loaves *after* the offer arrives, when accepting and refusing both look like betrayal to someone. Publishing the tests now — before any offer exists — is the discipline device (§5).
- Historical cases below are cited; the tests and verdicts are strategy — labelled judgement, argued openly.

## 1. Why the soft kill works

A movement is a pressure reservoir. Every co-optation play drains pressure without conceding the mechanism that generates the grievance. The generic sequence (judgement, but visible in each §2 case):

1. The demand becomes costly to ignore.
2. An incumbent offers something *adjacent* to the demand — smaller, voluntary, deferred, or procedural.
3. Moderate supporters, exhausted or satisfied, declare victory; radical supporters denounce them; the coalition fractures on the question "was that a win?"
4. Salience collapses. The offer is quietly diluted, defunded, or never enforced.
5. Re-mobilising requires re-arguing the original case from a weaker position ("didn't we already fix that?").

Stage 3 is the kill. Note that the opposition does not need the offer to be *accepted* — the argument over acceptance does the damage. That is why the answer must be decided before the offer exists (§5), and why [../14-communications/overton-strategies.md](../14-communications/overton-strategies.md) lists "respectability capture" — the idea sanded down to a forgettable tweak — among its named failure modes.

## 2. The co-optation playbook, with the record

### 2.1 Philanthropy as deflection

The oldest play: answer "your fortune is a problem" with "look what my fortune funds." It was contested from the beginning — when the Rockefeller Foundation sought a federal charter, the US Commission on Industrial Relations (the Walsh Commission) investigated, and its chairman Frank Walsh wrote in 1915 that "the huge philanthropic trusts, known as foundations, appear to be a menace to the welfare of society"; Rev. John Haynes Holmes testified that the foundation form "must be repugnant to the whole idea of a democratic society" [1][2]. The modern academic statement is Rob Reich's *Just Giving* (Princeton, 2018): big philanthropy is "the conversion of private assets into public influence" — largely unaccountable, often perpetual, and lavishly *tax-subsidised*, meaning the public co-funds the plutocrat's priorities whether it shares them or not [1]. The insider account is Anand Giridharadas's *Winners Take All* (Knopf, 2018): elite "win-win" philanthropy functions as a substitute for — and a quiet lobby against — the structural reforms that would actually bind elites, letting them "change the world while also protecting a system that is at the root of the problems they wish to solve" [3].

**Relevance here:** expect "the great fortunes are already pledged to charity" as a first-line defence against any transmission cap. The design pre-empts part of it — charitable bequests are genuinely exempt ([../16-second-order/charitable-giving.md](../16-second-order/charitable-giving.md)) — so the rebuttal is clean: *give it all away and owe nothing; the tax exists only for what you pass as private dynasty.* The deflection play is answered by making the philanthropist choose in public between the two.

### 2.2 The voluntary code in lieu of law

Philanthropy's procedural cousin: a self-imposed, unenforceable commitment offered where a statute is demanded. The flagship case is the Giving Pledge (2010): a public, explicitly non-binding promise by billionaires to give away most of their wealth. The Institute for Policy Studies' fifteen-year audit (2025) found that of the 22 original US pledgers who have died, only 8 fulfilled the pledge; 32 of the original signatories remain billionaires, with collective wealth up 283% nominally (166% after inflation) since signing — wealth is compounding faster than it is being given away, and an increasing share of what *is* given lands in family foundations and donor-advised funds rather than operating charities [4]. IPS's conclusion: "the Pledge is unfulfilled, unfulfillable, and not our ticket to a fairer, better future" [4].

**Relevance here:** a "wealth pledge" or voluntary heirs' covenant will be offered as proof that legislation is unnecessary. The verified arithmetic above is the standing rebuttal — fifteen years of the most celebrated voluntary code in history did not even hold the signatories' *own* fortunes flat.

### 2.3 The commission that buries

Where the demand cannot be refused outright, refer it. Two documented cases, one general and one on this exact subject:

- **Australia's Henry Review** ("Australia's Future Tax System," commissioned 2008, delivered 2010): 138 recommendations, of which the government implemented roughly three [5]. On inheritance specifically, the review found a bequest tax would be economically efficient — "gives it a thumbs-up… but then drops it because of its 'controversial history'" — and no Australian government has touched the subject since [6].
- **The UK's Office of Tax Simplification IHT reviews** (2018–19): two commissioned reports on inheritance-tax design; the government's response (30 November 2021) "decided not to proceed" with the design recommendations, accepting only a handful of administrative easements [7][8].

**Relevance here:** at some stage a minister under pressure will offer "an independent review of wealth transfer taxation." The record says a review with no legislative trigger is a parking mechanism. The test is not whether a review is offered but what is *pre-committed to follow it* (§3, test 4) — a review whose terms of reference include drafting the receipts-register legislation is a ratchet; a review "to examine the issues" is a burial.

### 2.4 The token concession that demobilises

A small, real concession, timed to drain a mobilisation — the mirror image of the salami tactics in [attack-playbook.md](attack-playbook.md). The live, repo-verified example runs in the opposite direction and is exactly why it belongs here: the UK's 2024–25 APR/BPR reform. Faced with fourteen months of farmer protest against a modest relief cap, the government raised the cap from £1m to £2.5m in December 2025 — halving its own estimate of affected APR-claiming estates from 375 to 185 a year — and the mobilisation subsided with the relief architecture (and most of the sheltering it enables) intact [9][10]. A concession worth a fraction of the original measure bought the end of the campaign; see [media-narratives.md](media-narratives.md) for the full episode.

**Relevance here:** what governments do *to* protest movements will be done to this one. When the movement's pressure peaks, the offer will be a real but small thing — a threshold bump, a one-year pilot, a consultation — priced to be just barely acceptable. The acceptance tests exist so that the price of demobilising the movement is posted in advance and is not negotiable downward in the room.

### 2.5 Funding the moderate alternative

The final play (judgement — the general pattern is widely alleged, and no single canonical citation is offered here): when a demand gains ground, adjacent money appears for a tamer version occupying the same space — a think-tank programme on "opportunity" rather than inheritance, a philanthropically funded scholarship scheme presented as the endowment's substitute, a rival campaign for a token grant with no funding link to wealth transfers. The moderate alternative is not fake; that is what makes it effective. It recruits from the same donor pool, hires the same staff, and gives every wavering politician a softer thing to endorse. The defence is not to attack the moderates — [../12-political-adoption/stepping-stones.md](../12-political-adoption/stepping-stones.md) *wants* moderate versions in play — but to apply §3's tests in public, so the difference between a stepping-stone and a cul-de-sac is legible to supporters and funders before loyalty is contested.

## 3. The acceptance tests

Pre-registered criteria for any offer, counter-offer, or partial reform. These operationalise the four stepping-stone design criteria ([../12-political-adoption/stepping-stones.md](../12-political-adoption/stepping-stones.md)) as pass/fail questions asked of *someone else's* proposal (all judgement, argued):

| # | Test | The question | Why it is the test |
|---|---|---|---|
| T1 | **Infrastructure** | Does it build machinery the full scheme needs and that is costly to reverse — the receipts ledger, valuation capacity, delivery institutions, treaties? | Registers and institutions outlast governments; optics do not ([sabotage-after-passage.md](sabotage-after-passage.md) inverted) |
| T2 | **Constituency** | Does it create named, identifiable beneficiaries who will defend and extend it? | The absent defender constituency is why the US estate tax fell ([attack-playbook.md](attack-playbook.md)); anything that mints defenders compounds |
| T3 | **Principle** | Does it concede — in statute or in official framing — that the *recipient's cumulative windfall* is the taxable object? | The recipient basis is the keystone concept; once the debate happens on recipient ground, arithmetic favours the full scheme ([../02-proposal/mechanism.md](../02-proposal/mechanism.md)) |
| T4 | **Binding force** | Does it bind future transfers (rates, indexation, treaty obligations, statutory triggers) or only rearrange current optics (one-off levies, reviews, pledges, targets)? | Everything in §2 that failed was non-binding by design |

**Scoring rule (pre-committed):**

- **Accept-and-bank:** passes ≥2 tests and actively reverses none — take it, thank its sponsors sincerely, and keep going. Never let banked steps be framed as the movement's defeat or its satisfaction.
- **Accept-with-conditions:** passes ≥1 test but embeds a trap — accept in public *conditional on* the named fix, so refusal of the condition, not the movement, kills the deal.
- **Refuse:** passes none, or actively entrenches the estate basis / consumes the crisis window — decline politely, on the published tests, without opposing the measure's standalone merits.

One meta-rule: **the tests are applied to the text, not the sponsor.** A hostile chancellor's gift that passes T1+T2 is banked; an ally's flagship that passes nothing is refused. Anything else converts the tests into tribal signalling.

## 4. The tests applied: verdicts on the likely counter-offers

The five offers most likely to arrive, scored in advance (all judgement):

| Counter-offer | T1 | T2 | T3 | T4 | Verdict |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Modest IHT rate rise (e.g. 40%→45–50%, estate basis) | ✗ | ✗ | ✗ (entrenches estate basis) | ~ | **Refuse as settlement** |
| Loophole-closing package (relief caps, trust look-through) | ✓ | ✗ | ✗ | ✓ | **Accept-and-bank** |
| One-off wealth levy | ✗ | ✗ | ✗ | ✗ (one-off by definition) | **Accept-with-conditions** |
| CTF-style savings/endowment scheme, no tax link | ✓ (delivery) | ✓✓ | ✗ | ✗ | **Accept-with-conditions** |
| Zucman-floor-only (2% minimum on top fortunes) | ~ (reporting) | ✗ | ✗ | ✓ | **Accept-with-conditions** |

### 4.1 The modest rate rise — refuse as settlement

A higher rate on the *estate* basis banks revenue but fails every structural test: no ledger, no beneficiaries, and it re-legislates the very basis the proposal exists to replace — every pound of political capital spent defending "the death tax, but more" is spent on the opponent's chosen ground ([../06-palatability/polling.md](../06-palatability/polling.md)). The movement should not oppose it, and should never trade the recipient-basis demand for it: neutral in public, absent from the barricades, and explicit that this is not the ask. The one variant that flips the verdict: a rate rise *enacted on a recipient basis* is step 2 plus step 6 and is banked gladly.

### 4.2 The loophole-closing package — accept and bank

This *is* stepping-stone 1 arriving under someone else's name. Relief hygiene passes T1 (removes the drains that would hollow any later rate) and T4 (binds future transfers), and its politics run our way — defenders must argue for tax planning ([../12-political-adoption/wedge-dynamics.md](../12-political-adoption/wedge-dynamics.md)). The only discipline required: bank it without triumphalism, so the "phase one of the radicals' plan" attack ([../09-objections/slippery-slope.md](../09-objections/slippery-slope.md)) finds no confirming quote.

### 4.3 The one-off levy — accept with conditions

A one-off wealth levy (the UK Wealth Tax Commission's 2020 form) raises real money and touches the stock the transfer tax concedes for a generation — but it is structurally a *discharge*: paid once, it becomes the receipt the wealthy brandish against all future claims ("we already paid"). Conditions for support, stated in advance: (a) it is named and legislated as a **time-limited bridge instrument**, per the boundary doctrine of [../03-case-for/wealth-tax-comparison.md](../03-case-for/wealth-tax-comparison.md) §§5–6 and [../15-transition/revenue-bridge.md](../15-transition/revenue-bridge.md); and (b) a visible share of proceeds funds the pilot endowment, converting a T2 fail into a pass. Unconditioned, it drains the crisis window ([../11-geographies/crisis-windows.md](../11-geographies/crisis-windows.md)) that the full scheme needs.

### 4.4 The endowment without the tax — the dangerous one

**This is the most dangerous counter-offer on the board**, precisely because it looks like victory: a government-funded savings or capital-grant scheme — a bigger Child Trust Fund — with no link to wealth-transfer taxation. It passes T2 spectacularly (millions of named beneficiaries) and T1 partially (delivery machinery), and both are genuinely valuable; refusing outright would be self-harm. The traps are three: **token scale** (a CTF-sized grant produces null results that "prove" endowments fail — the underpowered-pilot failure mode of [../12-political-adoption/stepping-stones.md](../12-political-adoption/stepping-stones.md)); **funding substitution** (paid from general revenue, it is fiscally fragile and severs the "their windfall funds your start" pairing that carries the whole political architecture — [../02-proposal/endowment.md](../02-proposal/endowment.md)); and **demand satisfaction** (the movement's most legible offer is delivered, and the coalition's young base demobilises while transmission continues untouched). Verdict: **accept-with-conditions** — life-shaping scale (or honest pilot geometry), payment at 25, statutory footing, and a named — even notional — accounting line from wealth-transfer receipts. Fight for the conditions, but if they fall, still take the beneficiaries: a constituency of endowment recipients is recoverable ground; a null-result token scheme is not, so scale is the condition that is never traded away.

### 4.5 Zucman-floor-only — accept with conditions, never as substitution

A 2% minimum effective tax on the largest fortunes shares this project's diagnosis and passes T4 (a binding annual floor) and part of T1 (beneficial-ownership reporting the register can later use). What it does not do is touch transmission: on Zucman's own figures the top fortunes compound at ~7.5% a year real, so a 2% floor slows the dynasty and passes it on intact — the full argument, and the complementarity boundary, are in [../03-case-for/wealth-tax-comparison.md](../03-case-for/wealth-tax-comparison.md) §5. Conditions: support it as a named, parallel instrument — "shared diagnosis, different disease" — and pre-empt the substitution frame ("the billionaires are handled now") the moment it is offered as *the* answer to dynastic wealth.

## 5. The discipline problem, and the pre-commitment device

### 5.1 How half-loaves split movements

The offer's arrival is the fracture point: accepting reads as betrayal to the committed, refusing reads as fanaticism to the moderate, and the argument between them is more damaging than either answer. The instructive case is documented from the *opposite* trench: Graetz and Shapiro's account of the US estate-tax repeal campaign shows the repeal coalition repeatedly holding together against exemption-raising compromises that would have taken virtually all of its sympathetic front constituencies — the farms and family businesses fronting the campaign — out of the tax entirely; the coalition's strategists understood that a satisfied constituency demobilises, and held out for repeal [11]. Their opponents' compromises were designed to co-opt; the coalition's pre-agreed answer — nothing less than repeal — was the co-optation defence working in the other direction. The lesson is uncomfortable and transferable: **discipline over half-loaves is a solved problem for movements that decide the answer before the offer.**

(The movement's answer differs from theirs in substance — this project's published position is that most half-loaves *should* be banked, per §4 — but the mechanism is identical: the criteria exist before the offer, so acceptance is a rule, not a fight.)

### 5.2 The pre-commitment device

The device is this file. Publishing the acceptance tests and verdicts *before* any counter-offer exists does four things (judgement):

1. **Removes the internal fight.** When the offer arrives, the movement consults a published rule instead of relitigating its identity; dissenters argue with a document, not with each other.
2. **Raises the offer's minimum price.** A counter-party who can read the tests knows a T-zero offer buys nothing, and prices the next offer to pass at least one — pre-commitment as bargaining posture.
3. **Protects the moderates.** A negotiator who accepts a qualifying step points at the published rule; "sell-out" attacks land on the paper, not the person — the institutional-fronting principle of [personal-attacks.md](personal-attacks.md).
4. **Makes co-optation legible as it happens.** Each §2 play, when run, can be named against this file in real time — the same prebunking logic [attack-playbook.md](attack-playbook.md) applies to hard attacks, extended to soft ones ([../14-communications/inoculation.md](../14-communications/inoculation.md)).

The corresponding cost is honesty about flexibility: pre-commitment binds the movement too, and the tests should therefore be amended only in public, in advance, and never during a live negotiation.

## 6. Doctrine summary

1. **Expect the embrace before the attack.** Budget for §2's five plays in every scenario exercise alongside [attack-playbook.md](attack-playbook.md).
2. **Bank ruthlessly, celebrate quietly.** Most half-loaves are stepping-stones with someone else's name on them; take them on the published tests and keep the map in the movement's pocket, not the minister's ([../12-political-adoption/stepping-stones.md](../12-political-adoption/stepping-stones.md)).
3. **The recipient basis and the funding pairing are the two non-tradables.** Every §4 verdict reduces to protecting T3 and the tax-endowment link; everything else is price.
4. **Answer philanthropy with the exemption.** "Give it all away and owe nothing" converts the deflection play into a public choice between charity and dynasty (§2.1).
5. **No review without a trigger, no pledge instead of a statute, no one-off as a discharge.** The three procedural traps, refused by rule (§§2.2–2.4, 4.3).
