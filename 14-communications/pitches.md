# The Pitch Book: One Message, Twenty-Three Doors — and a Dutch Edition

**Executive summary.** The same policy, pitched to every audience that matters — from the 60-second lift ride to the family office that manages the fortunes it would end. Every pitch obeys the ordering doctrine (lead with the universal inheritance; the 100% rate is how it is paid for), uses only facts verified elsewhere in this repo, and closes on the ask appropriate to the listener. All scripts are suggested language (judgement) for message-testing per [`../06-palatability/polling.md`](../06-palatability/polling.md) §7 — none has been focus-grouped yet. Delivery note: the 5-minute scripts below are written tight (~3 minutes verbatim); the remaining time is for the listener's first objection, which each pitch anticipates in its final beat.

Rules inherited from [narratives.md](narratives.md) and [naming-and-language.md](naming-and-language.md): the villain is the system, never a person; never say "estate tax" or lead with "100%"; concede the strongest counterpoint before the listener raises it; end with a concrete ask.

## 1. The 1-minute elevator pitch

Here's a policy idea you've never heard, from the most capitalist people who ever lived — Paine, Mill, Carnegie, Roosevelt.

Every one of your children can receive up to £500,000 from you, completely tax-free. And every single citizen — whatever their parents have — gets £50,000 at 25. A universal inheritance. Guaranteed.

How's it paid for? Anything a person *receives* above half a million in their lifetime goes to fund everyone else's start. Nothing is taken from anyone while they live. Nothing is taken from the dead. Only the excess that would otherwise found a dynasty.

Here's the number that got me: only 4.62% of UK deaths pay any inheritance tax at all. Almost everyone who hates this tax is defending money they will never see — while their kids can't buy a house.

Ninety per cent of heirs would be completely untouched. Every 25-year-old gets a real start. That's the whole idea. Want the long version?

## 2. The 2-minute elevator pitch

Start with a question: what actually separates the kids who get ahead now — effort, or parents?

The biggest wealth transfer in history is under way: $124 trillion in America alone by 2048, $105 trillion of it to heirs. 2023 was the first year on record that new billionaires got more money by inheriting than by building. The median first-time buyer was 28 in 1991; she's 38 now. Whatever you call that system, it isn't meritocracy — it's inheritance with extra steps.

So here's the proposal. Every child can receive up to £500,000 tax-free — on the IFS numbers that's roughly 90% of heirs completely untouched, and for most families who pay inheritance tax today it's a tax *cut*. Every citizen gets £50,000 at 25, whoever their parents are. It's funded by a 100% tax only on what any one person receives above the half-million — the slice that founds dynasties.

Two things people get wrong. First, this is not a wealth tax: no living person is ever taxed on anything they earn, hold, or spend. Build a billion, keep a billion, spend a billion — the owner never gets a bill. Second, it's not radical-left: Thomas Paine proposed the pairing in 1797, John Stuart Mill designed the cap, Andrew Carnegie and Theodore Roosevelt made the capitalist case, and mid-century Britain and America ran 77–80% top rates through their best decades of growth.

The one-line version: markets test everything except birth. This closes the exception — and hands every young person a stake in the system we're trying to save.

## 3. Five minutes — working class

Can I ask you something? When your parents pass — and I hope it's a long way off — what do you expect to inherit? Because for half the country the honest answer is: nothing. And of those who do inherit, the typical amount is about eleven grand. Meanwhile there's a tax everyone's been taught to hate — inheritance tax — and here's the thing: only 4.62% of deaths in this country pay a penny of it. It's a tax on the richest one-in-twenty, and somehow *we've* been recruited to defend it for them, while our kids hand half their wages to a landlord whose portfolio came from his dad.

So flip it. What if inheritance was something *everyone* got?

Fifty thousand pounds. At 25. Every citizen, no forms, no means test, no strings. Your kid, my kid, everyone's kid. A deposit. A trade qualification and a van. A stake in the business they work for. The thing rich families have always done for their kids — done once, for all of them.

Paid for how? Not by you. Not by anything you'll ever leave. Every child can still receive up to half a million tax-free — be honest, is anyone leaving your kids more than half a million? It's paid for entirely out of what individual heirs receive *above* that: the private-jet slice. The people who'd pay have grandkids who get the £50k too — they just don't get the extra ten million on top.

You'll hear it's the politics of envy. Notice who says that: the people who tell you £50,000 for your daughter is envy, but ten million for theirs is natural law. You'll hear the rich will leave. Some try; the evidence says most stay — their businesses, mates and lives are here — and the ones who go leave the assets behind, because you can't emigrate a street of houses.

Here's my ask. Nothing hard. Next time someone in the pub says "they want to tax your house when you die," just say: "Nah — four in a hundred pay that. What they're actually offering is fifty grand for every kid at 25." Watch what happens to the conversation.

## 4. Five minutes — middle class

You're the family this tax debate is supposedly about, so let's do your actual numbers.

Say you and your partner end up with a house and savings worth £900,000, and you have two children. Under today's system you're nervously googling nil-rate bands, because the current tax sits on the *estate* and the thresholds are frozen while your house isn't. Under this proposal? Each child can receive £500,000 tax-free — a *million pounds* of headroom for your two. Your family pays nothing. For the majority of families who pay inheritance tax today, this is a tax cut. That's not spin; it's the design: the line is drawn at £500k *per recipient* precisely because it clears virtually every ordinary family's wealth, including yours, with room to spare.

Now the part nobody tells you. That inheritance you're planning to leave? It's already contingent. Care costs in England are means-tested and — since the cap was scrapped in 2024 — uncapped. Live five years in care and the "certain" bequest becomes a maybe. This scheme adds something the current system never gives your family: a *guaranteed* floor. Every one of your children and grandchildren receives £50,000 at 25, whatever happens to your health, the markets, or the care bill. The fantasy bequest is taxed only at the very top; the real one is protected; and a guaranteed one is added.

And think about what your kids are actually competing against. You saved for twenty years so your children could maybe get a deposit; the family down the road just... transferred one. Over half of first-time buyers now get family money. You're running a race where some lanes start at the finish. The £500k allowance keeps everything you'll actually leave; the 100% above it just stops the race being fixed.

The objection you're thinking: "governments will lower that threshold until it hits me." Fair — so the design indexes it to the *higher* of wages and house prices, by law, so it can only rise in real terms; and once thirty million voters' kids are receiving the £50k, no parliament survives touching it. The threshold was even audited against the alternative — starting high and ratcheting down was considered and rejected in writing, precisely so this promise stays true.

Ask: read the two-page summary. Check my numbers against your family. That's all.

## 5. Five minutes — millionaire class (~£1m)

You've done well — a business or a career, a house that's worth more than you ever expected, a seven-figure balance sheet. Here's what this policy does to you: almost nothing. And what it does for you: quite a lot.

The arithmetic first. The threshold is £500,000 *per recipient*. Two children: £1m passes tax-free. Split anything extra with grandchildren and you can pass £2m untouched — the design deliberately rewards spreading wealth across the family rather than concentrating it on one heir. At your level, this scheme is not aimed at you; today's inheritance tax, with its frozen £325k estate-side band, arguably is. You are more likely to *gain* headroom than lose it.

What you're really buying, though, is the thing your million can't currently purchase: a fair market for your kids to win in. You made your money in competition. Your children will compete against people whose capital arrives by birth in quantities you'll never match — the £10m and £100m inheritances that buy the house, the business, the runway, the safety net. You can't out-save dynasties. You can outlaw them. This proposal caps *everyone's* windfall at a level just above what your family passes anyway — which means your children's talent finally prices at par.

And your grandchildren each get £50,000 at 25, on top of whatever you leave. For a family at your level, the package is: keep everything you built, pass on more than 90% of heirs will ever see, and add a guaranteed floor under every descendant, forever, funded by the fortunes above you.

The objection you're feeling: "I might get richer — what if I cross the line?" Then you'll be exactly the person the next pitch is for, and you should read it. But notice what crossing the line would mean: each child still receives half a million tax-free, you still spend freely till your last day, and the excess builds the country they live in rather than a rival dynasty's compound.

Ask: you're the most credible messenger there is — successful, self-made, unthreatened. Say publicly what you just heard privately.

## 6. Five minutes — £10 million class

Now it's real: at your level this policy takes something. Let's be exact about what, and what you get back.

What it takes: the ability to convert £10m into a single heir's £10m. What it leaves: everything else. Every pound stays yours for life — earn, hold, spend, sail, give; the owner never gets a bill. Each child receives £500k tax-free; four children and their spouses and your grandchildren can between them receive several million, if you disperse it — dispersal is the point, and the design rewards it. Unlimited spousal transfers. Unlimited charity, forever, with your name on it. What ends is only this: one person receiving your entire position, unearned.

Now the uncomfortable evidence, because at £10m you can afford honesty. The data on what large inheritances do to recipients is one of the more robust findings in applied economics: a single person inheriting around $150k was roughly four times likelier to quit the labour force than one inheriting under $25k, and the effect scales. Carnegie said it a century before the econometrics: great wealth "deadens the talents and energies" of the child. Warren Buffett — who could leave his kids anything — chose "enough that they can do anything, not so much that they can do nothing." You already half-believe this. You've seen the second generations of your acquaintance. The question isn't whether a £10m windfall helps your children. The evidence says it doesn't. The question is whether you can bring yourself to act on what you know.

This policy does it for everyone at once — which dissolves the trap you're actually in. You can't unilaterally disarm; leaving your kids less while your rivals' kids get everything feels like betrayal. A rule binding all of you isn't betrayal. It's a truce. Everyone's children get a generous start, a fair market, and lives that are provably their own — and no one's children get to buy your grandchildren's world out from under them.

You'll object that you could do better than the state with that money. So do it: charity above the line is unlimited. Endow the school. Found the institute. The only door that closes is the dynasty; every philanthropic door stays open with your name over it.

Ask: there's a pledge — support the cap when it binds everyone. Zero cost today. Signatories are the movement's most unanswerable argument.

## 7. Five minutes — £100 million class

At your level the question changes. It's no longer "what do my children get" — they'll be fine under any regime, including this one. The question is what happens to the *system* that let you build this, and whether your name ends up on the right side of it.

Two numbers. Since 1987, the wealth of the world's three thousand richest households has compounded at 7.5% a year in real terms, while world income per adult grew 1.4%. That gap doubles your class's relative weight roughly every twelve years. There is no version of that curve that ends quietly: it ends in confiscation, in capital controls, in the politics that produces them — or in this, the orderly version, designed by people who *like* capitalism. You are old enough to know that "none of the above" is not on the ballot.

Now the offer, and it's better than you expect. Nothing you hold is touched while you live — this is not a wealth tax, and it was designed by people who fought the wealth tax. Your spouse: unlimited. Each child: £500k, plus whatever you spend on them along the way — education, houses to live in, experiences — because lifetime consumption is deliberately untouched. Your business: it doesn't have to be broken up — the enterprise-foundation route (Bosch, Novo Nordisk, Zeiss) keeps the firm intact, professionally run, permanent, with your family's name and voice attached, and the evidence says foundation-owned firms perform as well as any. And philanthropy above the line is unlimited: Carnegie is remembered for the libraries, not the steel margins.

What actually dies is the part of your fortune that was going to become someone else's unearned power over your grandchildren's country. Read the succession data before you mourn it: heir-run firms measurably underperform; a quarter of £10m+ estates already pay under 9% while your class pays 0–0.5% of wealth in tax annually — a regressivity that is doing more to radicalise the young against you than any activist could. This policy is the antidote your position requires.

Ask: don't endorse it publicly yet if that's uncomfortable. Fund the research. Take the meeting. And run one private exercise: write down what you actually want to exist in eighty years — I'll wager it's an institution, not an heir.

## 8. Five minutes — billionaire class

You've already had this argument — with yourself. Every billionaire has. Half of you settled it the way Buffett did: the ovarian lottery is real, dynasties are bad for the heirs and worse for the country, give it away. More than 250 of your peers signed a pledge saying exactly that. In 2001, over a thousand wealthy Americans — Gates Sr. at the front — petitioned to *keep* the estate tax on themselves. The instinct is not fringe. It's the mainstream position of the most serious people in your class.

Here's what the voluntary version can't do, and you know it because the numbers are public: fifteen years into the Giving Pledge, the signatories are collectively far richer than when they signed. Compounding beats intention. Voluntary renunciation is a treadmill set faster than anyone can walk. Only a rule stops the ratchet — and a rule has one feature philanthropy never will: it binds your rivals. Your discipline about your children means nothing if their competitors inherit empires. A cap that binds everyone is the only version of your own ethic that actually works.

What the rule costs you: your heirs get £500k each plus everything you choose to spend on them alive — which at your scale means any education, any experience, any launch, all untouched. What it leaves you: your company (run it till your last breath; settle any eventual liability in non-voting equity rather than breaking anything); your foundation (unlimited, eternal, yours); your name (ask Carnegie or Nobel what a name is worth when it's attached to giving rather than to a lineage). What it takes: the one thing the evidence says you shouldn't want anyway — and the one thing that, multiplied across your class at 7.5% compounding, ends the market system that made you.

One more thing, said plainly: you currently pay the equivalent of 0–0.5% of your wealth in tax per year — less, proportionally, than your cleaner. You know that's not a stable equilibrium. The alternative to a designed settlement is an undesigned one. Every year of delay, the settlement on offer gets worse.

Ask: one conversation, off the record, with the people building this. You'd be the first-mover on the biggest legacy opportunity your generation will be offered: founder of the settlement, not defendant of the ancien régime.

## 9. Five minutes — trillionaire class

No one has introduced you two, so I will: you're the reason this policy exists, and it's the reason you get to keep existing.

At a trillion, the polite fictions stop. You are not a market participant; you are a jurisdiction. Your capital allocation is industrial policy; your platform decisions are speech law; your security spend is a foreign ministry. The wealth of the nineteen richest American households has already returned to the levels of the four richest of 1913 — and 1913 is remembered for what came next: the settlement or the storm, the 16th Amendment or the revolutions that hit everyone who refused one. At your altitude there are only two long-run states: a world that taxed the transmission of your position, or a world your heirs own outright — and the second state has a name, and the name is not capitalism, and history is unanimous about how hereditary sovereignty ends for the family that holds it.

Understand what is not being asked of you. Not your company. Not your projects — the Mars program, the labs, the foundations: all continue, all yours, for life, untaxed. Not your comfort, which no policy could dent. What is being asked is the answer to one question: *should your children rule?* Not "be rich" — rule, because at a trillion inherited, that's the only word left. Every serious thinker your class produces — Carnegie at the peak of the last Gilded Age, Buffett at this one — answered no. The heirs answer no themselves when asked honestly: the evidence on what dynastic position does to the people locked inside it is grim reading, and your children will live it with the whole world watching.

What you get is the only thing you can't currently buy: a legitimate way down — and immortality of the durable kind. Not genetic immortality, which the data says curdles in two generations, but institutional immortality: the endowment generation will be the largest cohort of capital-owning citizens in history, and the settlement that created it will carry founders' names the way constitutions do. You can be Octavian or you can be the last Ptolemy. That is genuinely the whole choice.

Ask: at your scale there's only one — pick up the phone first. The first trillionaire to endorse the settlement writes its terms. The last one gets written into it.

## 10. Five minutes — political class

You want three things: votes you don't currently have, revenue you don't currently have, and a legacy. This is the rare policy that carries all three, and its polling structure is the strangest — and most exploitable — in tax politics.

The polling secret first. Inheritance tax is the most hated tax in every country measured — *and* the most movable. The hatred rests on a misperception: majorities believe the tax reaches far down the wealth distribution, when only 4.62% of UK deaths pay it. In the landmark experiment, simply telling people who actually pays more than doubled support — the largest single-fact persuasion effect ever measured for any tax. You are looking at an electorate that already agrees with the policy's substance and merely hasn't been told. No other fiscal position offers that arbitrage.

The offer architecture does your work for you. You never propose a tax; you propose *£500,000 tax-free per child* — a tax cut for the majority of families who currently pay IHT — plus *£50,000 for every 25-year-old*. Your opponent is then forced to argue against a tax cut for ordinary families and a capital grant for every young voter, in defence of — say it slowly — billionaires' heirs. That's the wedge, and it only cuts one way. And the endowment mints roughly 890,000 new stakeholders every year: a permanent, growing, name-attached constituency. Policies die when their beneficiaries are abstract; this one pays its base by name, at 25, forever.

The statecraft. Phase it: the basis switch first (Mirrlees-orthodox, revenue-neutral, sellable as modernisation), the family tax cut second, the full settlement third — each rung worth having alone, each survivable across a change of government. The receipts fund the endowment at maturity and the transition is financed against a legislated revenue stream — the arithmetic is published, warts and all, which is more than can be said for most manifesto math.

And the legacy point, because you got into this for a reason you don't say out loud anymore: the People's Budget made careers and broke a veto; the 16th Amendment named a generation of reformers. The politicians who build the universal-inheritance settlement will be remembered the way the architects of the NHS and Social Security are. The ones who defended the dynasties will be remembered too.

Ask: a private briefing, then one parliamentary question. The polling arbitrage is checkable in a single commissioned survey — we'll show you the instrument.

## 11. Five minutes — entrepreneur class

Everything you're afraid this is — it's the opposite, and you're the audience with the most to gain.

First, the absolute guarantee, because you've been burned by fine print: nothing you build is ever touched. Not at founding, not at exit, not at a billion. No annual levy, no valuation of your holdings, no bill while you breathe. This was designed by people who killed the wealth-tax idea *specifically because* it taxes builders. The tax exists at exactly one moment: when someone who didn't build it *receives* it. You are the person capitalism's whole moral case is about — earn what you create — and this is the first tax that takes that case literally.

Now what's in it for you. Three things.

Your competition gets honest. Today you compete against firms whose capital, market position, and patience are inherited — and the data on heir-run firms says they're badly run (profitability drops measurably when the founder's child takes over) but they sit on their positions anyway, because inherited capital doesn't need to win to survive. This policy recycles those positions onto the market every generation. More acquisitions, more MBOs, more shelf space. Creative destruction, with the destruction finally applied to the one thing it never reached: dynastic incumbency.

Your talent pool gets funded. The single best predictor of whether someone starts a business is early capital access — a £5,000 windfall roughly doubles entry into self-employment. Right now that capital arrives by lottery of birth, at an average inheritance age of around 60 — the wrong people, at the wrong age. This puts £50,000 into every 25-year-old: the largest seed round in history, allocated by ambition instead of ancestry. Some meaningful fraction of your future co-founders, hires, and customers are currently locked out of the game. This unlocks them.

Your kids get what you actually want for them. You didn't grind through the early years so your children could be *spared* the experience of building something — be honest. £500k each plus everything you spend on them alive is a launchpad; £50m is a sedative, and the labour-force data proves it.

Ask: your voice is worth ten of anyone else's — "founder backs the inheritance cap" breaks the story open. Start smaller if you like: read the capital-flight evidence and try to poke a hole in it. Founders make the best red-teamers.

## 12. Five minutes — VC class

Model this as a market-structure change and you'll want it priced into your fund's thesis.

The demand shock: roughly 890,000 people receive £50,000 each, every year, at exactly age 25 — call it £45bn per annum of risk-capable capital hitting the demographic that founds companies, forever. The liquidity-constraint literature is unambiguous that early capital access gates entrepreneurial entry (small windfalls roughly double it). Your current deal flow is drawn from the sliver of that cohort with family runway — you are, right now, investing from a talent pool filtered by parental balance sheet, which is exactly the kind of inefficiency you'd short in any other market. This policy removes the filter. More founders, from wider distributions, with skin already in. The endowment is a nation-scale pre-seed program with no board seats.

The supply shock: dynastic asset positions recycle every generation. Heir-controlled firms — which measurably underperform and under-innovate — stop being permanent fixtures and start being acquirable. Family blocks unwind into dispersed holders, institutions, and foundations. That's exit liquidity and target inventory in the same event. Meanwhile the LP base shifts from preservation-minded family money toward institutions and foundations with actual return mandates.

What it costs you personally: your carry is income — untouched, obviously, since nothing earned is ever touched. Your own estate faces the same £500k-per-recipient cap as everyone's, and everything the £10-million pitch says about your kids applies to you; read it. What it costs your industry: nothing — there is no scenario in which venture does worse in a world with 890,000 newly capitalised 25-year-olds a year and a rotating asset market than in a world of locked dynastic positions.

The objection you'll raise: founders need the dynasty dream to grind. The founder-motivation literature says otherwise — autonomy, competition, the thing itself; dynastic transmission doesn't register in the primary motives — and the policy's live test is that founders keep everything for life, which is the horizon founders actually optimise on. The honest residual: this is the one macro parameter reasonable people still dispute, and the repo says so in print.

Ask: you diligence for a living. The revenue model, the flight evidence, and the entrepreneurship channel are all published with their weaknesses flagged. Tear one apart. If you can't, say so on a podcast.

## 13. Five minutes — an arts student

You already know the thing this policy fixes; you live it. The gallery internship is unpaid. The MA is £15k. The first studio year pays nothing. The friction that decides who gets an arts career isn't talent — it's whose parents can fund three years of London rent while it compounds. The nepo-baby discourse got the diagnosis right and stopped: the arts have become a hereditary profession wearing a meritocratic costume, and everyone inside quietly knows which colleagues are subsidised.

Here's the policy: £50,000, at 25, for every citizen. Unconditional. That's the unpaid-internship subsidy, the MA, the studio year, the first film, the eighteen months your rich classmates' parents currently buy them — nationalised, and handed to *everyone*. Not a grant you apply for, justified to a committee. Yours. The most radical arts-funding policy on any table isn't an arts policy at all: it's making the runway universal so the work is chosen by the people with something to say rather than the people who can afford to say it.

How it's paid for is the poetic part: a 100% tax on what any individual inherits above half a million — the exact mechanism that currently staffs the culture industries by birth. And the second-order effects run your way too. Heavily taxed estates historically flood museums and public collections — Britain's acceptance-in-lieu scheme exists because death duties once made masterpieces public; this scheme scales that channel. Foundations and endowed institutions grow as dynasties shrink. The private hoard becomes the public collection.

The honest bit, because artists smell cant: this doesn't make art pay. It makes *starting* possible, which is different. And so does everyone else's child, which is the part I want to talk you into, because it's the part that protects yours.

Here's the market your kids actually face. The country is short 410,000 homes. At every viewing, your child bids against buyers whose deposit was transferred, not saved. We ran that experiment — the jubelton: years of €100,000 tax-free gifts for the children of wealthy parents. The state's own evaluation found it pushed up prices and split the young generation into those with rich parents and those without, and we abolished it in 2024 on exactly that finding. But abolishing one subsidy didn't end the arms race: €973 billion is coming down the pipe, and homeowners' children catch up to €463,000 of it while renters' children catch €18,000. Your kids aren't competing against effort; they're competing against other people's inheritances. You can't out-save that. You can outlaw the top of it.

Your own retirement? That's the quiet Dutch advantage: €1.6 trillion in collective pensions means your pension is a pension. Your house doesn't have to be one, and your bequest isn't your children's safety net the way it is in Britain — the floor under your kids stops being your house price and becomes a birthright.

And you already agree, statistically. The CPB found this year that only 12 per cent of us want the erfbelasting abolished, and a majority wants large inheritances taxed *more* — with preferred rates rising with the size of the inheritance, across every age and wealth group. The Dutch position *is* this proposal. It just hasn't been on a ballot.

The honest bit: if your estate really will pass more than €700,000 to a single child, this costs your family something at the very top. Everything below the line stays, the €50,000 per child is added — and the excess funds every child's start, including your grandchildren's.

My ask is one conversation. Tonight, run your family's numbers — €700,000 per child, €50,000 each at 25 — and tell one other parent what you found. Then check the CPB study; don't take my word for it.
