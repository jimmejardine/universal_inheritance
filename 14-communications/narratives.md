# Narratives: The Stories That Carry the Argument

**Executive summary.** Persuasion research consistently finds that a single identifiable person moves audiences where statistics do not; the campaign therefore needs a small, fixed repertoire of stories told with iron discipline. This file specifies the four canonical narratives, the evidence for story-first communication, and the non-negotiable rules every telling must obey.

## Why stories, not statistics

- **The identifiable-victim effect.** Audiences donate more, and shift attitudes more, in response to one named individual than to statistics describing thousands — and adding statistics to the individual story *reduces* its effect (Small, Loewenstein & Slovic's experimental work; to verify exact effect sizes). The opposition already exploits this: every anti-inheritance-tax story is one named farm family, never a distribution table ([10-opposition/media-narratives.md](../10-opposition/media-narratives.md)).
- **Narrative transportation.** Absorption in a story lowers counter-arguing; audiences evaluate a story by coherence, not by generating rebuttals as they do with claims (Green & Brock; to verify).
- **The asymmetry we must correct.** The costs of inheritance concentration are statistical (mobility rates, Gini coefficients — [01-problem/](../01-problem/README.md)); the costs of the tax are anecdotal (the farm, the house). Statistics-versus-story is a fight statistics lose. The fix is not better charts; it is better stories on our side, with charts held in reserve as proof points ([channels.md](channels.md) — the repo website as the reference layer).

## The four canonical narratives

### 1. The Two 25-Year-Olds — the master narrative

Two people, same city, same year of birth. Call them Amy and Ben. Identical talent, identical effort, identical exam results. Amy's grandmother leaves her £2 million; Ben inherits the rent due on the first of the month. Amy buys a flat outright, starts a business with the surplus, absorbs two failures, and is called a success. Ben's salary services Amy's asset. Under Universal Inheritance, Amy still receives £500,000 tax-free — more than most people earn in a decade — and Ben receives £50,000 at 25: a deposit, a training fund, a stake ([02-proposal/endowment.md](../02-proposal/endowment.md)).

*Function:* makes the meritocratic wound visible ([01-problem/meritocracy-legitimacy.md](../01-problem/meritocracy-legitimacy.md)) and delivers the remedy inside the same scene. Every abstract argument in [debate-toolkit.md](debate-toolkit.md) pivots back to this story.

### 2. The Founder and the Heir

Celebrate the founder without reservation: the woman who built the logistics firm from one van deserves every pound and every honour. Then ask the quiet question: what did her grandson build? The story's villain is never the founder — it is the *conversion* of one generation's achievement into another's permanent, unearned position. Carnegie, the archetypal founder, made exactly this argument himself ([05-history/carnegie-1889.md](../05-history/carnegie-1889.md)): the man who dies leaving a dynasty "dies disgraced."

*Function:* splits wealth-admiration (which we keep) from dynasty-tolerance (which we target). Core script for conservative and business audiences ([audience-messaging.md](audience-messaging.md)).

### 3. National Renewal — every generation starts the race fresh

Britain once understood itself as the country where talent could rise. The narrative frames Universal Inheritance as *restoration*, not revolution: every generation re-runs the race from a common starting line, as the post-war settlement briefly and imperfectly promised. The US version speaks the language of the frontier and the self-made — Roosevelt's own case for steep estate taxation ([05-history/roosevelt-1906.md](../05-history/roosevelt-1906.md)).

*Function:* patriotic, forward-looking register for broadcast and political speech; the story a party leader can tell ([12-political-adoption/](../12-political-adoption/README.md)).

### 4. Family Security — for older voters

You are 68. Your house clears the threshold untouched — nothing changes for your children ([02-proposal/thresholds.md](../02-proposal/thresholds.md)). But your four grandchildren each receive £50,000 at 25, whatever happens to house prices, whatever happens to you, whatever care costs consume. The state cannot promise your estate will survive the care home; it *can* promise the endowment. This story addresses the decisive swing segment in the [coalition map](../06-palatability/README.md) on its own terms: not justice for strangers, but security for their own descendants.

*Function:* converts the demographic most hostile in polling ([06-palatability/polling.md](../06-palatability/polling.md)) by re-routing the bequest motive ([04-case-against/bequest-motive.md](../04-case-against/bequest-motive.md)) through the endowment.

## The rules (non-negotiable)

1. **Always a named person.** Amy and Ben, not "young people today." Percentages support; they never lead.
2. **Always the endowment first.** The story reaches "£50k at 25" before it reaches any tax mechanics. If the clip is cut at 20 seconds, the audience must already have heard the benefit.
3. **The villain is the system — the dynasty structure — never the parent.** Parents who want to provide for children are the sympathetic centre of the opposition's story; attack them and lose. Our stories contain no greedy heirs and no villainous mothers: only a rigged starting line and a fix. The £500k threshold exists precisely so that ordinary parental provision is untouched and we can say so in every telling.
4. **Concede reality inside the story.** Amy is not a monster; she is lucky. Realism inoculates ([inoculation.md](inoculation.md)); cartoon villains invite rebuttal.
5. **Stable characters, stable numbers.** The same Amy, the same Ben, the same £50k, for years. Familiarity is the point — the opposition ran "death tax" unchanged for a decade ([naming-and-language.md](naming-and-language.md)).
