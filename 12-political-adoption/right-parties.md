# Right Parties: The Selfish Conservative Case

**Executive summary.** The counter-intuitive but strategically strongest adoption route runs through conservative parties: the receipts tax funds the tax cuts conservatives actually want, speaks native conservative language (meritocracy, founder-capitalism, property-owning democracy), and — decisively — manufactures the asset-owning young voters without whom parties of property face demographic extinction. History shows the right stealing left policies for advantage repeatedly, from Bismarck's welfare state to Disraeli's franchise extension; this file sets out why a "Nixon to China" move on inheritance is the same play.

## The demographic survival problem

Start from the right's own numbers, not the movement's. Conservative parties are parties of asset owners; their historical business model was that voters *became* conservative as they acquired homes, pensions, and savings. That conveyor belt has stopped: youth homeownership has fallen for decades, the age gradient in right-party support has steepened to extinction-curve levels (UK 2017–2024 — to verify; see [electoral-arithmetic.md](electoral-arithmetic.md)), and voters who reach 40 without assets are not converting on schedule.

A party of property whose electorate cannot acquire property has two options: change the electorate's asset position, or die with its cohort. The universal endowment — £50,000 to every 25-year-old ([../02-proposal/endowment.md](../02-proposal/endowment.md)) — is the only policy on any party's menu that *directly manufactures asset owners at scale*: deposits, shareholdings, business capital, pension seeds. Every endowed 25-year-old is a potential future conservative voter created by policy. Thatcher's council-house sales demonstrated the mechanism — asset transfer converting voters (to verify the scale of the effect) — and the endowment is the same mechanism made repeatable and universal. This is not an argument the right can get from any competing proposal: more housebuilding helps eventually; the endowment writes the cheque.

## The tax-cut arithmetic

The second selfish pillar: the revenue funds what conservatives exist to do — cut the taxes their voters hate.

| Move | Framing available to a conservative chancellor |
|---|---|
| Abolish today's IHT for ordinary families | Current UK IHT: 40% above a £325,000 nil-rate band (to verify; residence bands complicate). The proposal: **0% below £500,000 per recipient, 100% above.** For the large majority of families who currently pay or fear IHT, this is a straightforward **tax cut** — and the chancellor gets to say "we abolished inheritance tax for every normal family in Britain" |
| Cut income tax / National Insurance | The receipts stream (net of the endowment — see [../02-proposal/thresholds.md](../02-proposal/thresholds.md) for the arithmetic) can part-fund cuts to taxes on work, converting the package into "tax work less, tax windfalls instead" |
| Fiscal-credibility repair | A right party emerging from a period of fiscal chaos can use a large, growth-neutral revenue base to rebuild market and voter confidence without austerity |

The "0% below £500k versus 40% above £325k" comparison is the single most under-exploited line in the whole debate: correctly presented, the proposal *outflanks the right on its own signature promise* (IHT abolition for ordinary families) while funding it from precisely the estates the promise was never really about. A conservative strategist should want to own that line before an opponent does.

## The ideological fit is native, not borrowed

The awkward secret of conservative inheritance-tax defence is that it defends the wrong people by conservatism's own lights:

- **Meritocracy and the self-made ethos.** Thatcherite conservatism celebrates the grocer's daughter and the garage founder, not the heir. "Founder-capitalism versus rentier-aristocracy" is native conservative language; a 100%-above-threshold receipts tax is arguably *more* Thatcherite than defending unearned dynastic transmission ([../03-case-for/meritocratic-legitimacy.md](../03-case-for/meritocratic-legitimacy.md), [../01-problem/rentier-economy.md](../01-problem/rentier-economy.md)).
- **The TR/Carnegie lineage.** The intellectual pedigree of heavy inheritance taxation runs through a Republican president ([../05-history/roosevelt-1906.md](../05-history/roosevelt-1906.md)) and the era's most celebrated capitalist ([../05-history/carnegie-1889.md](../05-history/carnegie-1889.md)). A conservative adopter is not importing socialism; he is repatriating conservatism.
- **Property-owning democracy, completed.** The phrase is the British right's own (Skelton, Eden, Thatcher — to verify attribution chain). Its logic — a democracy is stable when every citizen owns capital — was never completed because the mechanism (housing) ran out of cheap stock. The endowment completes it: *every 25-year-old a capitalist.* The slogan writes itself and the copyright belongs to the right.
- **Family values, correctly understood.** The design protects everything conservatives say they defend: spouses (exempt), family homes and ordinary bequests (below threshold), working farms and firms (deferred-payment carve-outs, [../02-proposal/carveouts.md](../02-proposal/carveouts.md)). What it ends is precisely what conservative rhetoric already condemns: the idle heir, the trust-fund rentier, the aristocracy of the undead hand.

## Neutralising the inequality attack — permanently

Every conservative campaign now fights on ground where "they govern for the rich" is the left's free ammunition. A right party that enacts the endowment takes that ammunition away *permanently*: no left opponent can out-bid a governing right party that literally capitalised every young citizen. This is the deepest office-seeking logic — not winning the inequality argument but deleting it — and it is available only to the first mover ([wedge-dynamics.md](wedge-dynamics.md) analyses the same move from the other side).

## Precedents: the right steals left policies when it pays

| Precedent | The theft | The selfish logic |
|---|---|---|
| Bismarck, 1880s | Invented state pensions, health and accident insurance | Undercut the socialists by delivering their material programme without their politics |
| Disraeli, 1867 | Doubled the franchise via the Second Reform Act | "Dishing the Whigs": enfranchise the workers and win their gratitude before the Liberals could |
| Peel, 1846 | Repealed the Corn Laws against his own landed interest | Chose the future coalition (industrial, urban) over the declining one |
| Nixon, 1971–72 | Wage-price controls, EPA, China opening | Only a verified anti-communist could go to China; only a verified conservative can tax inheritance without being called a socialist |
| Macmillan/mid-century Tories | Accepted and expanded the welfare state and council housing | Accommodation as electoral survival |
| Thatcher, 1980 | Right to Buy — a mass transfer of state assets to tenants | Manufactured Conservative-voting homeowners directly |

The pattern: right parties adopt left-coded policies when (a) a demographic or coalition threat makes the status quo untenable, and (b) the policy can be re-narrated in the right's own language. Both conditions are satisfiable here — (a) by the age curve, (b) by property-owning democracy and meritocracy framing. The "Nixon to China" asymmetry is real and valuable: a right party proposing this is read as pragmatic modernisation; a left party proposing the identical text is read as confiscation. Adoption is therefore *cheaper* for the right.

## The risks, from the conservative strategist's chair

| Risk | Weight | De-risking |
|---|---|---|
| Donor revolt — the party's funders are the tax's payers | The heaviest risk; right parties are structurally donor-dependent | Living donors keep everything while alive; charitable and foundation routes remain ([../02-proposal/carveouts.md](../02-proposal/carveouts.md)); some founder-donors are genuinely Carnegie-minded (recruit them first as validators — [../13-movement/messengers.md](../13-movement/messengers.md)); the small-donor and member base gains from accompanying income-tax cuts |
| Base accusation of betrayal ("not conservative") | Moderate; manageable with sequencing | Lead with abolition-for-ordinary-families and income-tax cuts; use stepping-stones ([stepping-stones.md](stepping-stones.md)) so no single announcement reads as revolution |
| Press hostility from proprietor-owned titles | High in the UK ([../10-opposition/elite-media-capture.md](../10-opposition/elite-media-capture.md)) | The tax-cut framing splits the press's readership from its proprietors; broadcast debate prep in [../14-communications/debate-toolkit.md](../14-communications/debate-toolkit.md) |
| Capital flight narrative | Standard | Evidence and design answers in [../08-capital-flight/evidence.md](../08-capital-flight/evidence.md) and [../08-capital-flight/exit-tax-design.md](../08-capital-flight/exit-tax-design.md) |

The donor risk is the binding constraint and deserves candour: a full-scheme conservative adoption likely requires either a leader with unusual internal authority, a party funding base already diversified toward members and small donors, or a crisis window in which fiscal necessity overrides donor preference ([../11-geographies/crisis-windows.md](../11-geographies/crisis-windows.md)). Short of that, the realistic conservative route is partial: stepping-stones 1, 2, 4, and 6 ([stepping-stones.md](stepping-stones.md)) plus a pilot endowment, each sellable as ordinary tax reform.

## Bottom line

The conservative case is not a stretch; it is the policy's strongest natural home in every respect except donor structure. Meritocracy, property-owning democracy, tax cuts on work, and demographic survival are the right's own goals, and the receipts tax funds all four. The movement's task with right parties is therefore narrow and concrete: cultivate founder-class validators to crack the donor constraint, and keep the "0% below £500k" tax-cut framing polished and waiting for the leader who needs it.
