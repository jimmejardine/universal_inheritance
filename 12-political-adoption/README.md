# 12 — Political Adoption: The Self-Interested Routes

**Executive summary.** This section maps the mechanisms by which incumbent political parties would adopt the recipient-based 100% inheritance tax and universal endowment ([../02-proposal/README.md](../02-proposal/README.md)) *for their own selfish reasons* — votes, money, coalition management, and the division of opponents. The organising claim: no party needs moral conversion; for every major party family there exists a self-interested route to at least partial adoption, and the movement's job ([../13-movement/theory-of-change.md](../13-movement/theory-of-change.md)) is to lower the price and raise the payoff of each route.

## The core claim

Parties do not adopt policies because they are right. They adopt policies because the policy is the answer to a problem the party already has. The analytical task is therefore not "how do we persuade politicians this is just?" but "which existing party problem does this policy solve, from the party strategist's chair?" Each file in this section is written from that chair.

Three consequences follow:

1. **Multiple independent routes.** The policy does not depend on any one party's fortunes. The left route (funding credibility, youth turnout), the right route (tax cuts, demographic survival, meritocratic branding), and the centrist route (differentiation, coalition price) can each carry the policy alone. See [left-parties.md](left-parties.md), [right-parties.md](right-parties.md), [centrists-and-liberals.md](centrists-and-liberals.md).
2. **Adoption is a ladder, not a leap.** No party will adopt the full scheme in one manifesto. The realistic path is a sequence of individually deniable steps, each defensible as "sensible reform" — see [stepping-stones.md](stepping-stones.md) and [../15-transition/sequencing.md](../15-transition/sequencing.md).
3. **The demographics do the heavy lifting.** The vote-maximising position on inherited wealth is shifting mechanically as the non-inheriting share of the electorate grows and the estate-owning cohort is replaced. Parties that move early capture the shift; parties that move late are dragged. See [electoral-arithmetic.md](electoral-arithmetic.md).

## The movement's role: change the payoff matrix

The files in this section repeatedly return to one point: advocates cannot make a party adopt anything, but they can alter the inputs to the party's own calculation. Concretely, the movement ([../13-movement/README.md](../13-movement/README.md)) lowers the price of adoption by:

- **Supplying oven-ready drafting and costings** so adoption carries no internal policy-development cost ([policy-entrepreneurs.md](policy-entrepreneurs.md)).
- **Pre-testing language and inoculating against attacks** ([../14-communications/README.md](../14-communications/README.md), [../14-communications/inoculation.md](../14-communications/inoculation.md)) so the communications risk is priced down.
- **Building visible constituency demand** so the vote payoff is legible in the party's own polling ([../06-palatability/polling.md](../06-palatability/polling.md)).
- **Providing deniable stepping-stones** so no single decision is career-ending ([stepping-stones.md](stepping-stones.md)).

And raises the payoff by:

- **Making the endowment a brandable deliverable** — "the party that gave every 25-year-old £50,000" is an asset comparable to founding-era welfare institutions ([left-parties.md](left-parties.md)).
- **Demonstrating the wedge value** against whichever coalition opposes it ([wedge-dynamics.md](wedge-dynamics.md)).
- **Timing offers to windows** — fiscal crises, leadership contests, coalition negotiations — when parties are actively shopping for ideas ([policy-entrepreneurs.md](policy-entrepreneurs.md), [../11-geographies/crisis-windows.md](../11-geographies/crisis-windows.md)).
