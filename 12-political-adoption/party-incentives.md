# Party Incentives: Why Parties Adopt Outsider Ideas

**Executive summary.** Political science models parties as pursuing three goals — votes, office, and policy — and a party adopts an outsider idea when doing so advances at least one of these goals at acceptable cost to the others. This file sets out that framework, the four recurring self-interested motives for adoption, the adoption ladder from backbench bill to government policy, and the design properties that make a policy "adoptable" — all of which the movement can supply or manipulate.

## The three-goals framework

The standard framework (associated with Kaare Strøm's work on party behaviour — to verify exact formulation) treats parties as trading off three objectives:

| Goal | The party as… | What it optimises | How this policy can serve it |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Vote-seeking** | Downsian competitor | Vote share at the next election | A benefit visible to ~every voter under 40, funded by a tax ~90%+ of voters never pay (to verify against [../02-proposal/thresholds.md](../02-proposal/thresholds.md)) |
| **Office-seeking** | Coalition negotiator | Cabinet seats, portfolios, survival in government | A distinctive, tradeable coalition demand; a revenue source that closes budget negotiations |
| **Policy-seeking** | Vehicle for factions | Ideological output; internal faction satisfaction | Egalitarian factions (left), meritocratic factions (right), opportunity factions (liberal) each get a native win |

Anthony Downs's median-voter logic supplies the vote-seeking baseline: parties converge on positions that maximise expected votes. The critical implication for this project is that the vote-maximising position on inherited wealth is not fixed — it moves with the composition of the electorate, and [electoral-arithmetic.md](electoral-arithmetic.md) argues it is moving mechanically in the policy's favour. A party does not need to be persuaded that the policy is good; it needs to be shown, in its own polling, that the median-relevant voter has moved.

Real parties are none of these purely. Leaderships tend to weight office; activists weight policy; campaign professionals weight votes. Adoption strategies should therefore be **multi-channel**: give the leadership an office argument, the activists a policy argument, and the professionals a vote argument, simultaneously. The files on [left-parties.md](left-parties.md), [right-parties.md](right-parties.md), and [centrists-and-liberals.md](centrists-and-liberals.md) do exactly this for each family.

## Four selfish motives for adopting an outsider idea

Historically, established parties absorb ideas from outside their mainstream for four recurring reasons:

### 1. Neutralise a flank

When a challenger party or internal faction is bleeding votes or energy from the main party, adopting a version of the challenger's signature idea removes its reason to exist. Classic cases: mainstream parties absorbing green policies in the 1990s–2000s; centre-right parties absorbing anti-immigration positions to squeeze populist challengers. For this policy: a left party facing a green-left or populist-left challenger, or a right party facing a young-libertarian or "founder-capitalism" insurgency, can adopt the endowment to defuse it.

### 2. Wedge the opponent

A policy is valuable to Party A precisely because it splits Party B's coalition. The endowment-funded receipts tax divides asset-rich donors from asset-poor voters inside *both* major coalitions, which means each side can weaponise it against the other — analysed in full in [wedge-dynamics.md](wedge-dynamics.md). A party strategist does not need to like the policy to want the other side impaled on it.

### 3. Answer "how will you pay for it?"

Every spending party has a permanent credibility problem: its promises are attacked as unfunded. A large, hypothecated revenue source that falls on almost no voters is the rarest commodity in fiscal politics. The receipts tax is, from this angle, not a policy but a **funding answer** — and funding answers get adopted by parties that have already decided what they want to spend money on. See [left-parties.md](left-parties.md).

### 4. Rejuvenate the brand

Parties in long-term decline, or recovering from a heavy defeat, shop for a "signature idea" that signals renewal — something big enough to force commentators to re-describe the party. Post-defeat policy reviews are the standard vehicle (and a key adoption surface — see [policy-entrepreneurs.md](policy-entrepreneurs.md)). The universal endowment is well-shaped for this: concrete, universal, generational, and unclaimed by any incumbent brand.

## The adoption ladder

Adoption is not a single decision. It is a ladder of escalating commitment, each rung individually deniable:

| Rung | Vehicle | Commitment level | Who decides | Reversal cost |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Backbench bill / opposition-day debate / parliamentary question | Individual MP only | One legislator | None — "raising a debate" |
| 2 | Party commission, policy review, or affiliated think-tank report | Arms-length | Review chair, leadership tolerance | Low — "options were examined" |
| 3 | Conference motion / party-platform plank | Activist base | Conference floor, NEC-equivalents | Moderate — leadership can still bury it |
| 4 | Manifesto line (often a stepping-stone version — see [stepping-stones.md](stepping-stones.md)) | Full-party | Leadership + manifesto team | High — a broken pledge |
| 5 | Government policy: Green/White Paper, Budget measure, Bill | State | Cabinet, Treasury | Very high |

Two strategic implications. First, the movement should be running candidates for *every rung simultaneously* — a sympathetic backbencher, a review chair, a conference motion, a manifesto drafter — rather than waiting for rungs to be climbed in order. Second, opponents attack the ladder at rung 2–3, where ideas are killable cheaply ([../10-opposition/attack-playbook.md](../10-opposition/attack-playbook.md)); inoculation resources should concentrate there.

## What makes a policy "adoptable"

From the strategist's chair, an idea's merits matter less than its handling properties. The adoptable policy has:

1. **A clear beneficiary map.** The strategist can see, constituency by constituency, who gains. The endowment gives every party a printable list: every resident aged under 25, plus their parents and grandparents. [electoral-arithmetic.md](electoral-arithmetic.md) supplies the map.
2. **Oven-ready drafting.** Bills, costings, and implementation notes that survive Treasury/CBO-equivalent scrutiny, produced outside the party at no cost to it. This is the think-tank pipeline's product ([policy-entrepreneurs.md](policy-entrepreneurs.md)); the proposal section ([../02-proposal/mechanism.md](../02-proposal/mechanism.md), [../07-implementation/administration.md](../07-implementation/administration.md)) is this project's contribution.
3. **Deniable stepping-stones.** A version small enough to adopt without owning the endpoint — "we are closing an abusive relief," not "we are abolishing inheritance." The full menu is in [stepping-stones.md](stepping-stones.md).
4. **Pre-tested language.** Framings already focus-grouped and survivable on broadcast media ([../06-palatability/framing.md](../06-palatability/framing.md), [../14-communications/debate-toolkit.md](../14-communications/debate-toolkit.md)).
5. **A survivable attack surface.** The strategist's first question is "what is the attack ad?" The design must have a pre-built answer for the farm, the family business, and the family home — which is why the carve-outs in [../02-proposal/carveouts.md](../02-proposal/carveouts.md) are political infrastructure, not policy detail.

## Failure modes to design against

- **Premature maximalism.** Presenting only the full scheme forces every party to reject it; rung-4 adoption of the full scheme without rungs 1–3 has essentially no precedent for tax policy of this scale.
- **Single-party capture.** If the policy becomes the property of one party, the other side's incentive flips from "steal it" to "kill it." The movement should maintain live adoption routes in at least two party families at all times ([../13-movement/coalitions.md](../13-movement/coalitions.md)).
- **Activist-only adoption.** Conference-floor victories that the leadership resents get buried in manifestos and blamed for defeats. Rung 3 without a rung-4 sponsor is a trap.
- **Adoption without inoculation.** A party that adopts before the attack-response toolkit exists will be burned, and a burned party salts the ground for a decade ([../10-opposition/media-narratives.md](../10-opposition/media-narratives.md)).

## Bottom line

Parties are not audiences to be convinced; they are machines with known inputs. The movement's leverage is over four inputs: the visibility of the beneficiary map, the cost of policy development, the deniability of the first steps, and the pre-tested survivability of the language. All four are cheap for outsiders to supply and expensive for parties to build themselves — which is precisely why supplying them buys influence.
