# Executive Summary

**Thesis.** Concentrated inherited wealth is a primary engine of the pathologies we label "late-stage capitalism": runaway inequality, collapsing social mobility, a housing market decoupled from labour income, democratic capture by dynastic interests, declining economic dynamism, and the erosion of meritocratic legitimacy. A 100% inheritance tax — specified as a 100% *marginal* rate above a generous per-recipient lifetime threshold, with proceeds funding a universal citizen's endowment — addresses these pathologies at the source without requiring the state to micromanage the rest of the economy. And the moment is arriving: the largest intergenerational wealth transfer in history (~$85–100tn in the US alone, to verify) is now underway, making inheritance the defining economic fact of the next three decades whether or not policy responds.

## The proposal in five commitments

| Commitment | In plain terms |
|---|---|
| Recipient-based, lifetime-cumulative | The tax falls on what a *person receives* over their lifetime, not on what a *dead person leaves*. Nothing is taken from the deceased. |
| 0% below £500k / $750k per recipient | Over 90% of heirs pay nothing (to verify); a couple's two children receive £1m tax-free between them. For most families who pay inheritance tax today, this is a tax **cut**. |
| 100% above the threshold | Beyond a generous inheritance, the excess funds every citizen's start rather than a dynasty's continuation. |
| A £50k / $60k endowment at 25, for everyone | Universal inheritance, unconditional, funded by the tax. The manifesto leads with this, not the tax. |
| Engineered against the historical failure pattern | Deferral for farms and businesses, spousal exemption, exit tails, trust look-through — each answering a specific failure that killed a real inheritance tax somewhere. |

## Is the thesis reasonable?

Yes — and not novel. Thomas Paine (1797) proposed the tax-plus-endowment pairing; John Stuart Mill (1848) originated the recipient-based cap; Andrew Carnegie (1889) and Theodore Roosevelt (1906) supplied the capitalist and conservative cases; twentieth-century US and UK top rates reached 77–80%; Atkinson, Ackerman–Alstott, and Piketty rebuilt the modern case, and optimal-tax theory puts efficient inheritance-tax rates at 50–80%+ for most recipients (to verify). The intellectual lineage is liberal-capitalist, not confiscatory-socialist — a point opponents' framing depends on obscuring. See [05-history/](05-history/) and [03-case-for/](03-case-for/).

## What the project now covers

The sixteen sections answer four questions:

1. **What is wrong?** Eleven diagnostic chapters trace the ailments to one root: capital compounding across generations, largely untaxed, at the moment of transfer. [01-problem/](01-problem/), with the historical record in [05-history/](05-history/).
2. **What exactly do we propose?** A full specification — mechanism, thresholds, endowment, carve-outs, anti-avoidance, revenue model, entrenchment ([02-proposal/](02-proposal/)) — plus a designed answer to every concrete difficulty, from valuing private companies to the nepo-job problem ([07-implementation/](07-implementation/)) and a phased path from today's law ([15-transition/](15-transition/)).
3. **Does it survive scrutiny?** The five strongest objections are steelmanned with residuals honestly left standing ([04-case-against/](04-case-against/)); capital flight gets its own evidence-led treatment — migration elasticities are real but low, concentrated, and a design variable ([08-capital-flight/](08-capital-flight/)); and the second-order consequences, welcome and unwelcome, are mapped ([16-second-order/](16-second-order/)).
4. **How does it actually happen?** Polling and framing evidence ([06-palatability/](06-palatability/)), a rebuttal armoury ([09-objections/](09-objections/)), the opposition's playbook pre-empted ([10-opposition/](10-opposition/)), the likeliest adopting societies ranked ([11-geographies/](11-geographies/)), the self-interested case made to each party family ([12-political-adoption/](12-political-adoption/)), and movement-building and communications doctrine ([13-movement/](13-movement/), [14-communications/](14-communications/)).

## What we concede

Credibility requires stating what the proposal cannot do. It cannot tax the untaxable inheritances — parental time, education, networks — though it removes the compounding capital that converts them into dynasties. It will lose some fortunes to emigration; the design keeps that loss small and survivable, not zero. Its transition is its most vulnerable decade. And a genuine value conflict with the liberty to give remains at the top of the distribution — a price to be argued for openly, not defined away. See [04-case-against/](04-case-against/).

## The single most important design choice

Every serious historical proposal pairs the tax on large inheritances with a universal benefit to those currently inheriting nothing. Pure confiscation without the redistribution half has no credible intellectual proponent and no political future. The endowment is therefore not an add-on but the load-bearing element three times over: **morally** (it converts the tax from taking to giving every citizen a real start), **politically** (a concrete £50k promise to every young voter — and every swing voter's grandchildren — is the single biggest polling lever), and **structurally** (once enacted, ~700,000 new stakeholders a year make repeal politically prohibitive — the endowment is the tax's bodyguard).

**Lead with the universal inheritance. The 100% rate is how it is paid for.**
