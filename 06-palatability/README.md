# 06 — Palatability: Bringing the Idea Mainstream

## The central puzzle

Inheritance tax is consistently one of the *most unpopular* taxes in Western polling — despite affecting a tiny minority directly and despite majorities nominally supporting reduced inequality. Solving this puzzle is the strategic task of the manifesto.

### Working hypotheses for why it polls badly

- **"Death tax" framing.** Focus-grouped by opponents for decades; it triggers family, grief, and parental-love associations rather than distributional ones.
- **Lottery-ticket psychology.** Voters model themselves as *potential* rich, not as the median they are now. They oppose taxes on the person they hope to become.
- **Hard-case salience.** Media coverage disproportionately features sympathetic farms and family businesses; the beneficiaries of redistribution are statistical.
- **Abstract benefit, concrete loss.** "Losing Dad's house" is visualisable; "better public services" is diffuse.

## Reframing levers (in descending importance)

1. **Make the benefit concrete and individual.** A £50,000 citizen's endowment at age 25 is a promise every young voter can see. This is the single biggest polling lever — it converts the proposal from "they take" to "you get."
2. **Recipient-based language.** "Tax on *receiving* a million pounds you did not earn" polls very differently from "tax on your dad's estate." The linguistic shift is substantive, not cosmetic.
3. **Meritocracy framing.** "Earn your way, don't inherit your way." Compatible with centre-right self-image and hard to attack without conceding the dynastic point.
4. **Generational framing.** Housing unaffordability is the most legible single issue to voters under 45. Inheritance tax reform is the obvious supply-side answer to "why can't young people buy homes?"
5. **Protect visible hard cases first.** Family-farm and family-business carve-outs are not concessions to opponents — they are prerequisites for everything else.

## Coalition map (first pass)

| Group | Alignment | Lever |
|---|---|---|
| Priced-out young voters | Strong ally | Universal endowment; housing framing |
| Small-c conservatives uneasy with dynastic wealth | Latent ally | Meritocracy framing; Carnegie lineage |
| Classical-liberal meritocrats | Persuadable | Equality-of-opportunity argument |
| Left-progressives | Base | Already aligned; not the persuasion target |
| Top 1–5% of wealth distribution | Opponent | Not the persuasion target |
| Older middle-class homeowners | Swing — decisive | Primary-residence carve-out; endowment for their grandchildren |
| Small business owners | Swing | Business carve-outs; visible design |
| Rural voters | Swing | Farm carve-outs; trust in delivery |

## Research gaps

- Cross-national polling on *recipient-based* vs. *estate-based* framings (Tax Justice UK, Demos, Fabian Society).
- Polling on universal-endowment proposals specifically (UK Resolution Foundation, IPPR).
- Case studies of successful inheritance-tax *campaigns* — there are few; the policy's high-water marks were usually byproducts of wartime fiscal needs, not campaigns.
- Case studies of *failed* anti-inheritance-tax campaigns (US "death tax repeal" 2001 as a negative case).
