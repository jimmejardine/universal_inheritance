# Framing: The Psychology of Inheritance-Tax Opinion

**Executive summary.** Inheritance-tax unpopularity is not a fixed public preference but the output of identifiable cognitive mechanisms — loss aversion, misperceived incidence, prospective-mobility bias, and deservingness heuristics — most of which were deliberately weaponised by the "death tax" campaign. Each mechanism has a documented counter, and every counter is already embedded as a design choice in [`../02-proposal/`](../02-proposal/README.md); this file maps the psychology to the design.

## Key findings

- "Death tax" is the most successful piece of fiscal linguistic engineering on record: a Luntz-tested relabelling that moved the frame from distribution to grief, family, and double taxation (see [case-studies.md](case-studies.md)) [1][2].
- Loss aversion explains why a tax with ~2–5% incidence generates majority opposition: the estate reads as an *endowment already owned by the family*, so the tax registers as a certain loss, while the public benefit registers as a diffuse, uncertain gain [3].
- The Benabou–Ok POUM hypothesis (prospects of upward mobility) and lottery-ticket psychology explain opposition among people who will never pay: voters assess the tax against the person they hope to be or hope their children to be [4].
- Deservingness research (Alesina, Stantcheva & Teso; Almås et al.) shows attitudes to redistribution hinge on *effort vs luck* attributions — and inheritance is the purest case of luck, which is why explicit source-of-wealth framing flips preferences ([polling.md](polling.md)) [5][6].
- Recipient-based framing ("a tax on receiving £1m unearned") polls differently from estate-based framing ("a tax on your father's estate") in survey experiments (to verify) — the design in [`../02-proposal/mechanism.md`](../02-proposal/mechanism.md) makes the better frame literally true.
- Moral-foundations analysis suggests conservatives are reachable through *proportionality* (reward should track contribution) and *merit* framings, not harm/care framings [7].

## 1. "Death tax": the case study in linguistic engineering

The term predates the 1990s, but its systematic deployment is credited to the US repeal coalition and pollster Frank Luntz, whose memos instructed Republicans to say "death tax" in every utterance because it tested dramatically worse for the tax than "estate tax" or "inheritance tax" (to verify against Luntz's published memos and Graetz & Shapiro's account) [1][2]. The relabelling worked because it performed three moves at once:

1. **Universalised the payer.** Everyone dies; almost nobody leaves a taxable estate. The name recruits the 98% who will never pay into the constituency of the 2% who will.
2. **Changed the taxed event.** "Estate" is a stock of wealth; "death" is a grief event. Taxing wealth is contestable; "taxing death" sounds like desecration.
3. **Implied double taxation.** "You're taxed when you earn it and taxed again when you die" — rhetorically potent, analytically wrong for the (mostly unrealised, never-taxed) capital gains that dominate large estates, but the rebuttal takes a paragraph and the attack takes four words.

The counter-lesson is not to out-argue the label but to change the taxed event *in the design itself*: this proposal taxes **receipt by a living person**, not death, not the deceased, not the estate ([`../02-proposal/mechanism.md`](../02-proposal/mechanism.md)). "Nothing is taken from the dead; something very large is given to the living" is a true sentence under this design and an impossible one under existing IHT. Naming doctrine lives in [`../14-communications/naming-and-language.md`](../14-communications/naming-and-language.md).

## 2. Loss aversion and the endowment effect

Kahneman–Tversky prospect theory: losses loom roughly twice as large as equivalent gains, and people code outcomes against a reference point [3]. Applied here:

| Mechanism | How it operates against the tax | Design counter |
|---|---|---|
| Reference point = "the family's money" | Heirs (and voters imagining themselves as heirs) treat the parental estate as already theirs; the tax is a *loss from an owned endowment*, not a forgone windfall | Recipient-based, lifetime framing resets the reference point to zero receipts; the untaxed £500k is the *gain* |
| Concentrated loss vs diffuse gain | "Losing Dad's house" is vivid; "better public services" is statistical | Hypothecation to a named, dated, personal £50k endowment converts the diffuse gain into an owned entitlement — creating a *counter-endowment effect* ([`../02-proposal/endowment.md`](../02-proposal/endowment.md)) |
| Certainty of loss vs uncertainty of benefit | The tax is certain; the benefit depends on trusting government delivery | Annual endowment statements from age 16 make the future grant psychologically owned years before receipt |

The deepest strategic point: the endowment does not merely compensate — it *recruits loss aversion to the tax's defence*. Once a cohort holds statements promising £50,000, repeal becomes the confiscation of an owned asset. This is the Alaska Permanent Fund dynamic, and it is why [`../05-history/abolitions.md`](../05-history/abolitions.md) identifies the missing beneficiary as the structural cause of every abolition.

## 3. Lottery-ticket psychology and POUM

Benabou & Ok (2001) formalised why the poor may oppose redistribution: if people expect upward mobility, they vote their expected future position, not their current one [4]. For inheritance the bias is doubled — voters over-estimate both their own prospects and their children's, and media coverage of fortunes inflates the perceived probability of being rich. Survey evidence that Americans wildly overestimate mobility (Alesina, Stantcheva & Teso 2018) while Europeans underestimate it explains part of the transatlantic gap in redistribution preferences [5].

Counters that survive this bias rather than fighting it:

- **Raise the threshold above the dream.** At £500k per recipient — with a four-child family passing £2m untaxed ([`../02-proposal/thresholds.md`](../02-proposal/thresholds.md)) — the realistic aspiration of almost every voter fits *inside* the 0% band. The lottery ticket is not taxed; only the jackpot the voter demonstrably will not win.
- **Give them an actual ticket.** The endowment is a guaranteed £50k — a certain claim that outperforms the fantasy claim for everyone below roughly the 95th percentile. "Stop defending a lottery you've lost; collect the prize you're owed" is the mobility bias turned around.
- **Mobility data as information treatment.** Kuziemko et al. and Alesina et al. both show mobility-pessimism information shifts redistribution preferences [5] — but see the durability caveat in [polling.md](polling.md).

## 4. Deservingness: effort vs luck

The fairness literature (Alesina & Angeletos; Almås, Cappelen & Tungodden; Stantcheva's open-ended elicitations) converges on a robust result: people are not egalitarians or libertarians in general — they are *choice egalitarians* who accept inequality from effort and reject inequality from luck [5][6]. Inheritance is the limiting case of luck: the recipient did nothing. This is why Fisman et al. find punitive preferences once wealth is labelled inherited ([polling.md](polling.md)).

The framing task is therefore to keep the *recipient's* desert in focus and out of the shadow of the *donor's* desert — because the opposition's strongest move is to switch the subject: "she earned it, she should decide where it goes." Both intuitions are genuine; the winner is whichever the frame activates. Responses:

- **Concede the donor's desert entirely.** The design does: the earner spends freely in life, gives to charity untaxed, and passes £500k per recipient. What is capped is not the donor's earning but the *recipient's unearned receipt* — see [`../03-case-for/ethical.md`](../03-case-for/ethical.md) and [`../09-objections/fairness.md`](../09-objections/fairness.md).
- **Name the third party.** The dispute is not donor vs state; it is heir vs every other 25-year-old. "Why should his start in life be £5m and yours £0?" restores the luck frame.

## 5. Moral foundations: reaching conservatives without borrowing left-wing language

Moral Foundations Theory (Haidt, Graham) finds conservatives weight proportionality, loyalty, authority, and sanctity alongside the harm/care and equality foundations that dominate progressive rhetoric [7]. Inheritance abolition is unusual among redistributive policies in scoring *natively well* on conservative foundations:

| Foundation | Anti-tax reading | Available pro-reform reading |
|---|---|---|
| Proportionality / fairness-as-desert | "He earned it, don't take it" | "The heir earned nothing — reward must track contribution" (the Carnegie argument, [`../05-history/carnegie-1889.md`](../05-history/carnegie-1889.md)) |
| Sanctity / degradation | "Taxing death is ghoulish" | "Idle heirs are degraded by unearned fortune" — purity-of-merit; see [`../03-case-for/heirs-burden.md`](../03-case-for/heirs-burden.md) and [`../16-second-order/heirs-and-work.md`](../16-second-order/heirs-and-work.md) |
| Liberty | "Confiscation by the state" | "Aristocracy of wealth is the old enemy of the free market" — anti-dynasty as anti-feudalism ([`../00-capitalism/feudal-remnant.md`](../00-capitalism/feudal-remnant.md)) |
| Loyalty / family | "Family comes first" | "Every family's children get the endowment; four children inherit £2m untaxed" |

The operational translation of this table — which messenger says which sentence to which audience — belongs to [`../14-communications/audience-messaging.md`](../14-communications/audience-messaging.md) and [`../13-movement/messengers.md`](../13-movement/messengers.md); the bloc-by-bloc application is in [coalition.md](coalition.md).

## 6. Counter-framing risks: where our own frames can backfire

Framing is symmetric warfare, and three of our levers carry documented failure modes:

- **Reactance.** Messages perceived as manipulative trigger boomerang effects (psychological-reactance literature, Brehm). "Death tax" survived exposure as a focus-grouped term because it flattered existing intuitions; an "engineered-sounding" counter-label that fights those intuitions head-on will be tagged as spin. The naming standard in [`../14-communications/naming-and-language.md`](../14-communications/naming-and-language.md) must therefore *describe the design truthfully* (receipts, endowment) rather than euphemise the tax.
- **Envy attribution.** Deservingness framings aimed at heirs can be re-coded by opponents as "the politics of envy" — a phrase with a long UK pedigree. The defence is to keep the *beneficiary* in the foreground (every 25-year-old) and the *heir* in the background; attack the system of dynastic transmission, never the named family ([`../10-opposition/personal-attacks.md`](../10-opposition/personal-attacks.md) covers the reverse direction).
- **Overclaiming the endowment.** If £50k is sold as solving housing, and housing stays broken, the whole package inherits the disappointment — the Child Trust Fund's obscurity in reverse ([case-studies.md](case-studies.md)). Promise a *floor and a start*, not a transformation; the second-order effects are catalogued honestly in [`../16-second-order/endowment-effects.md`](../16-second-order/endowment-effects.md).

Each risk argues for the same discipline: frames must be load-bearing restatements of true design features, because this proposal will face the best-resourced adversarial fact-checking of any tax reform in decades ([`../10-opposition/attack-playbook.md`](../10-opposition/attack-playbook.md)).
